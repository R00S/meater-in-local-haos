# Short Ribs × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Braising is not one of several options for short ribs — it is the method for which short ribs exist as a culinary cut. The English short rib (bone-in, chuck end, ribs 1–5 or the shorter plate end, ribs 6–10) is composed of heavily worked intercostal and serratus ventralis muscle fibres bound together by dense sheets of collagen: raw, this structure is rubbery and resistant; after three or more hours of moist heat at 88–96 °C (190–205 °F), the collagen hydrolyses into gelatin, the fat renders into the braising liquid, and the fibres that would be inedibly tough at medium-rare temperature become pull-apart tender with a silky, self-enriching mouth texture.

The method works because the braising liquid maintains the meat at or near 100 °C from the outside while the centre equilibrates — very slowly, because collagen and fat conduct heat poorly — to 88–96 °C. This is 30–40 °C above the temperature at which standard steak is served, and it is only reachable without drying out because the surrounding liquid replenishes the surface moisture that evaporative cooking would otherwise remove. The three or more hours of sustained heat at this range complete collagen-to-gelatin conversion throughout the entire cross-section of the rib.

Probe placement for braised short ribs is straightforward but requires inserting the probe before the meat goes into the braising liquid: place the probe from the flat cut end of the rib (the surface furthest from the bone), aiming towards the geometric centre of the largest muscle section. The probe tip must be surrounded by lean muscle, not resting against the bone or in a large pocket of intramuscular fat — both give false-low readings because they are slower to equalise than the surrounding lean fibres.

## Styckesprofil

Brässering är inte ett av flera alternativ för revbensspjäll — det är metoden som revbensspjäll som kulinarisk styckdel är gjord för. Det engelska revbensspjället (med ben, chuckände, revben 1–5 eller det kortare tallriksänden, revben 6–10) består av hårt arbetade mellangärdes- och serratus-ventralis-muskelfibrer bundna av täta kollagenlagerskivor: rå är strukturen gummiaktig och motständig; efter tre eller fler timmar av fuktig värme vid 88–96 °C (190–205 °F) hydrolyseras kollagenet till gelatin, fettet smälter ner i bräseringsvätskan och fibrerna som hade varit oätliga vid medium-rare-temperatur blir mjuka som smör med en silkeslen munbild.

Metoden fungerar eftersom bräseringsvätskan håller köttet nära 100 °C utifrån medan mitten långsamt equilibreras — mycket långsamt, eftersom kollagen och fett leder värme dåligt — till 88–96 °C. Det är 30–40 °C över den temperatur som en normal biff serveras vid, och det är bara möjligt utan uttorkning eftersom den omgivande vätskan ersätter den ytfukt som avdunstning annars skulle ta bort. De tre eller fler timmarna av ihållande värme vid detta intervall fulländar kollagen-till-gelatin-omvandlingen i hela snittets tvärsnitt.

Sondens placering för bräserade revbensspjäll är enkel men kräver att sonden sätts in innan köttet läggs i bräseringsvätskan: sätt in sonden från det skurna ändets sida (ytan längst från benet) och sikta mot det geometriska centret i den största muskelns tvärsnitt. Sondens spets måste omges av magert muskelkött, inte vila mot benet eller i en stor ficka av intramuskulärt fett — båda ger för låga avläsningar eftersom de equiliseras långsammare än de omgivande magra fibrerna.

## Source recipes

### 1. Nagi Maehashi — Braised Beef Short Ribs in Red Wine Sauce (Australian modern tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, recipetineats.com/braised-beef-short-ribs-in-red-wine-sauce/  
**Serves**: 5 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 3 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 5–6 bone-in beef short ribs, 300–400 g / 10–14 oz each
- 7 g (1½ tsp) salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 3 garlic cloves, crushed
- 1 large onion (brown, yellow or white), chopped
- 2 celery ribs, chopped
- 2 carrots, chopped
- 30 g (2 tbsp) tomato paste
- 500 ml (2 cups) dry full-bodied red wine (cabernet sauvignon, merlot, or shiraz)
- 500 ml (2 cups) low-sodium beef stock
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme (optional)
- 2 bay leaves

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 160 °C (325 °F).
2. Season short ribs all over with salt and pepper.
3. Heat oil in a large ovenproof pot over high heat. Brown half the ribs aggressively all over, 5–7 minutes total. Remove and repeat with remaining ribs. Remove all ribs.
4. Reduce heat to medium. Add onion and garlic to the same pot; cook 2 minutes. Add carrot and celery; cook 5 minutes until softened.
5. Add tomato paste; cook 1 minute. Add red wine, beef stock, thyme and bay leaves; stir until tomato paste dissolves.
6. Return short ribs to the pot, arranging so the meaty side is submerged (top with water if necessary to cover). Insert the MEATER probe from the flat cut-end of one rib, pushing the tip to the geometric centre of the thickest muscle section, clear of the bone.
7. Cover with lid and transfer to the oven. Braise 3 hours, or until the meat pulls apart easily with two forks.
8. **Pull at 90–93 °C (194–199 °F).** At this internal temperature the collagen has fully converted and the ribs will show Maehashi's doneness indicator: meat pulls free of the bone with light pressure from a fork.
9. Remove ribs carefully, keeping the meat on the bone. Strain the sauce, return to the pot, and simmer to reduce to a syrupy consistency. Adjust salt and pepper.
10. Serve ribs with sauce spooned over. Final serving temperature: **88–93 °C / 190–199 °F** (fall-apart, gelatin-rich, deep red-wine flavour).

### 2. Suzy Karadsheh — Sage-Wine Braised Beef Short Ribs and Potatoes (Mediterranean / American tradition)
**Source**: Suzy Karadsheh, themediterraneandish.com/braised-beef-short-ribs-and-potatoes/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 2 hr 45 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.4 kg (3 lb) bone-in beef short ribs, fat trimmed, cut into 7 cm (3-inch) pieces
- Vegetable oil, for browning
- 7 g (1½ tsp) seasoned salt (divided)
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 5 g (2 tsp) hot paprika
- 2 g (1 tsp) ground green cardamom
- 800–900 g (2–3 large) russet potatoes, peeled, cut into 5 cm pieces
- 3 stalks celery, roughly chopped
- 1 large red onion, roughly chopped
- 5 large garlic cloves, lightly crushed
- 5 fresh sage leaves
- 2 bay leaves
- 15 g (1 tbsp) plain flour
- 1 tin (425 g / 15 oz) diced tomatoes
- 240 ml (1 cup) dry red wine
- 960–1200 ml (4–5 cups) low-sodium beef broth

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F).
2. Mix 1 tsp seasoned salt, ½ tsp pepper, paprika and cardamom. Season short ribs generously on all sides with the spice mixture.
3. Heat 2 tbsp vegetable oil in a heavy Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown short ribs deeply on all sides. Remove and set aside.
4. Add more oil if needed; reduce to medium heat. Sauté potatoes, celery and red onion 10 minutes, tossing occasionally. Season lightly with remaining seasoned salt and pepper. Remove and set aside.
5. In the same pot, combine diced tomatoes, garlic, sage and bay leaves. Cook 5–7 minutes until the tomato turns a deep rusty red. Stir in flour to coat.
6. Add red wine and broth; raise heat and cook over high heat, stirring, until the liquid is slightly reduced, about 7 minutes.
7. Return short ribs and sautéed vegetables to the pot. Cook together on medium-high, uncovered, 5–7 minutes. Insert the MEATER probe from the flat cut-end of one rib into the centre of the meat, clear of the bone.
8. Cover the pot; transfer to the 150 °C (300 °F) oven. Braise 2 hours.
9. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** Karadsheh's indicator: short ribs are fork-tender and the meat yields easily to a fork inserted from above.
10. Serve in bowls with crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **86–91 °C / 187–196 °F** (fork-tender, sage-wine-cardamom aromatic sauce).

### 3. Judy and Kaitlin Leung — Asian Braised Short Ribs with Chili Lime Potatoes (Chinese-American tradition)
**Source**: Judy and Kaitlin Leung, thewoksoflife.com/asian-braised-short-ribs-with-chili-lime-potatoes/  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 6–24 hr (marinade) + 15 min · **Cook**: 3 hr 15 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.8 kg (4 lb) bone-in beef short ribs (choose ribs about 8 cm long with at least 5 cm of meat on the bone)
- **Marinade**: 25 g (2 tbsp) brown sugar, 10 ml (2 tsp) sesame oil, 60 ml (¼ cup) soy sauce, 1 small onion (grated), 3 garlic cloves (minced), 10 g (2 tsp) salt, 1 g (⅛ tsp) black pepper
- 360 ml (1½ cups) water (added to the roasting pan, not the marinade)

**Method**
1. Rinse short ribs under cold water; transfer to a bowl. Add all marinade ingredients; toss to coat. Cover and refrigerate 6–24 hours.
2. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F); place a rack in the middle of the oven.
3. Arrange ribs bone-side down in a 33 × 23 cm (9 × 13 inch) roasting pan. Pour the marinade over the ribs and add 360 ml (1½ cups) water.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the flat cut-end of one rib into the centre of the thickest meat section, keeping the tip clear of the bone and marrow channel.
5. Cover the pan tightly with foil. Roast 3 hours.
6. Remove the foil. Roast a further 15 minutes to caramelise the surface.
7. **Pull at 90–94 °C (194–201 °F).** The Leung family's test: the meat should yield without resistance when pressed with a finger through a folded paper towel — the connective tissue has fully dissolved.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **88–93 °C / 190–199 °F** (lacquered, soy-sesame-caramelised exterior, pull-apart meat).

### 4. TV amateur cook "Kicki Malmö" — Short Ribs Bräserade i Äppelmust (Swedish home tradition)
**Source**: Kicki Malmö, *Halv åtta hos mig* (TV4 / Köket.se); koket.se/shortribs-braserade-i-applemust  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 3 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg (2.2 lb) short ribs
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 litre (4 cups) äppelmust (Swedish sweet apple juice, not fermented — use unfiltered cloudy apple juice as a substitute)
- 500 ml (2 cups) äppelcider (dry sparkling apple cider)
- 1 yellow onion
- 1 stalk celery
- 1 carrot
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) calvados (apple brandy)
- 1 kalvbuljongtärning (veal bouillon cube)
- 5 g (1 tsp) dried thyme
- 2 garlic cloves
- A small knob of honey (optional, to adjust sweetness)

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F).
2. Season short ribs generously with salt and pepper. Brown the meat in butter or oil in a large ovenproof pot over high heat, building a deep crust on all surfaces.
3. Combine all remaining ingredients in the pot with the browned ribs: apple juice, cider, onion, celery, carrot, calvados, bouillon, thyme and garlic. The liquid should come to approximately two-thirds of the height of the meat.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the flat cut-end of one rib into the thickest part of the meat, clear of the bone.
5. Cover the pot. Transfer to the 150 °C oven. Braise 3 hours.
6. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** The meat should be tender when pierced and beginning to separate from the bone at the edges.
7. Remove ribs carefully. Strain the braising liquid; cool and skim off the solidified fat. Reduce the remaining liquid to a thick, apple-caramel glaze.
8. Brush ribs with the glaze; return to the oven at 150 °C for 5 minutes to caramelise. Final serving temperature: **86–91 °C / 187–196 °F** (apple-cider-caramel glaze, Swedish autumn character).

### 5. Erik Videgård — Shortribs i japansk lag (Swedish professional × Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Erik Videgård (Swedish celebrity chef), koket.se/shortribs-erik-videgards-recept  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 3 hr (or overnight at 90 °C) · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 400 g (14 oz) short ribs
- Rapeseed oil (for browning)
- 180 g (¾ cup) dashi (Japanese fish-based stock)
- 180 g (¾ cup) sake
- 240 g (1 cup) Japanese soy sauce (shoyu)
- 48 g (3 tbsp) Chinese soy sauce
- 180 g (¾ cup) mirin (sweet rice wine)
- 100 g (½ cup) white granulated sugar
- 28 g (2 tbsp) garlic, finely chopped
- 2 star anise
- ½ fresh red chilli, deseeded and chopped
- 240 g (8 oz) daikon (white radish), roughly chopped
- 180 g (6 oz) carrot, roughly chopped
- 180 g (6 oz) yellow onion, roughly chopped
- Shichimi tōgarashi (Japanese seven-spice blend), to serve

**Method**
1. Mix dashi, sake, Japanese soy sauce, Chinese soy sauce, mirin, sugar, garlic, star anise and chilli in a bowl. Stir until the sugar dissolves to form the braising liquid (lag).
2. Brown the short ribs in a frying pan with a little rapeseed oil until a deep crust forms on all sides.
3. Transfer ribs to an ovenproof dish. Add daikon, carrot and onion. Pour the braising liquid over everything.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the flat cut-end of one rib into the centre of the thickest meat section.
5. Cover tightly with parchment paper and a lid. Place in the oven at 125 °C (257 °F) for approximately 3 hours. Alternatively, cook overnight at 90 °C (194 °F) with a pan of water in the oven base. The meat should be tender but not disintegrating.
6. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F)** for the standard 3-hour cook at 125 °C. For the low-and-slow overnight method (90 °C oven), pull when the meat reaches 85–88 °C (185–190 °F) — at 90 °C oven temperature the meat cannot exceed the liquid temperature but the extended time completes collagen conversion.
7. Lift the meat and allow it to rest. Cool the braising liquid; skim fat, then strain and reduce to a glossy, syrupy sauce.
8. Slice the meat into smaller portions; warm in the oven at 150 °C (300 °F) for 5 minutes. Nap with sauce and finish with a dusting of shichimi tōgarashi. Final serving temperature: **85–90 °C / 185–194 °F** (lacquered, umami-deep, Japanese-Swedish fusion).

## What makes this method special

Short ribs braising is the definitive example of long moist-heat cooking converting structural weakness into strength: the connective tissue that makes raw short ribs inedibly tough is composed predominantly of type-I and type-III collagen, which hydrolyses into gelatin at sustained temperatures above ~70 °C (158 °F) over multiple hours. The surrounding braising liquid maintains the surface temperature at or near 100 °C throughout the cook while slowly driving the core to 88–94 °C, the range at which collagen conversion is complete. The rendered intramuscular fat and dissolved gelatin migrate into the liquid, thickening it into the rich, self-enriching sauce that is characteristic of the dish across every tradition surveyed. No other cooking method for short ribs achieves this: dry roasting without liquid desiccates the exterior before the interior reaches conversion temperature; pan searing produces a crust but the interior remains tough.

## Summary

Braising is the defining method for short ribs because it is the only technique that converts the cut's dense collagen-bound structure into its characteristic fall-apart, gelatin-rich texture: sustained moist heat drives the internal temperature to 88–94 °C (190–201 °F) over three or more hours, hydrolyzing collagen and rendering fat into the sauce. All five traditions surveyed — Australian, Mediterranean/American, Chinese-American, Swedish home, and Swedish/Japanese professional — arrive at the same internal temperature range and the same physical target (fork-tender, pull-apart meat), regardless of oven temperature or braising liquid composition. Short ribs are poorly suited to any method that does not provide this combination of time and moist heat.
