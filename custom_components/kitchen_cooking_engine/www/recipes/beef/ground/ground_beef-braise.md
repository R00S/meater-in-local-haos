# Ground Beef × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Ground beef (minced beef) is made by grinding beef, most commonly from the chuck primal or a blend of trimmings. The fat-to-lean ratio is the primary quality variable: 80/20 is standard for most preparations; 85/15 is leaner; 70/30 is richer and juicier. Because grinding ruptures muscle cells and distributes surface bacteria throughout the mass, the USDA and equivalent international food-safety authorities require a minimum internal temperature of 71°C (160°F) — there is no medium-rare option for ground beef the way there is for whole steaks. Carryover is minimal (1–2°C) given the fine grind and rapid heat transfer.

## Styckesprofil

Malet nötkött (köttfärs) framställs av nötmalning, vanligast från fransyska, bringa eller blandningar av dessa, och innehåller 15–30 % fett. Till skillnad från hela muskelstycken saknar köttfärs inre bindvävsstruktur — malningen har redan söndrat muskelfibrerna — så "brässering" av malet nötkött innebär att man kokar det i vätska för att mjuka smöret, absorbera smaker och eventuellt tjockna en sås snarare än att omvandla kollagen. Fuktigt tillagad köttfärs producerar ett mjukt, grynigt resultat; den interna temperaturen bör nå 74 °C av livsmedelssäkerhetsskäl (inte av texturskäl som för hela muskler).

## Source recipes

### 1. Ragù alla Bolognese (inspired by Marcella Hazan)
**Inspired by**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Knopf, 1992)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 3.5–4 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g coarsely-ground beef chuck (80/20)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) extra-virgin olive oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 medium onion, finely chopped
- 1 medium carrot, finely chopped
- 1 stalk celery, finely chopped
- 240 ml (1 cup) whole milk
- A grating of nutmeg
- 240 ml (1 cup) dry white wine
- 800 g (1 large tin) Italian plum tomatoes, crushed by hand
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Sauté onion, carrot and celery in oil and butter 8 minutes until softened and pale gold.
2. Add beef in clumps. Insert the MEATER probe into the deepest part of the beef. Brown 6–8 minutes, breaking apart.
3. Add milk and nutmeg. Simmer until milk is fully absorbed, 10 minutes.
4. Add wine; simmer until evaporated, 10 minutes.
5. Add tomatoes, salt and pepper. Bring to a bare simmer; reduce heat to the lowest setting at which an occasional bubble breaks the surface.
6. Cover partially. Cook 3 hours, stirring every 30 minutes. The probe will sit just below the simmer point.
7. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** Hazan describes the finished ragù as "thick, glossy, with the meat dissolving into the sauce" — the long simmer at 90 °C transforms texture and concentrates flavour.
8. Rest 10 minutes off the heat for the fat to layer; skim if desired. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (deeply unctuous, sauce-bound).

### 2. Köttfärsgryta med rotsaker (inspired by Tina Nordström)
**Inspired by**: Tina Nordström, *Tina lagar svenskt* (Bonniers Förlag, 2009); köket.se  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 1.5 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g blandfärs (mixed beef-pork mince) or pure nötfärs, 80/20
- 30 g (2 tbsp) smör (butter)
- 1 large yellow onion, finely chopped
- 2 medium morötter (carrots), small dice
- 1 stick palsternacka (parsnip), small dice
- 240 ml dry red wine
- 480 ml beef stock
- 400 g (1 tin) chopped tomatoes
- 2 bay leaves
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) white pepper
- 5 ml (1 tsp) soya sauce or kalvfond (veal stock concentrate) — Nordström's umami booster

**Method**
1. Melt butter in a heavy gryta (Dutch oven). Sauté onion, carrot and parsnip 8 minutes until pale gold.
2. Push veg aside; add färs in clumps. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the largest clump. Brown 5–6 minutes.
3. Mix vegetables and meat. Add wine; simmer until reduced by half, 4 minutes.
4. Add stock, tomatoes, herbs, salt, pepper, and soya sauce. Bring to a simmer.
5. Cover and reduce to lowest heat — Nordström specifies "knappt sjudande" (barely simmering) in this dish.
6. Braise 75–90 minutes, stirring every 20 minutes.
7. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** The dish is done when the rotsaker have melted into the sauce and the meat is glossy.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Serve with kokt potatis and inlagd gurka. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F**.

### 3. Aloo Keema (inspired by Madhur Jaffrey)
**Inspired by**: Madhur Jaffrey, *A Taste of India* (Pavilion Books, 1985)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 1 hr · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g lean ground beef (90/10)
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) ghee
- 1 large onion, finely chopped
- 6 garlic cloves, grated
- 5 cm fresh ginger, grated
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground turmeric
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground coriander
- 2 g (½ tsp) cayenne
- 2 g (½ tsp) garam masala
- 400 g (1 tin) chopped tomatoes
- 480 ml water
- 2 medium potatoes, peeled, diced 2 cm
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt
- 10 g chopped fresh coriander, to finish

**Method**
1. Heat ghee in a wide heavy karahi over medium-high heat. Add onion; cook 8 minutes until deep gold. Add garlic and ginger; 1 minute.
2. Add turmeric, cumin, coriander, cayenne; bloom in the oil 30 seconds.
3. Add beef in clumps. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the largest clump. Brown 6 minutes, breaking up.
4. Add tomatoes; cook 5 minutes until they have broken down. Add water and salt. Bring to a simmer.
5. Add potatoes. Cover; reduce to low. Braise 40–45 minutes until potatoes are tender.
6. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** Jaffrey's *aloo keema* is done when the gravy has reduced to coat the back of a spoon and the potatoes hold their shape but yield instantly to a fork.
7. Stir in garam masala. Rest 5 minutes covered. Top with fresh coriander. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (spice-saturated, glossy, potato-thickened).

### 4. Picadillo (inspired by Diana Henry)
**Inspired by**: Diana Henry, *Crazy Water, Pickled Lemons* (Mitchell Beazley, 2002) and her *Telegraph* recipe column at telegraph.co.uk/food-and-drink  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 45 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g coarsely-ground beef (80/20)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 1 large onion, finely chopped
- 1 green bell pepper, finely diced
- 4 garlic cloves, finely chopped
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin
- 2 g (½ tsp) sweet smoked paprika
- 1 g (¼ tsp) ground cinnamon
- 400 g (1 tin) chopped tomatoes
- 240 ml (1 cup) beef stock
- 30 g raisins
- 50 g pitted green olives, sliced
- 30 g capers, drained
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt
- 5 g chopped fresh oregano or flat-leaf parsley, to finish

**Method**
1. Heat oil in a wide heavy pan over medium heat. Add onion and pepper; cook 8 minutes until soft. Add garlic; 1 minute.
2. Add cumin, paprika, cinnamon; toast 30 seconds.
3. Add beef in clumps. Insert MEATER probe into largest clump. Brown 5–6 minutes, breaking up.
4. Add tomatoes, stock, raisins, olives, capers and salt. Bring to a simmer.
5. Cover partially; reduce heat to low. Braise 30 minutes, stirring twice.
6. **Pull at 86–90 °C (187–194 °F).** Henry describes the finished *picadillo* as "moist, glossy, the raisins plumped, the olives just translucent at the edges, and a slick of orange-red oil at the surface."
7. Rest 5 minutes off heat. Stir in fresh herbs. Serve with rice. Final serving temperature: **86–90 °C / 187–194 °F** (sweet-savoury, fruit-and-brine balanced).
