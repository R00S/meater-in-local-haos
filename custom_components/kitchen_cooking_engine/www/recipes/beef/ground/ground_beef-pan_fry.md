# Ground Beef × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Ground beef (minced beef) is made by grinding beef, most commonly from the chuck primal or a blend of trimmings. The fat-to-lean ratio is the primary quality variable: 80/20 is standard for most preparations; 85/15 is leaner; 70/30 is richer and juicier. Because grinding ruptures muscle cells and distributes surface bacteria throughout the mass, the USDA and equivalent international food-safety authorities require a minimum internal temperature of 71°C (160°F) — there is no medium-rare option for ground beef the way there is for whole steaks. Carryover is minimal (1–2°C) given the fine grind and rapid heat transfer.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Fried Browned Ground Beef (American food-science tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015); seriouseats.com/the-best-way-to-brown-ground-beef  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: n/a

**Ingredients**
- 500 g coarsely-ground beef chuck (80/20)
- 1 g (¼ tsp) baking soda dissolved in 15 ml water (Kenji's tenderising trick)
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) neutral oil (only if using a stainless pan; cast iron needs none)

**Method**
1. Combine ground beef with the baking-soda water and salt in a bowl. Toss until evenly distributed; rest 15 minutes. The baking soda raises the meat's pH so the proteins brown more readily and don't squeeze water out as harshly.
2. Heat a large heavy stainless or cast-iron pan over the highest heat for 3 minutes. Add oil if using stainless.
3. Add the beef in an even layer covering the pan. Do not stir.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the layer, tip resting in the deepest part of the pan.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes undisturbed — the underside develops a deep brown crust. Then break apart with a wooden spoon, flip the chunks, and continue cooking another 3–5 minutes, breaking down to small clumps as you go.
6. **Pull at 71–74 °C (160–165 °F).** Kenji notes ground beef destined for sauces or fillings should be cooked through but not over-rendered — over-cooked ground beef squeezes out fat and turns chalky.
7. Drain off excess fat (or leave it for sauce-building, depending on dish). Final serving temperature: **71–74 °C / 160–165 °F** (browned, juicy).

### 2. Marcella Hazan — Soffritto-Browned Beef for Ragù alla Bolognese (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Knopf, 1992)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 25 min (browning portion) · **Rest**: n/a

**Ingredients**
- 500 g coarsely-ground beef (80/20)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) extra-virgin olive oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 medium onion, finely chopped
- 1 medium carrot, finely chopped
- 1 stalk celery, finely chopped
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 240 ml (1 cup) whole milk
- A pinch of freshly grated nutmeg

**Method**
1. Heat olive oil and butter in a heavy heavy-bottomed pan over medium heat. Add onion, carrot and celery; sauté 8 minutes until softened and pale gold. This *soffritto* must be made first; never add ground beef to a cold or empty pan in the Italian style.
2. Increase heat to medium-high. Push the soffritto to one side; add the beef in clumps to the other side. Insert the MEATER probe into the deepest part of the beef, tip at the centre.
3. Brown 5–6 minutes, breaking up with a wooden spoon, then mix soffritto and beef together.
4. Hazan's signature: add the milk and a grating of nutmeg. Simmer until the milk has been absorbed and the meat looks dry, 10–12 minutes. The milk tenderises the meat and adds the characteristic creamy depth of Bolognese.
5. **Pull at 72–75 °C (162–167 °F).** This is the doneness at which the meat is tender and flavour-saturated, ready for the long simmer with wine and tomato that follows.
6. Continue with the broader ragù recipe (wine, tomato, 3+ hour simmer). Final serving temperature for the *ragù* base: **72–75 °C / 162–167 °F**.

### 3. Madhur Jaffrey — Kheema (Indian-style minced beef)
**Source**: Madhur Jaffrey, *Madhur Jaffrey's Indian Cookery* (BBC Books, 1982)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g lean ground beef (Jaffrey calls for 90/10 for kheema — less rendered fat suits the spice oil)
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) ghee or vegetable oil
- 1 medium onion, finely chopped
- 6 garlic cloves, finely grated
- 5 cm piece fresh ginger, finely grated
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground turmeric
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground coriander
- 2 g (½ tsp) cayenne pepper
- 5 g (1 tsp) garam masala
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 200 g (½ tin) chopped tomatoes
- 100 g (¾ cup) frozen peas (added in the last 5 minutes)
- 10 g chopped fresh coriander, to finish

**Method**
1. Heat ghee in a wide heavy karahi or sauté pan over medium-high heat. Add onion; cook 6–8 minutes until deeply golden. Add garlic and ginger; cook 1 minute.
2. Add turmeric, cumin, coriander and cayenne; toast 30 seconds in the oil — Jaffrey calls this "blooming the spices."
3. Add the beef, breaking it into clumps. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the largest beef clump.
4. Brown 6–8 minutes, breaking apart and stirring frequently — kheema texture is small, even crumbs, not big chunks.
5. Add tomatoes and salt. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer uncovered 10 minutes until the liquid has thickened to a clinging sauce.
6. Add peas; cook 5 minutes more.
7. **Pull at 72–75 °C (162–167 °F).** Jaffrey targets thoroughly cooked ground beef bathed in spice-bloom oil — not dry, with visible glistening fat from the ghee.
8. Off the heat, sprinkle with garam masala and fresh coriander. Rest 5 minutes covered. Final serving temperature: **72–75 °C / 162–167 °F** (spice-rich, glossy, slightly saucy).

### 4. Tina Nordström — Köttfärs på Stekpannan (Swedish home tradition)
**Source**: Tina Nordström, *Tina i köket* (Bonniers Förlag, 2007); köket.se  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g blandfärs (50/50 beef/pork) or rent nötfärs (pure beef mince), 80/20
- 15 g (1 tbsp) smör (butter)
- 1 small yellow onion, finely chopped
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) vitpeppar (white pepper)
- 5 g (1 tsp) Worcestershire-sås (optional, for glaze)
- 100 ml grädde (cream) or vatten (water) — cream is the Swedish preference
- 5 g chopped fresh persilja (parsley) or gräslök (chives) to finish

**Method**
1. Melt the butter in a wide heavy stekpanna over medium-high heat. Add onion; sauté 4–5 minutes until soft and pale gold.
2. Add the färs (mince) in clumps. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the largest clump.
3. Cook 3–4 minutes undisturbed for an underside crust, then break up with a wooden spoon and continue 4–5 minutes more, stirring, until the meat is no longer pink.
4. Sprinkle with salt and white pepper, add Worcestershire if using.
5. Pour in the cream. Stir; simmer 2 minutes until lightly thickened.
6. **Pull at 72–75 °C (162–167 °F).** Nordström notes that pan-fried mince for the Swedish *vardagsmat* (everyday food) standard is fully cooked but glossy — never dry.
7. Rest 2 minutes off the heat. Scatter with parsley or chives. Serve with potatismos and lingonsylt. Final serving temperature: **72–75 °C / 162–167 °F** (creamy, glossy, herb-finished).
