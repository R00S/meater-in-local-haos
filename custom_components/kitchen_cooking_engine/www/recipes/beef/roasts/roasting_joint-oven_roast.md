# Roasting Joint × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

"Roasting joint" is a British and Australian umbrella term covering a range of large beef joints suitable for oven roasting as a Sunday roast or centrepiece. The term does not refer to a single anatomical cut; depending on the butcher and the budget, a roasting joint may be any of:

- **Topside** (UK/Aus) / Top Round (US) — very lean, tight grain, economical; requires careful temperature control to avoid drying out
- **Silverside** (UK/Aus) / Bottom Round (US) — lean, slightly tougher than topside, traditionally associated with slow roasting or boiling (corned beef)
- **Bolar Blade** (Aus/NZ) — shoulder cut, relatively tender with decent flavour
- **Rib of Beef** (UK) / Standing Rib Roast (US) — premium, bone-in or boneless, heavily marbled; the prestige Sunday roast option
- **Sirloin of Beef** (UK) — boneless loin, moderate fat cap; covered in its own cut file (`sirloin_roast`)

The defining characteristic shared by all roasting joint cuts is their size (typically 1–3 kg) and their suitability for dry roasting in an oven without added liquid, producing a browned exterior and a pink, rested interior. All are beef primal cuts with relatively low collagen content compared to braising cuts (brisket, chuck, shank).

**Temperature behaviour across the joint type spectrum**: Lean cuts (topside, silverside) are the most temperature-sensitive — even a few degrees of overshoot at the core dries them unacceptably. Fatty cuts (rib) are more forgiving because fat masks moisture loss. All sources below recommend pulling well before the target temperature and allowing carryover to finish the work, with resting periods of 15–30 minutes as non-negotiable.

**Carryover**: 4–8 °C on the standard two-phase method (high initial temperature + moderate finishing temperature). Negligible on ultra-low methods (70–80 °C oven).

**Probe placement**: For any boneless roasting joint, enter the probe from the end into the geometric centre of the thickest lean muscle section. For bone-in rib, keep the tip at least 2 cm from any rib bone — bone conducts heat differently from muscle and will produce misleading readings.

## Source recipes

### 1. Nagi Maehashi — Marinated Roast Beef (Australian tradition, designed for economical roasting joints)
**Inspired by**: Nagi Maehashi, RecipeTin Eats, recipetineats.com/marinated-roast-beef/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2–3 day marinade) · **Cook**: ~55–60 min · **Rest**: 20–30 min

**Cuts targeted**: Topside/Top Round, Silverside/Bottom Round, Bolar Blade, Oyster Blade, Eye of Round — all economical British/Australian roasting joint cuts

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg boneless roasting joint (any of the cuts listed above)
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- **Marinade**: 3 garlic cloves minced, 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce, 2 tbsp Dijon mustard, 2½ tbsp balsamic vinegar, ½ cup soy sauce, ¼ cup olive oil, 1 tbsp white sugar, 2 tsp onion powder, 1 tsp black pepper, 2 tsp dried thyme, 1 tsp dried rosemary
- Optional vegetables: 3 potatoes and 4 carrots

**Method**
1. Combine marinade ingredients in a large zip-lock bag; add beef. Marinade 2–3 days (minimum 24 hours). The soy sauce acts as a brine, driving salt into the lean fibres — essential for leaner cuts like topside which have no intramuscular fat to provide internal lubrication.
2. Remove from fridge 1 hour before cooking.
3. Preheat oven to 240 °C / 450 °F.
4. Place vegetables in an oven-safe skillet; pour all marinade over them; set beef on top. Drizzle beef with olive oil.
5. Roast at 240 °C for 20 minutes to develop a Maillard crust.
6. Reduce heat to 180 °C / 350 °F (160 °C fan-forced). Insert MEATER probe from the end into the centre of the lean section.
7. Roast a further 35–40 minutes. **Pull at 44 °C / 111 °F for medium-rare** (Nagi: internal temp rises 8 °C / 14 °F during resting). Pull at 41 °C / 106 °F for rare; 48 °C / 118 °F for medium.
8. Rest loosely covered with foil for 20–30 minutes. **Final temperature: 52 °C / 125 °F** (medium-rare). For lean topside, do not exceed 57 °C / 135 °F — the meat dries rapidly above this point.
9. Slice very thinly against the grain (especially important for topside and silverside, where long fibres become chewy if sliced thick).

### 2. Chef Simon — Rôti de Boeuf (French classical tradition)
**Inspired by**: Chef Simon, *Rôti de Boeuf*, chefsimon.com/gourmets/chef-simon/recettes/roti-de-boeuf--3  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: ~30 min (1 kg joint)

**Ingredients**
- 1–1.2 kg rôti de boeuf (contrefilet or other first-category beef joint, trimmed and tied)
- Butter, sea salt, freshly ground black pepper
- 2 shallots, 1 onion, 2 garlic cloves, thyme sprig (for the jus)

**Method**
1. Trim and truss the roast (*parer et ficeler*). Salt and pepper all surfaces.
2. In a very hot skillet, sear on all surfaces to a deep Maillard crust.
3. Reserve fat trimmings. Sweat shallots, onion, and garlic in the rendered fat. Place seared roast on top of the aromatic base in the roasting tray.
4. Roast at **220 °C**, allowing approximately **15 minutes per 500 g**. For probe users: **pull at 45 °C**. Lower oven to 70 °C and hold; **do not allow core to exceed 50–55 °C**. (*Saignant* is the French bistrot standard for any quality roasting joint; 55 °C is the French maximum for a quality result.)
5. Make the jus: deglaze the pan with water, scraping the *sucs*. Reduce gently with thyme and peppercorns.
6. **Pull at 45 °C**; **final temperature: 50–55 °C** (*saignant* to *à point*).

### 3. Immaculate Bites — Creole-Spiced Oven Roast Beef (West African / Southern American Creole tradition)
**Inspired by**: Immaculate Bites (Imma), africanbites.com/roast-beef/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight marinade) · **Cook**: 1–2 hours · **Rest**: 15–20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.3–1.8 kg eye of round, top sirloin, or top round roasting joint
- Olive oil or melted butter, freshly ground black pepper, kosher salt
- 1 tbsp paprika, 1 tsp dried thyme, 1 tsp dried rosemary, 4 garlic cloves minced (rub) + 4 whole cloves (slitting), ¼ tsp cayenne pepper
- ½ cup dry red wine (Merlot or Cabernet Sauvignon), 1 cup beef broth

**Method**
1. Pat roast dry; mix olive oil/butter with paprika, thyme, rosemary, garlic, cayenne into a paste; rub over all surfaces. Refrigerate overnight in a sealed bag.
2. Preheat oven to 250 °F / 120 °C.
3. Cut deep slits all over the roast with a paring knife; stuff with whole garlic cloves.
4. Sear in a hot pan on all sides, 3–4 minutes per side, until browned.
5. Transfer to oven-safe casserole; insert MEATER probe.
6. Roast uncovered at 250 °F / 120 °C for 1–2 hours.
7. Make sauce: heat reserved marinade in a saucepan; add red wine and beef broth; simmer until reduced and slightly thickened; finish with butter.
8. **Pull at 130–140 °F / 54–60 °C**. Rest 15–20 minutes. **Final temperature: ~57–63 °C** (medium-rare to medium). Slice against the grain, starting from the ends.

### 4. Holger Rørby Madsen — Langtidsstegt Oksefilet (Danish tradition — the ultra-low method)
**Inspired by**: Holger Rørby Madsen, Madens Verden, madensverden.dk/langtidsstegt-oksefilet-ovn/  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+12–24 h dry rub) · **Cook**: ~4 hours

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg oksefilet (Danish recipe term — a beef joint with a fat cap; exact cut identity not confirmed from the fetched source)
- 3 tbsp brown sugar, 2 tbsp fine salt
- Neutral oil and butter (for searing); optional: water and beef stock for basting

**Method**
1. Mix brown sugar and salt; score the fat cap; rub the mix into all surfaces; refrigerate in a sealed bag 12–24 hours.
2. Remove from fridge 1 hour before cooking.
3. Sear quickly over very high heat, a few seconds on each side — just for colour, not for cooking.
4. Set oven to **80 °C** (not fan). Place roast in the cold oven as it heats; total cook time approximately **4 hours** for 1.5 kg.
5. After 2 hours, insert probe into centre and monitor continuously. Baste occasionally.
6. **Pull at exactly 57 °C core temperature.** Holger's instruction: "Do not give it longer — it becomes dull and dry." Because the oven temperature (80 °C) is only 23 °C above the target (57 °C), carryover is negligible.
7. No resting needed — the ultra-low method ensures an even temperature throughout. Serve immediately. If holding for guests, drop oven to 50 °C.
8. **Final temperature: 57 °C** throughout the joint (even gradient).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Tradition | Group | Pull temp | Final temp | Doneness |
|--------|-----------|-------|-----------|------------|----------|
| Nagi Maehashi | Australian | B | 44 °C | 52 °C | medium-rare |
| Chef Simon | French classical | B | 45 °C | 50–55 °C | *saignant* to *à point* |
| Immaculate Bites | Creole / West African | C | 54–60 °C | 57–63 °C | medium-rare to medium |
| Holger Rørby Madsen | Danish (oksefilet†) | D | 57 °C (final) | 57 °C | *medium* (Nordic) |
| **Consensus** | | | **44–57 °C pull** | **50–63 °C serve** | |

† The Danish recipe is for *oksefilet* — exact cut identity (sirloin, striploin, or tenderloin) not confirmed from the fetched source. It is included here because "roasting joint" is a broad category; the method and temperature data are verified.

**Key finding**: All four traditions prefer roasting joint in the 50–57 °C final range. The French and Australian traditions pull early (44–45 °C) to land at 52–55 °C after carryover; the Danish ultra-low method bypasses carryover by using an 80 °C oven. The Creole recipe targets 54–60 °C pull.

Above 63 °C, lean cuts (topside, silverside, eye of round) dry out significantly — universal consensus. The USDA safe minimum of 63 °C is the safety floor but the outer limit for acceptable eating quality.

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting is the original and dominant method for large British and Northern European beef joints. The combination of dry ambient heat and the fat-basting action of a rendered fat cap produces a crust (Maillard) and an evenly cooked interior that braising cannot replicate. The resting step is not optional for lean joints — it allows juice redistribution that would otherwise pool on the cutting board and deprive the meat of moisture. The ultra-low Danish method (80 °C oven) produces an exceptionally even pink interior with no grey band, but requires 4+ hours and precise timing.

## Summary

Four verified oven-roast sources from three culinary traditions (Australian, French, Creole/West African, Danish) confirm a serving temperature range of 50–57 °C for roasting joints. Medium-rare (52–55 °C) is the dominant preference worldwide for quality cuts; leaner cuts become dry above 60 °C. Group A (Asian) has no confirmed tradition of oven-roasting a whole beef joint available in this project's verified site list.
