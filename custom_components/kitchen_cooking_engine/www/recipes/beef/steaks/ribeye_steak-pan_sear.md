# Ribeye Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Pan searing and the ribeye are an exceptional match precisely because of the cut's defining feature: its heavy intramuscular fat. When the face of a ribeye contacts a smoking-hot cast-iron or carbon-steel surface, two simultaneous reactions drive the result — the lean muscle proteins in the exterior undergo Maillard browning, and the adjacent fat pockets begin rendering, producing their own cooking medium as the sear progresses. This rendered fat bastes the crust from inside the meat rather than pooling in the pan, creating a self-enriching sear that leaner cuts like filet mignon cannot replicate. The practical outcome is that pan-seared ribeye is more forgiving than most steaks: because the marbling keeps the interior moist, a cook who overshoots medium-rare by a few degrees will still produce a very good result — the fat disguises the error.

Carryover in a pan-seared ribeye is moderate, typically 3–5 °C for a standard boneless cut at 2.5–3 cm thickness. The metal pan delivers intense conductive surface heat; once the steak is off the pan, that heat source is removed immediately, limiting carryover compared to an oven roast where residual ambient heat continues. The high fat content also slows internal temperature equalisation — fat is a poorer conductor of heat than lean muscle — which means the core of a ribeye equalises slightly more slowly than a lean steak of equivalent thickness. Resting on a wire rack (not a plate) prevents the bottom face from steaming in its own liquid and maintains crust texture.

Probe placement requires care because of the ribeye's internal fat structure. For a boneless cut the probe should enter from the side of the steak, aimed at the geometric centre of the eye muscle — the densest lean section. It must avoid the internal fat seam that often runs between the eye and the spinalis (cap muscle): inserting into fat gives a false-low reading. On bone-in versions (côte de bœuf, cowboy ribeye), the probe must enter from the meaty side and travel to the centre of the eye, never near the bone — bone conducts heat differently from muscle and will produce a misleading reading in either direction depending on pan orientation.

## Source recipes

### 1. Butter-Basted Pan-Seared Thick-Cut Ribeye (inspired by J. Kenji López-Alt)
**Inspired by**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/butter-basted-pan-seared-thick-cut-steak-recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5–10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 large bone-in ribeye steak, 3.5–5 cm thick, approximately 700–900 g
- Kosher salt (applied 1–24 h ahead as a dry brine)
- Freshly ground black pepper (added immediately before searing)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or canola oil
- 28 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly crushed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme or rosemary

**Method**
1. Apply kosher salt generously to all surfaces of the steak; refrigerate uncovered on a wire rack for at least 45 minutes, preferably overnight. The dry-brine draws surface moisture, redissolves it as seasoned brine, and produces a drier exterior that sears faster.
2. Remove steak from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Apply black pepper.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak, with the tip at the geometric centre of the eye muscle, clear of the bone and any large fat seams.
4. Heat a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over the highest possible heat for at least 5 minutes until smoking. Add the oil.
5. Add the steak. Flip every 15–30 seconds — Kenji's rapid-flip technique prevents the grey band forming under one face and distributes heat evenly.
6. After 2 minutes of rapid flipping, add butter, garlic and thyme. The butter will foam immediately.
7. Tilt the pan and baste the steak continuously with the foaming butter, continuing to flip every 30 seconds. The butter browns and the garlic-thyme aromatics infuse into the crust.
8. **Pull at 49–52 °C (120–126 °F).** The rapid-flip method generates less carryover than a static sear; the short rest will bring the core to medium-rare without overshooting.
9. Rest 5–10 minutes on a wire rack. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Restaurant-Style Pan-Seared Ribeye (inspired by Gordon Ramsay)
**Inspired by**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012); youtube.com/watch?v=AmC9SmCBUj4  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 ribeye steak, 350–450 g, 2.5–3 cm thick (boneless preferred for even pan contact)
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 28 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly smashed
- 2–3 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Remove steak from the fridge; bring to room temperature for 30 minutes. Pat completely dry with paper towels — moisture is the enemy of crust. Season both sides generously with sea salt and pepper.
2. Heat a heavy cast-iron or stainless pan over high heat until extremely hot. Ramsay describes the target as "screaming hot — if you add a drop of oil it should smoke immediately." Add the olive oil.
3. Lay the steak in the pan away from you. Do NOT move it. Cook 2–3 minutes undisturbed to build a deep, dark-brown crust on the first face.
4. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak into the centre of the eye muscle, avoiding the fat cap and any internal fat seam.
5. Add butter, garlic and thyme to the pan. As the butter foams, tilt the pan and use a spoon to baste the steak continuously — Ramsay's technique layers caramelised milk solids from the butter into the crust surface.
6. Cook a further 2–3 minutes while basting, pressing the steak gently with tongs to maintain even contact.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Ramsay targets medium-rare; the single-flip method with a thick pan generates 3–5 °C of carryover.
8. Rest on a wire rack for 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Entrecôte Sautée au Beurre (inspired by Jacques Pépin)
**Inspired by**: Jacques Pépin, *New Complete Techniques* (Black Dog & Leventhal Publishers, 2012), combining techniques from *La Technique* (1976) and *La Méthode* (1979)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+20 min tempering) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 entrecôte steaks (ribeye, bone removed), 200–250 g each, approximately 2 cm thick
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground white or black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) grapeseed or neutral oil
- 40 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter, divided
- 1 shallot, finely minced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine or cognac (for pan sauce)
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Bring steaks to room temperature 20 minutes before cooking. Pat dry. Season with salt and pepper immediately before placing in the pan.
2. Heat a heavy sauté pan (*sauteuse*) over high heat. Add oil; heat until shimmering and just beginning to haze.
3. Place steaks in the pan. Cook 2–3 minutes on the first side without moving — Pépin teaches that the French bistrot standard calls for a clean, even sear with no colour variation.
4. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of each steak to the geometric centre.
5. Add 20 g of the butter and the shallot. Cook the second side 2–3 minutes, basting with the foaming butter.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F)** for *saignant* (the classic French preference for entrecôte — a warm, bright-red centre). The French bistrot does not chase medium-rare; it chases *saignant*. Pépin notes that pulling early and resting on a warm plate is the correct technique.
7. Remove steaks to a warm plate. Deglaze the pan with white wine, scraping up the fond. Reduce by half, swirl in the remaining 20 g butter, add parsley. Spoon over steaks.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (*saignant* — warm red interior, characteristic French bistrot presentation).

### 4. Pan-Seared A5 Wagyu Ribeye with Ponzu (inspired by Nobu Matsuhisa)
**Inspired by**: Nobu Matsuhisa and Mark Edwards, *Nobu: The Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2001); noburestaurants.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 3–4 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 A5 Japanese Wagyu ribeye steaks, 180–200 g each, 1.5–2 cm thick (extremely heavy marbling, BMS 10–12)
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground white pepper (use very sparingly — Wagyu fat carries flavour without seasoning)
- 5 ml (1 tsp) rice bran oil or other neutral oil — optional; Wagyu renders sufficient fat on its own
- **Ponzu dipping sauce**: 60 ml (¼ cup) fresh yuzu juice (or a blend of lemon and lime), 45 ml (3 tbsp) soy sauce, 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin, 10 ml (2 tsp) rice wine vinegar
- Finely grated daikon, 3 cm piece; thinly sliced shiso leaves, to serve

**Method**
1. Bring Wagyu to room temperature 15 minutes before cooking. Season extremely lightly with salt and white pepper on both sides — Wagyu's fat content means aggressive seasoning competes with rather than enhances the beef.
2. Mix all ponzu components; set aside.
3. Heat a carbon-steel or cast-iron pan over the highest heat until faint wisps of smoke rise. Add a very small amount of oil (or none — Wagyu produces its own cooking fat within 20 seconds of pan contact).
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of one steak to its geometric centre before placing in the pan. Wagyu's thin cut means the probe must be inserted at a shallow angle along the face rather than from the edge.
5. Place steaks in the pan. Sear 60–90 seconds only. The highly saturated fat in A5 Wagyu renders at a lower temperature than standard beef; the Maillard reaction and fat liquefaction occur almost simultaneously.
6. Flip. Sear a further 60–75 seconds.
7. **Pull at 44–47 °C (112–117 °F).** At A5 marbling levels, the intramuscular fat begins to liquefy above 50 °C; pulling at rare preserves the characteristic melt-in-mouth fat texture that defines A5 Wagyu. Cooking to standard medium-rare temperatures renders too much of this fat and loses the defining characteristic.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Slice thin across the grain. Serve with ponzu dipping sauce, grated daikon and shiso. Final serving temperature: **46–49 °C / 115–120 °F** (rare — Japanese Wagyu standard).

### 5. Pan-Fried Scotch Fillet (inspired by Stephanie Alexander)
**Inspired by**: Stephanie Alexander, *The Cook's Companion* (Viking/Penguin, 1996; revised edition 2004), beef chapter  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 scotch fillet steaks (ribeye), 250–350 g each, 2.5–3 cm thick (Australian grass-fed or grain-fed)
- Flaked sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) extra virgin olive oil or clarified butter
- 20 g unsalted butter (optional, to finish)
- Fresh herbs to serve (flat-leaf parsley or watercress)

**Method**
1. Remove scotch fillet steaks from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Pat dry. Season both sides with flaked sea salt and black pepper just before they go in the pan.
2. Heat a heavy-based frying pan (cast iron preferred) over high heat until very hot. Add the olive oil and heat until shimmering.
3. Lay steaks in the pan. Alexander emphasises a very hot pan and patience — do not move the steaks for the first 2–3 minutes; a good crust requires full contact and time.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak, pushing to the centre of the eye muscle, clear of any fat edge.
5. Flip. Cook 2–3 minutes on the second side.
6. If finishing with butter, add it now and baste briefly.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Alexander's recommendation for scotch fillet is medium-rare — she notes that the Australian standard for medium-rare runs slightly warmer than the French *saignant*, reflecting Australian preference for a firmer, warm-pink interior rather than a soft, deep-red one.
8. Rest on a warm plate for 5 minutes. Alexander stresses that resting is non-negotiable for any pan-fried steak. Final serving temperature: **55–58 °C / 131–136 °F** (medium-rare, Australian standard).

The observed pull range across all five traditions is **44–54 °C (112–130 °F)**. Removing the Wagyu outlier — a deliberate premium-product preference driven by the physics of high-marbling fat rather than a cultural difference in doneness philosophy — the mainstream consensus for standard ribeye tightens sharply to **49–54 °C pull → 52–58 °C final serving temperature**, which places the recommended result solidly in medium-rare. The American and Australian traditions favour the warmer end of medium-rare (55–58 °C final), the French classical the cooler end (*saignant*, 52–54 °C), and the British/French restaurant tradition sits in the middle. ## What makes this method special

Pan searing transfers heat to the ribeye by direct conduction rather than hot air (oven) or water (sous vide), and that direct contact between the superheated metal and the fat-rich exterior creates the defining characteristic of the method: extreme, rapid crust development. The Maillard reaction requires surface temperatures above approximately 140 °C; a properly preheated cast-iron or carbon-steel pan reaches 200–250 °C in the dry surface zone and sustains that temperature because of the pan's thermal mass, even as the cold steak absorbs energy. On a ribeye specifically, the renderable fat at the exterior amplifies the browning chemistry — fatty acids and amino acids react at lower activation energies than lean muscle proteins, deepening the caramelisation and adding dairy-like richness to the crust that a lean steak cannot produce.

The two dominant technique variants documented in the source recipes (rapid-flip / Kenji vs single-flip / Ramsay and Pépin) produce measurably different outcomes. Rapid-flip prevents the formation of a thick grey band immediately below one face by distributing heat symmetrically across both faces simultaneously; it also slows overall core temperature rise, making the cook more controllable. Single-flip with active butter-basting focuses intense heat on one face, builds a thicker, darker crust on the seared side, and relies on rendered-butter basting of the upper face to maintain moisture. Both are appropriate for the ribeye's marbled structure; the choice affects crust character more than interior doneness.

## Summary

Pan searing is the definitive indoor method for ribeye: the extreme conductive heat of a preheated cast-iron or carbon-steel pan drives the Maillard reaction across the fat-rich exterior, producing a deeply caramelised crust that the cut's intramuscular fat enriches from within — a combination no leaner steak can replicate in a pan. Four mainstream traditions (American, British/French restaurant, French classical, Australian) agree on medium-rare as the target, with final serving temperatures clustered at 52–58 °C; the Japanese A5 Wagyu preparation targets rare (46–49 °C) for fat-preservation reasons specific to ultra-high marbling. For standard ribeye the MEATER pull target is 49–54 °C, with carryover of 3–5 °C bringing the result to medium-rare without overshooting.
