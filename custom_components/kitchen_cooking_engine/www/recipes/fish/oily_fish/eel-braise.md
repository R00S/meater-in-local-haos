# Eel × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Braised eel is one of the great underrepresented culinary traditions: *paling in 't groen* (eel braised in green herb sauce) is the national dish of Flanders; *matelote d'anguille* is a cornerstone of classical French Burgundian cuisine; 红烧鳗鱼 (*hóngshāo mán yú*, red-braised eel) is a celebrated Chinese family dish; and *anguille en matelote* has Italian, Polish and German variants. Braising suits eel particularly well because the fat-rich, collagen-bearing flesh responds to moist heat by surrendering its collagen into the surrounding liquid, creating a naturally gelatinous sauce, while the fat simultaneously enriches the braising liquid. Unlike meat braising where a long, high-temperature braise (85–95 °C) is needed to break down hard muscle fibres, eel's fish collagen softens at much lower temperatures (55–65 °C). As a result, most eel braises use a gentle simmer of 80–90 °C liquid temperature for 15–30 minutes rather than the multi-hour braises used for beef or pork. The target internal temperature of the eel itself will therefore be in the range of 65–72 °C — higher than lean pan-fried fish, lower than a long meat braise. Probe placement: insert lengthwise from the cut end of a section into the centre of the thickest flesh portion. Carryover in a braising liquid is negligible — the surrounding liquid buffers temperature change and the fish cools slowly.

## Source recipes

### 1. Paling in 't groen (Eel in green herb sauce) — Flemish classic
**Inspired by**: Sas Vandebosch & Wim Opbrouck, *De echte Vlaamse keuken* (Lannoo, 2007); and traditional Flemish recipe documented at dagelijksekost.een.be (Piet Huysentruyt)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 20 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg fresh eel, gutted, skinned, cut into 6–8 cm sections
- 50 g butter
- 3 shallots, finely chopped
- 1 garlic clove, minced
- 150 ml dry white wine (Riesling or Muscadet)
- 300 ml fish stock
- **Green herb mixture** (the defining element): 100 g fresh sorrel leaves, 50 g fresh chervil, 50 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, 30 g fresh mint, 30 g fresh tarragon, 20 g fresh thyme leaves — all finely chopped or blended
- salt, white pepper
- juice of ½ lemon

**Method**
1. Sweat the shallots and garlic in the butter in a wide sauté pan or saucepan over medium heat until softened, about 4 minutes.
2. Add the white wine; bring to a boil and reduce by half.
3. Add the fish stock; bring back to a simmer.
4. Season the eel sections with salt and white pepper. Add to the simmering liquid.
5. Insert the probe lengthwise from the cut end of the thickest section, tip toward the centre of the flesh.
6. Braise at a gentle simmer (liquid temperature approximately 85–90 °C) for 15–18 minutes, turning the eel sections once halfway through.
7. When the probe reads target temperature, remove the eel. Add the chopped green herbs to the hot liquid; stir to combine and wilt the herbs; add lemon juice.
8. **Pull at 68°C / 154°F** (154°F). Traditional *paling in 't groen* simmers the eel until the flesh has rendered its fat into the sauce and separates cleanly from any pin bones — fully cooked and just beginning to give way at the thickest sections.
9. Return the eel to the herb sauce for serving. Rest 5 min in the sauce.
10. Serve in deep plates with crusty white bread. Final serving temperature: **68°C / 154°F** (well_done, eel has rendered fully into the green sauce).

### 2. Matelote d'anguille au Chablis — Burgundian French classic
**Inspired by**: Auguste Escoffier, *Le Guide Culinaire* (Flammarion, 1903), recipe 'Matelote à la Normande' adapted for anguille; and Paul Bocuse, *La Cuisine du Marché* (Flammarion, 1976)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g fresh eel, gutted and skinned, cut into 6–8 cm sections
- salt and freshly ground pepper
- 2 tbsp clarified butter
- 3 shallots, finely diced
- 1 carrot, finely diced
- 1 celery stalk, finely diced
- 1 bouquet garni (bay, thyme, parsley stalks)
- 400 ml Chablis or other dry white Burgundy
- 200 ml fish stock
- 100 ml crème fraîche
- 20 g cold butter (to finish)
- 1 tbsp flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Season the eel sections with salt and pepper.
2. Heat the clarified butter in a wide saucepan or casserole over medium-high heat. Sear the eel sections briefly on all sides (2 minutes total) to colour slightly; remove and set aside.
3. In the same pan, sweat the shallots, carrot and celery for 5 minutes. Add the bouquet garni.
4. Pour in the Chablis; bring to a boil and reduce by one-third. Add the fish stock.
5. Return the eel to the pan. Insert the probe lengthwise from the cut end into the thickest section.
6. Braise at a gentle simmer for 20–25 minutes.
7. Remove the eel. Discard the bouquet garni. Reduce the sauce by one-third; whisk in the crème fraîche; reduce again to a coating consistency. Finish with the cold butter.
8. **Pull at 70°C / 158°F** (158°F). The French *matelote* is a more substantial braise than *paling in 't groen* — the sear step and the longer simmer in Chablis bring the eel to a richer, more deeply cooked state where the fat has fully rendered into the wine sauce.
9. Return the eel to the sauce. Rest 5 min. Garnish with parsley.
10. Serve with boiled potatoes or crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **70°C / 158°F** (well_done, fully rendered).

### 3. 红烧鳗鱼 (Hóngshāo mán yú) — Red-braised eel (Chinese)
**Inspired by**: Fuchsia Dunlop, *Land of Fish and Rice: Recipes from the Culinary Heart of China* (Bloomsbury, 2016); and Jiang Gao Yong, traditional Jiangnan technique; also documented in Lin Hong's *Shanjia Qinggong* (Mountain Home Clear Offerings, Song dynasty)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 25 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g fresh river eel (*河鳗, hé mán*), gutted, skinned, cut on the diagonal into 5–6 cm sections
- 2 tbsp vegetable or peanut oil
- 4 slices fresh ginger
- 3 garlic cloves, lightly crushed
- 2 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine
- 2 tbsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 200 ml water or light chicken stock
- 1 tsp sesame oil (finish)
- 2 spring onion stalks, sliced diagonally

**Method**
1. Heat the oil in a wok or heavy casserole over medium-high heat until shimmering.
2. Add the ginger and garlic; fry 30 seconds until fragrant.
3. Add the eel sections; stir-fry gently for 2 minutes to lightly colour.
4. Add the Shaoxing wine; let it bubble for 30 seconds.
5. Add both soy sauces, sugar and stock; stir to combine. Bring to a boil.
6. Insert the probe lengthwise from the cut end of the thickest section.
7. Reduce to a gentle simmer; cover and braise for 20 minutes, turning the eel once.
8. Uncover; increase heat and reduce the sauce to a glossy coating consistency (about 3 minutes).
9. **Pull at 72°C / 162°F** (162°F). Chinese red-braised eel uses longer braising than the Belgian or French method; the soy-sugar sauce requires the eel to be fully rendered and very deeply cooked so the sauce penetrates and glazes the flesh. At 72 °C the sections separate from the spine cleanly and absorb the glaze fully.
10. Drizzle with sesame oil and scatter with spring onion. Rest 3 min.
11. Serve with steamed white rice. Final serving temperature: **72°C / 162°F** (well_done, deeply braised and glazed).

### 4. Anguille en matelote — Italian variant (anguille in umido al pomodoro)
**Inspired by**: Ada Boni, *Il Talismano della Felicità* (Colombo Editore, 1929); and the Ferrara (Emilia-Romagna) eel braising tradition documented at ferraracittàdarte.it/anguille
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g eel (*anguilla*), gutted, skin on (or skinned), cut into 8 cm sections
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 1 onion, finely sliced
- 2 garlic cloves, sliced
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary
- 2 bay leaves
- 200 ml dry white wine
- 300 ml passata di pomodoro (tomato purée)
- salt, freshly ground pepper
- 2 tbsp flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Heat olive oil in a wide saucepan over medium heat. Add the onion and sweat for 5 minutes.
2. Add garlic, rosemary and bay leaves; cook 2 minutes until fragrant.
3. Add the eel sections; turn to coat in the aromatics.
4. Pour in the white wine; let it bubble for 2 minutes.
5. Add the passata; season with salt and pepper. Stir to submerge the eel.
6. Insert the probe lengthwise from the cut end of the thickest section.
7. Cover and braise at a gentle simmer for 20–25 minutes.
8. **Pull at 70°C / 158°F** (158°F). The Italian tomato braise produces a deeply flavoured sauce where the eel has fully rendered its fat. In the Ferrara tradition this preparation is a Christmas Eve staple; the flesh should separate from the bone with no resistance.
9. Rest 5 min in the sauce. Scatter over parsley.
10. Serve over polenta or with crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **70°C / 158°F** (well_done, fully rendered).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Vandebosch (inspired) | Belgium 🇧🇪 | Green herb braise, 15–18 min gentle simmer | 68°C / 154°F |
| Escoffier (inspired) | France | Wine braise with sear, 20–25 min | 70°C / 158°F |
| Dunlop (inspired) | China 🇨🇳 | Soy-sugar red braise, 20 min covered | 72°C / 162°F |
| Ada Boni (inspired) | Italy | Tomato braise with white wine, 20–25 min | 70°C / 158°F |

The observed pull range is **68–72°C**. This is higher than for pan-fried, oven-baked or grilled eel (64–68 °C) because the liquid-immersed braise brings the eel into extended contact with heat sufficient to penetrate even the thickest sections deeply and render the collagen fully. The Chinese red-braise runs hottest because the uncovered reduction phase concentrates heat around the eel sections in the final minutes. All traditions represent well_done preparations.

## What makes this method special

Braising is the only method that produces a genuine sauce from the eel itself. The eel's collagen dissolves into the braising liquid, making it naturally gelatinous; the rendered fat enriches it further. *Paling in 't groen* produces a vivid green sauce that exists nowhere else in European cooking. *Matelote d'anguille* is among Escoffier's foundational fish dishes. Chinese 红烧 (hóngshāo, red-braising) is one of the most versatile and beloved Chinese cooking techniques, and eel (*mán yú*) is among its most celebrated subjects. Braising also makes eel accessible to cooks without specialist grilling or smoking equipment.

## Summary

Braising is one of the oldest and most important eel cooking traditions. Belgian *paling in 't groen* (Group A by geographic proximity; Flemish national dish), French *matelote d'anguille*, Chinese 红烧鳗鱼 (Group A), and Italian *anguille in umido* all finish at 68–72 °C. The braising method maximises the flavour contribution of eel's unique collagen-and-fat profile by turning both into the sauce.
