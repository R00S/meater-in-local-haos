# Eel × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Grilling eel is among the most culinarily significant fish-cooking traditions in the world: Japanese *unagi kabayaki* — butterflied eel charcoal-grilled with sweet soy sauce (*tare*) — is a culinary Group A cornerstone that has been practised for centuries. European and Western grilling of eel uses a different preparation (gutted whole eel, or skin-on sections) over direct heat on a grate or barbecue. The high fat content (15–25 g/100 g) makes eel self-basting and highly tolerant of high-heat grilling: the fat renders, bastes the flesh, and creates intense flavour from the Maillard reaction. The skin, if left on, char-crisp and becomes pleasantly chewy on the grill. Japanese kabayaki involves multiple rounds of glazing and grilling, and in the Kantō region includes a steaming step between grill rounds to render fat and tenderise the flesh. Carryover after grilling is moderate (2–3 °C) because of the high fat content buffering rapid temperature change. Probe placement: insert lengthwise along the body into the thickest cross-section behind the head; in butterflied preparations, insert from the thick edge into the flesh.

## Source recipes

### 1. Unagi Kabayaki — traditional Kantō style (Japan)
**Inspired by**: Traditional Japanese unagi kabayaki documented in Shizuo Tsuji, *Japanese Cooking: A Simple Art* (Kodansha International, 1980); and Naomi Duguid & Jeffrey Alford, *Flatbreads and Flavors* cross-referenced with contemporary Japanese culinary sources
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min (with steaming) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 large *unagi* (Japanese freshwater eel, *Anguilla japonica*), about 400 g, butterflied along the back, backbone removed, head removed (backcut, Kantō style)
- **Tare (sweet soy glaze)**: 100 ml soy sauce (*shoyu*), 80 ml mirin, 40 ml sake, 1 tbsp sugar — reduce by one-third until syrupy
- steamer, for the Kantō rest step

**Method**
1. Prepare the butterflied eel skin-side up on a cutting board; score the skin side lightly to prevent curling on the grill.
2. Skewer the butterflied eel lengthwise with two flat metal skewers, one near each long edge, to hold it flat.
3. Grill skin-side down over medium-hot charcoal (binchōtan preferred) for 4–5 minutes until the skin begins to char at the edges and the flesh firms. Flip and grill flesh-side for 2–3 minutes.
4. **Kantō-style steam rest**: Transfer the partially cooked eel to a steamer and steam for 8–10 minutes. This step renders the fat and tenderises the flesh — a defining feature of Tokyo-style kabayaki not used in Kansai.
5. Return the eel to the grill. Dip or brush with tare; grill 2 minutes per side. Repeat the glaze-and-grill step two more times, building up a lacquered coating.
6. Insert the probe (or take a spot reading) along the thickest section of the flesh immediately after the final glaze-and-grill pass.
7. **Pull at 68°C / 154°F** (154°F). Traditional kabayaki is cooked firmly through and glazed multiple times; at 68 °C the flesh is opaque, separated cleanly from the skin, and saturated with tare. Kabayaki temperatures run higher than simple grilled eel because of the multiple heat passes and the requirement that the fat fully render.
8. Rest 2 min. Serve over steamed white rice (*unaju* or *unadon*). Final serving temperature: **68°C / 154°F** (well_done — fully rendered, lacquered, the classic finish).

### 2. Paling op de rooster met mosterd-dillesaus — 24Kitchen.nl
**Inspired by**: https://www.24kitchen.nl/recepten/paling-op-de-rooster-mosterd-dillesaus
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g fresh eel, gutted, skinned, cut into 8–10 cm sections
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 1 tbsp lemon juice
- sea salt, freshly ground pepper
- **Mustard-dill sauce**: 2 tbsp Dijon mustard, 1 tsp honey, 2 tbsp chopped fresh dill, 1 tbsp white wine vinegar, 4 tbsp olive oil, salt and pepper

**Method**
1. Coat the eel sections in olive oil and lemon juice; season with salt and pepper.
2. Preheat a gas or charcoal grill to medium-high heat. Oil the grate.
3. Thread eel sections on two parallel skewers to keep them from spinning.
4. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest section from the cut end, tip toward the centre.
5. Grill the eel sections 5–7 minutes per side, basting once with the olive oil marinade.
6. Whisk together the sauce ingredients.
7. **Pull at 65°C / 149°F** (149°F). Dutch grilled eel is cooked through and the fat rendered; the sections are fully opaque and will release easily from the grate.
8. Rest 3 min. Serve with mustard-dill sauce. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 3. Gegrillter Aal nach Rhein-Art — Rheinfischerei-Rezepte
**Inspired by**: https://www.rheinfischerei-rezepte.de/gegrillter-aal-rheinart
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 15 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg fresh eel, cleaned, skin on, cut into sections 8–10 cm
- 4 tbsp sunflower oil
- 2 garlic cloves, crushed
- 1 tbsp fresh thyme
- 1 tbsp paprika
- salt and pepper
- lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Mix the sunflower oil, garlic, thyme, paprika, salt and pepper; coat the eel sections and marinate 20 minutes.
2. Preheat a charcoal grill to medium-high heat. Oil the grill bars.
3. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest section, tip at the mid-flesh.
4. Grill the eel skin-side down for 6–7 minutes until the skin crisps and chars lightly.
5. Flip and grill flesh-side for 5–6 minutes, basting occasionally with the remaining marinade.
6. **Pull at 65°C / 149°F** (149°F). The Rhine tradition grills eel firmly through to render the subcutaneous fat layer; the skin should be chewy and slightly charred.
7. Rest 3 min. Serve with lemon wedges and crusty bread.
8. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 4. Yılan balığı ızgara — Balık Lokantası tarifi (Turkish grilled eel)
**Inspired by**: Traditional Turkish Bosphorus eel preparation documented in Marianna Yerasimos, *500 Years of Ottoman Cuisine* (Boyut Yayıncılık, 2002); and contemporary Istanbul fish restaurant practice
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g fresh eel (*yılan balığı*), gutted and skinned, cut into 6–8 cm sections
- 4 tbsp olive oil
- 2 tbsp lemon juice
- 2 garlic cloves, grated
- 1 tsp cumin
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- salt and black pepper
- **Serving**: flat bread, fresh flat-leaf parsley, sumac, lemon wedges

**Method**
1. Mix the olive oil, lemon juice, garlic, cumin and oregano. Coat the eel sections and marinate for at least 30 minutes.
2. Thread on flat metal skewers; preheat the grill or charcoal mangal to high heat.
3. Insert the probe from the cut end of the thickest section, tip toward the centre.
4. Grill over high heat, turning every 3–4 minutes and basting with the marinade, for a total of 12–14 minutes.
5. **Pull at 65°C / 149°F** (149°F). Turkish and Ottoman tradition renders the eel fully — the sections should separate cleanly into juicy, slightly charred pieces.
6. Rest 3 min. Serve with flat bread, scattered flat-leaf parsley, sumac and lemon wedges.
7. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 145°F** (well_done).

The observed pull range is **65–68°C**. The Japanese kabayaki tradition uses multiple glaze-and-grill passes and a steaming step that drive the internal temperature higher than direct European grilling; the Dutch, German and Turkish methods all converge at 65 °C. All traditions treat the eel as a fully rendered, well_done fish rather than a medium-finish product. The fat content of eel gives it wide tolerance at these temperatures without drying.

## What makes this method special

Grilling activates eel's fat in a way no other method does: the heat renders subcutaneous fat into the flesh from both outside and inside simultaneously, while the grill surface crisps the exterior. In Japanese kabayaki, this fat-rendering is deliberately managed through the steaming step, which disperses the liquefied fat evenly through the flesh before the final glaze passes. In European grilling, the same fat self-bastes the sections during cooking, producing a rich, robust character that pairs naturally with acidic sauces (mustard, lemon, sumac). Grilled eel is also the best use case for the MEATER probe's lengthwise insertion technique along the body axis.
