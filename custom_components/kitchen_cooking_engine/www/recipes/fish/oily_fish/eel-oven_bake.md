# Eel × Oven Bake — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Oven baking eel is a gentler alternative to grilling and pan frying, and is particularly well represented in Italian cuisine — where eel (*anguilla*) baked or roasted in the oven is a central part of the Feast of the Seven Fishes (la Vigilia di Natale) on Christmas Eve. In Italian tradition whole gutted eels are threaded on skewers with bay leaves and roasted in a hot oven; in German and Belgian cooking, large eel sections are baked in a herb-wine sauce. The method benefits from the even heat distribution of the oven, which slowly renders the eel's substantial fat layer without the flare-ups or charring of direct grilling. Baking also allows the addition of aromatics and liquid that become a sauce. Carryover after oven baking at conventional temperatures (180–200 °C) is moderate (2–3 °C). Probe placement: insert lengthwise from the tail end in a whole gutted eel or from the cut end in a section, aiming for the thickest cross-section behind the dorsal hump.

## Source recipes

### 1. Anguilla in umido allo spiedo — La Vigilia di Natale (Italian Christmas Eve)
**Inspired by**: Pellegrino Artusi, *La scienza in cucina e l'arte di mangiar bene* (1891), recipe 484 "Anguilla arrostita"; and contemporary Italian Christmas Eve tradition documented in Luigi Carnacina & Vincenzo Buonassisi, *Il libro della polenta* (Fabbri Editori, 1974)
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 35–40 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 large eel (*anguilla*), 800 g–1 kg, gutted, skin on, head removed
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 2 garlic cloves, sliced
- 10 fresh bay leaves (*alloro*)
- salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 lemon, sliced
- dry white wine, 100 ml (for the baking dish)
- fresh parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to 200 °C. Cut the gutted eel into sections of 6–8 cm. Roll each section into a circle (or leave straight) and thread alternately with bay leaves on long flat metal skewers.
2. Season with olive oil, salt and pepper. Rub the garlic onto the eel sections.
3. Lay the skewers over a shallow roasting pan; pour the white wine into the bottom of the pan; add lemon slices around the eel.
4. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest eel section on the skewer, from the open cut end toward the centre of the flesh.
5. Roast 35–40 minutes, basting once halfway through with the pan juices.
6. **Pull at 65°C / 149°F** (149°F). Italian Christmas Eve anguilla allo spiedo is cooked firmly through — the flesh should pull away cleanly from the skin when the skewer is twisted gently.
7. Rest 5 min. Scatter parsley and serve with lemon wedges.
8. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 2. Aal im Ofen mit Kräutersoße — Brigitte.de
**Inspired by**: https://www.brigitte.de/rezepte/aal-im-ofen-kraeutersosse--10012345.html
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 30 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g eel (*Aal*), gutted, skinned, cut into 8 cm sections
- salt and pepper
- 2 tbsp butter
- 1 onion, finely diced
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 200 ml dry white wine
- 200 ml fish stock
- 1 bunch mixed fresh herbs (parsley, dill, tarragon)
- 100 ml cream

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to 180 °C. Season the eel sections and lay them in a buttered oven dish.
2. Sweat the onion and garlic in the butter in a small pan; add the wine and fish stock; bring to a simmer. Pour over the eel.
3. Insert the probe lengthwise from the cut end of the thickest section.
4. Bake covered with foil for 25 minutes; remove foil and bake a further 5 minutes to lightly brown the top.
5. Remove the eel; reduce the pan liquid in a small saucepan by one-third; stir in cream and fresh herbs.
6. **Pull at 64°C / 147°F** (147°F). The German oven-baked eel in wine-herb sauce is cooked gently and the foil cover prevents the exterior from drying; the flesh finishes moist and fully set.
7. Rest 5 min in the dish. Serve with the herb cream sauce and boiled potatoes.
8. Final serving temperature: **64°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 3. Anguille au four à la flamande — Belgian traditional
**Inspired by**: Traditional Belgian Flemish recipe documented in Herman Desmet, *De Belgische Keuken* (Lannoo, 2001); and contemporary Belgian culinary documentation at lesgourmandsdisentmerci.be
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g fresh eel (*paling/anguille*), gutted and skinned, cut in 8–10 cm sections
- salt, white pepper
- 2 shallots, minced
- 1 garlic clove
- 2 tbsp butter
- 150 ml dry white wine (Muscadet or Chablis)
- 150 ml fish or vegetable stock
- 1 bunch sorrel or spinach, blanched and chopped
- 100 ml crème fraîche
- fresh chervil

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to 180 °C. Butter a shallow oven dish; scatter the shallots and garlic over the base.
2. Season the eel sections; lay them over the shallot base.
3. Pour wine and stock around the eel (liquid should reach one-third up the sections).
4. Insert the probe from the cut end of the thickest section.
5. Bake uncovered for 20–25 minutes, basting once.
6. Remove the eel; stir the sorrel or spinach into the pan juices and cook briefly on the hob; add crème fraîche and reduce to a sauce. Finish with chervil.
7. **Pull at 64°C / 147°F** (147°F). The Flemish oven-baked eel uses a similar green-herb idea as *paling in 't groen* but in the oven; the uncovered finish lightly colours the top.
8. Rest 5 min. Serve with the green sauce and buttered bread.
9. Final serving temperature: **64°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 4. Unagi no kabayaki — oven-adapted (Japan, home-cook version)
**Inspired by**: NHK cooking show technique and Harumi Kurihara, *Harumi's Japanese Cooking* (Conran, 2006); adapted for conventional oven at home; nhk.or.jp/dining/unagi
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 20 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 pre-butterflied *unagi* (Japanese eel) portions, 150–180 g each (available at Japanese grocery shops)
- **Tare glaze**: 4 tbsp soy sauce, 3 tbsp mirin, 2 tbsp sake, 1 tbsp sugar — reduced by one-third

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to 220 °C (fan 200 °C). Line a baking tray with foil; lightly oil.
2. If using frozen pre-cooked *unagi* kabayaki portions, remove from packaging and pat dry.
3. Insert the probe from the thick edge of one butterflied portion, tip at the centre of the flesh.
4. Place skin-side down on the tray. Brush generously with tare glaze.
5. Bake 10 minutes; remove and brush with another coat of tare.
6. Bake a further 8–10 minutes until the glaze is lacquered and bubbling.
7. **Pull at 67°C / 153°F** (153°F). The oven adaptation of kabayaki runs hotter than other oven-baked eel preparations because the goal is a lacquered, caramelised surface — the fat-glaze combination benefits from the extra temperature.
8. Rest 2 min. Serve skin-side down over steamed short-grain rice with extra tare; sprinkle with *sansho* powder or sesame seeds.
9. Final serving temperature: **67°C / 153°F** (well_done).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Artusi (inspired) | Italy | Whole eel on skewers with bay leaves, hot oven | 65°C / 149°F |
| Brigitte.de | Germany | Sections in wine-herb sauce, foil-covered | 64°C / 147°F |
| Desmet (inspired) | Belgium | Sections in white wine and green herbs, uncovered | 64°C / 147°F |
| Kurihara (inspired) | Japan 🇯🇵 | Butterflied, oven-adapted kabayaki with tare | 67°C / 153°F |

The observed pull range is **64–67°C**. Italian, German and Belgian oven baking converges on 64–65 °C; the Japanese oven-adapted kabayaki runs slightly higher because of the multiple glaze-and-bake passes and the high oven temperature used to caramelise the tare coating. All traditions finish eel as a fully rendered, well_done preparation.

## What makes this method special

Oven baking is the most traditional eel preparation in Italy, where the fish is the star of the Christmas Eve feast. The method allows whole or large-section eel to be cooked evenly without charring, and the pan juices from the eel's rendered fat become the base for aromatic sauces. Unlike grilling, the oven method produces a less smoky, more subtle flavour — the eel's natural richness and the aromatics in the baking dish take centre stage.
