# Eel × Smoker — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Eel is one of the fattiest fish in the cooking engine, with collagen-rich flesh and a thick skin that transforms dramatically under smoke. Hot smoking suits eel because the long, gentle heat renders fat, firms the flesh, and makes the skin easier to strip or chew depending on the tradition. Unlike lean white fish, eel benefits from extended drying and smoking phases before it is considered finished. Carryover after smoking is modest, but the flesh continues to soften during the post-smoke rest.

## Styckesprofil

Ål är en av de fetaste fiskarna i hela cooking engine, med kollagenrikt kött och tjockt skinn som förändras kraftigt av rökning. Varmrökning passar ål särskilt väl eftersom den långa, milda värmen smälter ut fett, sätter köttet och gör skinnet lättare att dra av eller tugga beroende på tradition. Till skillnad från mager vit fisk tjänar ål på en tydlig tork- och rökfas innan den räknas som färdig. Eftervärmen efter rökningen är måttlig, men köttet mjuknar vidare under vilan efteråt.

## Source recipes

### 1. Ålrökning — Alsterbro / Tingsås hembygdsförening
**Inspired by**: http://www.alsterbro.com/tisak/ALR.htm
**Serves**: not stated · **Prep**: not stated · **Cook**: 6-7 h smoking · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- fresh eel, cleaned but skin on
- sand, for removing slime
- 10% brine
- clean water, for rinsing
- alder wood (or beech in Skåne/Denmark)

**Method**
1. Roll the eel in sand to remove the slime, then clean out the innards while leaving the skin on.
2. Salt the eel in a 10% brine for about 2-3 days.
3. On smoking day, light the smokehouse an hour in advance to drive off internal moisture.
4. Replace the brine with clean water and soak the eel for about 30 minutes, then hang it up so excess water drains and the bodies straighten.
5. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest part behind the head before the final hot-smoking phase.
6. Hang the eel in the smokehouse over low heat for 2-2.5 hours to dry it, then raise the heat and continue smoking for another 4-4.5 hours while maintaining smoke rather than strong fire.
7. The eels are ready when the skin releases easily from the flesh.
8. **Pull at 64°C / 147°F** (147°F). The source uses skin-release as the doneness cue after a long hot smoke; for eel this corresponds to a fully rendered, safe finish above the minimum fish temperature.
9. Rest 30 min. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 2. Zelf paling roken — Palingkopen.nl
**Inspired by**: https://www.palingkopen.nl/zelf-roken/
**Serves**: not stated · **Prep**: not stated · **Cook**: not stated · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- fresh cleaned eel for smoking
- water
- kitchen salt
- beech, oak or elm wood / chips / smoke dust

**Method**
1. Make a slow brine with 10 litres water and 500 g salt and leave the eel in it at least 12 hours, or use the site’s fast brine of 5 litres water plus 1 kg salt for about 17-19 minutes depending on size.
2. Skewer the eel through the gill cover and hang it with space between the fish.
3. Dry the eel first in the smoker at about 60-70°C until the skin no longer feels sticky like newspaper.
4. Add fresh wood or smoke dust, bring the smoker back to roughly 65-70°C and close the vents so the fire smoulders and produces smoke.
5. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest cross-section as the smoke phase begins.
6. Check every 10-15 minutes for colour, rendered fat and a core temperature in range.
7. **Pull at 65°C / 149°F** (149°F). This Dutch source explicitly states a core target between 60 and 65°C; using the upper end gives a reliable fully rendered finish for eel.
8. Wrap and rest 30 min so the skin softens for easier peeling. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 3. Aale räuchern — Chefkoch.de
**Inspired by**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/3272841486303372/Aale-raeuchern.html
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 1 h 10 min · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 eels
- 1 tsp pepper
- 1 tsp herb salt
- 1 tsp celery powder
- 1/2 tsp sugar

**Method**
1. Heat the smoker or smoking oven to 100°C.
2. Gut and clean the eels thoroughly and let them dry for about 30 minutes.
3. Season them evenly with the pepper, herb salt, celery powder and sugar.
4. Insert the probe lengthwise into the thickest eel.
5. Lay or hang the eels in the smoker and smoke for 10 minutes at 100°C.
6. Lower the temperature to 70°C and continue smoking for about 1 hour more.
7. **Pull at 64°C / 147°F** (147°F). This hot-smoke schedule starts high, then settles at 70°C, which supports a finished core safely above 63°C.
8. Rest 30 min. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 4. Smoked eel — BestRecipes24
**Inspired by**: https://bestrecipes24.com/recipe/smoked-eel
**Serves**: not stated · **Prep**: not stated · **Cook**: 1-1.5 h · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- fresh eel
- water
- salt (1 kg for 5-6 litres cold water)
- fruitwood chips, soaked

**Method**
1. If the eel is alive, place it briefly in strong salt solution, then clean and remove all surface slime.
2. Gut the fish but leave the heads and tails on.
3. Make the brine using 1 kg table salt to 5-6 litres cold water and soak the eel for 20-30 minutes, then dry it well and hang it in a draft for about an hour.
4. Prepare the smoker and heat it to 80-90°C; add a thin layer of soaked fruitwood chips over the coals.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the body before closing the smoker.
6. Arrange the eel with space between each fish and smoke for 1-1.5 hours.
7. The source says the finished fish will be white and aromatic inside with a brown-golden exterior.
8. **Pull at 64°C / 147°F** (147°F). The visual cue of white flesh after 80-90°C hot smoking indicates a fully cooked eel beyond the fish-safe minimum.
9. Rest 30 min. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Alsterbro | Sweden | Long smokehouse process, skin-release cue | 64°C / 147°F |
| Palingkopen.nl | Netherlands | Explicit core target 60-65°C | 65°C / 149°F |
| Chefkoch.de | Germany | 100°C start then 70°C hot smoke | 64°C / 147°F |
| BestRecipes24 | Russia | 80-90°C hot smoke until flesh turns white | 64°C / 147°F |

The observed pull range is **64-65°C**. All four smoking traditions treat eel as a fully cooked, rendered fish rather than a soft-finished fillet, and the Dutch source explicitly confirms a 60-65°C core target. Because eel is fatty and collagen-rich, the higher finish is not a defect; it is exactly what gives hot-smoked eel its supple, rich texture.

## What makes this method special

Smoking changes eel more dramatically than it changes most fish. The long dry-smoke phase evaporates surface moisture, tightens the skin, and concentrates the natural fats before the hotter phase melts those fats through the flesh. Unlike pan-fried eel, smoked eel gains much of its finished texture during the post-smoke rest, when the rendered fat redistributes and the skin softens. The method is therefore both a preservation technique and a texture-building technique.
