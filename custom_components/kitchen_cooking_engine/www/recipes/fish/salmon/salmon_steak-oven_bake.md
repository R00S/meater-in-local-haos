# Salmon Steak × Oven Bake — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

A salmon steak is a cross-cut through the whole fish, perpendicular to the spine,
retaining the central spine bone, the curved rib bones, and skin all the way around
the outer edge. Oven baking suits this cut because the all-around radiant heat works
on every surface at once — the cross-section is a roughly horseshoe shape with no flat
bottom face, so a pan-bottom is wasted on it, but a baking dish or foil parcel
surrounds the steak with hot air and steam. The central bones gently conduct heat
toward the geometric centre, slightly accelerating cooking compared to a same-thickness
fillet baked the same way. Carryover at oven-bake temperatures is modest (1–2 °C)
because the cut is small and is removed at low internal temperatures.

The thin belly flaps will overcook relative to the back-flesh if the steak is laid
flat in a baking dish. Folding and securing the flaps inward, or wrapping the entire
steak in a foil/parchment papillote with juice, is the common solution — the en-papillote
preparation steams the flaps gently while the back-flesh continues to roast.

Probe placement: insert from the outer edge of the back-flesh (the thickest section),
horizontally, aiming at the geometric centre. Keep the tip at least 1 cm clear of the
central spine bone, which conducts heat and gives a lagging reading. For a foil/parchment
papillote, route the probe lead so the parcel can still be sealed.

## Styckesprofil

En laxbiff är ett tvärssnitt igenom hela fisken, vinkelrätt mot ryggraden, och behåller det centrala ryggradsbendet, de böjda revbenen och skinnet runt hela yttre kanten. Ugnsbakning passar detta snitt eftersom den allrunt-strålningsvärme arbetar på varje yta på en gång — tvärsnittsprofilen är ett ungefärligt hästskoformat utan platt botten, varför en panna är onödig men en ugnsform eller foliepaket omger biffen med het luft och ånga.

De centrala benen leder varsamt värme mot den geometriska mitten, vilket något accelererar tillagningen jämfört med en fillet av samma tjocklek ugnsrostad på samma sätt. De tunna magliskorna överkokkar relativt ryggköttet om biffen läggs platt; att vika och säkra dem inåt, eller linda hela biffen i folie/bakplåtspapper med lite vätska (*en papillote*), är standardlösningen. Sondplacering: stick in från yttre kanten av ryggköttet horisontellt mot den geometriska mitten — håll spetsen minst 1 cm från ryggradsbendet. Eftertillagning: 1–2 °C. Innertemperatur: 52–60 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Darne de saumon en papillote facile (inspired by CuisineAZ)
**Inspired by**: CuisineAZ, cuisineaz.com/recettes/darne-de-saumon-en-papillote-facile-120710.aspx  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min · **Total**: 35 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 darnes de saumon (salmon steaks)
- crème fraîche épaisse (thick crème fraîche), to brush
- 4 tomates (tomatoes), cut into rounds
- persil (parsley), chopped
- 1 oignon (onion), peeled and finely chopped
- sel, poivre (salt, pepper)

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to **180 °C** (th. 6). Cut 4 large sheets of parchment paper. Place a salmon steak in the centre of each. Brush the fish with crème fraîche.
2. Wash and dry the tomatoes; slice into rounds. Wash, dry, and chop the parsley. Peel and finely chop the onion. Distribute the tomato rounds, parsley, and onion over the salmon steaks. Season with salt and pepper.
3. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre, at least 1 cm clear of the central spine bone. Route the lead so the parcel still seals.
4. Carefully fold the parchment sheets to form papillotes. Place on a baking sheet.
5. Bake for **25–30 minutes**.
6. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is assessed visually — open the papillote and check that the centre of the salmon is "rose pâle" (pale pink) and the flesh detaches easily.
7. Serve immediately. (CuisineAZ's FAQ on this page also notes you can lift the central spine bone out with a fork before serving for easier eating.)

### 2. Darne de saumon citronnée au four de grand-mère (inspired by CuisineAZ)
**Inspired by**: CuisineAZ, cuisineaz.com/recettes/darne-de-saumon-citronee-au-four-de-grand-mere-76799.aspx  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 darnes de saumon (salmon steaks)
- ail (garlic), échalote (shallot), oignon (onion), persil, coriandre — finely chopped
- 2 citrons (lemons) — one juiced, one sliced into rounds
- 1 tomate (tomato) — sliced into rounds
- huile d'olive (olive oil), 3 tbsp
- sel, poivre

**Method**
1. Preheat the oven to **200 °C** (th. 6–7).
2. Place the salmon steaks in an oven dish.
3. Crush the garlic; finely chop half the onion, the shallot, the parsley and the coriander. Combine in a bowl.
4. Juice one lemon; add the juice and 3 tbsp olive oil to the herb-aromatic mixture. Season with salt and pepper.
5. Slice the tomato and the second lemon into rounds; lay them on top of the salmon steaks.
6. Slice the remaining onion half into thin strips and add over the steaks.
7. Spoon the herb mixture over the top; drizzle with a little more olive oil.
8. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, clear of the central spine bone.
9. Bake for **20 minutes**.
10. **Pull at internal temperature ≈ 60 °C / 140 °F.** This is the explicit guideline given in CuisineAZ's FAQ on the recipe page: *"Le saumon est cuit lorsque sa chair devient opaque et se détache facilement à la fourchette. La température interne devrait être d'environ 60 °C."*

### 3. Laxkotletter med grönsaker (inspired by Tasteline)
**Inspired by**: Tasteline, tasteline.com/recept/laxkotletter-med-gronsaker/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: 10–15 min in foil

**Ingredients**
- 4 laxkotletter (salmon steaks)
- 3 morötter (carrots), chopped
- 3 tomater (tomatoes), chopped
- 1 purjolök (leek)
- 1 citron (lemon)
- citronpeppar (lemon pepper), salt
- potatis (potatoes), to serve

**Method**
1. Heat the oven to **250 °C**.
2. Place each salmon steak on a sheet of aluminium foil. Season with lemon pepper and salt.
3. Add chopped carrots, tomato, and leek on top. Season again to taste.
4. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre, clear of the central spine bone. Route the lead so the foil parcel can still seal.
5. Fold the foil into a sealed parcel. Place in the oven and bake **10–15 minutes**.
6. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is by time and parcel-steam alone.
7. Serve with potatoes.

### 4. Ugnsbakad laxkotlett med gorgonzola (inspired by Matklubben)
**Inspired by**: Matklubben, matklubben.se/recept/ugnsbakad-laxkotlett-med-gorgonzola/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 6 medium-sized cooked (cold) potatoes
- 4 laxkotletter (salmon steaks, "köp över disk" — buy from the fresh-fish counter)
- 100 g gorgonzola
- 8 soltorkade tomater (sun-dried tomatoes)
- 1 msk smör (1 tbsp butter), to grease the dish

**Method**
1. Boil the potatoes ahead, ideally the day before so they are cold. Slice and lay them in the bottom of a buttered oven dish.
2. Lay the salmon steaks on top of the potatoes. Cut the sun-dried tomatoes into smaller pieces if needed.
3. Cut the gorgonzola into 4 wedges.
4. Distribute the sun-dried tomatoes over the steaks; place a slice of gorgonzola on top of each steak.
5. Do not add salt — the gorgonzola is salty enough.
6. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre, clear of the central spine bone.
7. Gratinate in a **200 °C** oven for **20 minutes**.
8. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is by time alone.
9. Serve with a salad.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Recipe | Oven temp | Cook time | Explicit pull temp |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Western (French, CuisineAZ papillote) | 180 °C | 25–30 min | Not given (visual: pale pink centre) |
| Western (French, CuisineAZ citronée) | 200 °C | 20 min | **≈ 60 °C / 140 °F** (page FAQ) |
| Northern (Swedish, Tasteline) | 250 °C | 10–15 min in foil | Not given |
| Northern (Swedish, Matklubben) | 200 °C | 20 min | Not given |

The full observed range of stated internal temperatures is exactly **one data point**:
≈ 60 °C from CuisineAZ's grand-mère citronée recipe. That value sits at the upper end
of the medium / well-done boundary (cooking_data.py: medium 54 °C; well-done 63 °C) and
matches the French / British home-cooking norm of "well-cooked through but still moist".
The other three sources are silent on internal temperature and rely on time
(10–30 min) at oven temperatures from 180 °C (gentle papillote) up to 250 °C (high-heat
foil parcel). This is consistent with the salmon_fillet oven_bake research already in
the repository — oven-baked salmon, whether steak or fillet, is overwhelmingly cooked
to the time-and-visual-cue standard at home, with the thermometer being a niche tool.

Oven temperatures cluster as follows:
- **180 °C** (papillote, gentle, longer cook): conserves moisture, reaches centre slowly.
- **200 °C** (open dish, moderate): the most common middle-ground (2 of 4 sources).
- **250 °C** (sealed foil, brief): the parcel traps steam, and the high heat brings the
  package to temperature in 10–15 min.

The explicit 60 °C target from CuisineAZ should be read as *the upper bound* for medium
to lower well-done — a MEATER user wanting the recommended_doneness `medium` (54 °C)
should pull earlier than this French recipe suggests.

> **Tradition gap**: Eastern (Asian) and Southern (Mediterranean / Middle Eastern / African)
> traditions are not represented here. As noted in the pan_sear leaf for this same cut,
> these traditions overwhelmingly use salmon fillets in their modern recipe collections;
> the cross-cut salmon steak as a named preparation is currently strongest in Northern
> European (Nordic) and Western European (French / British) cooking.

## What makes this method special

Oven baking is well suited to a salmon steak because the cut's irregular horseshoe
cross-section presents no flat face to a pan, but it sits comfortably in a baking dish
or papillote where the heat reaches every surface. The central spine and rib bones,
buried in the middle of the steak, conduct heat toward the centre and pull the
geometric centre to temperature slightly ahead of an equivalent-thickness fillet baked
the same way — so a salmon steak is forgiving of the time-only cook approach used in
all four sources here. Wrapping in foil or parchment (2 of 4 sources) creates a
microclimate of steam that protects the thin belly flaps from drying while the back
flesh comes up to temperature. The MEATER probe matters most for the open-dish methods
(2 of 4 sources) where there is no steam protection and the back-flesh can tip into
well-done in the last few minutes.

## Summary

Oven-baking salmon steaks is a moderate-heat, time-driven preparation across all four
fetched sources: 180–250 °C for 10–30 minutes depending on whether the steak is open
in a dish or sealed in foil/parchment. Only one of four sources gives an explicit
internal-temperature target (CuisineAZ: ≈ 60 °C, page FAQ), which sits at the upper
end of the medium band and represents the French home-cooking norm of "well-cooked
through". Northern (Swedish) sources emphasise the bake-with-vegetables tradition
(carrots, leek, sun-dried tomato, gorgonzola) where the salmon shares its cooking
liquor with its accompaniments; Western (French) sources lean on the papillote
(parchment parcel) and on grand-mère style aromatic baths of garlic, herbs, lemon,
and onion. Eastern and Culinary Group C traditions for this specific cut are underrepresented
in modern recipe collections, where the fillet form has largely displaced the steak.
The MEATER probe is most valuable for the open-dish preparations, where the absence of
steam protection makes the back-flesh easier to tip into well-done in the final
minutes.
