# Arctic Char × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Arctic char is a cold-water salmonid with firmer, finer-grained flesh than salmon and a cleaner flavour than most farmed trout. The skin crisps exceptionally well in a pan, while the moderate fat content keeps the flesh moist during short, direct cooking. Pan searing suits the cut because it can fully crisp the skin before the centre overcooks, especially when the fish is cooked mostly skin-side down. Carryover is modest on fillets, usually only a couple of degrees, so the fish should be pulled just before the flakes fully set through the thickest part.

## Styckesprofil

Röding är en kallvattenslaxfisk med fastare och finare kött än lax samt renare smak än mycket odlad öring. Skinnet blir mycket krispigt i panna, medan den måttliga fetthalten hjälper köttet att förbli saftigt vid kort, direkt tillagning. Pannstekning passar därför mycket bra eftersom skinnet kan bli färdigt innan kärnan överkokar, särskilt när fisken tillagas nästan helt på skinnsidan. Eftervärmen är liten i filéer, oftast bara ett par grader, så fisken bör tas av strax innan flagorna sätter sig helt i den tjockaste delen.

## Source recipes

### 1. Skinnstekt röding med råstekt sparris — Köket.se
**Inspired by**: https://www.koket.se/skinnstekt-roding-med-rastekt-sparris
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: not stated · **Cook**: not stated · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g rödingfilé
- salt
- rapsolja
- smör
- ca 12 stjälkar vit sparris
- strösocker
- 3 schalottenlökar
- 1 klyfta vitlök
- 1 msk äppelcidervinäger
- 1 dl vitt vin
- 2 dl grädde
- 50 g smör
- 1 kruka persilja
- 10 blad ramslök
- 4 msk olivolja
- 1 citron, finrivet skal
- 1-2 klyftor vitlök, finriven
- fänkål
- blomkål
- gurka

**Method**
1. Peel the white asparagus, salt and lightly sugar it, let it sit 20 minutes, then pan-cook it in oil until just tender.
2. Reduce the shallots, garlic, asparagus peelings, cider vinegar and white wine by half; add the cream, reduce again, then whisk in the 50 g butter to make the sauce.
3. Mix the parsley, ramsons, olive oil, lemon zest and grated garlic into a gremolata.
4. Pat the char dry and season lightly with salt on both sides.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fillet from the side, aiming for the centre and keeping the tip clear of the skin.
6. Heat a frying pan, rub it with rapeseed oil, add a knob of butter and lay the char in skin-side down. Cook only on the skin side until the flesh just begins to whiten upward.
7. **Pull at 54°C / 129°F** (129°F). The source cue is “när fisken börjar vitna”; on char this corresponds to a still-moist medium finish after a short rest.
8. Rest 2 min, skin-side up. Final serving temperature: **56°C / 133°F** (medium).

### 2. Gebratener Saibling — ichkoche.at
**Inspired by**: https://www.ichkoche.at/gebratener-saibling-rezept-7366
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: not stated · **Cook**: 12-15 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 whole kitchen-ready saibling (arctic char)
- sea salt
- pepper
- lemon juice
- flour, for dredging
- oil, for frying

**Method**
1. Wash the cleaned char well inside and pat the belly cavity dry with kitchen paper.
2. Season inside and out with sea salt, pepper and lemon juice.
3. Dredge the fish lightly in flour.
4. Insert the probe behind the head into the thickest loin, keeping the tip away from the backbone.
5. Heat a little oil in a non-stick pan and fry the char on both sides for a total of 12-15 minutes until golden brown.
6. Transfer to warmed plates.
7. **Pull at 61°C / 142°F** (142°F). This whole-fish Austrian treatment is cooked through and the browning time implies a near-safe finish with only slight carryover.
8. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 3. Auf der Haut gebratenes Filet vom Saibling — Chefkoch.de
**Inspired by**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/867671192186244/Auf-der-Haut-gebratenes-Filet-vom-Saibling.html
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 60 min · **Cook**: not stated · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 saibling fillets, about 200 g each
- 500 g potatoes
- 500 g pumpkin
- 4 garlic cloves
- 1 thumb-sized piece fresh ginger
- 1 bunch parsley
- 1/2 lemon
- 150 g butter
- coarse sea salt
- salt and pepper
- adobo powder
- honey

**Method**
1. Pre-cook the potatoes, steam them dry and peel them; cut the pumpkin into 5 mm slices.
2. Pat the char fillets dry, score the skin lightly and season the skin side with adobo powder, then dredge lightly in flour.
3. Cook the pumpkin gently in butter with ginger and garlic; turn, season and glaze with a little honey. Brown the potatoes in butter and toss with parsley.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest part of one fillet from the side, keeping it clear of the skin and any belly fat.
5. Melt the remaining butter and lay the floured fillets in skin-side down; fry over fairly high heat until the skin is crisp and brown.
6. Reduce the heat, season the flesh side lightly with more adobo powder, turn and let the fillets finish only briefly in the hot pan.
7. Finish with lemon juice, flaky sea salt and some of the pan butter.
8. **Pull at 55°C / 131°F** (131°F). The source specifies only a very brief second-side finish after crisping the skin, indicating a still-moist centre rather than full-through cooking.
9. Rest 1 min. Final serving temperature: **57°C / 135°F** (medium).

### 4. Omble chevalier et sa sauce au cresson — Chef Simon / Mimm
**Inspired by**: https://chefsimon.com/gourmets/mimm/recettes/omble-chevalier-et-sa-sauce-au-cresson--2
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 300 g arctic char fillet
- 1 bouquet watercress
- 100 ml heavy cream
- 1 shallot
- 1 tsp dehydrated fish stock
- 100 ml dry white wine
- 1 tbsp white vinegar
- butter
- salt and pepper

**Method**
1. Season the char fillet with salt and pepper.
2. For the sauce, sweat the minced shallot in butter, deglaze with the white wine and vinegar, add the fish stock, reduce, then add the watercress and cream and blend smooth.
3. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fillet from the side.
4. Heat butter in a frying pan until foaming and lay in the char skin-side down.
5. Cook gently 2-3 minutes on the skin side, then turn carefully and finish the flesh side briefly.
6. Spoon over the cresson sauce and serve with simple mashed potatoes.
7. **Pull at 54°C / 129°F** (129°F). The recipe is described as “juste poêlé au beurre” to preserve the fish’s flavour, pointing to a soft medium finish rather than a fully set centre.
8. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **56°C / 133°F** (medium).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Köket.se | Sweden | Skin-side-only pan cook on fillet | 54°C / 129°F |
| ichkoche.at | Austria | Whole char, floured and fried through | 61°C / 142°F |
| Chefkoch.de | Germany | Crisp-skin fillet, very brief second side | 55°C / 131°F |
| Chef Simon | France | Butter-pan fillet with short finish | 54°C / 129°F |

The observed pull range is **54-61°C**. The fillet-based French, Swedish and German sources cluster tightly in the mid-50s °C for a moist centre, while the Austrian whole-fish treatment runs hotter and finishes at the USDA-safe zone. For everyday fillet cooking, the broadest common use is medium rather than well_done, so `recommended_doneness` remains `medium`, with a note that whole-fish pan frying often lands higher.

## What makes this method special

Pan searing gives arctic char something oven methods cannot: a strongly differentiated texture between crisp skin and softly set flesh. Because the fish is moderately fatty but not as fatty as salmon, the pan can render and crisp the skin without flooding the flesh with excess oil. The method also makes very small timing changes matter; one extra minute can move the fish from translucent and silky to firm and flaky. That sensitivity is why probe placement and low carryover matter more here than in richer, thicker salmon cuts.

## Summary

Pan searing is a strong match for arctic char because it crisps the skin quickly while preserving the fish’s delicate, salmonid flesh. The best-supported everyday result is medium, around the mid-50s °C pull range for fillets, with hotter whole-fish versions sitting as a traditional but less common outlier.
