# Burbot × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Burbot (*Lota lota*) is the only freshwater member of the cod family (Gadiformes). The fish has a distinctive body shape: a thick, rounded loin above the ribcage that transitions into a thin, eel-like tapering tail. These two sections have different thicknesses and behave differently under dry heat — thick loins (20–30 mm) fry up exactly like Atlantic codfish; the thin tail sections (5–10 mm) cook in seconds and are best given shorter heat or reserved for shallow-fry or fish cakes. The flesh is brilliantly white, extremely lean (under 1 g fat per 100 g), and firm. The liver is the most prized part of the fish — enormous by proportion and rich in Vitamin D — and is typically cooked separately (see below). The skin is tough and scaled-free but must be removed before frying; the traditional technique is "stocking removal": score around the head and pull the skin off in one piece with pliers. Pan-frying in fat (butter, oil, or lard) is the primary cooking method across all traditions where burbot is eaten. Flour dredging before pan-frying is near-universal. Probe placement: insert into the thickest loin section, from the thick side, parallel to the spine, avoiding the central backbone.

## Source recipes

### 1. Налим (Nalim/Burbot) — Едим Дома
**Attribution**: https://www.edimdoma.ru/retsepty/77316-nalim
**Country**: Russia · **Language**: Russian · **Culinary group**: D (Slavic/Russian)
**Serves**: 2–3 from 1 whole burbot · **Time**: ~1 hour

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole burbot (налим), fresh — must be cooked while fresh
- Flour, for coating (in a bag with less mess)
- Salt and pepper to taste (author prefers pepper with lemon)
- Vegetable oil for frying
- 1 medium onion
- Burbot liver (reserved for separate preparation)

**Method**
1. Skin "stocking style": make cuts around the head, grip the edge with pliers, and pull the skin off in one clean motion toward the tail.
2. Gut carefully: remove roe or milt, and most importantly the liver — set aside the liver.
3. Separate the meat from the spine. Head (without gills), spine, and skin → use for fish soup (ukha).
4. Wash the portions; pat slightly dry.
5. Add flour + salt + pepper to a bag; drop portions into the bag and coat them well.
6. Heat oil in a pan until hot. Place flour-coated portions in the pan.
7. Insert the probe into the thickest loin portion, from the thick side, parallel to the bone.
8. Fry both sides until a golden crust forms. "I like to cook it a bit longer to get a slight crunch."
9. Remove fish. Slice onion and add to the remaining oil in the pan (no salt). Stir-fry until golden. Do not overcook or the onion will become bitter.
10. **Pull at 63°C / 145°F.** The golden crust with slight crunch indicates a fully cooked, well-done interior. The probe confirms the centre is done without relying solely on the colour of the crust.
11. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done, with 2°C carryover).
12. Liver — separate preparation: Cut liver into large pieces, place in a glass jar with a lid; salt. Set the jar in a small pot on a folded cloth (to protect the glass). Pour water into the pot halfway up the jar. Bring to boil then simmer for 15 minutes. Remove jar and let cool before serving.

### 2. Miekojärvi Burbot Patties — Lapland Wild Fish
**Attribution**: https://laplandwildfish.fi/en/fish-recipes/burbot-patties/
**Country**: Finland (Lapland, Miekojärvi fishing company, Pello) · **Language**: English · **Culinary group**: D (Scandinavian/Finnish)
**Serves**: ~6–8 patties from 1 kg · **Time**: ~45 min

**Ingredients**
- Burbot fillet pieces and liver, 1 kg total
- Salt, 2 tsp
- Black pepper, ½ tsp
- 2 eggs
- Melted butter (cooled), 50 g
- Whipping cream, 2 dl
- 2 bay leaves, finely chopped
- Chives, pinch (to taste)
- Dill, pinch (to taste)
- Thyme, pinch (to taste)
- Juice and grated zest of ½ lemon
- Butter for frying

**For the optional smetana sauce**
- Smetana (sour cream), to taste
- Juice of ½ lemon
- Dill, to taste
- Salt and turmeric, to taste

**Method**
1. Chill the blender bowl and blades in the refrigerator before starting (prevents the mixture from warming during blending).
2. If using a boneless ground fillet, pass it first through a sieve or colander to drain excess liquid.
3. Blend burbot fillet + liver + salt + pepper together until smooth.
4. Add eggs, cooled melted butter, and whipping cream; blend to combine.
5. Add bay leaves, chives, dill, thyme, lemon zest and juice; blend briefly.
6. Use a mold or spoon to form patties of uniform size.
7. Heat butter in a pan over medium heat. Place patties in the pan.
8. Insert the probe into the thickest patty from the side edge, avoiding the centre divot.
9. Fry on both sides until brown — do not cook thoroughly in the pan.
10. Transfer patties to a baking sheet; bake at **150°C** for about **10 minutes**.
11. **Pull from pan at 55°C / 131°F** (not fully cooked; the oven finish completes cooking). Final oven pull: **63°C / 145°F**.
12. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done, with carryover).
13. Serve warm with rye bread or salad. Optional: smetana sauce (stir ingredients together).
14. Note: Patties keep in the refrigerator and are also good as a cold snack. Can be made ahead and frozen.

### 3. How to Cook Burbot — Hunter Angler Gardener Cook
**Attribution**: https://honest-food.net/burbot-recipe/
**Country**: USA (North American wild food tradition) · **Language**: English · **Culinary group**: B (North American)
**Author**: Hank Shaw (wild food chef and cookbook author)
**Format**: Authoritative culinary guide with method descriptions and serving suggestions

**Key recipe notes**
- Burbot (also: eelpout, ling, lawyer fish, mariah) is found in Great Lakes, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, Montana, Dakotas, and across Canada
- The body has thick loins above the ribcage → best for pan-frying or searing; thin eel-like tail section → best sautéed quickly, deep-fried, or used in fish cakes
- "Frying is very popular, especially because those thick loins fry up exactly like Atlantic codfish"
- Very lean fish (like all cod cousins) — fat must be added; author recommends "the best butter you can afford" or cold-pressed extra-virgin canola oil for a northern character
- Recommended batter/coating: buttermilk fried (see linked walleye recipe for burbot application)
- Liver: sear in pan, serve on toast with balsamic vinegar, flaky salt, and freshly ground pepper; note that burbot liver is very high in Vitamin D — eat only occasionally to avoid toxicity
- Roe: brine overnight, dust in flour, fry in bacon fat; serve with fresh herbs (chervil) and lemon
- Cheeks: large for the fish's size; poach and serve with sambal, high-quality olive oil, and roasted pumpkin seeds
- "Poor man's lobster": chunk skinless burbot meat, simmer gently (not boil) in 7-Up or Sprite + a healthy pinch of salt for about 10 minutes; serve with melted butter
- Bones and heads → make excellent stock for chowder or risotto

**Estimated method parameters**
- Thick loin fillets: coat in flour or buttermilk batter; fry in butter/oil over medium heat until golden and flaking
- Insert the probe into the thick loin section from the thickest side
- **Pull at 63°C / 145°F.** "Flakes easily with fork" is the doneness cue used throughout North American freshwater fish cooking. Burbot is lean like cod; it will dry out if taken past 65°C.
- Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done, with 2°C carryover).

## What makes this method special

Pan-frying burbot is one of freshwater fishing's most underrated experiences: the thick loin, handled correctly, is indistinguishable from Atlantic codfish in texture but has a sweeter, more delicate flavour. The key technique — flour coating + hot pan — is identical across three cultures separated by thousands of kilometres. The Russian tradition adds the onion fried in the same fat (a natural jus), while the Finnish tradition uses the whole fish including liver in formed patties, maximising yield and showcasing the organ-meat culture of Lapland winter fishing. The North American tradition treats burbot as a game fish whose image is rising; Hank Shaw's "thick loins fry exactly like codfish" framing is exactly right. All three traditions agree that fat is non-negotiable for this lean fish: whether butter (Finnish/Russian) or quality oil (American). The liver preparation — water-bath steaming in Russia, blended into patties in Finland, seared on toast in North America — shows consistent cross-cultural appreciation for burbot liver as a premium side product. The probe is particularly useful with burbot because the thick loin and thin tail sections on the same fillet cook at very different rates: insert only into the thick loin and remove the thin tail first (or deep-fry the tail separately for a crispier result).

## Summary

Pan-frying is the primary and most universal cooking method for burbot across all traditions where the fish is eaten. The thick loin section fries exactly like Atlantic codfish: flour-coated, in butter or oil, until golden and flaking at 63°C. The liver is always treated as a prized secondary product and prepared separately. All 3 confirmed sources (Russian, Finnish, North American) agree on well-done doneness and golden-crust technique. Groups A and C have no burbot culinary tradition.
