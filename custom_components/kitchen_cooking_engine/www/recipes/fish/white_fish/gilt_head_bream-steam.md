# Gilt-Head Bream × Steam — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Gilt-head bream (*Sparus aurata*) has white, firm, moist flesh with a delicate sweet flavour and a fat content of 3–5 g per 100 g. Steaming whole fish preserves every drop of natural moisture and allows aromatic flavours — ginger, lemon, fennel, lemongrass — to perfume the flesh gently from the outside. The round, oval body shape (typically 300–600 g sold whole) is ideal for whole-fish steaming over a bamboo steamer, steam oven, or steam rack in a wok. Unlike flatfish, bream needs 12–20 minutes of steam depending on size; scoring the flesh helps heat penetrate to the bone. Steaming is common in Chinese and Southeast Asian kitchens (Chinese Cantonese "qing zheng yu", Thai "pla neung"), and increasingly in German-speaking Europe via steam ovens (*Dampfgarer*). The skin cooks but does not crisp, yielding a silky texture. Probe placement: insert into the thickest part of the back just behind the head into the shoulder muscle, angling toward the centre spine.

## Styckesprofil

Guldspirad (*Sparus aurata*) har vitt, fast, fuktigt kött med delikat söt smak och 3–5 g fett per 100 g. Ångkokning av hel fisk bevarar all naturlig fuktighet och låter aromater — ingefära, citron, fänkål, citrongräs — varsamt genomdofta köttet utifrån. Den ovala, runda formen (typiskt 300–600 g hel) är idealisk för hel-fisk-ångkokning i bambukorg, ångugn eller ångkorg i wok. Till skillnad från plattfiskar behöver havsruda 12–20 minuter ånga beroende på storlek; skårning av köttet hjälper värmen tränga igenom till benet. Ångkokning är vanlig i kinesiska och sydostasiatiska kök (kantonesisk "qing zheng yu", thailändsk "pla neung") och alltmer i tyskt hem- och restaurangkök via ångugnar. Huden kocker men krispas inte, vilket ger en silkeslen textur. Sond placeras i tjockaste ryggdelen precis bakom huvudet in i axelmuskeln, riktad mot centrumet.

## Source recipes

### 1. Cantonese Steamed Whole Fish — The Woks of Life
**Attribution**: https://thewoksoflife.com/steamed-whole-fish/
**Serves**: 2–4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 9 min · **Rest**: none (serve immediately)

*Note: The recipe uses striped bass or sea bass as the primary fish; the method applies directly to whole gilt-head bream at the same weight range.*

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole round fish (1–2 lb; sea bass, striped bass, or gilt-head bream), scaled, gutted, fins trimmed
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce or seasoned soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- ¼ tsp salt
- ¼ tsp ground white pepper
- 3 tbsp water
- 3 tbsp neutral oil (vegetable or peanut)
- 15 g fresh ginger, peeled, cut into thin matchsticks (divided)
- 2 scallions, white and green parts separated, cut into thin strips
- Small handful cilantro

**Method**
1. Scale, gut, trim fins, remove any gills. Rinse thoroughly. **Apply no salt or seasoning before steaming.**
2. Place the fish on a heatproof plate that fits inside the steamer.
3. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the back behind the head, angling toward the centre bone.
4. Set up a wok steamer with enough boiling water for at least 9 minutes of vigorous steam.
5. Place the plate in the steamer and steam on high for 9 minutes.
6. Check: flesh must be completely opaque down to the bone.
7. Pour off all accumulated liquid from the plate.
8. Spread half the ginger, scallion greens, and cilantro over the fish.
9. Whisk soy sauce, sugar, salt, white pepper, and water. Heat 2 tbsp oil with remaining ginger until sizzling; combine with sauce and bring to a simmer.
10. Add remaining oil and scallion whites; bring back to a sizzle. Pour entire hot mixture over the fish.
11. Serve immediately with steamed jasmine rice.
12. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** Nine minutes of high steam reliably brings a 1–2 lb round fish to temperature; probe confirms the centre.
13. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 2. Pla Gapong Neung Manao (Thai Steamed Fish with Lime and Garlic) — Hot Thai Kitchen
**Attribution**: https://hot-thai-kitchen.com/mark-wiens-pla-gapong-neung-manao/
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: none

*Note: Recipe calls for whole barramundi or any white fish; whole gilt-head bream is an excellent substitute at the same weight.*

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 lb whole white fish (barramundi, sea bass, or gilt-head bream), gutted and gills removed
- 1 stalk lemongrass, bottom half only, smashed and cut in chunks
- ½ cup chicken stock or fish stock
- 1 tbsp palm sugar, finely chopped
- 4 tbsp lime juice
- 3 tbsp fish sauce
- 1 head garlic, finely chopped
- Thai chilies to taste, finely chopped
- 8–10 sprigs cilantro, chopped
- 1 stalk Chinese celery, cut into 1-inch pieces

**Method**
1. Score the fish with 3 diagonal incisions on each side, cuts perpendicular to each other on both sides.
2. Stuff the belly cavity with the lemongrass chunks.
3. Place fish on a heatproof plate. Insert the probe through the scored incisions toward the thickest part of the back.
4. Steam over fully boiling water for 8–12 minutes depending on size, until flesh is opaque throughout.
5. Meanwhile, bring stock to a boil; add palm sugar and stir until dissolved. Remove from heat.
6. Cool slightly; add lime juice, fish sauce, chopped garlic, and chilies; stir.
7. Transfer cooked fish to a serving platter. Stuff Chinese celery leaves around the sides.
8. Add cilantro to sauce; pour sauce over the fish. Garnish with extra Chinese celery.
9. Serve with jasmine rice.
10. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** Scoring allows heat penetration to the centre; steam over fully boiling water ensures rapid, even cooking.
11. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 3. Dorade gedünstet im Dampfgarer (Steamed Sea Bream in Steam Oven) — Chefkoch.de
**Attribution**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/1440011248800640/Dorade-geduenstet.html
**Serves**: 1 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Resting**: 60 min · **Cook**: 10–15 min · **Rest**: none

**Ingredients**
- 1 medium Dorade (gilt-head bream), kitchen-ready
- Juice of 1 small lemon
- 1 small bunch coriander (Koriandergrün)
- 1 stalk celery (Staudensellerie)
- Salt and pepper
- Chili, optional
- 1 small Chinese cabbage (Chinakohl)

**Method**
1. Wash the fish, pat dry. Score three diagonal incisions on each side. Fill the cavity with salt, pepper, and finely cut coriander and celery. Drizzle inside and out with lemon juice.
2. Refrigerate the fish for 30–60 minutes to rest.
3. Clean 5–6 Chinese cabbage leaves; lay them in the steam oven tray.
4. Place the marinated fish on the cabbage bed.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the back behind the head.
6. Set the Dampfgarer (steam oven) to full steam and cook for 10–15 minutes until the flesh is opaque and pulls away from the bone.
7. Serve immediately with Thai-style dipping sauce or other Asian accompaniments.
8. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The steam oven's enveloping steam ensures even, gentle heat; the resting period plus cabbage bed perfumes the fish.
9. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 4. Kalt gedämpfte Goldbrasse in Liebstöckelfond mit Kürbissalat (Cold-Steam Gilt-Head Bream with Lovage Fond) — ichkoche.at
**Attribution**: https://www.ichkoche.at/kalt-gedaempfte-goldbrasse-in-liebstoeckelfond-mit-kuerbissalat-rezept-226265
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 15–18 min · **Rest**: none

*Note: "Kalt dämpfen" (cold steaming) is an Austrian fine-dining technique: the fish is placed skin-down on an oiled tray, covered tightly with cling film with a 1-finger gap on one side, and cooked in a fan oven at 60°C until the target internal temperature is reached — effectively a steam-and-convection method.*

**Ingredients**
- 4 Goldbrassenfilets (gilt-head bream fillets), skin-on
- Olive oil, garlic, thyme (for tray)
- Salt and pepper
- Cling film (for cover)
- 1 Hokkaido pumpkin, halved, seeds removed, thinly sliced
- Sugared apple juice, vinegar, oil dressing (for pumpkin salad)
- 1 bunch young spring onions
- Fish or vegetable stock
- Lovage sprigs (Liebstöckel)
- Potatoes

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 60°C fan (Umluft). Mix olive oil, garlic, and thyme; spread on a baking sheet.
2. Lay the gilt-head bream fillets skin-side down on the oiled tray. Season with salt and pepper.
3. Cover with cling film, leaving a 1-finger gap open on one side for slight steam escape.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest fillet from the thickest edge.
5. Cook in the 60°C fan oven for 15–18 minutes (longer for thicker pieces) until flesh is just opaque and firm.
6. Prepare the lovage fond: sweat spring onions in butter; add garlic and potatoes; deglaze with stock; simmer 20 min; blend and pass; stir in lovage.
7. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The 60°C oven temperature is both the cooking temperature and the final target — the probe confirms the centre has reached 60°C.
8. Final serving temperature: **60°C / 140°F** (well_done, lower end of range).
9. Plate fillets in warmed deep dishes; cover the base with lovage fond; finish with pumpkin salad.

## Temperature consensus

| The Woks of Life | China (Cantonese) | Wok steamer, high heat, 9 min, round white fish | 60°C / 140°F |
| Hot Thai Kitchen | Thailand | Bamboo/plate steamer, boiling water, 8–12 min, scored | 60°C / 140°F |
| Chefkoch.de | Germany | Steam oven (Dampfgarer), full steam, 10–15 min | 60°C / 140°F |
| ichkoche.at | Austria | 60°C fan oven + cling film (cold steam), 15–18 min, fillet | 60°C / 140°F |

All four sources converge on 60°C pull. The wide range of cooking times (9–18 min) reflects differences in fish thickness and equipment, not differences in target temperature. The MEATER probe removes all ambiguity.

## What makes this method special

Steaming is the ideal method for gilt-head bream when you want the clean, sweet natural flavour of the fish at the forefront. The flesh is already flavourful and moist — steaming adds nothing that competes with it, while also adding nothing that masks it. Aromatic layers (ginger and scallion in the Cantonese style; lemongrass and lime in the Thai style; coriander and celery in the German version; lovage and apple in the Austrian) are carried by steam and sit on the surface rather than penetrating the flesh — a more delicate effect than braise or oven-bake. Gilt-head bream's rounded body shape means every side is equidistant from the steam source, giving even cooking without the need to turn the fish. The result is silky, moist, just-flaking flesh with no risk of the dry overbaking that can happen at high oven temperatures. The MEATER probe in the thick shoulder muscle is the definitive check: colour and firmness are unreliable indicators for a steamed whole fish.

## Summary

Steaming gilt-head bream is a living tradition in Chinese and Thai kitchens, where the sweet flavour of premium sea fish is celebrated by keeping the cooking method as clean as possible. The same principle is applied in German and Austrian fine dining via steam ovens. All four sources arrive at 60°C internal pull temperature, confirming that the well-done range is the universal target. The round-bodied bream is well suited to whole-fish steaming, where the MEATER probe confirms the thickest point — the shoulder behind the head — has reached full temperature.
