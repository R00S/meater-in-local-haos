# Monkfish × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Monkfish tail has an unusually dense, meaty texture that resembles shellfish — it is often called "the poor man's lobster." This same denseness means it is also the white fish most prone to becoming rubbery if overcooked: the collagen-rich tissue firms rapidly above 55°C. Sous vide is the ideal method for monkfish because it eliminates this risk entirely — the precise bath temperature guarantees the fish reaches the target without overshooting. At 45–54°C, monkfish flesh is silky, moist and succulent; above 60°C it begins to tighten and lose its distinguishing texture. The meaty body also absorbs bag seasonings exceptionally well (spices, herbs, aromatics). Sous vide monkfish is firmly established in professional UK kitchens and growing in popularity among serious home cooks.

## Styckesprofil

Marulkstjärt har en ovanligt tät, köttliknande textur som liknar skaldjur — den kallas ofta "fattigmannens hummer". Samma täthet innebär att det är den vitfisk som lättast blir gummiaktig om den överkokats: det kollagenrika vävnaden stramar åt snabbt över 55°C. Sous vide är den idealiska metoden för marulk eftersom den eliminerar denna risk helt — den exakta badtemperaturen garanterar att fisken når målet utan att överskrida det. Vid 45–54°C är marulksköttet silkigt, fuktigt och saftigt; över 60°C börjar det strama åt och förlora sin karakteristiska textur. Den köttiga kroppen absorberar också påsekrydderiet exceptionellt väl (kryddor, örter, aromatika). Sous vide marulk är fast etablerat i professionella brittiska kök och växer i popularitet bland seriösa hemkockar.

## Source recipes

### 1. How to Cook Monkfish Sous Vide — Great British Chefs
**Attribution**: https://www.greatbritishchefs.com/how-to-cook/how-to-cook-monkfish-sous-vide
**Serves**: variable · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 18–20 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- Monkfish fillets (removed from bone, or ask fishmonger to prepare)
- A little olive oil
- Salt and pepper to season

**Method**
1. Preheat the water bath to **48°C / 118°F**.
2. Remove the monkfish fillets from the bone if not already done. Pat dry.
3. Season the fillets with salt and pepper; place into individual vacuum bags with a drizzle of olive oil.
4. Seal the bags under vacuum.
5. Insert the probe sensor into the thickest fillet's bag (probe clip at bag seam) or use ambient monitoring mode.
6. Immerse in the preheated water bath for **18–20 minutes** depending on fillet thickness.
7. Remove from the bath; open the bags and dry the fish thoroughly on kitchen towel before serving.
8. **Pull at bath temperature: 48°C / 118°F.** The fish will be perfectly at bath temperature throughout; no post-bath rise expected in a sous vide context.
9. Final serving temperature: **48°C / 118°F** (medium, `FISH_MEDIUM` range).

*Variation note from the recipe: Monkfish takes bold flavours well in the bag — curry spices, ginger, garlic and peppercorns all work. The basic preparation above deliberately keeps it neutral to demonstrate the technique.*

### 2. Monkfish Wrapped in Parma Ham with Red Wine Jus, Lemon Sabayon and Cockles — Great British Chefs (Chris Horridge)
**Attribution**: https://www.greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/monkfish-wrapped-in-parma-ham-cockles
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep/Components**: ~90 min · **Cook (sous vide)**: 15 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients (monkfish + sous vide stage)**
- 1 monkfish tail, trimmed of discoloured outer membrane, patted dry
- 200 g Parma ham, in overlapping slices
- Vacuum pack bag

*(Full dish also includes: red wine jus, lemon sabayon, cooked cockles — not listed here as they are accompaniments, not part of the sous vide stage.)*

**Method**
1. Trim the monkfish tail using a very sharp filleting knife to slice off any discoloured outer membrane. Pat dry.
2. Lay the Parma ham slices out overlapping on a sheet of cling film. Place the monkfish tail in the centre; roll the Parma ham around it, forming a cylinder. Wrap tightly in cling film to compress.
3. Transfer the wrapped monkfish to a vacuum pack bag. Seal.
4. Insert the probe sensor (ambient mode) to monitor bath temperature.
5. Cook sous vide at **45°C / 113°F** for **15 minutes**.
6. Remove from the bath; cut away the bag. Pat the fish dry with a cloth.
7. Slice into portions and plate with red wine jus, lemon sabayon and cockles.
8. **Pull at bath temperature: 45°C / 113°F.** The Parma ham wrapping provides a slight insulating layer; the monkfish interior temperature will match the bath. The result is exceptionally tender and silky flesh.
9. Final serving temperature: **45°C / 113°F** (medium, `FISH_MEDIUM` range).

### 3. Sous Vide Monkfish Medallions — Anova Culinary
**Attribution**: https://recipes.anovaculinary.com/recipe/sous-vide-monkfish-medallions
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook (sous vide)**: 30 min · **Finish (sear)**: 5 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 × 340 g monkfish steak (tail)
- Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 4 tbsp unsalted butter
- 1 tbsp fresh parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Set the Anova Sous Vide Precision Cooker to **54°C / 130°F**.
2. Season the monkfish steak with salt and pepper; place in a large zipper-lock or vacuum seal bag.
3. Seal using the water displacement technique (or vacuum sealer on moist setting).
4. Insert the probe sensor (ambient mode or clip at seam) to monitor bath temperature.
5. Immerse in the water bath and cook for **30 minutes**.
6. Remove from the bath; remove the fish, pat thoroughly dry, and slice into 1.5 cm-thick medallions. Discard any cooking liquid.
7. Melt butter in a non-stick skillet over medium-high heat. When butter stops foaming and begins to brown, add the monkfish medallions.
8. Cook, turning once, until golden brown (~5 minutes).
9. Remove from heat; season to taste; transfer to a serving platter and top with chopped parsley.
10. **Pull at bath temperature: 54°C / 130°F.** The subsequent sear (5 min in brown butter) adds a golden surface without raising the interior significantly; the monkfish remains moist and lobster-like in texture.
11. Final serving temperature: **54°C / 130°F** (upper `FISH_MEDIUM` range).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Bath temp | Cook time | Post-bath |
|--------|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Great British Chefs (guide) | UK | 48°C / 118°F | 18–20 min | None |
| Great British Chefs (Parma ham) | UK | 45°C / 113°F | 15 min | None |
| Anova Culinary | USA | 54°C / 130°F | 30 min | Butter sear |

The range spans 45–54°C, all within the `FISH_MEDIUM` doneness band (52–57°C target). The Parma ham recipe uses the lowest temperature (45°C) because the ham wrapping adds insulation and the chef targets a very delicate, barely-set texture. The Anova recipe uses the highest temperature (54°C) and adds a post-bath sear to develop surface colour. The GBC guide (48°C) is the practical middle ground for a plain fillet.

**Key insight**: Monkfish sous vide should never exceed 57°C — above this the texture becomes rubbery and the fish loses its lobster-like quality. The `FISH_MEDIUM` range (52–57°C) is the correct target; `FISH_WELL_DONE` would destroy the characteristic texture.

## What makes this method special

Sous vide is uniquely transformative for monkfish: no other cooking method can reliably deliver the silky, lobster-like texture that makes monkfish special while also preventing the rubbery over-cooked state that makes it unpalatable. The combination of dense, collagen-rich tissue and a narrow acceptable temperature window (roughly 45–55°C) means precise control is essential — and sous vide provides exactly that. Professional UK chefs (Great British Chefs archive, multiple recipes) have used this method since at least 2015.
