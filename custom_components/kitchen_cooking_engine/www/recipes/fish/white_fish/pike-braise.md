# Pike × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Pike is a lean, firm freshwater predator with a complex bone structure and dense white flesh that holds together in liquid better than more fragile fish. Braising exploits this density: a slow, covered cook in wine or stock softens the connective tissue, keeps the flesh moist despite the lack of intramuscular fat, and creates a rich cooking liquid that becomes the sauce. The classical European matelote tradition — Alsatian, French Burgundian, Germanic — is virtually the only culinary family that braises pike deliberately, using red or white wine, aromatics, and a liaison of cream or egg yolks to finish the sauce. The Scandinavian tradition approaches the same result by searing the fillet and then simmering it briefly in a cream-and-anchovy pan sauce.

## Styckesprofil

Gädda är en mager, fast sötvattensfisk med ett komplext bensystem och tätt vitt kött som håller sig bättre i vätska än känsligare fiskar. Bräsering utnyttjar denna täthet: en långsam, täckt tillagning i vin eller buljong mjukar upp bindväven, håller köttet saftigt trots bristen på intramuskulärt fett och ger en rik kokvätska som sedan blir sås. Det klassiska europeiska matelote-arvet — alsacisk, fransk burgundisk, germansk — är i princip den enda kulinariska familj som bräserar gädda avsiktligt, med rött eller vitt vin, aromater och en liaison av grädde eller äggulor som avslutning. Den skandinaviska traditionen uppnår liknande resultat genom att steka filén och sedan koka den kort i en krämig anchovy-pansås.

## Source recipes

### 1. Elsässer Fischtopf Matelote — Chefkoch.de
**Attribution**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/1510171256334562/Elsaesser-Fischtopf-Matelote.html
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 45 min · **Cook**: ~1 h 15 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kg mixed river fish (eel, perch, tench, pike, pikeperch, burbot — at least 4 species)
- 3 carrots
- 3 leek stalks
- 3 onions
- 700 ml Riesling
- 700 ml red wine
- 600 ml water
- 150 g butter
- 2 tbsp flour
- 200 g crème fraîche
- 3 egg yolks
- salt, pepper

**Method**
1. Clean and chop the vegetables. Simmer with spices in the 600 ml water for about 1 hour to make a vegetable stock.
2. Clean the fish and cut into bite-sized pieces.
3. Strain the stock, combine with the wine and bring to a boil. Simmer the fish pieces in the wine-stock for approximately 15 minutes. Remove and keep warm.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest piece of pike before step 3. Pull when the display reads the target temperature.
5. Reduce the braising liquid by about half over high heat.
6. Make a light roux with half the butter and the flour. Whisk the reduced liquid in gradually; simmer until thickened.
7. Stir the crème fraîche with the egg yolks, temper with a little hot sauce, then return to the pot and heat gently — do not boil.
8. Remove from heat, incorporate the remaining butter, and season. Pour the sauce over the warm fish. Serve with fresh bread or baguette.
9. **Pull at 63°C / 145°F.** Fifteen minutes of simmering in wine-stock fully cooks the pike; the flesh should be opaque and flaking cleanly from the bone.
10. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done).

### 2. Matelote de Poissons — 750g.com
**Attribution**: https://www.750g.com/matelote-r4353.htm
**Serves**: not stated · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: ~30 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- Pike and other river fish, cut into chunks
- Red wine
- Marc de Bourgogne (or Cognac), for flambéing
- Onions, carrots, celery, 1 garlic clove
- 1 clove, bouquet garni
- Starch, for thickening
- Mushrooms, small glazed onions, croutons (garnish)
- Butter, salt

**Method**
1. Salt the fish pieces and stiffen them briefly in butter over medium heat.
2. Flambé with the marc de Bourgogne.
3. Add the vegetables cut in pieces. Sweat over low heat for 5 minutes.
4. Pour in the red wine; add the clove and bouquet garni. Adjust seasoning.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest piece of pike through the skin. Simmer gently for 20 minutes.
6. Lift out the fish. Strain the cooking liquid and thicken lightly with starch. Pour back over the fish.
7. Sauté mushrooms and small onions in butter with a light seasoning. Fry the croutons. Arrange as garnish.
8. **Pull at 63°C / 145°F.** Twenty minutes of gentle simmering in red wine leaves the pike fully opaque and just beginning to release from the bones; the sauce compensates for the lean flesh.
9. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done).

### 3. Gäddfilé med ansjovisgrädde — Köket.se
**Attribution**: https://www.koket.se/gaddfile-med-ansjovisgradde
**Serves**: not stated · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: ~25 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g pike fillet
- 125 g anchovy fillets
- 3 dl (300 ml) double cream
- 1 bunch dill, chopped
- butter
- salt
- boiled potatoes, to serve

**Method**
1. Fry the pike fillets in butter with a little salt, 2–3 minutes per side, over medium heat. Lift the fish out and set aside.
2. In the same pan, combine the anchovies, cream and most of the chopped dill. Simmer, stirring, until the sauce is slightly reduced.
3. Insert the probe into the thickest part of a fillet from the side.
4. Return the fish to the pan and let it simmer gently in the sauce for approximately 2 minutes.
5. Garnish with reserved anchovy fillets and fresh dill. Serve with boiled potatoes.
6. **Pull at 62°C / 144°F.** The initial sear partially cooks the fillet; the short simmer in cream sauce completes the cook with the sauce keeping the surface moist.
7. Rest 1 min. Final serving temperature: **63°C / 145°F** (well_done).

### 4. Hecht in Sahne-Pfeffer-Soße — Chefkoch.de
**Attribution**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/3562011533932882/Hecht-in-Sahne-Pfeffer-Sosse.html
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: ~40 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg pike fillets or steaks
- 2 medium onions, thinly sliced
- 200 ml dry white wine
- 75 g butter
- 1 tbsp capers
- 150–200 g sour cream
- 200 g whipping cream
- 1 tbsp flour
- 1 tbsp grated horseradish
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 tbsp black peppercorns, coarsely ground
- salt, lemon juice
- 2 tbsp breadcrumbs (for crust)

**Method**
1. Clean and portion the pike. Season with salt and a drizzle of lemon juice.
2. Melt the butter in a flameproof dish. Add the pike pieces; sprinkle with coarsely ground pepper. Lightly brown on both sides.
3. Add bay leaves and the thinly sliced onion. Once the onion is soft, pour in the white wine in stages. Scatter the capers. Turn pieces occasionally, basting with the pan juices.
4. Whisk the whipping cream, sour cream, flour, and horseradish together. Pour over the fish.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest piece before placing in the oven.
6. Transfer to the oven at 160°C / 320°F and cook for 30 minutes total.
7. Sprinkle with breadcrumbs and allow to form a light golden crust.
8. Serve with boiled potatoes tossed in butter and dill, and a cucumber or lettuce salad.
9. **Pull at 63°C / 145°F.** Thirty minutes in a moderate oven in a cream sauce brings the pike to fully opaque and pulling away from the pin bones.
10. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **65°C / 149°F** (well_done).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Chefkoch.de – Elsässer Matelote | Germany / Alsace | 15 min simmer in Riesling + red wine | 63°C / 145°F |
| 750g.com – Matelote | France | 20 min simmer in red wine, flambéed | 63°C / 145°F |
| Köket.se – Anchovy cream | Sweden | Sear + 2 min simmer in cream sauce | 62°C / 144°F |
| Chefkoch.de – Cream-pepper | Germany | White wine braise, 30 min oven at 160°C | 63°C / 145°F |

All four European traditions consistently finish braised pike in the 62–63°C range. Braising in liquid prevents surface drying so the flesh can be cooked to full opacity without becoming tough. The lean muscle of pike tolerates this fully cooked endpoint well because the sauce replaces the moisture that dry-heat methods would strip away.

## What makes this method special

Braising is among the few ways to cook whole bone-in pike without its complex skeleton becoming a hazard — the slow, wet cook softens the smaller pin bones so they can be felt and removed as the fish is served. The wine-based cooking liquid dissolves collagen, extracting flavour from the bones and skin, and concentrates into a naturally body sauce that pairs the pungency of freshwater fish with the acidity and depth of Alsatian Riesling or French Burgundy red wine. Cream or egg-yolk liaisons bring richness back to an otherwise very lean protein.
