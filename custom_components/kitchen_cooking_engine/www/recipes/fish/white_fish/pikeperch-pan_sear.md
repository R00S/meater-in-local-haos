# Pikeperch × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Pan searing pikeperch means cooking a skin-on fillet almost entirely skin-side down in clarified butter or a butter-oil mixture over medium-high heat until the skin is crisp and the flesh has whitened almost all the way through, then briefly finishing the flesh side. This method is distinguished from pan frying by the emphasis on a single-sided render: the pikeperch skin is thin but tightens and crisps very well, and the thick-sided fillet can absorb enough heat from the pan surface to reach the centre without turning. The flesh is lean and compact — far less prone to albumin weeping than salmon — so it tolerates moderate heat. Carryover is small (1–2 °C on a 2 cm fillet), so the fish should be pulled only when the probe reads within 1 °C of the target. Probe placement: insert laterally from the thicker edge, pointing the tip toward the geometric centre of the fillet, keeping it clear of the skin.

## Styckesprofil

Pannstekning av gös innebär att man tillagar en skinnsida-nedfilé nästan uteslutande på skinnsidan i klarat smör eller en blandning av smör och olja på medelhög värme tills skinnet är krispigt och köttet har vitnat nästan ända igenom, för att sedan hastigt avsluta köttsidan. Metoden skiljer sig från pannfrittering genom betoningen på ensidigt stekande: gösskinnnet är tunt men drar ihop sig och spricker upp fint, och den tjocksidigare filén kan absorbera tillräckligt med värme från pannan för att nå kärnan utan att vändas. Köttet är magert och kompakt — mycket mindre benäget att läcka albuminpärlor än lax — och klarar måttlig värme. Eftervärmen är liten (1–2 °C på en 2 cm filé), så fisken bör tas av först när sonden visar inom 1 °C av måltemperaturen. Sonden placeras lateralt från den tjockare kanten och pekar mot filénens geometriska centrum, håll sondspetsen fri från skinnet.

## Source recipes

### 1. Skinnstekt gös med brynt smör och kapris — Köket.se
**Inspired by**: https://www.koket.se/skinnstekt-gos-med-brynt-smor-och-kapris
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 gösfiléer med skinn, ca 200 g styck
- salt, vitpeppar
- 1 msk rapsolja
- 60 g smör
- 1 msk kapris
- 1 msk citronsaft
- 1 kruka persilja

**Method**
1. Pat the pikeperch fillets dry and score the skin lightly in three places with a knife to prevent curling.
2. Season the flesh side only with fine salt and white pepper.
3. Heat the rapeseed oil in a heavy pan over medium-high heat until it shimmers.
4. Insert the probe laterally into the thicker fillet from the skin-side edge, aiming for the centre of the flesh.
5. Lay the fillets skin-side down in the pan and press lightly for the first 30 seconds to ensure full skin contact.
6. Cook on the skin side for 5–6 minutes until the flesh has turned white two-thirds of the way up the side of the fillet. Do not move.
7. Add 40 g butter; when it foams, tilt the pan and baste the flesh side continuously for 1–2 minutes until the probe approaches target.
8. Flip the fillet only briefly (30 seconds) to seal the flesh side, then remove.
9. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F** (140°F). The Swedish skin-sear convention expects a just-cooked, moist centre; carryover and the brief flip complete the cook.
10. Rest 2 min skin-side up. Brown the remaining 20 g butter to noisette in the same pan, add capers and lemon juice and pour over the fish.
11. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 2. Auf der Haut gebratener Zander mit Kräuterbutter — Chefkoch.de
**Inspired by**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/2394341374540984/Auf-der-Haut-gebratener-Zander.html
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 Zander fillets with skin, 180 g each
- salt and freshly ground pepper
- 1 tbsp clarified butter (*Butterschmalz*)
- 40 g unsalted butter
- 1 garlic clove, halved
- 1 thyme sprig
- 1 rosemary sprig

**Method**
1. Pat the pikeperch fillets dry; season the flesh side with salt and pepper. Leave the skin side unseasoned (salt draws moisture out and impedes crisping).
2. Heat the clarified butter in a heavy stainless or cast-iron pan over medium-high heat.
3. Insert the probe laterally into the thickest fillet from the skin edge, tip centred in the flesh.
4. Lay the fillets skin-side down. Press gently with a spatula for 20 seconds to prevent buckling.
5. Reduce heat slightly to medium; cook for 6–7 minutes undisturbed until the skin is gold and the flesh colour change has reached about 70% of the thickness.
6. Add the butter, garlic and herbs; tilt the pan and spoon the foaming butter over the flesh for 1–2 minutes.
7. Flip for a final 30 seconds and remove.
8. **Pull at 59°C / 138°F** (138°F). The single-sided German method expects that the butter-basting completes the cook in the flesh; the brief flip is mainly for presentation.
9. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **61°C / 142°F** (well_done).

### 3. Sandre poêlé au beurre noisette et amandes — Chef Simon
**Inspired by**: https://chefsimon.com/gourmets/chefsimon/recettes/sandre-poele-beurre-noisette
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 sandre fillets with skin, 140–160 g each
- fine salt, white pepper
- 2 tbsp sunflower oil
- 80 g unsalted butter, diced cold
- 40 g flaked almonds
- 1 lemon
- 1 tbsp flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Score the skin of each fillet three times diagonally, cutting only through the skin and the first millimetre of flesh.
2. Season the flesh side lightly with fine salt and white pepper.
3. Heat the oil in a wide sauté pan over medium-high heat.
4. Insert the probe laterally into the thickest fillet at the skin edge, tip aimed at the flesh centre.
5. Lay the fillets skin-side down; press lightly with the back of a spatula for the first 30 seconds. Cook on the skin side undisturbed for 5–6 minutes.
6. When the flesh is opaque two-thirds up the side, add two-thirds of the butter to the pan. Tilt and baste the flesh side with the foaming butter.
7. Flip for 30 seconds, then remove and rest on a rack.
8. Pour the pan butter away; wipe the pan and melt the remaining butter until it turns amber (*beurre noisette*). Add almonds and toast briefly. Deglaze with a squeeze of lemon; add parsley.
9. **Pull at 58°C / 136°F** (136°F). The French technique relies on carryover from the basting phase; the fish exits the pan soft at the core and firms during the 2-minute rest.
10. Rest 2 min. Pour beurre noisette with almonds over the fish.
11. Final serving temperature: **60°C / 140°F** (well_done).

### 4. Paistettua kuhaa yrtti-voikastikkeella — Aromi.fi
**Inspired by**: https://www.aromi.fi/reseptit/paistettua-kuhaa-yrtti-voikastikkeella
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kuha (pikeperch) fillets with skin, 200 g each
- sea salt, white pepper
- 1 tbsp butter
- 1 tbsp oil (rapeseed)
- 1 shallot, minced
- 50 ml dry white wine
- 80 g cold butter, diced
- 1 tbsp fresh dill, chopped
- 1 tbsp fresh chervil, chopped
- lemon zest

**Method**
1. Pat the kuha fillets dry and season the flesh side with sea salt and white pepper. Let rest at room temperature for 5 minutes.
2. Heat the butter and oil in a non-stick pan over medium-high heat until the butter foam subsides.
3. Insert the probe laterally into the thicker fillet at the skin edge, tip toward the flesh centre.
4. Place fillets skin-side down. Press for 20 seconds with a spatula. Cook undisturbed for 5–6 minutes.
5. Meanwhile, sweat the shallot in a small pan in butter; add the wine and reduce by two-thirds; whisk in the cold butter cubes off the heat to make beurre blanc; stir in the herbs and lemon zest.
6. Baste the kuha with the pan butter for 1 minute, then flip briefly for 20 seconds.
7. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F** (140°F). Finnish skin-searing finishes the fillet just cooked through; the lean flesh firms completely during the short rest.
8. Rest 2 min skin-side up. Serve with the herb butter sauce.
9. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Köket.se | Sweden | Skin-only sear + baste, 5–6 min skin side | 60°C / 140°F |
| Chefkoch.de | Germany | Clarified butter skin sear + herb baste | 59°C / 138°F |
| Chef Simon | France | Sunflower-oil skin sear + beurre noisette baste | 58°C / 136°F |
| Aromi.fi | Finland | Butter-oil skin sear, brief flip | 60°C / 140°F |

The observed pull range is **58–60°C**. All four traditions emphasise a single-sided or strongly one-sided skin sear with the flesh barely touched by direct heat, relying on basting or brief contact to finish the top. The lean pikeperch flesh reaches its ideal firm-but-moist texture at 58–62 °C after a 2-minute rest, which maps squarely to `well_done` on the existing fish scale.

## What makes this method special

Pan searing produces something oven baking cannot: a deeply blistered, crackling pikeperch skin. Because the fillet is laid entirely skin-side down for most of the cook, the Maillard reaction and moisture loss are both concentrated on the skin surface, where they are desirable. The flesh side remains cushioned from direct heat and effectively braises in the butter vapour. The probe tip sitting in the geometric centre of the flesh is essential here because the temperature gradient from skin to flesh is steeper than in any other pikeperch method.

## Summary

Pan searing is the showpiece method for pikeperch across Scandinavian, German, and French cooking. Swedish, Finnish, German, and French sources all converge on a pull temperature of 58–60 °C, with carryover resting the fish to a moist well_done finish. The technique is primarily a skin-texture technique, and the probe positioned in the fillet centre prevents the common mistake of over-cooking while chasing full skin crispness.
