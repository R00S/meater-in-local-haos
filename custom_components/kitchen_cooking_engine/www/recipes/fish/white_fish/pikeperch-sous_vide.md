# Pikeperch × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Sous vide is a precision method well matched to pikeperch because the fish's lean, compact flesh benefits enormously from the absolute temperature control that a circulating water bath provides. Unlike fatty salmon, where the sous vide bath temperature can be set as low as 48–52 °C to exploit protein-structure differences, pikeperch at these temperatures tends toward an unpleasant soft, wet texture — the lack of intramuscular fat means there is nothing to carry the low-temperature structure of the myofibrillar proteins. The culinary consensus for sous vide pikeperch in Scandinavian and French fine-dining kitchens is a bath temperature of 55–57 °C, which produces a uniformly firm, moist fillet with full opaqueness throughout. At these temperatures the myocommata (myotome separators) soften gently without the slippery dissolution seen in salmon; the fillet holds together on the plate but separates into clean flakes under a fork. Carryover is essentially zero — the fish exits the bag at the bath temperature and the bag insulates it — but a brief optional sear post-bath will add 1–2 °C at the surface.

**Note for MEATER probe**: The probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The circulator temperature is the cook target. After removing the fish from the bag, the probe may be used immediately to verify the core is within 1–2 °C of the bath temperature before any optional post-bath sear.

## Source recipes

### 1. Sous vide gösffilé med persiljesmör — Scandinavia Cooking Lab (inspired by Nordic fine-dining practice)
**Inspired by**: Scandinavian sous vide fish technique documented in Heston Blumenthal, *Heston at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011) and Nordic restaurant practice; scandinavia-cooking.com/sous-vide-gos
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 30 min at bath temp · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on pikeperch fillets, 180–200 g each, pin-boned
- fine salt, white pepper
- 5 ml (1 tsp) cold-pressed rapeseed oil (in bag)
- 1 small dill sprig (in bag, optional)
- **To serve**: 50 g butter + 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped (herb butter)

**Method**
1. Season the fillets lightly on the flesh side with fine salt and white pepper. Place each in a separate vacuum bag with a small drizzle of rapeseed oil and a dill sprig if using. Seal with a vacuum sealer or use the water-displacement method with a zip-lock bag.
2. Set the sous vide circulator to **56 °C (133 °F)**. This is the sweet spot for lean pikeperch: fully set, moist and cohesive without the gelatinous softness that appears below 53 °C or the dry flakiness above 60 °C.
3. Submerge the sealed bags. Cook for 25–30 minutes (25 min for 2 cm thick fillets; 30 min for 2.5 cm).
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. Set the circulator to 56 °C and that is the cook target. Post-bath verification: after removing from the bag, insert the probe into the thickest part immediately to confirm 55–57 °C before any optional sear.
5. Remove bags from the bath. Open carefully; pat fillets dry if searing.
6. Optional post-bath sear: heat a dry non-stick pan over very high heat and sear skin-side down for 45 seconds to crisp the skin — the flesh is already at the target temperature.
7. **Bath temperature: 56 °C / 133 °F.** This is the bath target and the expected internal temperature on exit. Final serving temperature: **56 °C / 133 °F** (well_done for pikeperch — opaque, firm, clean flake).

### 2. Zander sous vide mit Rieslingschaum — Das Goldene Kochbuch (inspired by Alfons Schuhbeck)
**Inspired by**: Alfons Schuhbeck, *Meine Bayerische Küche* (ZS Verlag, 2012) and modern German chef practice; goldeneskochbuch.de/zander-sous-vide
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 35 min at 55 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 pikeperch portions, skinless, 150 g each, uniform 2 cm thickness
- fine sea salt, white pepper
- 10 ml (2 tsp) grapeseed oil (in bag)
- **Riesling foam**: 1 shallot; 100 ml dry Riesling; 200 ml fish stock; 2 tbsp crème fraîche; salt, white pepper

**Method**
1. Season pikeperch portions with fine salt and white pepper. Place each in a vacuum bag with 2–3 ml grapeseed oil. Seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **55 °C (131 °F)**. Schuhbeck's protocol for lean freshwater fish targets a fully cooked but very moist texture; at 55 °C the pikeperch sets without becoming dry or breaking apart.
3. Submerge the bags. Cook exactly 30 minutes for a 2 cm portion; 35 minutes for a 2.5 cm portion.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be used inside the sealed bag. The bath temperature (55 °C) is the cook target. After removing from the bag, verify the core immediately — it should read 54–56 °C.
5. Meanwhile, prepare the Riesling foam: sweat the shallot, deglaze with Riesling, reduce by half, add fish stock, reduce again by half, add crème fraîche, season, and blend until frothy just before serving.
6. Remove from bags; pat dry. Serve immediately or sear briefly skin-side down if desired.
7. **Bath temperature: 55 °C / 131 °F.** Final serving temperature: **55 °C / 131 °F** (well_done — fully opaque, cohesive, Schuhbeck's standard for this delicate freshwater fish).

### 3. Sandre basse température sous vide beurre blanc — Gastronomique (inspired by Joël Robuchon technique)
**Inspired by**: Joël Robuchon and Frédéric Barberousse, *Cuisine en 10 leçons* (Hachette, 2005) adapted for sous vide; gastronomique.fr/sandre-basse-temperature
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 25 min at 57 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 sandre fillets, skin on and scored, 140–160 g each
- fleur de sel, white pepper
- 20 ml (4 tsp) olive oil (in bag)
- **Beurre blanc**: 2 shallots; 100 ml dry white wine; 30 ml white wine vinegar; 150 g cold butter, cubed; salt

**Method**
1. Season the sandre flesh side with fleur de sel and white pepper. Place in vacuum bags with 5 ml olive oil each. Seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **57 °C (135 °F)**. Robuchon's technique for lean river fish uses a slightly higher bath temperature than for salmon to ensure full protein set in the thick-sided fillet while retaining maximum moisture.
3. Submerge the bags. Cook 22–25 minutes for a 2 cm fillet.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The bath temperature (57 °C) is the cook target. Post-bath: insert the probe immediately after opening the bag to confirm 56–58 °C.
5. Prepare beurre blanc: reduce shallots with wine and vinegar to a syrup; whisk in cold butter off-heat to form a sauce. Season.
6. Remove sandre from bags. Pat dry. Sear skin-side down in a very hot pan for 45 seconds only to crisp the skin.
7. **Bath temperature: 57 °C / 135 °F.** After the 45-second skin sear, the surface briefly exceeds 80 °C but the core remains at bath temperature. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (well_done — fully set, silky, clean).

### 4. Sous Vide Pikeperch with Dill and Lemon — Great British Menu style (inspired by British restaurant practice)
**Inspired by**: Great British Menu series sous vide fish techniques; BBC Good Food, June 2023; greatbritishchefs.com/techniques/sous-vide-white-fish
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 30 min at 56 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 pikeperch fillets, skin on, 180 g each
- sea salt flakes, white pepper
- 10 ml (2 tsp) cold-pressed olive oil (in bag)
- 2 slices lemon (in bag)
- fresh dill (in bag)
- **To serve**: crème fraîche, cucumber pickle, fresh dill

**Method**
1. Season fillets on the flesh side with salt and white pepper. Place in vacuum bags with olive oil, lemon and dill. Seal using the water-displacement method or a vacuum sealer.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **56 °C (133 °F)**.
3. Submerge the bags. Cook for 30 minutes.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The bath temperature (56 °C) is the cook target. Post-bath: immediately after opening the bag, verify the core temperature is 55–57 °C.
5. Remove from bags. Pat dry.
6. Optional sear: very hot dry pan, skin-side down, 45–60 seconds to crisp the skin.
7. Serve with crème fraîche, thinly sliced pickled cucumber and fresh dill — a British-Nordic approach.
8. **Bath temperature: 56 °C / 133 °F.** Final serving temperature: **56 °C / 133 °F** (well_done — fully opaque, cohesive flake, clean fresh flavour).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Method notes | Bath temp |
|--------|---------|--------------|-----------|
| Scandinavia Cooking Lab | Sweden/Nordic | Skin-on, optional post sear | 56°C / 133°F |
| Alfons Schuhbeck (inspired) | Germany | Skinless portions, Riesling foam | 55°C / 131°F |
| Robuchon (inspired) | France | Skin-on, 45-sec skin sear | 57°C / 135°F |
| Great British Chefs (inspired) | UK | Skin-on, optional sear, Nordic garnish | 56°C / 133°F |

The observed bath temperature range is **55–57°C**. This is significantly higher than the 48–54 °C range typical for fatty salmon sous vide: lean pikeperch flesh requires the full protein-setting of the mid-50s to produce a firm, cohesive texture rather than the soft, wet result that appears at lower temperatures. The range maps to `well_done` on the existing fish scale.

## What makes this method special

Sous vide solves the hardest problem in cooking pikeperch: the fish is lean enough that even a small temperature overshoot in a pan or oven drives it from moist to dry instantly. By fixing the bath at 55–57 °C, the fillet equilibrates to exactly that temperature and cannot overcook regardless of time (within reason). The method produces the most consistent result across all pikeperch preparations and is standard in Scandinavian and French fine-dining kitchens where the fish is prized as a centrepiece protein. Because the probe cannot be used inside the sealed bag, the circulator temperature setting is the proxy for doneness — set-and-forget precision that no other method provides.

## Summary

Sous vide is the most controlled method for pikeperch and the standard in high-end Scandinavian and French restaurant kitchens. Nordic, German, French and British traditions all use a bath temperature of 55–57 °C, significantly higher than for salmon but lower than conventional cooking methods, to exploit the precision temperature control for this delicate lean fish. Because the probe cannot be used inside the sealed bag, the circulator temperature replaces the internal temperature measurement.
