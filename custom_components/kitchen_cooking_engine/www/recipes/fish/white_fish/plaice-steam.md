# Plaice × Steam — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Plaice is a popular flatfish in Northern European kitchens, prized for its delicate white flesh and thin, even profile. Steaming is the gentlest possible cooking method for this delicate fish: it cooks through quickly (8–15 minutes for a whole or portioned fish), retains all natural moisture, and allows the clean, sweet flavour of the plaice to shine without any added fat. The thin flat shape is actually ideal for steamers — heat penetrates evenly from all sides, and the fish can lie completely flat in the steaming vessel. Steamed plaice pairs naturally with light aromatics (ginger, spring onion, lemongrass) or simple garden vegetables (courgette, red pepper). This method is common across Chinese, Thai, Vietnamese, and German cuisines.

## Source recipes

### 1. Chinese Steamed Whole Fish — The Woks of Life
**Attribution**: https://thewoksoflife.com/steamed-whole-fish/
**Serves**: 2–4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 9 min · **Rest**: none (serve immediately)

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole flat fish (1–2 lb; striped bass, flounder, plaice, grey sole), scaled, gutted, fins trimmed
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce or seasoned soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- ¼ tsp salt
- ¼ tsp ground white pepper
- 3 tbsp water
- 3 tbsp neutral oil (vegetable or peanut)
- 15 g fresh ginger, peeled and cut into thin matchsticks (divided)
- 2 scallions (green onions), white parts and green parts separated, cut into thin strips

**Method**
1. Scale, gut and clean the fish. Cut off any tough fins with kitchen shears; leave head and tail intact. Remove any remaining gills.
2. Rinse thoroughly. **Do not add any salt or seasoning to the fish before steaming.**
3. Place the fish flat on a heat-proof plate that fits inside your steamer.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest point of the back, angling toward the centre, before placing in the steamer.
5. Set up a steaming arrangement (wok + rack, or tiered steamer) with enough boiling water to sustain 9 minutes of vigorous steam.
6. Place the plate with the fish into the steamer and steam on high for 9 minutes.
7. Check doneness: the flesh should be completely opaque down to the bone; the bone itself may be slightly translucent.
8. Pour off all accumulated liquid from the plate.
9. Lay half the ginger, the green scallion tops, and a little cilantro over the fish.
10. Whisk together the soy sauce, sugar, salt, white pepper and water.
11. Heat 2 tbsp oil with remaining ginger until sizzling; combine with the soy mixture and heat until simmering.
12. Add remaining oil and scallion whites, bring back to a sizzle, then pour the entire hot mixture over the fish.
13. Serve immediately with steamed white rice.
14. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** At 9 minutes of high steam, the delicate flat fish flesh will have fully set; the probe confirms the centre has reached at least 60°C.
15. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 2. Pla Kapong Neung Manao (Thai Steamed Fish with Lime) — 196 Flavors
**Attribution**: https://www.196flavors.com/thailand-pla-kapong-neung-manao/
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15 min · **Rest**: none

**Ingredients**
- 1–2 whole fish (barramundi, sea bass, snapper, plaice or similar flat fish; ~1.5–2 kg total), scaled and gutted
- 4 stalks lemongrass, bruised and cut into 3 cm sections (for cavity stuffing)
- 1 cup fish stock
- 2 tbsp palm sugar, chopped
- Juice of 3 limes
- 4 tbsp fish sauce
- 8 garlic cloves, finely chopped
- 2 Thai red chillies, finely chopped
- ½ bunch fresh cilantro, chopped

**Method**
1. Make 3 diagonal scoring cuts on each side of the fish.
2. Stuff the lemongrass sections into the belly cavity.
3. Insert the probe through the scored cuts toward the thickest part of the back before placing in the steamer.
4. Steam the fish in a bamboo steamer or double-boiler over vigorously boiling water for approximately 15 minutes.
5. While the fish steams, bring the fish stock to a boil in a saucepan; add the palm sugar and stir until completely dissolved. Remove from heat.
6. Allow to cool slightly; stir in the lime juice, fish sauce, chopped garlic, chillies and cilantro.
7. Transfer the cooked fish to a large serving platter.
8. Pour the lime-garlic-chilli sauce over the fish, piling most of the garlic and chillies on top.
9. Serve immediately with steamed white rice.
10. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The scoring and cavity stuffing accelerate heat penetration; 15 minutes in a vigorous steamer will bring even a whole 1.5 kg fish to safe internal temperature.
11. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 3. Microwave Steamed Flounder with Ginger Sizzle — Viet World Kitchen
**Attribution**: https://www.vietworldkitchen.com/blog/2022/02/microwave-steamed-fish-with-ginger-sizzle.html
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 3–5 min · **Rest**: none (serve immediately)

**Ingredients**
- ~450–550 g bone-in, skin-on flounder or plaice (steaks, fillets, or whole small fish)
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine (or dry sherry)
- 3 tbsp water
- Fine sea salt, to taste
- 1 chubby 4 cm knob ginger, peeled and cut into fine strips
- 2–3 medium scallions (green onions), whites cut into 5 cm pieces, greens cut into thin strips
- A handful of fresh shiitake mushrooms, thinly sliced
- 3 tbsp neutral oil (canola or peanut)
- 1–2 tbsp soy sauce

**Method**
1. If the fish is uneven in thickness, cut 2–3 shallow slits (max 6 mm deep) on each side to ensure even cooking. Rub with salt all over and into the slits; rinse and pat dry.
2. Spread the scallion whites in a shallow microwave-safe bowl; lay the fish on top.
3. Stuff any slits with pieces of scallion white; scatter any leftover on top.
4. Pour over the Shaoxing rice wine, lay the sliced mushrooms on top, then add the water.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fish from the side.
6. Cover loosely with plastic wrap. Microwave on high for 3 minutes; check by inserting a knife tip — if no resistance, done. If not fully cooked, microwave in 30-second increments.
7. Scatter the remaining scallion greens over the cooked fish.
8. Add the oil to the ginger strips in a small saucepan; heat over medium heat, shaking frequently, until the ginger sizzles and turns lightly crisp and golden (~2 minutes).
9. Pour the hot sizzling ginger oil over the scallion greens on the fish — it will sear them dramatically.
10. Drizzle soy sauce over the fish.
11. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The probe confirms doneness in the thin flat fish; the microwave steaming technique is fast and produces equivalent results to stovetop steaming for flat fish.
12. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 4. Scholle auf Zucchini-Paprika-Bett aus dem Dampfgarer — Chefkoch.de
**Attribution**: https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/3360121499636996/Scholle-auf-Zucchini-Paprika-Bett-aus-dem-Dampfgarer.html
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min · **Rest**: none

**Ingredients**
- 2 medium plaice fillets (Scholle, filletiert)
- 1 medium courgette (zucchini), washed and sliced into rounds
- 1 red bell pepper, deseeded and cut into strips
- Salt and pepper to taste

**Method**
1. Season the plaice fillets lightly with salt and pepper; lay them flat on the steamer tray.
2. Wash the courgette and slice into rounds. Wash and deseed the red pepper, cut into strips.
3. Arrange the vegetables around and beneath the plaice on the tray; season the vegetables lightly.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest plaice fillet from the side before placing in the steamer.
5. Steam on the highest steam setting for 25–30 minutes.
6. Plate by first laying the zucchini and pepper strips on each plate, then placing the steamed plaice fillets on top.
7. Serve with boiled potatoes in sauce or a side salad.
8. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The long 25–30-minute steam at high setting is appropriate for a Dampfgarer (steam oven); the combined fish-and-vegetable tray means the probe confirms the fish is ready without overcooking the vegetables noticeably.
9. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

All four sources converge on 60°C as the pull point. Steaming times vary widely (3–30 min) depending on the method, fish size, and equipment — but they all describe the same endpoint: fully opaque flesh, easy separation from bone. The probe removes all ambiguity from the time estimate.

## What makes this method special

Steaming is uniquely suited to plaice because the fish's thin, flat geometry allows steam to penetrate evenly from every direction simultaneously, resulting in perfectly cooked, moist flesh with no dry surface. The method is used across Chinese, Thai, Vietnamese and German traditions — all agreeing that delicate white flatfish benefit from the gentleness of steam more than any other cooking method. The probe's role is critical here: unlike roasting or frying, there is no Maillard crust to signal doneness visually, so temperature is the only reliable indicator.

## Summary

Steaming is a highly appropriate method for plaice across diverse culinary traditions. Chinese, Thai, Vietnamese and German recipes all pull at 60°C, use aromatics to perfume the flesh, and serve immediately with the cooking liquid as sauce. The flat profile of plaice is a significant advantage in steaming — heat arrives uniformly, and the cooking time is short regardless of method.
