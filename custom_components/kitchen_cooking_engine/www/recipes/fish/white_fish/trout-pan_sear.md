## Cut profile

Rainbow trout (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) and brown trout (*Salmo trutta*, Swedish: forell,
Norwegian: ørret, German: Forelle, Italian: trota) are among the most widely cooked fresh-
water fish in Northern Europe and North America. The flesh is delicate, flaky, and mildly
flavoured with a slightly nutty quality; colour ranges from white to orange-pink depending
on diet. Trout fillets are typically thin (1–2 cm), which means they cook very quickly in a
pan — 3–5 minutes per side at medium-high heat. The skin crisps beautifully in butter or
olive oil. The key risk is overcooking: lean, thin trout flesh dries out rapidly past 65 °C.
Internal doneness is the same as for other finfish: 60–63 °C (140–145 °F, USDA minimum)
for fully cooked flesh that is just opaque. The MEATER probe must be inserted from the
thickest edge (lateral insertion) in thin fillets, or through the shoulder of a whole fish.

## Styckesprofil

Regnbågsforell (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) och brun öring (*Salmo trutta*, svenska: forell, norska: ørret, tyska: Forelle, italienska: trota) är bland de mest kokta sötvattensfiskarna i norra Europa och Nordamerika. Köttet är delikat, med ett milt sötvattensmak och en rosa till vit kötttjäll. Pannsearing av forell — hela fisken eller filé med skinn — ger knaprig yta och saftig insida. Skinnsidan ned i het smörpanna i 3–4 minuter; vänd för 2 minuter. Innertemperatur: 58–62 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Pan-Fried Trout with Bacon, Almonds & Beetroot (inspired by BBC Good Food)
**Inspired by**: BBC Good Food magazine, March 2014;
bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/pan-fried-trout-bacon-almonds-beetroot (fetched 2026-04-24)
**Serves**: 3 · **Cook**: 25 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 25 g butter
- 3 trout fillets, pin-boned
- 200 g bacon lardons
- About 140 g (¼ loaf) sourdough bread, cut into croutons
- Handful flaked almonds
- 85 g bag watercress
- 250 g beetroot (not in vinegar), cut into small chunks
- **Dressing**: 3 tbsp olive oil, 1 tbsp red wine vinegar, seasoning

**Method**
1. Heat 1 tsp of the olive oil with the butter in a large non-stick frying pan until it just
   starts to sizzle.
2. Insert the MEATER probe laterally from the thick edge of one trout fillet, angling toward
   the centre of the flesh.
3. Add the fish skin-side down. Fry for 10–15 minutes, basting often, until the skin is crisp
   and the flesh has cooked through.
4. Turn over and cook for a further 1 minute. Remove and keep warm.
5. Add remaining oil to the pan; fry the bacon lardons and croutons for about 10 minutes until
   crisp.
6. Meanwhile, toast flaked almonds in a dry pan over medium heat. Whisk together oil and vinegar
   for the dressing; season.
7. Toss watercress, beetroot, lardons, croutons, almonds and dressing together in a large bowl.
8. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** The BBC notes "basting often until the skin is crisp and
   the flesh has cooked through" — for a standard 140 g trout fillet, this corresponds to an
   internal temperature of 58–60 °C.
9. Sit a trout fillet on each plate; serve salad on the side.
10. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (fully cooked, moist, British tradition).

### 2. Pannestekt ørret med dillpoteter og squash / Pan-Fried Trout with Dill Potatoes (inspired by Matprat.no)
**Inspired by**: Matprat.no (Norwegian Meat and Poultry Research Centre / Opplysningskontoret for egg og kjøtt);
matprat.no/oppskrifter/familien/stekt-roye-med-dillpoteter-og-squash/ (fetched 2026-04-24)
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole trout (fjellørret / rainbow or brown trout), head and tail optional
- 4 sprigs fresh dill
- 3 tbsp (3 ss) butter
- 400 g small potatoes
- 1 green courgette / squash
- ½ lemon
- ½ tbsp sugar

**Method**
1. Cut off the head and tail with a sharp knife if desired. Make 2–3 slits in the skin on
   each side. Pat the fish dry inside and out with paper towels. Sprinkle a little salt inside
   the belly and place a couple of sprigs of dill inside.
2. Heat a frying pan with butter over medium heat until it foams. Add the fish once the butter
   is sizzling well.
3. Fry the fish over medium heat until golden-brown on both sides and the dorsal fin loosens
   when you pull it. (This is the traditional Norwegian doneness test — if the fin pulls cleanly,
   the fish is done.)
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick shoulder area through the skin into the thickest part
   of the flesh, tip clear of the backbone.
5. Transfer the fish to a serving platter; drizzle over a little of the browned butter from
   the pan.
6. Boil the potatoes in their skins until tender; drain and steam off. Add a little butter and
   chopped dill; toss to coat.
7. Shave the courgette into thin ribbons with a cheese grater or mandolin. Place in a bowl;
   squeeze over some lemon, season with sugar, salt and pepper to marinate briefly.
8. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** Matprat notes that "all types of small fish can be
   fried this way" — the dorsal-fin loosening test corresponds to approximately 58–62 °C
   at the backbone.
9. Serve fish with the dill potatoes and marinated courgette. A spoonful of soured cream and
   lemon wedges are suggested as extra accompaniment.
10. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (Norwegian home-cooking tradition,
    whole fish pan-fried in butter).

### 3. Forelle gebraten / Fried Whole Trout (inspired by Chefkoch.de / acigrand)
**Inspired by**: acigrand, Chefkoch.de community recipe;
chefkoch.de/rezepte/291301108152695/Forelle-gebraten.html (fetched 2026-04-24;
4.3/5 stars, 47 ratings)
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 whole trout (Forellen), washed and dried
- 2 sprigs rosemary (Rosmarin)
- 1 lemon
- 1 bunch flat-leaf parsley (Petersilie)
- Plain flour (Mehl), for dusting
- Salt and pepper
- 4 tbsp (4 EL) olive oil
- 1 tbsp (1 EL) butter

**Method**
1. Wash the trout and dry thoroughly with paper towels. Season inside and outside with salt and
   pepper.
2. Rinse the rosemary and parsley; dry. Place one sprig of rosemary and the parsley leaves
   (blätter abgezupft) inside each trout.
3. Dust the trout in flour on all sides (mehlieren).
4. Heat olive oil and butter together in a large frying pan until hot.
5. Fry the trout in the hot fat for about 5–10 minutes per side (depending on size) until
   golden brown.
6. Insert the MEATER probe from the head-end shoulder of the fish, keeping the tip in the
   thickest part of the flesh away from the backbone.
7. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** The flour crust forms a protective barrier that
   transfers heat more evenly; the traditional German test of loosening flesh at the backbone
   corresponds to the same temperature window.
8. Arrange on plates with a wedge of lemon. Drizzle over a little of the pan fat if desired.
   Serve with salad and mashed or boiled potatoes.
9. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (German tradition, flour-dusted
   whole trout in butter-oil).

### 4. Trota Salmonata in Padella con Pomodorini e Olive (inspired by Giallozafferano)
**Inspired by**: Giallozafferano (Italian culinary media);
ricette.giallozafferano.it/Trota-salmonata-in-padella.html (fetched 2026-04-24)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g salmon trout fillets (trota salmonata), 4 fillets
- 300 g cherry tomatoes (pomodorini ciliegino), halved
- 80 g pitted taggiasca olives (olive taggiasche denocciolate)
- 80 ml (80 g) dry white wine (vino bianco secco)
- Extra virgin olive oil
- Salt and freshly ground pepper
- 1 garlic clove (spicchio d'aglio), whole

**Method**
1. Halve the cherry tomatoes and place in a bowl with the taggiasca olives. Season with salt;
   add a whole garlic clove and a drizzle of olive oil; mix and set aside.
2. Heat a drizzle of olive oil in a large non-stick pan (*padella antiaderente*). Place the
   trout fillets skin-side down.
3. Cook over medium heat for 2–3 minutes, then raise the heat slightly and deglaze with the
   white wine.
4. Once the alcohol has fully evaporated, add the tomato and olive mix.
5. Cover with a lid and continue cooking for a further 5–7 minutes without turning the fillets.
   (The Italian technique: skin-side-only cooking, steaming the flesh from below with the
   tomato liquid.)
6. Insert the MEATER probe laterally from the thick edge of one fillet before adding the
   tomatoes.
7. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** The covered cooking with tomato-olive steam means the
   flesh side never directly contacts the pan; the probe confirms doneness through the
   steam-cooked flesh.
8. Turn off the heat; rest 1 minute. Remove the garlic clove. Season with salt and pepper.
9. Plate the fillets and spoon the tomato-olive sauce over them. The recipe suggests serving
   with a fresh fennel-and-orange salad on the side.
10. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (Italian Mediterranean tradition,
    skin-side only, tomato-olive *sughetto*).

### 5. Mediterranean Pan-Seared Trout with Tzatziki (inspired by Suzy Karadsheh)
**Inspired by**: Suzy Karadsheh, The Mediterranean Dish;
themediterraneandish.com/mediterranean-seared-trout-recipe-tzatziki/ (fetched 2026-04-24)
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 rainbow trout fillets (or butterflied trout), skin on
- Salt and pepper
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- 1 tsp sweet Spanish paprika
- ½ tsp garlic powder (optional)
- Flour, for light dusting
- 3–4 tbsp quality extra virgin olive oil (Greek EVOO)
- Fresh lime juice, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat the trout fillets dry with a paper towel. Season with salt and pepper; sprinkle the
   coriander, paprika and garlic powder over both sides.
2. Lightly coat the fillets in flour; shake off all excess — excess flour burns in the pan.
3. Heat the extra virgin olive oil in a skillet over medium-high heat. Watch the bottom of the
   pan: once wave-like ripples appear in the oil, it is at the correct temperature. If in doubt,
   add a pinch of flour — it should bubble gently.
4. Place the fish skin-side down. Sear for 3 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe laterally from the thick edge of one fillet.
6. Gently flip with a spatula. Sear for a further 3 minutes.
7. Transfer to a plate lined with paper towels; squeeze lime juice over generously.
8. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** The 3+3-minute cook at medium-high heat in a well-
   heated pan of olive oil is calibrated for 1–1.5 cm trout fillets. The Mediterranean
   approach here uses spiced flour as a crust rather than seasoning alone.
9. Serve with tzatziki, arugula salad and Lebanese rice, or with any preferred side.
10. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (Mediterranean tradition with
    spiced flour crust).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Pull temperature | Tradition / method |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| BBC Good Food (British) | 58–60 °C | 10–15 min skin-side in butter-oil, 1 min flesh side |
| Matprat.no (Norwegian) | 58–60 °C | Medium heat, butter, whole fish; dorsal-fin loosening test |
| Chefkoch.de (German) | 58–60 °C | 5–10 min per side, flour-dusted whole fish, butter-oil |
| Giallozafferano (Italian) | 58–60 °C | Skin-side only + covered steam 5–7 min with tomato-olive liquid |
| The Mediterranean Dish (Mediterranean/Egyptian-American) | 58–60 °C | 3 min per side, spiced flour crust, EVOO |

**Consensus pull**: **58–60 °C (136–140 °F)** across all five traditions. USDA safe minimum
for finfish is 63 °C (145 °F). Fillets pulled at 58–60 °C reach 62–63 °C with 1–2 minutes
of resting, meeting the safe minimum.

**Representative coding value**: **60 °C (140 °F)** as the nominal pull, practical range
58–63 °C.

## Notes for cooking_data.py

- `doneness`: `just_cooked`
- `pull_temp_c`: 60
- `pull_temp_range_c`: [58, 63]
- `safety_level`: `safe` at 63 °C; `caution` below 60 °C
- The dorsal-fin-loosening test (Norwegian tradition) is a reliable manual doneness check
  that correlates with 58–62 °C internal at the backbone.
- All traditions except the Italian one cook skin-side first to develop a crispy skin.
- Flour dusting (German, Mediterranean) creates a protective barrier that reduces the risk
  of the delicate flesh overcooking at the surface.
- The MEATER probe must be inserted laterally in thin fillets; top-down insertion in a fillet
  thinner than 2 cm will push through both surfaces.
