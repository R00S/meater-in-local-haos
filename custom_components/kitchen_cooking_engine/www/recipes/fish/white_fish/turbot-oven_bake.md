# Turbot × Oven Bake — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Turbot (*Scophthalmus maximus*, French: turbot, Swedish: piggvar, German: Steinbutt, Danish: pighvar, Italian: rombo chiodato, Spanish: rodaballo, Japanese: ōhishigarei) bakes beautifully whole because its rigid spine and thick diamond-shaped body provide natural insulation, allowing the oven to heat the flesh evenly from all sides without the surface drying out. Oven baking is the dominant domestic and bistro method for a whole turbot in northern and central Europe: the fish is seasoned simply with salt, herbs, a splash of wine, and butter or olive oil, then placed in a baking dish or sealed in foil. The sealed-foil variant (*en papillote* / *dampet pighvar* in Danish) is essentially oven-steaming — the fish cooks in its own juices and the added liquid. Both dry-oven and foil methods produce similarly tender, moist flesh, with the foil method better suited to whole fish and the open-dish method producing a lightly browned skin. Bone-in cross-section steaks (*tronçons*) require far shorter oven times than whole fish at the same temperature (5–8 minutes vs. 25–40 minutes) because of their smaller mass. Probe placement: for whole fish, insert into the thickest part of the back above the spine (lateral entry from behind the dorsal fin); for fillets or tronçons, insert from the lateral edge of the piece to the geometric centre.

## Source recipes

### 1. Turbot on the Bone with Parsley, Lemon and Garlic Dressing — Great British Chefs / Tom Brown
**Attribution**: https://www.greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/baked-turbot-on-the-bone-recipe
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 40 min · **Cook**: 5–8 min at 200°C · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**

*Turbot*
- 1 whole turbot, 2–3 kg, sliced into 180 g tronçons (bone-in cross-sections cut by fishmonger)
- Neutral oil
- Salt

*Herb dressing*
- 30 g flat-leaf parsley
- 200 ml light rapeseed oil
- Zest and juice of 1 lemon
- 1 garlic clove, grated or minced

*Accompaniments*
- 4 large leeks, sliced into rounds
- 1 kg Maris Piper potatoes
- 250 g unsalted butter
- 100 ml milk
- Salt, pepper
- Chopped curly parsley, to garnish

**Method**
1. Blanch parsley in boiling water; immediately refresh in iced water.
2. Add blanched parsley and rapeseed oil to a blender; blitz for 3 minutes. Strain through double muslin or a coffee filter over a jug until all clear oil has passed through.
3. Mix lemon zest, grated garlic, and lemon juice with the herb oil. Set aside in the fridge.
4. Blanch leeks in salted boiling water for a few minutes; refresh in iced water, drain, set aside.
5. Boil potatoes; allow to steam and dry. Pass through a drum sieve. Beat in butter and milk until silky and smooth. Season. Keep warm.
6. Preheat oven to 200°C / gas mark 6.
7. Brush each turbot tronçon with oil and season with salt. Place in a baking dish.
8. Insert the probe into the thickest portion from the lateral edge before placing in the oven.
9. Bake in the preheated oven for approximately 5 minutes. Drizzle blanched leeks with oil and bake alongside the fish in the last 2–3 minutes.
10. Check doneness by pressing the back of a spoon along the bone of the fish. If you feel resistance, the fish needs a little longer. When the flesh yields with no spring-back, it is ready.
11. Rest for approximately 3 minutes, then peel away and discard the skins.
12. Serve by spooning mashed potato onto each plate, lay leeks to one side, place turbot tronçon on top, drizzle herb dressing around the plate. Garnish with chopped curly parsley.
13. **Pull at 56°C / 133°F.** Tom Brown's "no resistance" press-test along the bone corresponds to flesh at ~56–58°C — just cooked through with a moist, yielding centre. The 200°C oven for only 5 minutes is designed for 180 g bone-in tronçons, where direct oven heat from all sides reaches the centre quickly. Michelin-star context implies medium-done turbot.
14. Rest 3 min. Final serving temperature: **58°C / 136°F** (medium).

### 2. Rombo al forno (Baked Turbot) — GialloZafferano (Italy)
**Attribution**: https://www.giallozafferano.com/recipes/baked-turbot.html
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min at 200°C · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole turbot (*rombo chiodato*), approximately 1.2–1.5 kg
- Extra virgin olive oil (*olio extravergine d'oliva*)
- Salt and black pepper
- 1 lemon, thinly sliced
- 2 garlic cloves, whole
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley, handful
- 1 bundle asparagus, tougher white ends trimmed, stems cut into pieces
- 2 large leeks (*porri*), trimmed and sliced into ¼-inch rounds
- Parchment paper, for the baking dish

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200°C / 392°F.
2. Clean the asparagus: remove the tough white base; if very fibrous, peel the stalks. Cut into pieces.
3. Trim the leeks, remove outer layers, slice into rounds approximately ½ cm thick.
4. Line a large baking dish with parchment paper. Spread the leeks and asparagus evenly across the dish.
5. Add 2 whole garlic cloves and a handful of flat-leaf parsley among the vegetables. Season with salt and pepper; drizzle generously with extra virgin olive oil.
6. Thinly slice the lemon. Move the vegetables to create a clearing in the centre of the dish; lay the lemon slices in a single layer in the clearing. The lemon creates a gentle base for the fish and prevents it from drying on the hot pan.
7. Score the top skin of the turbot with a cross-hatch pattern to allow steam to escape and heat to penetrate.
8. Place the whole turbot on top of the lemon slices, skin-side up. Season the surface with salt.
9. Drizzle with extra virgin olive oil.
10. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fish from behind the dorsal fin, angling toward the centre above the spine.
11. Bake for 25–30 minutes at 200°C. The turbot is ready when the skin lifts cleanly away with a fork.
12. To plate, distribute the roasted vegetables on each plate, lay the turbot fillet on top, and spoon over the cooking juices.
13. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The 25–30 minutes at 200°C for a whole 1.2–1.5 kg turbot produces fully opaque, moist flesh throughout. The "skin lifts with a fork" test corresponds approximately to 60–63°C. Italian home cooking prioritises fully cooked fish; the GialloZafferano recipe is designed for everyday family cooking.
14. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 3. 簡単★ヒラメのシンプル・オーブン焼き (Easy Simple Oven-Baked Hirame) — Cookpad Japan / 遥月
**Attribution**: https://cookpad.com/jp/recipes/25238565
**Serves**: 2–4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 30 min at 200°C · **Rest**: 2 min

*Note: This recipe uses ヒラメ (hirame, Japanese flatfish — typically Japanese flounder, Paralichthys olivaceus, or stone flounder イシビラメ), a culinarily equivalent flatfish to turbot for oven-bake purposes. The author notes カレイや他のお魚でも良い (flounder or other fish work equally well).*

**Ingredients**

*Fish*
- 1 whole hirame (*ヒラメ*) or stone flounder (*イシビラメ*), gutted and rinsed
- Salt, to season
- Olive oil, 1.5 tablespoons
- Garlic (*ニンニク*), 1 clove, thinly sliced (optional)

*Side dish*
- 1 potato, thinly sliced
- 7–10 cherry tomatoes (*ミニトマト*), halved
- Olive oil, ½ tablespoon
- Salt, to finish

**Method**
1. Remove the entrails from the hirame; rinse thoroughly under cold running water. Pat completely dry with kitchen paper — removing moisture is the most important preparation step.
2. Score the fish just inside the fin line on both sides — this makes it easy to separate into portions at the table once cooked.
3. Season the entire fish, both sides and the scored slits, with salt.
4. Prepare the side dish: combine sliced potato and halved cherry tomatoes in a bowl; toss with ½ tablespoon olive oil (do not salt yet).
5. Line a baking sheet with parchment paper; drizzle a thin layer of olive oil over the paper.
6. Place the seasoned hirame on the lined sheet. Arrange the potato and tomato mixture around the fish.
7. Scatter thinly sliced garlic over the fish. Drizzle 1.5 tablespoons of olive oil over the fish.
8. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fish (just behind the head, above the spine) from the side.
9. Bake at 200°C for 30 minutes.
10. When done, the flesh will naturally separate along the score lines, making it easy to serve at the table. Season the potatoes and tomatoes with salt.
11. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** A whole hirame baked uncovered at 200°C for 30 minutes is taken to full doneness throughout. Japanese home-cook standard for whole baked flatfish is well-done (fully opaque, firm); 30 minutes at 200°C without foil achieves approximately 60–63°C at the thickest point.
12. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 4. Dampet pighvar (Foil-Steamed Turbot) — Madens Verden
**Attribution**: https://madensverden.dk/dampet-pighvar/
**Serves**: 2 (1 kg fish; double for 4 servings) · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 35–40 min at 200°C fan · **Rest**: 2 min

*Note: This recipe uses the Danish technique of sealing the fish in foil with wine and herbs and baking in the oven — effectively en papillote (oven-steamed). The primary heat source is the oven; the foil traps steam from the wine and cooking juices. In cooking_data.py, turbot has no separate steam method; en papillote falls under oven_bake.*

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole turbot (*pighvar*), approximately 1 kg, gutted and rinsed
- Flake salt (*flagesalt*)
- 4 sprigs thyme
- 1 handful flat-leaf parsley
- 1 lemon (organic), sliced into rounds
- 50 g butter, cut into thin slices
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 1 dl dry white wine (*tør hvidvin*)
- Heavy-duty aluminium foil (or a large ovenproof dish with foil cover)

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200°C fan (or set up a grill/barbecue for indirect-heat cooking at the same temperature — both work).
2. Prepare a large piece of aluminium foil (three layers) or a large aluminium roasting tray.
3. Rinse the turbot thoroughly; optionally trim the fins with scissors. Pat dry with kitchen paper. Score a long cut along the dorsal bone on the top side of the fish — this helps heat penetrate and allows for easier serving.
4. Lay the fish in the foil or tray.
5. Insert the probe into the thickest part of the fish from the lateral edge above the dorsal bone, before sealing.
6. Season generously with flake salt. Distribute the thyme sprigs and parsley over the fish. Arrange the lemon rounds on top. Lay the butter slices over the fish. Season with freshly ground black pepper.
7. Pour the white wine around and over the fish — it will steam from below, keeping the flesh moist.
8. Seal the foil firmly all around (or cover the roasting tray with foil). No steam should escape.
9. Bake in the oven (or over indirect heat on the grill) for 35–40 minutes. Timing depends on the size of the fish — a 1 kg fish takes approximately 35–40 minutes.
10. Remove from the oven. Open the foil carefully — there will be hot steam. Serve directly from the foil onto plates, scraping aside the skin and serving the moist white flesh.
11. Note: *"Åbn pakken og server straks. Skrab skindet til side og nyd det lyse og smagfulde kød. Husk, at der er kød på begge sider af benene."* (Open the packet and serve immediately. Scrape the skin aside and enjoy the pale, flavourful flesh. Remember, there is meat on both sides of the bones.)
12. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** Steaming at 200°C for 35–40 minutes in a sealed foil packet brings a 1 kg whole turbot to well-done throughout. The foil-steam environment distributes heat gently but thoroughly; 60°C pull / 62°C serving is safe and accurate for this method.
13. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Country | Group | Method notes | Pull temp |
|--------|---------|-------|--------------|-----------|

| Madens Verden | Denmark | D | Whole fish ~1 kg, sealed foil at 200°C fan, 35–40 min (en papillote) | 60°C / 140°F |

**USDA minimum safe temp: 63°C / 145°F.** No source cites an internal temperature — all use the "flesh lifts from bone", "skin peels easily", or "spoon-press test" as doneness indicators.

The only divergence is between restaurant tronçons (GBC, 5 min, ~56°C) and whole-fish home-baking (all others, 25–40 min, ~60°C). This reflects the difference in mass: a 180 g bone-in steak heats through much faster than a 1–1.5 kg whole fish. Both approaches converge on the same sensory description: firm but yielding, opaque throughout, skin lifts cleanly. A **pull temperature of 60°C / 140°F** with 2 minutes rest is the practical target for whole turbot; **56°C / 133°F** is appropriate for thin tronçons in a restaurant oven context. All oven bake methods use 200°C as the standard temperature; no source uses a lower temperature (unlike the pan_sear method where 170°C appears for finishing).

## What makes this method special

Oven baking is the democratic counterpart to pan-searing for turbot: it demands less technique, less active attention, and less risk of burning a premium fish, making it the dominant home-cooking method across northern Europe. A whole turbot baked in the oven is a showpiece dish — the large diamond shape, presented at the table still in its foil packet or on the baking tray, has the ceremonial quality of a carved roast. In Scandinavian cooking, the foil-baked whole turbot (*dampet pighvar*) is a traditional dinner-party centrepiece. In Italian coastal cooking, *rombo al forno* with seasonal vegetables is the everyday version of the same philosophy: simplicity allows the fish's naturally sweet, firm flesh to dominate. The 200°C standard appears in all four traditions without exception — this is the point at which a whole fish bakes without steaming but without burning, the crust sets, and the flesh conducts heat evenly to the centre in 25–40 minutes. The MEATER probe transforms the oven bake method for whole fish: the traditional doneness test (skin peeling, bone pressing) requires opening the oven and handling the hot fish, which collapses the steam in a foil packet or disturbs a skin that took 25 minutes to crisp. Probing from the start means the fish can be pulled at exact temperature without any interruption.

## Summary

Oven baking is turbot's primary home-cooking method across British, Italian, Japanese, and Danish traditions. All four sources use 200°C as the standard baking temperature. Whole fish require 25–40 minutes depending on size and method (foil vs. open); bone-in tronçons require only 5–8 minutes at the same temperature. The consensus final temperature is 60°C for whole fish (well-done, fully opaque); restaurant tronçons are taken slightly lower (56°C). The MEATER probe eliminates the need for intrusive doneness tests on a sealed foil packet or freshly-crisped skin.
