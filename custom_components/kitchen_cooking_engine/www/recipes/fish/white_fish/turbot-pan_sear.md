# Turbot × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Turbot (*Scophthalmus maximus*, French: turbot, Swedish: piggvar, German: Steinbutt, Danish: pighvar, Spanish: rodaballo, Japanese: ōhishigarei / 大菱鰈) is a large diamond-shaped flatfish of the North Atlantic and Baltic, widely regarded as the finest European flatfish. Pan-searing is the dominant restaurant method: thick skin-on fillets or bone-in steaks (*tronçons*) are placed skin-side down in clarified butter or hot oil and cooked at moderate-high heat, searing the skin to a golden crust while the thick white flesh gently cooks through with minimal moisture loss. Because turbot has exceptionally firm, dense white flesh (thicker than plaice or sole — typical portions are 25–40 mm) the pan-sear phase is often followed by a brief oven finish at 180–200°C to ensure even heat penetration without burning the surface. The French tradition (*turbot sauté au beurre*) emphasises extreme restraint in cooking — Chef Simon's famous instruction that "overcooking turbot is a culinary crime" defines this approach. British and Nordic restaurants prefer a medium finish; Japanese hirame (a closely related flatfish treated identically) is typically taken to well-done. Probe placement: insert from the thickest lateral edge of the fillet into the geometric centre, perpendicular to the surface, avoiding any bone if cooking on the bone.

## Styckesprofil

Piggvar (*Scophthalmus maximus*; franska: turbot; tyska: Steinbutt; danska: pighvar; spanska: rodaballo) är en stor diamantformad plattfisk från Nordatlanten och Östersjön, allmänt ansedd som den finaste europeiska plattfisken. Pannstekning är den dominerande restaurangmetoden: tjocka fileer eller bitar med skinn läggs med skinnsidan ned i klarat smör eller het olja och tillagas vid medel-hög värme, skapar en gyllenbrun krispig yta medan det tjocka vita köttet mjukt tillagas med minimalt fukttapp. Den franska traditionen betonar extrem återhållsamhet i kokning — det är en fisk som aldrig ska överstekta. Nordiska och brittiska restauranger föredrar en medium stekgrad; japansk hirame (en nära besläktad plattfisk som behandlas identiskt) tas vanligtvis till genomstekt. Sonden placeras från den tjockaste sidokanten av filén in till geometriskt centrum, vinkelrätt mot ytan, undviker ben om man steker på benet.

## Source recipes

### 1. Filets de turbot sautés au beurre et poivre de Sichuan — Chef Simon
**Attribution**: https://chefsimon.com/gourmets/chef-simon/recettes/filets-de-turbot-sautes
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 turbot fillets (with dark/black skin — *à peau noire*)
- Salt
- Sichuan pepper, freshly ground (or black pepper from the mill)
- Clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*) or neutral oil

**Method**
1. Have the fishmonger fillet the turbot, or do it yourself. Ensure fillets have the dark skin intact.
2. Pat the fillets thoroughly dry with kitchen paper — moisture is the enemy of a good sear.
3. Heat a pan over moderate heat. Add a small amount of clarified butter or oil.
4. Place the fillets skin-side down in the hot pan.
5. Insert the probe from the thickest lateral edge of the fillet, angling to reach the centre.
6. Season the flesh side with salt and freshly ground Sichuan pepper.
7. During cooking, baste continually with the aromatic cooking fat.
8. When the skin is golden and the flesh has turned opaque roughly two-thirds of the way through, turn the fillets carefully.
9. Finish on the flesh side for a few minutes until just cooked through.
10. Remove the dark skin before plating — it will peel away cleanly.
11. Serve with a butter sauce (beurre blanc, hollandaise or a butter compound) and seasonal vegetables. A wilted spinach (*tombée d'épinards*) is Chef Simon's recommendation.
12. **Pull at 54°C / 129°F.** Chef Simon's emphatic instruction — "overcooking turbot is a culinary crime" — defines the target for this recipe: the flesh should be just opaque with the very centre still glistening. Ten minutes total in a moderate pan achieves this for a 2–3 cm fillet.
13. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **56°C / 133°F** (medium).

### 2. Turbot with Prawns, Braised Fennel and Borage — Great British Chefs / Marcus Eaves
**Attribution**: https://www.greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/turbot-with-prawns-recipe
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 40 min (for turbot and garnish; full recipe 2 h 15 min) · **Cook**: 5–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**

*Turbot*
- 4 turbot fillets, 180 g each, skinned and boned
- Neutral oil
- Salt

*Braised fennel*
- 1 fennel bulb, trimmed and cut into 8 wedges
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter
- 1 shallot, finely sliced
- Salt; vegetable stock to cover

*Prawn emulsion*
- 100 ml fennel braising liquor
- 30 g unsalted butter

*Prawn crackers*
- 70 g raw peeled prawns
- 70 g tapioca flour
- 32 g water

*To finish and serve*
- Sea kale, samphire, monk's beard
- Lemon vinaigrette (10 ml lemon vinegar, 20 ml lemon oil, 20 ml olive oil, salt)
- 4 live prawns (to accompany, blanched briefly)

**Method**
1. For the prawn crackers: blitz prawns to a paste, mix with tapioca flour and water to form a dough. Shape into cylinders and refrigerate 24 hours.
2. Slice into 1.5–2 mm discs and dehydrate at 57°C for 3 hours. Store airtight until needed.
3. For the braised fennel: sweat shallot in butter until soft. Add fennel wedges; sweat 3–5 min. Add salt and enough stock to cover. Cook gently until completely soft, then leave to infuse 30 min. Reserve braising liquor.
4. For the prawn emulsion: reduce 100 ml braising liquor to a simmer; whisk in 30 g butter to form an emulsion. Hold at room temperature. Add cooked prawns just before serving.
5. Preheat oven to 200°C / gas mark 6.
6. Blanch sea kale briefly in boiling salted water; refresh in ice water. Drain and dress with lemon vinaigrette. Blanch samphire and monk's beard 30–40 sec in unsalted boiling water; combine with kale and vinaigrette.
7. Deep-fry the dehydrated prawn cracker discs at 180°C until light and crisp. Drain on kitchen paper.
8. Place a non-stick frying pan over medium-high heat. When hot, add a dash of oil.
9. Season the turbot fillets lightly with salt. Add skin-side down (or presentation-side down for skinned fillets). Sear until golden brown and crisp, pressing the fish down with a spatula for the first 30 seconds to ensure even contact.
10. Insert the probe into the thickest part of a fillet from the lateral edge. Turn the fillets. Cook a further 2–3 minutes in the pan, or transfer to the preheated oven for 4–6 minutes until cooked through — the oven finish is recommended for thick fillets.
11. To finish the fennel: reheat with a knob of butter, stir in borage leaves, season with salt and lemon juice.
12. Plate by placing a spoonful of mash on each plate; lay the fennel alongside. Place turbot on the mash. Dress with prawn emulsion and sea vegetables. Add a prawn cracker and serve immediately.
13. **Pull at 56°C / 133°F.** The combination of initial pan-sear plus 4–6 minutes at 200°C for a 180 g skinned fillet brings the geometric centre to just above medium. Marcus Eaves' restaurant context implies the fish should be tender and translucent in the very centre — the oven finish provides even heat across the thick fillet.
14. Rest 3 min. Final serving temperature: **58°C / 136°F** (medium).

### 3. 時短でヒラメのバターソテー (Quick Hirame Butter Sauté) — Cookpad Japan
**Attribution**: https://cookpad.com/jp/recipes/18020576
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8 min · **Rest**: 1 min

*Note: This recipe uses ヒラメ (hirame), the Japanese flatfish (typically Japanese flounder, Paralichthys olivaceus), which is culinarily equivalent to turbot for pan-sear purposes. The same technique and temperature targets apply.*

**Ingredients**
- 2 hirame (flatfish) fillets, *切身* (cut portions)
- Potato starch (*かたくり粉*), as needed
- Salt and pepper (*塩コショウ*), pinch
- Butter (*バター*), 2 small knobs
- Japanese water parsley (*セリ*), ½ bunch, cut short

**Method**
1. Pat the hirame fillets thoroughly dry with kitchen paper — this is the most important step; moisture prevents a proper sear.
2. Season both sides of the fillets with salt and pepper.
3. Coat evenly with potato starch; shake off all excess.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest part of one fillet from the lateral edge, angling to reach the centre.
5. Melt the butter in a warm pan over medium heat until foaming.
6. Add the hirame fillets to the pan and sauté until golden on both sides (approximately 3–4 minutes per side depending on thickness).
7. In the remaining space in the pan, add the short-cut seri and sauté briefly by rolling it around until lightly wilted.
8. Plate the fish and pour any remaining pan butter over. Garnish with the seri.
9. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The brief butter sauté in a warm (not blazing hot) pan brings this thin Japanese flatfish portion to fully opaque throughout. The potato-starch coating creates a delicate crust while keeping the interior moist.
10. Rest 1 min. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

### 4. Pighvar med beurre blanc (Turbot with Beurre Blanc) — Madens Verden
**Attribution**: https://madensverden.dk/pighvar-med-beurre-blanc/
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 20 min (5 min pan + 15 min oven) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**

*Turbot*
- 600 g turbot fillet (*pighvar*) with skin, from the thick end
- Butter, for the pan

*Beurre blanc*
- 2 shallots, finely chopped
- 1 tbsp white wine vinegar
- 2 dl dry white wine
- 4 dl cream (heavy)
- 200 g butter, cut into cubes, cold

**Method**
1. Ask the fishmonger for pighvar fillet from the thick end, skin-on. Alternatively, fillet a whole turbot yourself.
2. Cut the turbot into portion-sized pieces. Season with a little salt.
3. Melt a knob of butter in a warm frying pan over medium-high heat.
4. Insert the probe into the thickest portion from the lateral edge before adding to the pan.
5. Brown the turbot pieces on both sides in the butter until golden.
6. Transfer the browned portions to an ovenproof dish and bake at 170°C fan for 15 minutes.
7. While the turbot bakes, prepare the beurre blanc: sweat the shallots in a little butter in a saucepan over medium heat without colouring. Add the white wine vinegar and white wine; reduce by half.
8. Add the cream and reduce again to approximately half the volume.
9. Remove from heat. Whisk in the cold butter cubes one at a time until the sauce is rich and glossy.
10. Pass through a sieve into a sauce boat.
11. Remove the turbot from the oven. Serve immediately with the beurre blanc poured over.
12. **Pull at 60°C / 140°F.** The 15-minute oven stage at 170°C fan, following the initial pan-sear, brings the thick turbot portions to fully opaque throughout. This is the classic home and brasserie approach to thick flatfish in Denmark and Scandinavia.
13. Rest 2 min. Final serving temperature: **62°C / 144°F** (well_done).

The data shows a clear gradient: French and British restaurant traditions target medium turbot (54–58°C) because the species' exceptional quality is best expressed with a slightly translucent, silky centre. Japanese and Danish home-cooking traditions target well-done (60–63°C). The cooking_data.py `recommended_doneness: "well_done"` is conservative and matches the home-cook sources; advanced users may prefer 52–56°C. A pull at **60°C / 140°F** with 2 min rest is a reliable target for first-time users; **54°C / 129°F** targets the culinary preference of professional chefs.

## What makes this method special

Pan-searing is the definitive restaurant method for turbot and the technique around which the fish's legendary status in European haute cuisine is built. The exceptionally firm flesh — firmer than any other European flatfish — holds together perfectly under high-heat searing, and the thick profile means the skin can be rendered fully crisp while the interior cooks gently. Unlike thinner flatfish (plaice, sole) that risk drying out, turbot forgives a minute or two of extra searing time on a thick fillet. The black upper skin of turbot is unique: it protects the delicate flesh during searing and peels away cleanly after cooking, carrying concentrated flavour compounds developed by the Maillard reaction. In French haute cuisine, *turbot sauté au beurre* is considered the Platonic ideal of pan-seared fish — the test dish in which every element (heat control, butter quality, timing, restraint) must be perfect. Chef Simon's warning that "eating overcooked turbot is without any interest — it is a culinary crime" is widely cited in European culinary education. The pan-then-oven hybrid approach (GBC and Madensverden) solves the practical challenge of cooking thick fillets evenly without burning the crust, and is the preferred technique for restaurant service. The MEATER probe is uniquely valuable with turbot because its extreme quality makes the difference between 54°C and 63°C a commercially meaningful distinction.

## Summary

Pan-searing is turbot's signature restaurant method across French, British, Japanese, and Danish cooking traditions. All four sources use the same fundamental technique — high-heat sear skin-side (or presentation-side) down, optional oven finish for thick portions — but differ in final target temperature and finishing style. French and British restaurant chefs converge on 54–56°C (medium) for exceptional texture; Japanese and Danish home cooking goes to 60°C (well-done). The culinary case for the MEATER probe is compelling: turbot is an expensive, unforgiving fish where a 5–10°C difference separates world-class results from a dry, expensive mistake.
