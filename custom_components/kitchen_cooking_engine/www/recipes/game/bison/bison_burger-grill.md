## Cut profile

Bison burger patties are made from ground bison meat, typically sourced from the shoulder, chuck, or round — the same primal cuts used for beef hamburgers. Bison is notably leaner than beef, often containing only 2–7% fat depending on the grind, compared to 15–20% in a typical beef burger blend. This leanness is both the appeal and the challenge: bison has a clean, slightly sweet, mineral-forward flavour but is prone to drying out if overcooked or if insufficient fat is present.

Because ground bison is so lean, fat management is critical. Many cooks either blend pure ground bison with a fattier cut (e.g., bison back fat or beef suet) to reach roughly 10–15% total fat, or they compensate with binding ingredients such as egg, mustard, and ketchup that add moisture and help hold the patty together. Cooking over high direct heat — whether on charcoal, a gas grill, or a cast-iron pan — is the preferred method, as it quickly builds a crust that seals in juices before the lean meat has time to dry out.

Food safety regulations in most countries classify ground meat as a high-risk product requiring thorough cooking: the USDA standard for ground meat is 71 °C / 160 °F internal temperature. BBC Good Food echoes this at 70 °C. However, gourmet and restaurant preparations for trusted-source ground bison sometimes target 63–65 °C for a medium result with a pink centre. The appropriate pull temperature depends on the intended doneness, the diners' preferences, and local food safety guidelines.

A MEATER probe inserted sideways into a patty gives reliable internal readings even for thin cuts, provided it is placed horizontally through the thickest part of the patty, keeping the tip and ambient sensor both clear of the grill grates.

## Source recipes

### 1. BBC Good Food — Barbecue Beef Burger (British tradition)

**Source**: Good Food magazine, [Barbecue beef burger](https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/barbecue-beef-burger) (fetched 2026-06-08)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min + 30 min chilling · **Cook**: 9–10 min · **Rest**: 2–3 min

**Ingredients**
- 400 g beef mince (substitute ground bison; consider blending with ~15% fat to compensate for leanness)
- 1 banana shallot, finely chopped
- 1 tbsp strong horseradish sauce
- 1 tsp dried mixed herbs
- 1 large garlic clove, finely grated
- 40 g blue cheese (optional, for stuffing)
- 2 tsp vegetable oil
- 4 burger buns
- Iceberg lettuce, tomato slices and relish, to serve

**Method**
1. Tip the mince, shallot, horseradish sauce, herbs and garlic into a bowl, season well with salt and black pepper. Combine everything well using your hands.
2. Divide the mixture into four portions and shape each into a 3 cm-thick patty. Press a small 50p-sized piece of blue cheese into the middle of each patty if using, and pack the meat around it.
3. Put the burgers on a plate, cover and chill for at least 30 minutes or overnight. Light the barbecue and wait for the coals to turn ashen, or heat a gas barbecue to medium.
4. Insert the MEATER probe sideways into the thickest part of a patty, avoiding the edges.
5. Lightly oil the burger patties all over and cook for 5 minutes until just starting to char. Carefully flip over and cook for a further 4–5 minutes (for medium) or longer if you prefer well done.
6. **Pull at 65–70 °C (149–158 °F)** — BBC Good Food states a probe thermometer should read 70 °C for "cooked through" (ground meat food safety). For medium with a slight pink centre, pull at 65 °C; for fully cooked through, pull at 70 °C and the carry-over will reach ~71 °C.
7. Remove the burgers to a board and leave to rest for 2–3 minutes before serving.
8. Final serving temperature: **70–72 °C / 158–162 °F** (food-safe, cooked through).
9. Serve the burgers in the buns with lettuce, tomato slices and relish. Alternatively, fry over a medium heat for 4–5 minutes per side until cooked through (probe reads 70 °C).

### 2. Madensverden.dk — Saftige Burgerbøffer (Danish tradition)

**Source**: Holger Rørby Madsen, [Saftige burgerbøffer](https://madensverden.dk/saftige-burgerboeffer/) (fetched 2026-06-08)
**Serves**: 4 (8 patties of 100 g each) · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6 min total · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g hakket oksekød / ground beef (substitute ground bison; author notes bison is very lean — consider adding fat to reach ~10% fat content)
- 1½ tsp salt
- 1 tsp løgpulver (onion powder)
- 1 tsp hvidløgspulver (garlic powder)
- 1 tsp sennep (mustard)
- 1 tbsp tomatketchup
- 1 egg
- Friskkværnet peber (freshly ground pepper)

**Method**
1. Crumble the ground meat into a bowl, add the salt and mix together.
2. Add onion powder, garlic powder, mustard, ketchup, egg and freshly ground pepper. Knead well with hands until the mince is fully combined.
3. Transfer the mince to the work surface and divide into 8 equal pieces so each patty weighs approximately 100 g. Shape into patties (a burger ring can be used for uniform results). Hack the surface of each patty on both sides with the back of a knife — this helps the meat hold together during cooking.
4. Insert the MEATER probe sideways into the thickest part of a patty, avoiding the edges.
5. Grill over direct high heat, or cook in a pan over high heat. Depending on thickness, grill for approximately 2–3 minutes on each side.
6. **Pull at 65–70 °C (149–158 °F)** — the author does not specify an internal temperature but notes the goal is a juicy patty cooked through. Given the lean nature of bison, pulling at 68–70 °C ensures food safety while retaining as much moisture as possible from the lean grind.
7. Rest for 1–2 minutes before serving.
8. Final serving temperature: **70–71 °C / 158–160 °F** (food-safe, cooked through).
9. Note: The author recommends approximately 10% fat for the best patties (8–12% is typical supermarket ground beef). Pure ground bison may require added fat to prevent dry patties.

### 3. Chefkoch.de — Der Bisonburger (German tradition)

**Source**: kkellings, [Der Bisonburger](https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/3007371453624664/Der-Bisonburger.html) (fetched 2026-06-08)
**Serves**: 1 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 4 min

**Ingredients**
- 200 g Burger Patties aus Bisonfleisch, tiefgekühlt (frozen bison burger patty)
- 1 Burgerbrötchen (burger bun)
- 2 EL Dip: Rote Bete Dip mit Pecanüssen (beetroot dip with pecans)
- 2 Scheiben Sandwichgurken (sandwich pickles)
- 4 Scheiben Bacon, hauchdünn (thin-sliced bacon)
- 1 medium egg
- 2 EL Portweinschalotten (port wine shallots)
- 2 Scheiben Raclettekäse (Raclette cheese)
- 1 EL Currysauce (curry sauce)
- Butterschmalz zum Braten (clarified butter for cooking)
- 1 TL Meersalz (sea salt)

**Method**
1. Allow the bison patty to thaw in the refrigerator and bring to room temperature approximately 1 hour before cooking.
2. Cut the burger bun and toast the inside golden-brown. Spread the bottom half with beetroot dip and lay on pickle slices. Spread the top half with curry sauce. Fry bacon until crispy and drain on kitchen paper. Fry an egg sunny-side up with runny yolk in clarified butter. Warm the port wine shallots.
3. Heat clarified butter in a cast-iron pan until very hot. Salt the top of the bison patty with sea salt and place salted-side down in the pan.
4. Insert the MEATER probe sideways into the thickest part of the patty, avoiding the edges.
5. Fry for 3 minutes with the lid closed. Turn the patty over, cover the cooked side with Raclette cheese and cook for a further 3 minutes with the lid on.
6. **Pull at 65–68 °C (149–154 °F)** — the recipe does not specify an internal temperature, but the 3+3 minute method in a very hot cast-iron pan with a lid is designed to cook the patty through while the cheese melts. Given the 200 g patty size and cast-iron retained heat, pulling at 65–68 °C and resting allows carry-over to reach ~70–71 °C.
7. Move the pan aside and let the patty rest for approximately 4 minutes before assembling.
8. Final serving temperature: **70–71 °C / 158–160 °F** (food-safe after resting).
9. Assemble the burger: place the bison patty on the pickle slices, add the port wine shallots onto the melted cheese, lay on the crispy bacon, followed by the fried egg. Secure the top bun with a skewer.

### 4. RecipeTin Eats — Hamburger Recipe (Australian tradition)

**Source**: Nagi, [Hamburger recipe](https://www.recipetineats.com/hamburger-recipe/) (fetched 2026-06-08)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8 min · **Rest**: 2–3 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g–1 kg ground beef (substitute ground bison; Nagi strongly recommends 20%+ fat — critical note for lean bison: blend with added fat or use a fattier grind)
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 3 onions, sliced into rings
- 2 tbsp oil
- 4–8 slices cheese (optional)
- 4 soft hamburger buns, lightly toasted
- Lettuce, tomato slices, ketchup, mustard, relish and sliced pickles, to serve

**Method**
1. Separate the ground meat into 4 equal portions. Using hands, lightly form each into a patty the size of the bun (approximately 10 cm / 4 inches in diameter). Do not overwork the meat — handle only enough to shape.
2. Using fingers, make a shallow dent in the surface of each patty. This prevents the patty from doming upward as it cooks and ensures even contact with the grill.
3. Season generously with salt and pepper on both sides just before cooking (salting too early draws out moisture).
4. Insert the MEATER probe sideways into the thickest part of a patty, avoiding the edges.
5. Cook over high direct heat (charcoal grill, gas grill or a very hot pan). Grill for approximately 3–4 minutes per side.
6. **Pull at 65–70 °C (149–158 °F)** — Nagi does not state an internal temperature in this recipe but the "fat = flavour" philosophy and high-heat method are aimed at a juicy, medium-cooked result. For bison (lean), pull at 68–70 °C to ensure food safety while the carry-over finishes the cook.
7. Add cheese slices in the final minute of cooking if using, allowing them to melt.
8. Rest for 2–3 minutes before assembling.
9. Final serving temperature: **70–72 °C / 158–162 °F** (food-safe, cooked through).
10. Note: Nagi states that 20%+ fat is essential for flavour and texture. For bison adaptation, adding fat (e.g. mixing in beef back fat, or using a bison/beef blend) is strongly recommended to achieve a comparable result.

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Pull temperature | Method | Doneness |
|--------|-----------------|--------|---------|
| BBC Good Food (UK) | 70 °C / 158 °F | Charcoal/gas grill, 5 + 4–5 min | Cooked through |
| Madensverden.dk (DK) | Not stated; ~68–70 °C inferred | Direct high heat, 2–3 min per side | Cooked through / juicy |
| Chefkoch.de (DE) | Not stated; ~65–68 °C pull, ~70 °C after rest | Cast-iron pan, 3+3 min + 4 min rest | Cooked through |
| RecipeTin Eats (AU) | Not stated; ~68–70 °C inferred | High-heat grill/pan, 3–4 min per side | Medium–cooked through |

**Consensus pull**: **68–70 °C (154–158 °F)** for food-safe cooked-through bison burger. BBC Good Food explicitly states 70 °C. All other sources use time-based methods that align with this range given typical patty thickness (2–3 cm). For a medium result with a slight pink centre (gourmet/restaurant style), 63–65 °C is used, but this is below USDA ground meat safety standards.

**Representative coding value**:
- `well_done` / food-safe: **71 °C / 160 °F** pull (USDA ground meat standard)
- `medium`: **65 °C / 149 °F** pull (gourmet compromise; below USDA standard — note in UI)
