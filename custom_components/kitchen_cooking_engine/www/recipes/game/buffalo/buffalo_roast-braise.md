# Buffalo Roast × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

American bison (Bison bison) roasts braise extremely well. The lean, dense muscle fibres that make bison susceptible to drying in high dry-heat methods become an advantage in braising: long cooking in moist heat with gentle, consistent temperatures (typically 250–325°F / 120–165°C) allows the fibres to soften without the fat-driven self-basting that compensates for dryness in beef. What braising provides instead is moisture from the cooking liquid — stock, wine, or Indigenous preparations like corn stock — and collagen breakdown from the connective tissue. Bison's higher collagen-to-fat ratio compared to the equivalent beef cut means bison braises can become deeply gelatinous and rich when cooked long enough. Braising also allows aromatic complexity that complements bison's naturally sweeter, more mineral flavour compared to beef. Probe placement should be in the thickest portion of the meat, away from the cooking liquid; internal temperature is less critical in braising (fork-tender is the primary indicator), but the cooking temperature keeps the internal temperature well above any safety threshold throughout.

## Styckesprofil

Amerikansk bison (*Bison bison*) bräseras utomordentligt väl. De magra, täta muskelfibrerna som gör bison sårbar för torrhet vid höga torrvärme-metoder blir en fördel vid bräsering: lång tillagning i fuktig värme med milda, konsistenta temperaturer (typiskt 120–165 °C) tillåter fibrerna att mjukna.

Vid 88–95 °C internt, hållet i 2–3 timmar, ger bison-bogbräsering otroligt rik, gelatinös konsistens. Innertemperatur: **88–95 °C (190–203 °F)** för fall-från-benet-möra resultat. Eftertillagning: försumbar (braiseringsvätska dämpar temperatursvängningar). Sond i tjockaste köttmassan, borta från ben.

## Source recipes

### 1. Sean Sherman (The Sioux Chef) — Cedar-Braised Bison
**Inspired by**: https://www.thekitchn.com/cedar-braised-bison-recipe-23094370  
**From**: *The Sioux Chef's Indigenous Kitchen* (Univ. of Minnesota Press) — Sean Sherman (Oglala Lakota)  
**Tradition**: Indigenous North American (Lakota / Ojibwe) — **Culinary Group B** (North American)  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight hominy soak) · **Cook**: ~3 hours 20 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**

*Corn stock (make ahead or substitute beef/vegetable stock):*
- 1 or more fresh corncobs, kernels removed (simmer covered in water for 1 hour to produce stock)

*Bison:*
- 2–3 lb bison or beef chuck roast
- 1 tbsp coarse salt
- 2 tbsp maple sugar
- 3 tbsp sunflower oil (for searing)
- 2–4 cups corn stock (from above)
- Several sprigs fresh sage
- 1 sprig fresh cedar
- 2 cups dried hominy, soaked overnight and drained
- 1 tbsp sumac
- ½ cup maple syrup

**Method**
1. Soak hominy overnight in water; drain before use.
2. Prepare corn stock: simmer corncobs covered in water for 1 hour; strain and discard cobs.
3. Preheat oven to 250°F (120°C).
4. Season bison with salt and maple sugar on all sides.
5. Film a Dutch oven or large ovenproof baking dish with sunflower oil. Heat over high heat. Sear the bison on all sides until dark and crusty, about 10 minutes total.
6. Remove bison and set aside. Add corn stock to the Dutch oven, scraping up the caramelised bits. Stir in sage, hominy, sumac, and maple syrup.
7. Return bison to the Dutch oven. Cover tightly (use aluminium foil if lid is unavailable). **No probe needed** — cooking until fork-tender is the doneness indicator for braised bison.
8. Braise at 250°F for approximately 3 hours, until meat falls apart when pulled with a fork.
9. Transfer bison to a cutting board; tent with foil to keep warm.
10. Strain cooking liquid through a fine-mesh strainer into a saucepan; reserve hominy. Bring liquid to a boil and reduce by half. Adjust seasoning.
11. Carve bison and serve over reserved hominy with reduced sauce drizzled over.

**Temperature note**: No pull temperature applies to braised bison prepared to "fork-tender." Internal temperature throughout most of the braise exceeds 82°C / 180°F, well above the USDA safe minimum of 63°C / 145°F.
