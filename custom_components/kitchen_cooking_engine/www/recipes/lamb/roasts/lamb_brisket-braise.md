# Lamb Brisket × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Lamb brisket (also called lamb breast, especially in UK/European usage) comes from the belly of the lamb — the same anatomical position as pork belly. It consists of layered intercostal muscles, rib bones, and significant deposits of intramuscular fat held together by dense connective tissue. Because the breast muscles are used constantly for breathing, they are tough, chewy, and collagen-rich — the exact qualities that reward low-and-slow moist cooking. At 85–93°C the collagen converts to gelatin, the fat renders, and the meat becomes fork-tender or pull-apart. Carryover cooking is significant (4–6°C) owing to the high fat content, so the meat will continue to rise after being pulled from heat. Sold boneless/rolled or bone-in; both forms braise equally well.

## Styckesprofil

Lammbrisket (UK: *lamb breast*) är lammets mage — anatomiskt ekvivalent med fläskbuk. Det består av skiktade interkostalsmuskler, revbensben och substansiella intramuskulära och yttre fettdepåer bundna av tät bindväv. Magemusklerna arbetar konstant vid andning.

För att nå ätbar konsistens kräver lammbrisket låg-och-långsam tillagning: bräsering (160–170 °C, 2–3 timmar täckt), ugnsrostning (150 °C, 3–4 timmar). Innertemperatur: **88–95 °C (190–203 °F)** för fall-från-benet-möra resultat. Eftertillagning: försumbar (braiseringsvätska). Sond in i tjockaste köttmassan, borta från revbenslben.

## Source recipes

### 1. The Woks of Life / Cantonese — Zhi Zhu Yangrou Bao (枝竹羊肉煲) — Chinese Braised Lamb Casserole Hong Kong Style
**Source**: https://thewoksoflife.com/chinese-braised-lamb-casserole/  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 60 min · **Cook**: 1 hr 15 min · **Rest**: n/a (served in braise liquid)

**Ingredients**
- 1.1 kg (2½ lb) lamb breast, cut into 5 cm (2-inch) pieces
- 15 slices fresh ginger
- 2 tbsp neutral oil
- 6 scallions (white and green parts separated)
- 10 g rock sugar
- 3 pieces fermented red bean curd (*nan ru*, 南乳)
- 60 ml (¼ cup) *zhu hou* / *chee hou* sauce (*zhū hóu jiàng*, 柱侯醬)
- 1–2 star anise (optional)
- 3 tbsp Shaoxing wine
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 2 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 piece dried tangerine peel
- 6 dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked + halved (plus soaking water)
- 4 small carrots, cut into chunks
- 1 small bamboo shoot, thinly sliced
- 6 bean thread sticks (*fu zhu*), soaked and cut into large chunks
- Salt to taste
- ¼ head iceberg or romaine lettuce, roughly chopped

**Method**
1. Blanch: bring a large pot of water to a boil. Add lamb pieces and 4 ginger slices, return to boil, cook 1 minute. Drain, rinse clean. Set aside.
2. Heat oil in a wok over medium heat. Add remaining ginger, scallion whites, and rock sugar. Cook 2 minutes.
3. Add fermented red bean curd and zhu hou sauce. Stir and cook 2 minutes on medium-low.
4. Turn heat to high. Add blanched lamb and stir to coat evenly in sauce.
5. Insert a probe into the largest piece of lamb now, avoiding any bone, from the thickest side.
6. Add star anise (if using), Shaoxing wine, both soy sauces, oyster sauce, tangerine peel, mushrooms, mushroom soaking water, and enough additional water to just cover everything. Bring to a boil.
7. Reduce heat immediately to medium-low. Cover and simmer 1 hour, stirring every 20 minutes to prevent sticking.
8. Add carrots, bamboo shoots, and bean threads. Cook uncovered 15–20 minutes until carrots are tender.
9. **Pull when probe reads 88–93°C** (190–200°F). Lamb should be completely fork-tender and bones (if any remain) pull away without resistance. The internal temperature is secondary to texture for this braise.
10. Salt to taste, stir in scallion greens. Place lettuce in serving bowl. Ladle casserole over lettuce. Final serving temperature: **88–93°C / 190–200°F** (pulled/fork-tender). Serve immediately with white rice.

### 2. Alan Bergo / Forager Chef — Braised Goat or Lamb Breast (French-style press method)
**Source**: https://foragerchef.com/goat-or-lamb-breast-with-wild-herb-tomato-sauce/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight marinate) · **Cook**: 3 hr · **Press**: 8 hr (overnight)

**Ingredients**
- ~900 g (2 lb) goat or lamb breasts (2 breasts)
- Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
- 1 tbsp chopped fresh rosemary and thyme, combined
- 1 small yellow onion
- 2 small carrots
- 2 ribs celery
- 3 cloves garlic, smashed
- 2 L (8 cups) stock or water

**Method**
1. Night before: season breasts all over with salt, pepper, and chopped herbs, pressing to adhere. Cover and refrigerate overnight.
2. Next day: preheat oven to 150°C (300°F).
3. Combine carrots, onion, celery, garlic, and stock/water in a large baking dish. Nestle breasts meat-side down in the liquid.
4. Insert probe into thickest part of one breast from the side, avoiding rib bones and keeping it parallel to the bone structure.
5. Top with a sheet of parchment, then cover tightly with foil. Bake until breasts are cooked and soft — approximately 3 hours.
6. **Pull when probe reads 88–93°C** (190–200°F) and meat is soft throughout. Note: recipe targets texture, not a specific temperature — "cooked and soft, about 3 hours." The 88–93°C reading is the practical endpoint for pulled/tender lamb breast.
7. Remove from oven and cool to room temperature. Transfer breasts to a cutting board.
8. Gently remove rib bones, saving any meat that falls off. Trim large fat deposits if desired but keep breasts intact.
9. Reassemble any loose meat on top of the breasts. Wrap tightly in plastic, place in a container, and press with a weight overnight or until cold.
10. To serve: preheat oven to 175°C (350°F). Unwrap breasts and cut into 6 triangles each on a slight diagonal. Bread in flour → egg → breadcrumbs. Bake on a sheet 10–15 minutes until hot throughout. Serve with spicy tomato sauce and watercress salad. Final serving temperature: **88–93°C / 190–200°F** (pulled, then reheated to serve hot).

*Recipe credits French Provincial Cooking by Elizabeth David (1960) and Richard Olney. Method described by Alan Bergo, Forager Chef.*

### 3. Selin Kiazim / Great British Chefs — Crispy Pomegranate-Glazed Lamb Breast
**Source**: https://www.greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/crispy-lamb-breast-with-yogurt-recipe  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 3 hr 30 min (+overnight press) · **Rest**: n/a (pan-seared to serve)

**Ingredients**

*Braise:*
- 2 whole bone-in lamb breasts, approx. 1 kg total
- 1 large onion, roughly sliced
- 1 bay leaf
- Water to cover (approx. 2 L)

*Pomegranate sauce:*
- 100 ml pomegranate molasses
- 50 ml cider vinegar
- 25 g dark brown sugar
- ¼ tsp black peppercorns
- ¼ tsp fennel seeds
- ¼ tsp coriander seeds
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 4 cloves

*To serve:*
- 4 tbsp Turkish or Greek yoghurt
- 4 sprigs mint, leaves finely shredded
- 4 sprigs flat-leaf parsley, leaves finely shredded
- Sea salt

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 160°C fan / 180°C conventional (320°F fan / 355°F conventional).
2. Place lamb breasts, sliced onion, and bay leaf in a large roasting tray or casserole. Add water to cover lamb by about 2 cm.
3. Insert probe into the thickest part of one breast, from the side, avoiding bone.
4. Cover tightly with foil or a lid. Braise in oven for approximately 3 hours.
5. **Pull when probe reads 88–93°C** (190–200°F) and bones pull away from the meat without any resistance. Temperature is secondary to texture — recipe criterion is "bones pull away without any resistance."
6. Remove lamb from stock. Refrigerate stock. Let lamb cool enough to handle.
7. Remove bones and cartilage while still warm (easier than when cold). Remove very large fat deposits. Place meat into a square roasting tin, press down, refrigerate with a weight on top, preferably overnight.
8. Skim and discard fat from chilled stock. Simmer stock with pomegranate sauce ingredients (molasses, vinegar, sugar, peppercorns, fennel, coriander, cinnamon, cloves) until reduced to single-cream consistency. Strain and reserve.
9. Cut pressed lamb into 3 cm cubes. Sear in a dry medium-hot pan until crispy all over, 8–12 minutes, seasoning with sea salt as you go.
10. Drain excess fat. Serve lamb on yoghurt, topped with pomegranate sauce and fresh herbs. Final serving temperature: **88–93°C / 190–200°F** (pulled, reheated crispy to serve hot).

*Recipe by Selin Kiazim, Great British Chefs, first published 2019.*

### 4. ICA — Bräserad lammbringa med vitlök, rosmarin och citron (Swedish Braised Lamb Breast with Garlic, Rosemary and Lemon)
**Source**: https://www.ica.se/recept/braserad-lammbringa-med-vitlok-rosmarin-och-citron-714953/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 2 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- ~1 kg lammbringa (*lamb breast / lamb brisket*, boneless)
- 4 garlic cloves, finely chopped
- 2 lemons, zested
- 3 tbsp fresh rosemary, chopped
- 1 tbsp flaky sea salt
- Freshly ground black pepper
- Butcher's twine
- 2 yellow onions, cut into 3 cm pieces
- 3 carrots, cut into 3 cm pieces
- 2 parsnips, cut into 3 cm pieces
- 4 tomatoes, quartered
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 250 ml dry white wine
- 500 ml chicken stock (from fond or cube)
- 2 tsp cornstarch (*Maizena*), for sauce
- Salt

*To serve:* roast potatoes, spinach, olives, shaved Parmesan

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 150°C (300°F).
2. Lay out the lamb breast meat-side up. Season with black pepper. Spread garlic, lemon zest, rosemary, and flaky salt evenly over the surface. Roll up tightly lengthwise to form a long, narrow roll. Tie with butcher's twine at intervals.
3. Brown the rolled lamb all over in olive oil in a heavy pan over medium-high heat.
4. Transfer to an ovenproof dish. Insert probe lengthwise through one end of the roll, aiming for the geometric centre and avoiding the outer fat layer.
5. Brown onions and root vegetables in the same pan. Add wine, stock, and tomatoes. Bring to a simmer for a few minutes, then pour the entire contents over the lamb roll.
6. Roast in the oven approximately 2 hours, turning the roll a few times during cooking.
7. **Pull at 85°C** (185°F). Recipe states: "tills lammbringans innertemperatur är 85°C" (until internal temperature is 85°C).
8. Rest the lamb 10 minutes. Lift out the roll and root vegetables. Strain cooking liquid into a saucepan. Simmer 10 minutes, thicken with cornstarch dissolved in a little water, season with salt and pepper.
9. Remove twine and slice the lamb roll. Serve with sauce, root vegetables, and roast potatoes mixed with spinach, olives, and shaved Parmesan. Final serving temperature: **85–88°C / 185–190°F** (well done / pulled tender).

## Temperature summary

| Source | Method | Pull temp | Doneness descriptor |
|--------|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Woks of Life (Cantonese) | Stovetop braise | ~88–93°C / 190–200°F | Fork-tender, bones free |
| Forager Chef (American/French) | Oven braise, covered, 150°C | ~88–93°C / 190–200°F | "Cooked and soft" — texture criterion |
| Selin Kiazim / Great British Chefs (Turkish-Cypriot) | Oven braise, covered, 160°C fan | ~88–93°C / 190–200°F | "Bones pull away without resistance" |
| ICA.se (Swedish) | Oven braise, 150°C | **85°C / 185°F** (explicit) | Fork-tender |

**Consensus**: Lamb brisket is pulled at **85–93°C** (185–200°F). ICA.se gives the lowest explicit temperature (85°C) where the meat is described as tender. Most other sources target texture (bones releasing, fork-tender) which corresponds to 88–93°C. There is no culinary disagreement on doneness level — all sources cook to well done / pulled.

The ICA temperature of 85°C represents the lower end for rolled boneless breast (more surface area, less density). 93°C is appropriate for the target for bone-in or thicker whole breast.
