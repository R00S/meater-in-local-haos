## Cut profile

Pork cheek braised in a covered vessel with liquid (wine, stock, aromatics) is one of the most rewarding preparations in modern restaurant cooking. The dense, collagen-rich jaw muscle, roughly 80–120 g, benefits from long gentle oven- or stove-braising at 150–170 °C / 300–340 °F oven or gentle stovetop simmer for 1–2.5 hours. During this time collagen hydrolyses to gelatin, the cheeks absorb the braising liquid's aromatics, and the intramuscular fat renders fully, leaving an exceptionally silky texture that no lean cut can replicate. The culinary target pull temperature is 88–96 °C / 190–205 °F — confirming that collagen has fully converted. The MEATER probe is inserted from the thickest end of the largest cheek, to the geometric centre, parallel to the length of the muscle, clear of the braising liquid surface.

## Styckesprofil

Fläskkind bräserat i ett täckt kärl med vätska (vin, fond, aromater) är en av de mest givande beredningarna i modern restaurang- och hemmatlagning. Den täta, kollagenrika käkmuskeln — ungefär 80–120 g — gynnas av lång, skonsam ugns- eller spisbräsering.

Bräsera: het panna för att brynna, sedan 160–170 °C ugn 2–3 timmar i täckt form med fond, vin, mirepoix, timjan och lagerblad. Innertemperatur: **85–90 °C (185–194 °F)** för spoon-tender, gelatinös konsistens. Eftertillagning: försumbar (braiseringsvätska). Sond in i tjockaste köttmassan.

## Source recipes

### 1. Geoffrey Smeddle — Pork Cheek with Crispy Ham Hock Bon Bon (Scottish fine dining tradition, Culinary Group B)
**Source**: Geoffrey Smeddle (chef-patron, The Peat Inn, Fife, Scotland), greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/pork-cheek-recipe  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 30 min + 24 hr marinating · **Cook**: approx. 1 hr 10 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Note**: This is a fine dining restaurant preparation from The Peat Inn (1 Michelin star). The braising liquid is a fusion of classic European aromatics (brandy, white wine, fennel, carrot, shallots) with Southeast Asian flavouring agents (fish sauce, soy sauce, lemongrass, ginger) — a contemporary British approach that reflects the tradition of Asian influence in modern Scottish fine dining. The recipe also has a sous vide option (80 °C / 176 °F for 6 hours), but this leaf documents the stove-braise method.

**Ingredients** (pork cheeks component only)
- 8 pork cheeks (approximately 700–900 g total)
- ½ garlic clove, crushed
- 1 star anise
- 2 cardamom pods, crushed
- 1 tsp black peppercorns
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme, chopped
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary, chopped
- 50 ml olive oil
- Pinch of salt

*Braising liquor*:
- 1 carrot, finely diced
- 2 shallots, finely diced
- ½ fennel bulb, finely sliced
- 75 ml brandy
- 100 ml white wine (reduced to 50 ml)
- 60 ml fish sauce
- 60 ml light soy sauce
- 1 dash rice wine vinegar
- 1000 ml brown chicken stock
- 2 lemongrass stalks
- 100 g fresh ginger, thinly sliced
- Vegetable oil for sweating

**Method**
1. Trim all sinew from pork cheeks with a sharp paring knife.
2. Combine garlic, star anise, cardamom, peppercorns, thyme, rosemary, olive oil and salt; rub all over cheeks. Cover and refrigerate for 24 hours.
3. In a large heavy-based pan, heat vegetable oil over medium heat. Sweat shallots, carrots and fennel until soft and lightly golden.
4. Add brandy and reduced white wine; reduce by half. Add fish sauce, soy sauce, vinegar, chicken stock, lemongrass and ginger. Bring to the boil; reduce to a gentle simmer for 30 minutes. Remove from heat, cover and allow to cool.
5. Remove pork cheeks from fridge; add to the cooled braising liquor. Bring to the boil; reduce to a simmer.
6. **Insert the MEATER probe from the thickest end of the largest cheek, to the geometric centre of the muscle, parallel to the length of the cheek.**
7. Simmer for approximately **1 hour**, or until "the cheeks will hold their shape, but easily fall apart when cut into with a knife."
8. **Pull at 88–93 °C (190–199 °F).** Smeddle's doneness criterion ("easily fall apart when cut into with a knife") corresponds to full collagen-to-gelatin conversion at this temperature range. At a gentle stovetop simmer (approximately 95–100 °C liquid temperature) the cheeks reach this internal temperature in 45–75 minutes depending on their size.
9. Pass liquid through a fine strainer; reduce to a glossy jus. Rest cheeks in the jus 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **88–93 °C / 190–199 °F** (silky-tender, gelatinous, Asian-inflected European fine dining presentation).
