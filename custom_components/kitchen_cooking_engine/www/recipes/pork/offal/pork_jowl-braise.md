# Pork Jowl × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

Note: pork is halal-prohibited in Muslim majority cultures, so Group C sources are most likely to come from Filipino, Christian West African, or Latin American traditions.

**Anatomy note**: all three sources below are for *pork cheeks* (buccinator muscle, 80–120 g per cheek), which are the same anatomical muscle as the facial component of pork jowl. "Jowl" also includes surrounding subcutaneous fat and skin; for whole-jowl fresh braising (American BBQ style, 400–800 g slabs) the same internal temperature targets apply — only the cook time increases due to the greater mass and fat layer.

## Cut profile

Pork jowl / pork cheeks braised in a covered vessel with aromatic liquid (wine, stock, soy, mirin) is one of the most rewarding preparations in modern restaurant and home cooking. The dense, collagen-rich buccinator muscle — roughly 80–120 g per cheek in the smaller restaurant portion, or 400–800 g for a full American-style jowl slab — contains very little intramuscular fat but large quantities of intermuscular collagen and surrounding fat. During a long gentle braise the collagen hydrolyses to gelatin, the cheeks absorb the braising liquid's aromatics, and the intramuscular fat renders fully, leaving an exceptionally silky, almost gelatinous texture that no lean cut can replicate.

**Braising parameters**: conventional oven or stovetop braise at 150–170 °C / 300–340 °F oven temperature (liquid simmers at 95–100 °C) for 1–3 hours (depending on portion size). Pressure cooker: equivalent collagen conversion in 40–50 minutes at medium-high pressure. Sous vide: 80 °C / 176 °F for 6 hours delivers full collagen hydrolysis with slightly more structure retained than a conventional braise.

**Culinary pull temperature (conventional braise)**: 88–93 °C / 190–199 °F. At this internal temperature the collagen has fully converted to gelatin; the cheeks hold their shape but "easily fall apart when cut into with a knife" (Smeddle) / "nice and tender — still hold their shape but easily fall apart once cut" (Wishart). Below 82 °C the texture remains pleasantly firm but not yet fully gelatinous; at 96 °C the structure starts to disintegrate.

**Probe placement**: insert from the thickest end of the largest cheek, tip at the geometric centre of the muscle, parallel to the long axis of the muscle, clear of any bone fragment or fat pocket. For a whole braised jowl slab, insert horizontally from the side into the thickest meaty section, avoiding the thick fat cap.

## Styckesprofil

Fläskkind / fläskkäk bräserat i ett täckt kärl med aromatisk vätska (vin, fond, sojasås, mirin) är en av de mest givande beredningarna i modern matlagning. Den täta, kollagenrika *buccinator*-muskeln — ca 80–120 g per kind — gynnas av lång, skonsam bräsering.

Bryn kinden, lägg i braiseringskärl med fond, vin eller sojasås-mirin, täck och koka vid 160 °C i 2–3 timmar. Innertemperatur: **85–90 °C (185–194 °F)** för spoon-tender, gelatinöst resultat. Eftertillagning: försumbar (braiseringsvätska). Sond in i tjockaste köttmassan.

## Source recipes

### 1. Geoffrey Smeddle — Pork Cheek with Crispy Ham Hock Bon Bon (Scottish fine dining, Culinary Group B)
**Inspired by**: Geoffrey Smeddle (chef-patron, The Peat Inn, Fife, Scotland — 1 Michelin star), greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/pork-cheek-recipe  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 24 hr marinating + 30 min · **Cook**: approx. 1 hr · **Rest**: 10 min in jus

**Ingredients** (pork cheeks component)
- 8 pork cheeks (approx. 700–900 g total), all sinew removed
- ½ garlic clove, crushed
- 1 star anise
- 2 cardamom pods, crushed
- 1 tsp black peppercorns
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme, chopped
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary, chopped
- 50 ml olive oil
- Pinch of salt

*Braising liquor*:
- 1 carrot, finely diced
- 2 shallots, finely diced
- ½ fennel bulb, finely sliced
- 75 ml brandy
- 100 ml white wine (reduced to 50 ml)
- 60 ml fish sauce
- 60 ml light soy sauce
- 1 dash rice wine vinegar
- 1000 ml brown chicken stock
- 2 lemongrass stalks
- 100 g fresh ginger, thinly sliced
- Vegetable oil

**Method**
1. Trim all sinew from pork cheeks. Combine garlic, star anise, cardamom, peppercorns, thyme, rosemary, olive oil and salt; rub all over cheeks. Cover and refrigerate 24 hours.
2. Sweat shallots, carrots and fennel in vegetable oil until soft and lightly golden.
3. Add brandy and white wine; reduce by half. Add fish sauce, soy sauce, vinegar, chicken stock, lemongrass and ginger. Bring to the boil; simmer 30 min. Remove from heat, cover and cool.
4. Remove pork cheeks from fridge; add to the cooled braising liquor. Bring to the boil; reduce to a gentle simmer.
5. **Insert the MEATER probe from the thickest end of the largest cheek, to the geometric centre of the muscle, parallel to the length of the cheek.**
6. Simmer for approximately **1 hour**, or until "the cheeks will hold their shape, but easily fall apart when cut into with a knife."
7. **Pull at 88–93 °C (190–199 °F).** This is the temperature at which Smeddle's doneness criterion is met. At a gentle stovetop simmer (liquid ~95–100 °C) the cheeks reach this range in 45–75 min depending on size.
8. Pass liquid through a fine strainer; reduce to a glossy jus. Rest cheeks in the jus for 10 min. Serve at **88–93 °C / 190–199 °F**.

### 2. Martin Wishart — Braised Pork Cheeks with Five Spice and Pak Choi (Scottish fine dining, Culinary Group B)
**Inspired by**: Martin Wishart (chef-patron, Restaurant Martin Wishart, Edinburgh — 1 Michelin star), greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/braised-pork-cheek-recipe  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 45 min (pressure cooker) + 15 min sauce · **Rest**: none

**Note**: This recipe uses a pressure cooker for efficiency. 45 minutes at medium-high pressure in a domestic pressure cooker is equivalent to approximately 2–2.5 hours of conventional braising. The five spice profile (star anise, Sichuan peppercorns, cinnamon, coriander, cloves) combined with soy sauce reflects the Anglo-Asian influence characteristic of modern Scottish fine dining.

**Ingredients**
- 12 pork cheeks (whole, excess fat trimmed)
- 1 onion, chopped
- 4 cloves
- 10 g coriander seeds
- 1 star anise
- 10 g Sichuan peppercorns
- 20 g cinnamon stick
- 1 bulb of garlic, halved
- 60 ml soy sauce
- 1.5 l chicken stock
- 80 g unsalted butter, soft
- 50 g plain flour
- 2 tbsp clear honey
- 50 ml vegetable oil

**Method**
1. Season cheeks. Heat oil in a large pan over medium heat; brown cheeks all over. Remove and set aside.
2. Reduce heat; add onion and cook until soft and translucent.
3. Add spices, garlic, soy sauce and stock; bring to the boil. Pour into a pressure cooker with the browned cheeks.
4. Seal pressure cooker; cook over medium-high heat for **45 minutes**. Remove from heat; allow to depressurise for 15–20 minutes.
5. **Remove lid and check doneness: cheeks should be "nice and tender — they should still hold their shape but easily fall apart once cut."**
6. This doneness criterion corresponds to **88–93 °C (190–199 °F)** internal temperature; the pressure environment drives the cheeks to this range reliably within the 45-minute cook time.
7. Transfer cooking liquid to a clean pan; reduce to 800 ml. Pass through a fine sieve.
8. Bring liquid to a simmer; whisk in butter-flour paste (beurre manié) in small pieces until sauce is thick. Add honey; taste for seasoning.
9. Serve cheeks with the sauce, wilted pak choi (sautéed in sesame oil at high heat) and toasted sesame seeds.

### 3. Graham Campbell — Braised Pork Cheeks with Baby Leeks, Sultana Ragu and Caraway Jus (Scottish fine dining, Culinary Group B)
**Inspired by**: Graham Campbell (chef, Culpeper, London — formerly Edinburgh fine dining), greatbritishchefs.com/recipes/braised-pork-cheeks-recipe  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 24 hr marinating + 30 min · **Cook**: 6 hr (sous vide at 80 °C) · **Rest**: none needed

**Note**: This recipe uses a sous vide water bath at 80 °C for 6 hours. In sous vide, the internal temperature of the cheeks equilibrates to the bath temperature (80 °C / 176 °F) and is held there continuously, achieving full collagen hydrolysis over 6 hours at a lower temperature than a conventional braise. The result is slightly more structured / firmer than the conventional braise at 88–93 °C, while still being tender enough to "hold their shape when removed from the bag." This is a legitimate alternative doneness target: 80 °C / 176 °F sustained × 6 hours (MEATER will report 80 °C at hold point). The conventional braise equivalent is 88–93 °C.

**Ingredients** (pork cheeks component)
- 1000 g pork cheeks
- 50 ml sherry vinegar
- 400 ml white wine
- 200 ml Worcestershire sauce
- 5 g salt
- 1 garlic clove, finely chopped
- ½ onion, finely chopped
- 1 carrot, finely chopped
- 1 celery stick, finely chopped
- 1 bay leaf
- 10 g caraway seeds

**Method**
1. Sweat onion, celery, carrots and garlic over low-medium heat in a large saucepan until soft but not coloured.
2. Add sherry vinegar, white wine and Worcestershire sauce; bring to a gentle boil and simmer 30 min.
3. Add bay leaf, 10 g caraway seeds and a pinch of salt; simmer a further 10 min. Pass through a fine strainer; cool.
4. Trim sinew and fat from pork cheeks; place into a large vacuum bag. Add the cooled liquor, seal and marinate 24 hours.
5. Seal in a vacuum bag; **cook in a water bath at 80 °C (176 °F) for 6 hours**.
6. **Insert the MEATER probe from the thickest end of the largest cheek, to the geometric centre of the muscle, before sealing the bag.** The probe will report a stable 80 °C after approximately 30–45 minutes of the bath time; the 6-hour duration (not the peak temperature) is what ensures full collagen conversion at this lower temperature.
7. Remove from bath; serve immediately or cool and refrigerate in the bag for up to 3 days. Final internal temperature: **80 °C / 176 °F (sous vide sustained 6 h)**.

## Temperature consensus (Group B only — provisional)

| Source | Method | Pull temp | Doneness criterion |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Smeddle (GBC) | Stovetop simmer | 88–93 °C / 190–199 °F | Falls apart when cut |
| Wishart (GBC) | Pressure cooker | 88–93 °C / 190–199 °F | Holds shape, falls apart when cut |
| Campbell (GBC) | Sous vide | 80 °C / 176 °F × 6 h | Tender, holds shape |

**Group B consensus (conventional braise)**: pull at **88–93 °C (190–199 °F)**. Target 91 °C / 196 °F (midpoint) for KCE display — maps to `pulled` doneness in the cut file.

**Sous vide alternative**: 80 °C / 176 °F held for minimum 6 hours. Not a probe-monitoring use case for MEATER (bath temperature controls the cook).
