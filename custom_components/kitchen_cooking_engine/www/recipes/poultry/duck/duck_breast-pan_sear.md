## Cut profile

Duck breast is a rich, dark-meat cut distinguished by its thick subcutaneous fat cap. Unlike any other common poultry breast, it is routinely served medium-rare (135–145 °F / 57–63 °C) — a practice endorsed by top culinary authorities and practiced universally in professional kitchens. Pan-searing is the definitive cooking method for duck breast: the skin goes into a cold, dry skillet and the fat is slowly rendered over 12–15 minutes before the breast is flipped. This unique cold-pan start is essential to producing the hallmark thin, crackling, golden skin without burning it before the underlying fat has melted away.

Target internal temperature: **135–145 °F (57–63 °C)** — medium-rare; pull at 130 °F and rest 5 min. For USDA well-done, target 165 °F (74 °C).

## Source recipes

### 1. BBC Good Food — Slow-Roast Duck (fat-rendering philosophy adapted for pan sear)
**Source**: BBC Good Food editorial team, BBC Good Food; https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/slow-roast-duck (fetched 2025-07-15).
**Serves**: 4 (whole duck) → adapted for 2 duck breasts · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 18–22 min · **Rest**: 5–8 min

> **Note**: The BBC recipe is for a whole slow-roast duck seasoned with salt and cumin, cooked at very low
> temperature for 13 hours. The central principle — low and slow fat rendering, never rush the fat, keep
> the skin in contact — maps directly onto the cold-pan breast technique. The final honey glaze applied
> at the end also adapts well to a pan-sear finish.

**Temperature guidance**:
- Pan heat: medium-low (never medium-high for duck breast)
- Optional oven finish: 400 °F (204 °C) for 3–5 min for even doneness throughout
- Internal target medium-rare: 135–145 °F (57–63 °C)
- Internal target well-done: 165 °F (74 °C)
- Time: 12–15 min skin-side, 3–5 min flesh-side

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on, boneless duck breasts (Moulard or Pekin), 6–9 oz each
- Flaky sea salt (generous)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- ½ tsp ground cumin (optional, echoes BBC recipe)
- 1 tsp honey (for glaze at end, optional)
- Orange zest (optional garnish)

**Method**
1. Take duck breasts out of refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking.
2. Pat skin completely dry with paper towels. Any moisture prevents crisping.
3. Score the skin in a tight diagonal cross-hatch, ¼ inch deep, taking care not to cut into the breast meat. The scoring allows fat to escape and skin to shrink and crisp evenly.
4. Season both sides generously with salt and pepper. Apply cumin to skin side if using.
5. Place breasts skin-side down in a cold, heavy-bottomed pan (cast iron or stainless steel). Do NOT preheat the pan.
6. Set heat to medium-low. This cold start is the key technique for duck — it allows the thick fat layer to render gradually without the skin surface burning before the fat melts.
7. Cook without moving for 12–15 minutes. The fat will render out visibly. Pour off excess fat into a heatproof jar every 4–5 minutes to prevent the breast from frying in its own fat (which causes steaming instead of crisping).
8. After 12–15 min the skin should be deep golden, thin, and crackling. If not, continue 2–3 more minutes.
9. Flip breasts to flesh side. Increase heat to medium. Cook 2–4 minutes for medium-rare (130 °F pull) or 5–7 minutes for well-done (160 °F pull).
10. Optional honey glaze: in the final 60 seconds, brush skin side with a thin layer of honey and tilt pan to caramelise briefly.
11. Transfer to a cutting board, rest 5 minutes uncovered or loosely tented. Final temp: 135–142 °F (medium-rare).
12. Slice across the grain in ¼-inch slices. Fan on warm plates.

### 2. Classic Culinary Technique — Professional Kitchen Duck Breast Pan Sear
**Source**: Standard professional culinary technique, documented across culinary schools and restaurant kitchens; general knowledge synthesis.
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+ optional 1–24 hr dry brine) · **Cook**: 18–22 min · **Rest**: 5–7 min

**Temperature guidance**:
- Pan: cold start, medium-low for rendering (12–15 min), medium for flesh side (3–5 min)
- Optional oven finish: 400 °F (204 °C), 3–5 min
- Internal target: 130 °F pull → rest to 135–140 °F (medium-rare)
- Well-done: 160 °F pull → rest to 165 °F

**Ingredients**
- 2 Moulard or Pekin duck breasts, skin-on, boneless
- Kosher salt, freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter (for basting)
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- 1 garlic clove, lightly smashed
- 1 shallot, halved (for pan sauce)
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry red wine or Port (for pan sauce)
- 120 ml (½ cup) duck or chicken stock (for pan sauce)

**Method**
1. **Dry brine** (optional but recommended): Salt duck breasts uncovered on a rack in the refrigerator for 1–24 hours. This draws surface moisture out, seasons deeply, and produces superior skin crispness.
2. Score skin in 1-inch diagonal cross-hatch, ¼ inch deep, without piercing meat.
3. Season both sides with pepper (salt already applied if dry-brined; add light salt if skipping brine).
4. Place skin-side down in cold cast-iron or stainless skillet. Do not add fat — duck has more than enough.
5. Turn heat to medium-low. The fat will begin rendering within 3–4 minutes.
6. Drain rendered fat every 4–5 minutes into a heatproof container. Reserve for another use (roast potatoes, sauté vegetables).
7. After 12–15 minutes the skin is uniformly golden and crackling; press lightly — it should feel firm and dry, not soft and pliable.
8. Flip to flesh side. Add butter, thyme, garlic. Tilt pan and baste flesh with butter repeatedly for 2 minutes.
9. For medium-rare: remove at 130 °F internal (approximately 3–5 minutes flesh-side). For well-done: cook to 160 °F (~7–9 min flesh-side).
10. Rest on a wire rack for 5–7 minutes.
11. **Quick pan sauce** (optional): Pour off all but 1 tbsp fat. Sauté shallot 1 min. Deglaze with wine, reduce 1 min. Add stock, reduce by half. Season, mount with 1 tsp cold butter.
12. Slice against the grain; serve skin-side up to maintain crispness.

### 3. BBC Good Food — One-Pan Duck with Savoy Cabbage

**Source**: Barney Desmazery, BBC Good Food magazine (July 2008); https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/one-pan-duck-savoy-cabbage (archived: https://web.archive.org/web/20241110020656/https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/one-pan-duck-savoy-cabbage, fetched 2026-04-24).
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 30 min

**Temperature guidance**:
- Skin-side down in non-stick sauté pan on LOW heat for 15 min (renders fat, browns skin)
- Flip to flesh side for 5 min
- Internal target: medium 140–145 °F (60–63 °C) pink; well done 165 °F (74 °C)
- Rest before carving and slicing

**Ingredients**
- 2 duck breasts
- 1 tsp black peppercorns, crushed
- Salt
- 600g cooked new potato, thickly sliced
- 1 bunch flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
- 1 garlic clove, finely chopped
- 6 rashers smoked streaky bacon, chopped
- 1 Savoy cabbage, trimmed, quartered, cored and finely sliced
- 1 tbsp balsamic vinegar
- 2 tbsp olive oil

**Method**
1. Lightly score the skin of the duck breasts, then generously season with the peppercorns and a sprinkling of salt. Lay the duck breasts, skin-side down, in a non-stick sauté pan, then place over a low heat. Leave the duck for 15 mins to brown and release its fat, then flip over onto the flesh side for 5 mins.
2. Remove the duck from the pan, then turn up the heat. Add the potatoes to the pan, fry until brown and crisp, then scatter over the parsley and garlic. Scoop out with a slotted spoon onto a plate, then season with salt.
3. Keep the pan on the heat. Fry the bacon until crisp, then add the cabbage. Cook for 1 min, add a splash of water, then fry for 2 mins, just until the cabbage is wilted. While the cabbage is cooking, whisk any juices from the duck with the vinegar and olive oil. To serve, carve the duck breast into slices, fan out on large dinner plates, spoon a neat bundle of cabbage on one side, then pile a serving of potatoes on the other. Drizzle over the dressing and serve.

**Recommended MEATER settings**:
- **Medium-rare** (chef-preferred): Set alert at **130 °F (54 °C)** → pull from pan, rest 5–7 min → carryover brings to ~138 °F (59 °C)
- **Well-done** (USDA safe): Set alert at **160 °F (71 °C)** → pull and rest → carryover brings to 165 °F (74 °C)
- Rest 5–7 minutes minimum
- Duck breast carryover is typically 5–8 °F with pan-sear method (pan retains heat)

## Notes

- **Cold pan start is mandatory** — this is the single most important rule for duck breast. Preheating the pan causes the skin surface to burn before the fat cap underneath has rendered.
- Never cover the pan — steam will ruin the skin crispness.
- Pour off fat regularly — if the breast sits in pooled fat it fries (soft skin) rather than renders (crispy skin).
- Duck fat collected is one of the most prized cooking fats; store in sealed jar, refrigerate up to 1 month.
- Moulard ducks (used for foie gras production) have larger, fattier, more flavourful breasts than Pekin. Cook time may be 2–3 min longer for the thicker fat cap.
- Resting is non-negotiable — juices need time to redistribute, and carryover cooking must complete.
