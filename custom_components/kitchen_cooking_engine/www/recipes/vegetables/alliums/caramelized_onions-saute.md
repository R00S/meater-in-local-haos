# Caramelized Onions × Saute — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Slow-caramelised onions via sauté are one of the most fundamental flavour-building techniques in global cooking. Sliced onions sautéed over medium-low heat lose 60–70 % of their water content over 30–60 minutes, concentrating their sugars (fructose and glucose) until Maillard and caramelisation reactions produce a deep amber-brown, jammy condiment with complex sweet, savoury, and slightly bitter flavours. The process requires patience — onions that are cooked fast at high heat brown through rapid Maillard rather than true caramelisation, producing a different result. The target internal temperature of 90–96 °C (194–205 °F) corresponds to the point where nearly all free water has evaporated from the residual pan-mass and the sugars have concentrated and transformed. Carryover after removal is irrelevant — the slow cook is self-regulating.

## Styckesprofil

Långsamt karameliserade lökar via sauté är en av de mest grundläggande smaksättningstekniker i global matlagning. Skivad lök sauterad vid medel-låg värme förlorar 60–70 % av sin vattenhalt under 30–45 minuter och omvandlas till en söt, djupbrun massa. Nyckeln är tid och tålamod — inte hög värme. Innertemperatur: inte relevant — lök är klara när de är djupt gyllenbruna och silkeslena.

## Source recipes

### 1. Julia Child — Oignons Glacés à Brun (French braised-caramelised onions)
**Inspired by**: Julia Child, Louisette Bertholle & Simone Beck, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking, Vol. I* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961)
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 35–40 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g small pearl onions or large onions sliced into half-moons
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 tbsp neutral oil
- ½ tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp caster sugar
- 60 ml (¼ cup) beef stock or water (to add if the pan dries out)
- Freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. If using pearl onions, blanch 30 seconds in boiling water and peel. If using sliced large onions, no preparation beyond slicing is needed.
2. Melt butter with oil in a wide, heavy-bottomed sauté pan over medium heat. Add the onions; toss to coat. Season with salt and sugar. (Child notes the sugar "helps the browning along.")
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest section of the onion mass or the largest piece.
4. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring every 5 minutes, for 30–40 minutes. The onions will first turn translucent, then pale gold, then deep amber. If they threaten to burn or stick, add a splash of stock.
5. **Pull at 92–96 °C (198–205 °F).** Child specifies that the onions should be "a very deep, rich brown — almost mahogany — completely tender and glazed with their own concentrated syrup." At this temperature, the residual water is nearly gone and the sugar glaze is thick.
6. Taste and adjust salt and pepper. Final serving temperature: **90–95 °C / 194–203 °F** (jammy, deeply sweet-savoury, the foundational French garnish for *boeuf bourguignon* and *soupe à l'oignon*).

### 2. Madhur Jaffrey — Kachumber Pyaz (North Indian slow-cooked onions for gravy base)
**Inspired by**: Madhur Jaffrey, *Madhur Jaffrey's Indian Cookery* (BBC Books, 1982)
**Serves**: 4–6 (as a masala base) · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g medium onions, peeled and finely sliced into half-moons
- 60 ml (4 tbsp) vegetable oil or ghee (clarified butter)
- 1 tsp whole cumin seeds
- 2 garlic cloves, finely grated
- 1 tsp finely grated fresh ginger
- ½ tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp ground turmeric (optional — adds colour)
- ½ tsp coriander powder (optional — adds fragrance to the base)

**Method**
1. Heat oil or ghee in a wide karahi or heavy sauté pan over medium heat. Add cumin seeds; sizzle 10–15 seconds until they pop and darken a shade.
2. Add the onions and salt; toss well. Cook over medium heat, stirring every 3–4 minutes, for 20–25 minutes. The onions will progressively turn translucent, then pale gold, then deep amber.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest cluster of onions in the pan.
4. When the onions are golden, add garlic and ginger; cook 2 more minutes. Add turmeric and coriander powder if using; stir 20 seconds.
5. **Pull at 90–94 °C (194–201 °F).** Jaffrey's masala base — bhunao technique — aims for onions that are *gahra bhuna* (deeply fried and cooked), golden-brown throughout with all moisture expelled. They should clump slightly.
6. This is the essential masala base for most North Indian curries. Use immediately, or cool and refrigerate. Final serving temperature: **90–94 °C / 194–201 °F** when using as a fresh garnish or side.

### 3. Yotam Ottolenghi — Slow-Cooked Onions with Thyme and Cream (Middle Eastern / Israeli tradition)
**Inspired by**: Yotam Ottolenghi, *Plenty More* (Ebury Press, 2014)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 45–55 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 900 g medium onions (about 4), peeled and sliced into 5 mm half-moons
- 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 6 sprigs fresh thyme (leaves stripped)
- 1 tsp fine sea salt
- 1 tsp caster sugar
- 100 ml (⅓ cup) double cream
- 1 tbsp sherry vinegar
- 30 g Parmesan, finely grated, to finish
- Freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Heat olive oil and butter together in a wide, heavy pan over medium-low heat. When the butter melts, add the onions, thyme, salt and sugar. Toss well.
2. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest cluster of onions.
3. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring every 8–10 minutes, for 40–50 minutes. Ottolenghi prescribes patience: "The onions must be given the full time or they will taste raw-sweet rather than richly complex."
4. When the onions are deep amber, add the cream and vinegar; increase heat to medium and cook 5 minutes, stirring, until the cream reduces to a glossy sauce that coats the onions.
5. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** The cream will have reduced and the onions will be meltingly tender.
6. Scatter Parmesan and black pepper. Final serving temperature: **90–95 °C / 194–203 °F** (rich, creamy, deeply caramelised — serve on toast or as a base for a tart).

### 4. Najmieh Batmanglij — Piyaz Davag (Persian slow-caramelised onions for garnish)
**Inspired by**: Najmieh Batmanglij, *Food of Life: Ancient Persian and Modern Iranian Cooking and Ceremonies* (Mage Publishers, 2011)
**Serves**: 6–8 (as a garnish) · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 40–50 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g large yellow onions, peeled and thinly sliced into rings
- 60 ml (4 tbsp) vegetable oil or clarified butter (roghan)
- ½ tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp ground turmeric (gives the characteristic Persian golden colour)
- Optional: 30 g dried barberries (zereshk) — soaked 10 min, then stirred in during the last 5 minutes for a sour-jewel garnish

**Method**
1. Heat oil in a wide frying pan over medium heat. Add the onion rings; toss. Add salt and turmeric; toss again to coat the rings in the golden oil.
2. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into the thickest onion cluster.
3. Cook over medium-low heat, turning frequently, for 35–45 minutes. The onions must go past pale gold to a rich amber-brown, deeply flavoured from the turmeric-oil interaction.
4. If using barberries, add them in the last 5 minutes; toss and cook until the barberries plump slightly and the whole mixture is deep amber.
5. **Pull at 90–94 °C (194–201 °F).** Batmanglij's *piyaz davag* is the essential Persian garnish for rice dishes (polo), stews (khoresh) and stuffed vegetables. The turmeric gives them a distinctive golden-amber colour and slight earthiness. Final serving temperature: **room temperature or warm** — sprinkled as a garnish over rice or meat dishes.
