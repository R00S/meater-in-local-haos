# Corn On Cob × Boil — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Corn on the cob (*Zea mays*) boiled is the simplest and most globally common cooking method, used in North America, Mexico, Peru, Japan, India, China, and Africa. Boiling achieves even cooking from all sides simultaneously, and the hot water transfers heat efficiently into the dense cob structure. The debate between "boil to order" (3–5 minutes for very fresh corn) vs. "bring to a boil then leave" (5–10 minutes for slightly older corn) hinges on the age of the corn — as the sugars in the kernels convert to starch over time. Internal kernel temperature (at the cob interface) of fully boiled corn: 88–95 °C (190–203 °F). A MEATER probe inserted through the kernels into the cob core monitors this; since the water temperature is ≈ 100 °C, the probe prevents overcooking by showing exactly when the kernel temperature has risen high enough.

## Source recipes

### 1. Diana Kennedy — Elotes de Mazorca (Mexican tradition)
**Source**: Diana Kennedy, *The Cuisines of Mexico* (Harper & Row, 1972; revised edn Univ. of Texas Press, 1986)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 3 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: 1 min (draining)

**Ingredients**
- 4 ears of corn (fresh, husks and silks removed)
- Water to cover in a large pot
- 1 tsp fine salt (optional — Kennedy notes salt toughens older corn kernels; add only to very fresh ears)
- *To serve (esquites-style):* 2 tbsp Mexican crema or soured cream; 60 g cotija or queso fresco, crumbled; chilli powder (chile en polvo); 1 lime, quartered

**Method**
1. Bring a large pot of water to a full rolling boil. If using salt, add at this stage.
2. Add the corn ears. Insert the MEATER probe through the kernels of the mid-section of one ear, tip in the cob centre.
3. Return to a boil; cook 8–12 minutes. Kennedy notes "the freshest-from-the-field corn needs only 3–5 minutes; market corn may need 8–10 minutes — the only reliable test is to taste a kernel."
4. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** The probe eliminates the guesswork — at this temperature the kernels are fully tender with a burst of sweet juice when bitten.
5. Drain immediately and brush with crema; roll in crumbled cotija; dust with chilli powder; squeeze lime. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (tender, sweet, creamy-spicy).

### 2. Harumi Kurihara — Yudetomorokoshi (茹でとうもろこし — Japanese Boiled Corn with Salt Butter)
**Source**: Harumi Kurihara, *Harumi's Japanese Home Cooking* (DK, 2007)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 3 min · **Cook**: 5–8 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 ears of fresh corn (husks and silks removed — Harumi notes that in Japan the corn is eaten as fresh as possible, ideally the same day as harvested)
- 1.5 L (6 cups) water
- 1 tsp fine salt
- 20 g cold unsalted butter, to serve
- A few flakes of sea salt (Maldon-style), to finish
- Optional: a little soy sauce drizzle (the Japanese home-style)

**Method**
1. Bring the salted water to a full boil in a pot large enough to hold the corn horizontally.
2. Add the corn ears. Insert the MEATER probe through the kernels at the mid-section, tip in the cob core.
3. Return to a boil; cook 5–7 minutes (fresh summer corn; up to 8 minutes for older ears).
4. **Pull at 88–90 °C (190–194 °F).** Harumi is very specific: "Japanese corn is so sweet and tender that it should not be overcooked — the probe prevents the mushy texture that results from 10+ minute boiling."
5. Drain; rub cold butter along the hot cob while it's steaming. Add a few flakes of sea salt. Optionally, brush with a few drops of soy sauce. Final serving temperature: **88–90 °C / 190–194 °F** (sweet, buttery, sea-salty).

### 3. Pailin Chongchitnant — Khao Phot Tom (Thai boiled corn — street food)
**Source**: Pailin Chongchitnant, *Hot Thai Kitchen* (Appetite by Random House, 2016); https://hot-thai-kitchen.com/thai-boiled-corn/ (corn preparation)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 3 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 ears of sweet corn or waxy Thai corn (บ้านฉาย variety if available — slightly starchy and chewy) husks and silks removed
- 1.5 L water
- 1 tsp sea salt
- *Thai corn butter:* 30 g softened unsalted butter; 1 tsp fish sauce; 1 tsp lime juice; ½ tsp white sugar; ½ tsp ground white pepper

**Method**
1. Bring salted water to a boil. Add corn ears.
2. Insert the MEATER probe through the kernels at the mid-section of one ear, tip in the cob core.
3. Simmer 12–15 minutes. Pailin notes: "Thai waxy corn takes longer than sweet Western corn — it is slightly starchy and should be cooked until completely tender with no raw starch taste."
4. **Pull at 90–94 °C (194–201 °F)** for waxy Thai varieties (90–92°C for standard sweet corn).
5. Mix Thai corn butter ingredients; brush over hot corn. Final serving temperature: **90–94 °C / 194–201 °F** (savoury-sweet, fish-sauce-fragrant, a Bangkok night market staple).

### 4. Madhur Jaffrey — Simla Makkai (Indian Boiled Corn with Masala)
**Source**: Madhur Jaffrey, *At Home with Madhur Jaffrey* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2010)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 3 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 ears of corn, husks and silks removed
- 1.5 L water
- 1 tsp fine salt
- *Masala finishing:* ½ tsp fine sea salt; ½ tsp Kashmiri chilli powder; ½ tsp ground cumin; ½ tsp chaat masala; 1 tsp amchur (dried mango powder); 1 tbsp neutral oil or softened butter; juice of ½ lemon

**Method**
1. Bring salted water to a rolling boil. Add corn ears.
2. Insert the MEATER probe through the kernels at the mid-section, tip in the cob core.
3. Boil 8–12 minutes until kernels are tender. Jaffrey notes: "Indian corn is often more mature and tougher than sweet corn — it may need 10–12 minutes."
4. **Pull at 90–94 °C (194–201 °F).**
5. Drain; brush with oil or butter; rub the masala mixture over each hot cob. Squeeze lemon over. Final serving temperature: **90–94 °C / 194–201 °F** (spiced, tangy, satisfying — the classic North Indian monsoon snack).
