# Eggplant × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Eggplant (*Solanum melongena*; UK aubergine) achieves its most dramatic transformation on a live flame or a very hot grill. The outer skin chars to a brittle black shell, the interior softens and collapses, and the flesh takes on a deep, irreplaceable smokiness from the volatiles released as the skin carbonises. Whole-grilled eggplant is the foundational technique for baba ganoush, baingan bharta, and countless other mashed or puréed dips across South Asian, Middle Eastern, East Asian, and Mediterranean traditions. Halved and grilled (dengaku-style), the cut face browns via Maillard reactions while the flesh steams from the inside. A MEATER probe inserted through the side into the thickest part of the flesh monitors the transition from dense and opaque (~60 °C) to fully collapsed and silky (~90–95 °C for whole; 82–88 °C for halved). Carryover is minimal once the flesh is at this level of softness.

## Source recipes

### 1. Swasthi Shreekanth — Baingan Bharta (Indian / Punjabi tradition)
**Source**: Swasthi Shreekanth, IndianHealthyRecipes.com; adapted from Chef Sanjeev Kapoor; https://www.indianhealthyrecipes.com/baingan-bharta/ (fetched 2026-04-24).
**Serves**: 3–4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min grill + 12 min masala · **Rest**: 5 min (cooling)

**Ingredients**
- 1 large purple eggplant, 450–500 g (firm skin, no brown streaks)
- 4 garlic cloves, plus 3 extra (for masala)
- 1–2 green chillies
- Oil to brush generously over eggplant (any neutral or mustard oil)
- 1½ tbsp mustard oil (for masala)
- ½–¾ inch fresh ginger, finely chopped
- 1 medium onion (~½ cup), finely chopped
- ¾ cup (≈ 120 g) ripe tomatoes, deseeded and chopped
- ¾ tsp fine salt (or to taste)
- ½ tsp Kashmiri red chilli powder
- ¼–½ tsp garam masala (optional)
- Small handful fresh coriander (cilantro) leaves, to garnish

**Method**
1. Make 6 slits through the skin of the eggplant. Push 4 garlic cloves and the green chillies deep inside the slits (they must go deep so they don't burn). Brush the entire surface generously with oil.
2. Place the eggplant directly over a moderately high direct flame using tongs (stovetop, charcoal grill, or a grill pan at very high heat). Rotate every 3–4 minutes to char all sides including the base.
3. Insert the MEATER Plus probe through one of the existing slits into the geometric centre of the thickest part of the flesh (not touching the skin-charred zone).
4. Continue grilling and rotating until the skin is completely charred all over and the flesh feels completely soft — a fork should glide through without resistance. Pull when internal temperature reads **90–95 °C (194–203 °F)**.
5. Transfer to a bowl; cover for 5 minutes so the eggplant sweats and the skin loosens easily. Peel off and discard the charred skin. Chop or mash the flesh together with the grilled garlic and chillies.
6. For the masala: heat mustard oil in a pan until smoking; reduce heat. Add ginger and 3 remaining garlic cloves; sauté 30–60 seconds. Add onion; cook 6–7 minutes until golden. Add tomatoes and salt; cook until tomatoes break down (2–3 min). Add chilli powder; cook 1 min. Add the mashed eggplant; sauté 7–8 minutes over medium heat, stirring, until the masala is fragrant and most moisture has evaporated.
7. Add garam masala if using; do not cook further after this (it darkens the bharta). Garnish with coriander.
8. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F)** for the whole eggplant on the grill. Final serving temperature: **75–80 °C / 167–176 °F** (hot, smoky mash — serve immediately with roti or naan).

### 2. Yotam Ottolenghi — Whole Chargrilled Aubergine (Middle Eastern / Israeli tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi, *Plenty* (Ebury Press, 2010)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 30–35 min · **Rest**: 10 min (peeling)

**Ingredients**
- 4 medium eggplants (≈ 250 g each), left whole
- 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 2 tbsp lemon juice
- 2 garlic cloves, finely grated
- ½ tsp fine sea salt
- ¼ tsp ground cumin
- Small handful flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
- ½ tsp sweet smoked paprika, to finish
- A drizzle of good olive oil, to finish

**Method**
1. Place whole eggplants directly on the bars of a gas burner over maximum flame, or on a preheated charcoal grill. They must sit over live fire — no grill pan will replicate the smokiness.
2. Insert the MEATER Plus probe through the skin into the thickest section of one eggplant, with the tip at the geometric centre of the flesh.
3. Cook 30–35 minutes, turning every 6–8 minutes, until the skin is utterly blackened on all sides and the flesh feels completely collapsed and yielding — Ottolenghi describes correctly cooked eggplant as feeling "almost empty" when gently squeezed with tongs.
4. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** The flesh is now a smoky, silky mass.
5. Transfer to a colander over a bowl; allow to cool 10 minutes (the liquid that drains is bitter — discard it). Peel off and discard the charred skin.
6. Chop the flesh roughly (do not over-mash — a coarse texture is preferable). Stir in olive oil, lemon juice, garlic, salt and cumin.
7. Pile onto a platter; drizzle with extra olive oil, dust with smoked paprika, and scatter parsley. Final serving temperature: **room temperature or slightly warm** — Ottolenghi serves this at ambient.

### 3. Harumi Kurihara — Nasu no Dengaku (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Harumi Kurihara, *Harumi's Japanese Home Cooking* (DK/Dorling Kindersley, 2007)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 Japanese eggplants (nasu), about 100–120 g each, halved lengthways
- 2 tbsp neutral oil (sunflower or rice bran), for brushing
- *Dengaku miso paste:*
  - 3 tbsp white (shiro) miso
  - 2 tbsp mirin
  - 1 tbsp caster sugar
  - 1 tbsp sake
- Toasted sesame seeds and thinly sliced spring onion (negi), to finish

**Method**
1. Score the cut face of each eggplant half with a shallow crosshatch (this helps the flesh cook through and absorb the glaze). Brush the cut face and skin with oil.
2. Combine miso, mirin, sugar and sake in a small saucepan over low heat; stir until the sugar dissolves and the paste turns glossy, ~2 minutes. Remove from heat.
3. Preheat a charcoal grill or gas grill to medium-high. Place eggplant halves cut-face-down on the grill.
4. Insert the MEATER probe through the skin side of one eggplant half at a slight angle, pushing into the centre of the flesh so the tip is at the geometric centre of the half.
5. Grill cut-face-down 4–5 minutes until the scored face is golden-brown with char marks.
6. Turn skin-side-down. Brush the cut face liberally with the dengaku miso paste. Grill skin-side-down a further 4–5 minutes until the miso begins to caramelise at the edges and the flesh is soft throughout.
7. **Pull at 82–86 °C (180–187 °F).** Harumi targets eggplant that is silky and sweet — fully cooked through but still holding its half shape, not collapsed.
8. Scatter sesame seeds and spring onion. Final serving temperature: **82–86 °C / 180–187 °F** (sweet, savoury, miso-glazed).

### 4. Francis Mallmann — Berenjena a las Brasas (Argentine / Patagonian tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann with Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan Books, 2009)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 40–50 min over embers · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 medium eggplants, left whole with stems on
- Coarse sea salt (sal gruesa)
- Extra-virgin olive oil, to finish
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- 2 garlic cloves, finely chopped
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) red wine vinegar (for a chimichurri drizzle)

**Method**
1. Build a wood fire and let it burn down to moderate-heat coals and embers (no open flame — Mallmann specifically uses the heat of the coals, not the flame, for whole vegetables).
2. Place the whole eggplants directly onto the coals — not on a grill grate, but buried in and surrounded by glowing embers.
3. Insert the MEATER Plus probe through the side of one eggplant into the thickest centre of the flesh.
4. Cook the eggplants in the embers 40–50 minutes, using tongs to rotate them a quarter turn every 10 minutes, until the skin is completely blackened and ash-covered and the flesh inside is utterly collapsed and soft.
5. **Pull at 92–96 °C (198–205 °F).** Mallmann pushes longer and hotter than stovetop methods — the ember cooking achieves a "double smoke" from both the charring skin and the wood coals, resulting in an intensely mineral smokiness unavailable on a gas grill.
6. Remove with tongs; rest on a board 10 minutes until cool enough to handle. Tear open, scoop out the flesh, discard the charred skin.
7. Season with coarse sea salt, drizzle with olive oil and red wine vinegar; scatter parsley and garlic. Final serving temperature: **warm to room temperature** — Mallmann serves this as a starter or side at around **65–70 °C / 149–158 °F**.
