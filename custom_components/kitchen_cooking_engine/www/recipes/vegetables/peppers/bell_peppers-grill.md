# Bell Peppers × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Bell peppers (*Capsicum annuum*) on the grill transform dramatically: the skin blisters and chars, the interior softens from crisp to silky, and the natural sugars concentrate into a deep sweetness. The technique is two-phase — direct high heat to blister and char the exterior, then steam-peeling to remove the skin and reveal the smoky, tender flesh beneath. Grilled peppers are a foundational ingredient in Spanish (pimientos asados), Turkish (közlenmiş biber), Moroccan (chermoula-dressed peppers), and Italian (peperoni arrostiti) cuisines. Whole peppers can also be grilled and stuffed without peeling. A MEATER probe inserted through the shoulder of the pepper into the thickest part of the wall detects when the flesh reaches 88–95 °C (190–203 °F) — the point at which it is fully soft and the skin separates easily. Carryover is negligible; the peel-and-rest period is for practical skin removal, not for carryover cooking.

## Styckesprofil

Paprika (*Capsicum annuum*) på grillen transformeras dramatiskt: skinnet blåser och förkolnas, insidan mjuknar från krispig till silkeslen, och de naturliga sockerarterna koncentreras. Grilla hel direkt på flamman i 20–30 minuter tills helt förkolnad. Klar när fullständigt mjuk och skinnet lätt avlägsnas efter ångning i påse.

## Source recipes

### 1. Claudia Roden — Biber Közleme (Moroccan / Turkish tradition)
**Inspired by**: Claudia Roden, *Arabesque: A Taste of Morocco, Turkey and Lebanon* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2005)
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 15 min (steaming)

**Ingredients**
- 4 large red bell peppers (or a mix of red, orange and yellow)
- 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil (to dress after peeling)
- 1 garlic clove, finely grated
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped
- 1 tsp ground cumin (optional, for Moroccan style)
- 1 tsp harissa paste (optional, for a hot version)

**Method**
1. Preheat a charcoal or gas grill to very high direct heat (≈ 250 °C surface temperature). Clean and oil the grates.
2. Place the whole peppers directly on the grate. Insert the MEATER Plus probe through the shoulder of one pepper into the thickest part of the flesh wall, with the tip in the interior (not through to the seed cavity).
3. Grill, turning with tongs every 3–4 minutes, until the skin is evenly blackened and blistered on all sides (15–20 minutes total). The peppers will deflate slightly as they cook.
4. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** Roden describes correct doneness as peppers that feel "completely limp and very soft" when gently pressed with tongs — the skin should slide off the flesh.
5. Transfer to a bowl; cover tightly with cling film (or place in a sealed bag). Steam 15 minutes — the trapped heat and moisture loosens the skin.
6. Peel off and discard the charred skin; remove seeds and cut the flesh into strips. Dress with olive oil, garlic, salt, pepper, parsley and optional cumin or harissa. Final serving temperature: **room temperature** — these peppers are best served at ambient temperature or slightly warm.

### 2. Francis Mallmann — Pimientos a la Parrilla (Argentine / South American tradition)
**Inspired by**: Francis Mallmann with Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan Books, 2009)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min over embers · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 large bell peppers (red preferred — Mallmann uses the sweetest he can find)
- 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- 1 garlic clove, finely minced
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) red-wine vinegar
- Coarse sea salt (sal gruesa)

**Method**
1. Build a wood fire; allow to burn to glowing coals. Mallmann places whole peppers directly on a clean steel plancha set over the coals, or uses heavy cast-iron over the embers.
2. Place the whole peppers on the plancha. Insert the MEATER probe through the shoulder into the thickest flesh wall.
3. Cook over moderate-to-high coal heat 20–25 minutes, turning a quarter turn every 5 minutes, until the skin is evenly blackened on all faces and the peppers feel completely soft when pressed.
4. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** Mallmann pushes until "the pepper sags when lifted — the flesh has given up entirely to the heat."
5. Transfer to a bowl; cover 15 minutes. Peel, deseed and tear the flesh into wide strips.
6. Toss with olive oil, parsley, garlic and vinegar; season with coarse sea salt. Final serving temperature: **room temperature** (sweet, charred, tangy chimichurri-dressed).

### 3. Yotam Ottolenghi — Charred Peppers with Walnut Sauce (Middle Eastern / Israeli tradition)
**Inspired by**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 15–18 min · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 3 large red bell peppers + 1 yellow bell pepper (or 4 red)
- 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- *Walnut sauce:* 60 g (½ cup) toasted walnuts; 1 tbsp pomegranate molasses; 1 tsp ground cumin; ½ tsp sweet smoked paprika; 1 small garlic clove; 30 ml olive oil; 2 tbsp lemon juice; salt
- 30 g pomegranate seeds, to garnish
- 5 g fresh basil leaves, torn, to garnish

**Method**
1. Preheat a gas grill or oven broiler to maximum (≈ 250 °C). If using a gas grill, set to high direct heat with the lid open.
2. Place whole peppers on the grill (or line a tray, set under the broiler). Insert the MEATER probe through the shoulder into the flesh wall.
3. Grill or broil 15–18 minutes, turning every 4–5 minutes, until all sides are evenly charred and the peppers collapse slightly.
4. **Pull at 90–93 °C (194–199 °F).** Transfer immediately to a bowl; cover with cling film for 15 minutes to steam.
5. Peel and deseed the peppers; cut into wide strips. Toss with olive oil.
6. Blend walnut sauce: pulse walnuts, pomegranate molasses, cumin, paprika, garlic, olive oil and lemon juice until slightly chunky. Season with salt.
7. Arrange pepper strips on a platter; spoon walnut sauce over; scatter pomegranate seeds and basil. Final serving temperature: **room temperature** (smoky, sweet-sour, walnut-rich).

### 4. Marcella Hazan — Peperoni Arrostiti all'Aglio (Italian tradition)
**Inspired by**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1992)
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 15 min (steaming)

**Ingredients**
- 4 large red and yellow bell peppers
- 2 garlic cloves, thinly sliced (not grated — Hazan is specific about sliced garlic for this preparation)
- 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- Fine sea salt
- Small handful flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Optional: 4–6 canned anchovy fillets, drained and roughly chopped

**Method**
1. Preheat the broiler to maximum. Place whole peppers on a foil-lined sheet pan directly under the broiler, as close to the element as possible.
2. Insert the MEATER probe through the shoulder into the thickest part of the flesh wall.
3. Broil 15–20 minutes, turning as each face blisters and chars — Hazan emphasises turning often, every 4–5 minutes, to char all sides evenly. The skin should be thoroughly blackened, not just spotted.
4. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** Remove; immediately seal in a bowl covered tightly with cling film. Steam 15 minutes.
5. Peel off the charred skin (let it slip off over a bowl so all the juice is captured — Hazan stresses: "The juice is precious — do not lose a drop"). Deseed; cut into wide strips.
6. Combine the strips with their captured juice, olive oil, sliced garlic and anchovies (if using). Allow to marinate at room temperature 30 minutes before serving. Season with salt and scatter parsley.
7. Final serving temperature: **room temperature** (the true Hazan instruction — never cold, never reheated). Flavour improves after several hours.
