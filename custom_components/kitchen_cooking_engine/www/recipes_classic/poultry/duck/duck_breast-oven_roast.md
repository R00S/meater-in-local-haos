## Cut profile

Duck breast is a dark, rich cut with a thick layer of subcutaneous fat beneath a scored skin. Unlike chicken breast, duck breast is best cooked to medium-rare (135–145 °F / 57–63 °C), where the meat remains pink, juicy, and tender. The oven-roast method typically follows a stovetop fat-rendering sear: the breast starts skin-side down in a cold or lightly warmed oven-safe pan to slowly render the fat cap, then transfers to a moderate oven to finish gently and evenly without overshooting the internal temperature.

Target internal temperature: **135–145 °F (57–63 °C)** — medium-rare, pull at 130 °F and rest; carryover brings it to target. For well-done (USDA), pull at 160 °F for a final resting temp of 165 °F.

## Source recipes

### 1. BBC Good Food — Slow-Roast Duck (adapted for breast)
**Source**: BBC Good Food editorial team, BBC Good Food; https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/slow-roast-duck (fetched 2025-07-15).
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 13 hr (whole duck — breast adaptation below) · **Rest**: 15 min

> **Note**: The BBC recipe is for a whole slow-roast duck (Czech-style, from Apetit Online). The fat-rendering
> philosophy — low heat, patience, avoid basting in excess fat — applies directly to duck breast oven finishing.
> Adaptation for breast: stovetop-render the fat cap, then oven-finish at moderate temperature.

**Temperature guidance**:
- Slow-roast oven temp (whole): 90 °C / 194 °F, then raised to 120 °C / 248 °F
- For breast adaptation: stovetop render at medium-low, then oven at 350 °F (177 °C)
- Internal target: 135–145 °F (57–63 °C) medium-rare
- Time (breast only): approximately 8–12 minutes in oven after stovetop render

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on duck breasts (6–8 oz / 170–225 g each)
- Flaky sea salt (generous — salt draws moisture and helps crisp skin)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- ½ tsp ground cumin (optional, echoes the Czech seasoning tradition)
- 1 tsp honey (for glaze in final 2 minutes, optional)

**Method**
1. Remove duck breasts from refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking.
2. Pat thoroughly dry with paper towels — moisture is the enemy of crispy skin.
3. Score the skin in a diagonal cross-hatch pattern every ½ inch, cutting through fat but not into the meat. This is critical for fat rendering.
4. Season generously on both sides with salt and pepper. Apply cumin if using.
5. Place breasts skin-side down in a cold, heavy oven-safe skillet (cast iron preferred). Do not preheat the pan.
6. Set burner to medium-low. The cold start allows fat to render gradually without burning skin. Cook 10–14 minutes, pouring off excess rendered fat periodically.
7. Meanwhile, preheat oven to 350 °F (177 °C).
8. When skin is deep golden and most fat has rendered, flip breasts to flesh side for 2 minutes.
9. Transfer skillet to oven, skin-side up. Roast 8–12 minutes until internal temp reaches 130–132 °F (pull point).
10. Optional: brush skin with honey in the last 2 minutes for a lacquered glaze.
11. Transfer to cutting board, tent loosely with foil, rest 5–8 minutes. Internal temp will rise to 135–140 °F during rest.
12. Slice across the grain at a slight angle. Serve immediately.

### 2. Classic Culinary Technique — Cold-Pan Duck Breast (standard restaurant method)
**Source**: Documented culinary technique, widely used in professional kitchens; general knowledge synthesis.
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 5–8 min

**Temperature guidance**:
- Stovetop render: medium-low heat, 12–15 min skin-side
- Oven temp (optional finish): 350–375 °F (177–190 °C)
- Internal target medium-rare: 135–140 °F (57–60 °C)
- Internal target well-done: 165 °F (74 °C)
- Time: 8–12 min oven after render (medium-rare); 15–18 min (well-done)

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on, boneless duck breasts (Moulard or Pekin), 6–9 oz each
- Kosher salt
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter (for basting, optional)
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- 1 clove garlic, smashed

**Method**
1. Dry-brine option: salt breasts uncovered in refrigerator 1–24 hours before cooking for deeper seasoning and drier skin.
2. Score skin in 1-inch diagonal cross-hatch, ¼ inch deep, without piercing the meat.
3. Season both sides with salt and pepper.
4. Place skin-side down in cold, dry, heavy oven-safe skillet. Set to medium-low.
5. As fat begins to render (3–4 minutes), keep heat consistent. Do not move or press the breasts.
6. Pour off rendered fat into a heatproof container (save — duck fat is prized for cooking potatoes).
7. Continue rendering 10–15 min total until skin is uniformly golden, crispy, and thin.
8. Preheat oven to 375 °F (190 °C) during this step.
9. Flip breasts. Add butter, thyme, garlic. Baste flesh side for 2 minutes.
10. Slide skillet into oven, skin-side up. Roast 8–10 min for medium-rare (target 130 °F pull point).
11. Rest on wire rack over a plate, 5–8 minutes. Final temp: 135–142 °F.
12. Slice thin (¼ inch) against the grain. Fan on plate. Serve with pan jus or fruit sauce.

### 3. Xiachufang (下厨房) — 低温慢烤法式鸭胸肉 (Chinese Low-Temperature Slow-Roasted French Duck Breast)
**Source**: User recipe on Xiachufang.com (China's largest recipe-sharing platform, comparable to Allrecipes); https://www.xiachufang.com/recipe/102126154/ (fetched 2026-04-24, language: Chinese — ingredients and method translated below).
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 10 min oven after sear · **Rest**: 5 min

**Temperature guidance**:
- Sear (cold pan start): medium-low; skin side down until fat fully renders and skin is golden
- Oven: 160 °C (320 °F) after sear — low temperature preserves moisture
- Internal target: pink centre (6成熟 = ~60% done = medium) ~62 °C (144 °F); serve with balsamic/black vinegar
- Time: 10 min oven; rest 5 min before slicing

**Ingredients** *(translated from Chinese)*
- 500 g French duck breast (skin on), e.g. Moulard or Barbary
- 2 sprigs fresh rosemary
- Himalayan pink salt, to taste
- Ground black pepper, to taste
- Black vinegar (镇江香醋) for dipping
- *Side vegetables (optional)*: 50 g oyster mushrooms, 50 g cherry tomatoes, 50 g asparagus tips, small radish, lettuce

**Method** *(translated from Chinese)*
1. Score the duck skin in a crosshatch pattern; pat breast dry with paper towels.
2. Grind a small amount of black pepper onto the flesh side; press rosemary sprigs onto the breast and massage in. Let sit 10 minutes.
3. Add salt; massage again and rest a further 20 minutes at room temperature.
4. Heat a dry (no added fat) skillet over medium-low; place breast skin-side down. The duck will release its own fat.
5. Cook skin-side down, rendering fat slowly, until skin is deep golden-brown and crisp; then flip and cook flesh side to about 60% done (surface turns opaque).
6. Transfer breast and vegetables (mushrooms, tomatoes, asparagus) to a foil-lined roasting tray; drizzle the rendered duck fat over everything. Add a rosemary sprig on top.
7. Roast at 160 °C (320 °F) top-and-bottom heat for 10 minutes.
8. Rest on a board 5 minutes; slice against the grain. Interior should be rosy pink.
9. Plate over green leaves; serve with black vinegar as dipping sauce.

*Tip*: Always apply other seasonings before salt — they penetrate better. The fat layer on duck breast is thick; the crosshatch scoring and cold-pan start allow it to render fully without burning the skin.

**Recommended MEATER settings**:
- **Medium-rare** (chef-preferred): Set alert at **130 °F (54 °C)** → pull from oven, tent, rest 5–8 min → carryover brings to ~138 °F (59 °C)
- **Well-done** (USDA safe): Set alert at **160 °F (71 °C)** → pull and rest → carryover brings to 165 °F (74 °C)
- Rest 5–8 minutes minimum
- Note: Duck breast carryover is typically 5–8 °F; account for this when setting the alert

## Notes

- Always start with a cold pan for duck breast — this is the single most important technique difference from other poultry.
- Do not use high heat during rendering; rushing causes burnt skin and an undercooked fat layer.
- The thick fat cap (often ¼–½ inch on Moulard breeds) must be fully rendered or it will be soft and unpleasant.
- Duck fat rendered off is excellent for roasting potatoes; store in a jar in the refrigerator up to 1 month.
- Resting is non-negotiable — slicing immediately causes all juices to run out.
