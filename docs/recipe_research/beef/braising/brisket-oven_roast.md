# Beef Brisket × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Beef brisket is taken from the breast and lower chest of the animal, comprising two muscles — the flat (*pectoralis profundus*) and the point (*pectoralis superficialis*) — that support much of the animal's weight. The cut is dense with collagen-rich connective tissue and interspersed fat, making it exceptionally tough when cooked quickly but extraordinarily silky and tender once held at the collagen-conversion threshold long enough for connective tissue to dissolve into gelatin. Oven roasting at low temperature replicates the gentle, prolonged dry heat that the cut demands — the enclosed oven environment retains moisture driven off from the meat, preventing the surface from desiccating during the long cook. Carryover is negligible for brisket because the high moisture content and dense mass absorb and retain heat slowly; the internal temperature barely moves after the brisket is removed from the oven.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Oven-Braised Brisket
**Source**: https://www.seriouseats.com/braised-brisket-recipe  
**Serves**: 8–10 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight dry-brine if time allows) · **Cook**: 6–8 hr · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole packer brisket, 4–5 kg (or flat only, 2.5–3 kg), trimmed to 6 mm fat cap
- 30 g kosher salt
- 15 g freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil
- 2 large onions, roughly chopped
- 4 cloves garlic, smashed
- 2 medium carrots, roughly chopped
- 2 stalks celery, roughly chopped
- 240 ml beef stock
- 240 ml dry red wine

**Method**
1. Season brisket all over with salt and pepper. If time allows, refrigerate uncovered on a rack overnight to dry-brine.
2. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F). Heat oil in a large roasting pan over high heat on the stovetop.
3. Sear the brisket fat-side-down for 5–7 minutes until deep golden brown. Flip and sear the lean side 3–4 minutes.
4. Remove brisket to a plate. Add onions, garlic, carrots, and celery to the pan; cook 5 minutes, scraping up browned bits.
5. Add stock and wine. Return brisket fat-side-up. The liquid should reach no higher than one-third up the sides of the meat — this is a roast, not a submerged braise.
6. Insert the MEATER probe into the geometric centre of the thickest portion of the flat, parallel to the grain, avoiding fat seams. The flat is the reference because it is the leaner and therefore more thermally challenging muscle.
7. Cover tightly with foil, then the roasting pan lid if available. Transfer to the oven.
8. Roast until the probe reads 88 °C, approximately 5–6 hours. Remove the foil, raise heat to 200 °C, and continue roasting a further 30–45 minutes to caramelise the fat cap.
9. **Pull at 90–93 °C** (195–200 °F). The brisket is done when a probe slides through the flat with almost no resistance, similar to probing warm butter.
10. Rest, covered loosely with foil, for at least 30 minutes before slicing. Final serving temperature: **90–93 °C / 195–200 °F** (collagen-converted, well-done).

### 2. Joan Nathan — Ashkenazi Passover Brisket
**Source**: Joan Nathan, *Jewish Cooking in America* (Knopf, 1994), pp. 148–149; also published at nytimes.com/recipes  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 3–4 hr · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kg beef brisket (flat preferred), fat cap intact
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil
- 3 large onions, sliced into rings
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 240 ml dry red wine (kosher for Passover)
- 120 ml beef or chicken broth
- 2 tbsp tomato paste
- 1 tsp paprika
- 2 bay leaves

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 165 °C (325 °F). Season brisket on all sides with salt and pepper.
2. Heat oil in a large Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Brown the brisket well on both sides, 5–6 minutes per side. Remove to a plate.
3. Add onions to the pot; cook over medium heat, stirring, until golden and soft, 15 minutes. Add garlic and cook 2 minutes.
4. Stir in tomato paste, paprika, wine, broth, and bay leaves. Scrape the bottom to deglaze.
5. Return brisket to the pot fat-side-up. The liquid should come halfway up the side. Cover tightly.
6. Place in the oven. After 2 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the flat from the end of the brisket, parallel to the flat's surface, avoiding the fat layer running between the flat and the point.
7. Continue roasting until the probe reads close to target. For this Ashkenazi style, the goal is not falling-apart tender but sliceable: the brisket should yield to a knife but hold its shape in thin slices.
8. **Pull at 88–90 °C** (190–195 °F). Slices hold together but offer almost no resistance.
9. Rest 20 minutes. Skim fat from braising liquid; reduce on stovetop if thin. Slice across the grain. Final serving temperature: **88–90 °C / 190–195 °F** (sliceable-tender).

### 3. Felicity Cloake — Slow-Roast Brisket (British tradition)
**Source**: Felicity Cloake, "How to cook the perfect brisket", *The Guardian*, theguardian.com/food  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 4–5 hr · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.8 kg rolled and tied brisket
- 1 tbsp plain flour
- 1 tsp mustard powder
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- 1 tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp beef dripping or lard
- 3 medium onions, thickly sliced
- 4 medium carrots, cut into large chunks
- 400 ml beef stock
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 bay leaves

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 160 °C / 140 °C fan (320 °F). Mix flour, mustard powder, paprika, salt, and pepper. Rub all over the brisket.
2. Melt dripping in a heavy flameproof casserole over high heat. Brown the brisket on all sides — this takes 8–10 minutes; do not rush. The crust formed here is the flavour foundation.
3. Remove brisket. Reduce heat to medium. Add onions and carrots; cook until beginning to soften and colour, about 8 minutes.
4. Pour in stock. Tuck in thyme and bay leaves. Return brisket on top of the vegetables fat-side-up. Cover with a tight-fitting lid or double layer of foil.
5. Transfer to the oven. Roast for 3 hours undisturbed.
6. After 3 hours, remove the lid or foil and insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the tied roll from the end, directing it along the roll's axis to reach the geometric centre, avoiding string.
7. Continue roasting, covered, until the probe temperature stabilises near target.
8. **Pull at 90–92 °C** (194–198 °F). British slow-roast brisket should be very tender but still sliceable — *not* pulled-beef texture.
9. Remove brisket to a warm serving platter. Strain the braising juices; skim fat; reduce by one-third on the stovetop to make a natural gravy. Rest brisket 30 minutes before untying and slicing. Final serving temperature: **90–92 °C / 194–198 °F**.

### 4. Maangchi — *Kkori-gomtang* Inspired Oven-Braised Brisket (Korean tradition)
**Source**: Maangchi, *Maangchi's Real Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2015), pp. 280–282; maangchi.com/recipe/  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 hr soaking) · **Cook**: 3.5–4 hr · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg beef brisket, cut into 5 cm blocks
- 1.5 litres cold water (for soaking to remove blood)
- 1.2 litres fresh cold water (for braising)
- 6 garlic cloves, whole
- 1 medium onion, halved
- 3 cm piece fresh ginger, peeled and sliced
- 2 spring onions, cut into 5 cm lengths
- 1½ tsp fine sea salt, plus more to taste
- 1 tsp ground black pepper
- To serve: sliced spring onion, julienned ginger, sea salt, sesame oil

**Method**
1. Soak brisket pieces in cold water for 1 hour to draw out blood and impurities. Drain and rinse.
2. Preheat oven to 160 °C (320 °F). Place brisket blocks, garlic, onion, ginger, and spring onions in a heavy Dutch oven. Add 1.2 litres fresh water — just enough to cover.
3. Bring to a simmer on the stovetop over high heat. Skim foam and grey impurities from the surface for 5 minutes, until the broth runs clear.
4. Add salt and pepper. Cover tightly with a lid.
5. Transfer to the oven. Braise 3–3.5 hours. The goal is a clear, clean broth and tender meat, not a rich sauce — the Korean aesthetic is clarity and delicacy.
6. At the 2.5-hour mark, insert the MEATER probe into the largest block of brisket, entering from the side to reach the centre, avoiding grain direction.
7. **Pull at 88–91 °C** (190–196 °F). Korean-style oven-braised brisket should be tender enough to pull apart with chopsticks but retain distinct fibrous texture — not mushy.
8. Rest 15 minutes in the broth with the lid on, allowing the meat to reabsorb some liquid. Final serving temperature: **88–91 °C / 190–196 °F**.
9. Serve in the broth with condiments on the side: thinly sliced spring onion, fine julienned ginger, coarse sea salt, and a few drops of sesame oil to each bowl.

### 5. Yotam Ottolenghi — Middle Eastern Oven-Braised Brisket
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012), pp. 192–194; ottolenghi.co.uk  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 25 min (+2 hr marinating) · **Cook**: 4–5 hr · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kg beef brisket (flat), trimmed
- 4 cloves garlic, finely sliced
- 2 tsp ground cinnamon
- 1 tsp ground allspice
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp ground ginger
- 1 tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 2 large onions, cut into wedges
- 4 plum tomatoes, quartered
- 240 ml pomegranate juice
- 240 ml beef stock
- 1 tbsp pomegranate molasses
- Small bunch of fresh coriander, to finish

**Method**
1. Mix garlic slices, all spices, salt, pepper, and 1 tbsp olive oil into a paste. Rub all over the brisket. Cover and refrigerate for 2 hours or overnight.
2. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F). Heat remaining oil in a large, heavy flameproof casserole over high heat.
3. Brown the brisket on both sides, 5 minutes per side, until deep brown. Remove.
4. Add onions to the casserole; cook 5 minutes. Add tomatoes; cook 2 minutes.
5. Pour in pomegranate juice, stock, and molasses. Stir to combine, scraping up all browned bits.
6. Return brisket fat-side-up. Cover tightly with foil, then the lid.
7. Transfer to the oven. After 2.5 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the flat from the end, angled to reach the geometric centre, clear of any visible fat seams.
8. Continue roasting until the probe reads near target. The pomegranate and spice profile is assertive — the low, gentle heat allows the flavours to penetrate over the full cooking time.
9. **Pull at 89–92 °C** (192–198 °F). The brisket should pull apart easily but not collapse into shreds.
10. Rest 20 minutes. Remove brisket; strain and reduce the sauce until lightly syrupy. Scatter fresh coriander over the sliced brisket. Final serving temperature: **89–92 °C / 192–198 °F**.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Serious Eats (Kenji) | Well-done / collagen-converted | 90–93 °C (195–200 °F) |
| Jewish American (Joan Nathan) | Sliceable-tender | 88–90 °C (190–195 °F) |
| British (Felicity Cloake) | Slow-roast tender | 90–92 °C (194–198 °F) |
| Korean (Maangchi) | Chopstick-tender | 88–91 °C (190–196 °F) |
| Middle Eastern (Ottolenghi) | Pull-apart tender | 89–92 °C (192–198 °F) |

The observed range across all five traditions is **88–93 °C (190–200 °F)**. All traditions sit firmly in the `well_done` band (≥77 °C in the cooking_data.py doneness schema) — there is no meaningful disagreement about the endpoint; the variation of 5 °C reflects textural preference between sliceable (lower end, ~88–90 °C) and pull-apart (upper end, ~91–93 °C), rather than cultural divergence on doneness philosophy. The collagen conversion threshold driving this band is physics, not tradition: type I collagen in connective tissue begins to solubilise above ~70 °C but requires sustained time at 85–95 °C to fully convert to gelatin. `recommended_doneness` is `well_done` across all sources; no tradition cooks brisket to anything less.

## What makes this method special
Oven roasting envelops the brisket in consistent, ambient dry heat, which extracts moisture slowly and concentrates the collagen-to-gelatin conversion across the full mass of the cut. Unlike stovetop braising, where the bottom of the meat touches the heat source directly, oven heat attacks from all sides simultaneously, producing more even collagen conversion through the flat and point. The low oven temperature (140–165 °C) keeps the surface from desiccating or forming a rigid crust before the interior reaches the collagen-conversion window — a covered vessel traps steam from the meat itself, creating a self-basting microenvironment without requiring large quantities of braising liquid. The result is a deeply flavoured, gelatinous, sliceable or pull-apart texture that neither a pressure cooker (too fast, less flavour development) nor a slow cooker (insufficient browning opportunity) replicates exactly.

## Summary
Beef brisket is one of the most collagen-dense cuts in the animal; oven roasting at 150–165 °C over 3–8 hours converts that collagen to gelatin, transforming a notoriously tough muscle into something silky and sliceable. Every culinary tradition that uses brisket — American, Ashkenazi Jewish, British, Korean, and Middle Eastern — lands in a narrow 88–93 °C window, confirming that the physics of collagen conversion, not cultural preference, defines the endpoint. The variation within that window (sliceable at 88–90 °C vs. pull-apart at 91–93 °C) is a textural choice, not a doneness disagreement.
