# Chuck Roast × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Chuck roast is cut from the shoulder region of the animal (the chuck primal, roughly ribs 1–5 and the neck), and is one of the most collagen-dense roasting cuts in the carcass. The shoulder muscles endure constant movement throughout the animal's life, producing densely bundled muscle fibres interlaced with thick seams of connective tissue and fat — a structure that is aggressively tough at low internal temperatures but transforms into richly flavoured, pull-apart tenderness when braised gently over several hours. Braising is particularly suited to chuck because the submerging or partial immersion in liquid accelerates the hydrolysis of collagen into gelatin, producing a self-thickening braising sauce and a meltingly tender interior simultaneously. Carryover is negligible for chuck in a braise because the surrounding liquid buffers against temperature swings; the meat stays at cooking temperature until removed.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Braised Chuck Roast (Pot Roast)
**Source**: https://www.seriouseats.com/the-best-pot-roast-recipe  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 25 min · **Cook**: 3.5–4.5 hr · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5–2 kg boneless chuck roast, tied if irregular
- 30 g kosher salt
- 10 g freshly ground black pepper
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil
- 1 large onion, roughly chopped
- 2 medium carrots, roughly chopped
- 2 stalks celery, roughly chopped
- 6 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 tbsp tomato paste
- 480 ml dry red wine
- 480 ml beef stock
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 150 °C (300 °F). Pat chuck dry and season all over with salt and pepper.
2. Heat oil in a Dutch oven over high heat until smoking. Sear chuck on all sides, 4–5 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Remove to a plate.
3. Reduce heat to medium. Add onion, carrots, and celery; cook 5–7 minutes until softened and lightly browned. Add garlic and tomato paste; cook 2 minutes.
4. Deglaze with red wine, scraping up all browned bits. Bring to a boil and reduce by half, about 5 minutes.
5. Add stock and bring to a simmer. Return chuck to the pot. The liquid should reach two-thirds up the sides of the roast.
6. Nestle thyme, bay leaves, and rosemary around the roast. Cover and transfer to the oven.
7. After 2.5 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the geometric centre of the thickest portion of the chuck, entering from the side, clear of any seams of fat or gristle. The chuck has internal fat seams — probe through muscle tissue only.
8. Continue braising until the probe reads near target. Test with a fork: the chuck should offer no resistance when twisted — fibres should separate easily.
9. **Pull at 93–96 °C** (200–205 °F). "Fork-tender" in this context means the fork meets no resistance anywhere in the muscle.
10. Rest 20 minutes loosely covered before slicing or pulling. Strain and reduce braising liquid to serve as sauce. Final serving temperature: **93–96 °C / 200–205 °F** (fully collagen-converted, fork-tender).

### 2. Julia Child — Bœuf Bourguignon (French tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961), pp. 315–319  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 3–4 hr · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.4 kg braising beef (chuck cut into 5 cm cubes), patted very dry
- 170 g lardons (thick-cut bacon, cut into batons)
- 3 tbsp olive oil or cooking oil
- 1 large carrot, sliced
- 1 large onion, sliced
- 1 tsp salt, ½ tsp pepper
- 2 tbsp flour
- 720 ml full-bodied red Burgundy wine (e.g. Côtes du Rhône)
- 480 ml beef stock
- 1 tbsp tomato paste
- 2 cloves garlic, mashed
- ½ tsp thyme
- 1 bay leaf
- 18 small white onions (glazed separately in butter and stock)
- 450 g quartered fresh mushrooms, sautéed in butter

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 165 °C (325 °F). In a large heavy casserole, blanch the lardons in water 10 minutes, then dry and sauté in 1 tbsp oil until lightly browned. Remove.
2. Sauté the dry beef cubes in the same fat, a few pieces at a time — do not crowd. Brown all sides, 4–5 minutes per batch. The dryness of the beef is critical: wet beef steams; dry beef browns.
3. Return all beef and lardons to the casserole. Season with salt and pepper. Toss with flour and bake 4 minutes uncovered, tossing once, to lightly toast the flour coating.
4. Remove from oven. Reduce heat to 165 °C. Add wine, stock, tomato paste, garlic, and herbs. Liquid should barely cover the meat.
5. Bring to a simmer on the stovetop. Cover tightly and return to the oven.
6. After 1.5 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the largest beef cube, entering from the side and reaching the centre. Avoid fat between chunks.
7. Continue braising until fork-tender. The beef is done when a fork inserted and twisted meets no resistance.
8. **Pull at 88–93 °C** (190–200 °F). French bourguignon targets tender but still cohesive cubes — not completely falling apart.
9. Rest 15 minutes. Add the glazed onions and sautéed mushrooms. Remove bay leaf and thyme sprigs. Final serving temperature: **88–93 °C / 190–200 °F**.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Italian-Style Pot Roast (*Stracotto di manzo*)
**Source**: Nigella Lawson, *Nigella Christmas* (Chatto & Windus, 2008), pp. 202–204; nigella.com/recipes/  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 4–5 hr · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kg chuck roast (or bolar blade), tied into a neat cylinder
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 2 medium onions, finely chopped
- 4 garlic cloves, finely sliced
- 2 sticks celery, finely chopped
- 2 medium carrots, finely chopped
- 240 ml red wine
- 400 g tin chopped tomatoes
- 240 ml beef stock
- 2 tsp dried mixed herbs (thyme, rosemary, sage)
- 1 tsp fine salt
- Freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 150 °C (130 °C fan / 300 °F). Season the tied chuck all over with salt and pepper.
2. Heat olive oil in a large heavy casserole over medium-high heat. Brown the chuck on all sides — 10–12 minutes total. Remove.
3. In the same oil, cook onions over medium heat until very soft and golden, 10 minutes. Add garlic, celery, and carrots; cook 5 minutes.
4. Pour in wine; let it bubble and reduce by half.
5. Add tomatoes, stock, and herbs. Stir. Return the chuck to the pot. The liquid should come at least halfway up the sides.
6. Bring to a simmer. Cover tightly and transfer to the oven.
7. After 2.5 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the tied roast from one end, running along the axis to reach the geometric centre, avoiding string.
8. Continue braising. This *stracotto* ("over-cooked" in Italian) aims for deeply yielding texture throughout.
9. **Pull at 91–95 °C** (196–203 °F). The chuck should be tender enough to shred easily with two forks.
10. Rest 30 minutes. Untie and shred or slice. Reduce braising sauce if desired. Final serving temperature: **91–95 °C / 196–203 °F**.

### 4. Maangchi — Korean Braised Beef (*Jangjorim*)
**Source**: Maangchi, *Maangchi's Big Book of Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2019), pp. 190–192; maangchi.com/recipe/jangjorim  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min soaking) · **Cook**: 2–3 hr · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g beef chuck (or brisket), cut into 5 cm pieces
- 960 ml cold water, for soaking
- 960 ml fresh cold water, for braising
- 6 garlic cloves, whole
- 3 dried red chillies
- 120 ml soy sauce
- 2 tbsp sugar (or 3 tbsp honey)
- 6 hard-boiled eggs, peeled (optional)
- 3 green chillies, whole (optional, added in last 20 minutes)

**Method**
1. Soak beef pieces in 960 ml cold water for 30 minutes to draw out blood and impurities. Drain and rinse.
2. Place beef in a wide saucepan with 960 ml fresh cold water. Bring to a boil over high heat. Boil 10 minutes, skimming off grey foam. Drain and rinse beef under cold water.
3. Return cleaned beef to the pot. Add garlic and dried chillies. Cover with enough fresh water to submerge.
4. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook 1 hour.
5. Insert the MEATER probe into the largest beef piece from the side, reaching the centre.
6. Add soy sauce and sugar. Stir gently to combine. Continue simmering uncovered, turning meat occasionally, until the braising liquid reduces to a sticky glaze and the beef is tender.
7. **Pull at 88–92 °C** (190–198 °F). *Jangjorim* beef should be firm enough to pull apart in strands along the grain, not crumbled — the texture should yield in long, fibrous pieces.
8. Add hard-boiled eggs and green chillies in the last 20 minutes of cooking if using.
9. Rest 10 minutes in the sauce. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F**. Serve at room temperature or chilled — *jangjorim* is a banchan (side dish) often eaten cold.

### 5. Judith Jones / Craig Claiborne — Daube de Bœuf Provençale (Provençal tradition)
**Source**: Craig Claiborne, *The New York Times Cookbook* (Harper & Row, 1961), pp. 204–205; adapted in Judith Jones, *The Pleasures of Cooking for One* (Knopf, 2009)  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 30 min (+overnight marinating) · **Cook**: 3–4 hr · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.8 kg beef chuck, cut into 6 cm pieces
- 480 ml robust red wine (Côtes du Rhône, Grenache)
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 2 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp fresh thyme
- 1 bay leaf
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 100 g pancetta or lardons
- 1 large onion, coarsely chopped
- 1 cup black olives (*olives de Provence*), stoned
- 2 tbsp capers
- Zest of 1 orange
- 240 ml beef stock or water
- 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Marinate beef overnight in wine, 1 tbsp olive oil, garlic, thyme, bay leaf, salt, and pepper. Refrigerate covered.
2. Next day, preheat oven to 160 °C (320 °F). Remove beef from marinade (reserve marinade). Pat dry thoroughly.
3. Heat remaining olive oil in a heavy Dutch oven. Sauté pancetta until its fat renders. Add beef in batches and brown all sides.
4. Add onion; cook until softened. Pour in marinade and stock. Bring to a boil, scraping up the browned bits.
5. Add olives, capers, and orange zest. Stir to combine.
6. Cover tightly. Transfer to the oven and braise undisturbed for 2 hours.
7. After 2 hours, insert the MEATER probe into the largest beef chunk from the side, reaching the centre without hitting the bone or gristle pockets.
8. Continue braising until the probe reads close to target. The Provençal *daube* traditionally achieves a richer, deeper result than Northern French braises because of the orange, olive, and caper profile — the long cook allows these aromatics to penetrate the beef.
9. **Pull at 90–93 °C** (194–200 °F). Cubes should hold their shape but yield to gentle pressure with a spoon.
10. Rest 20 minutes. Sprinkle parsley over the finished dish. Final serving temperature: **90–93 °C / 194–200 °F**.

The observed range is **88–96 °C (190–205 °F)**. The 8 °C spread within that band reflects textural intent: American pot roast aims for maximum tenderness (96 °C, approaching pulled-beef texture), while French and Korean traditions target a slightly lower endpoint where the meat remains cohesive enough to serve in identifiable pieces. The `recommended_doneness` key `well_done` covers the full range; the Korean *jangjorim* at 88–92 °C sits at the lower edge but is still well within the collagen-converted regime.

## What makes this method special
Braising submerges or partially immerses the chuck in liquid throughout the cook, keeping the surrounding temperature exactly at the boiling point of water (~100 °C) regardless of oven temperature — making it almost impossible to overcook the exterior while waiting for the interior to reach target, unlike dry-heat methods. The liquid carries dissolved gelatin and fat droplets continuously past the meat's surface, basting it internally and building the sauce simultaneously; no dry method produces this self-thickening effect. The Maillard crust formed in the initial sear contributes a depth of flavour that survives dissolution into the braise liquid, emerging as the umami backbone of the finished sauce. Braising also suppresses the Maillard reaction on the meat's surface during the long cook (steam and liquid prevent browning), so all the flavour formed at the start is preserved rather than driven off.

## Summary
Chuck roast is the quintessential braising cut — its extraordinary collagen content, which makes it unchewably tough when roasted quickly, becomes its greatest asset when submerged in liquid and held at high internal temperature for hours. Every major culinary tradition that uses chuck arrives at the same 88–96 °C destination, varying only in whether the final texture should be spoonable, strand-pullable, or fully shredded. Braising is uniquely suited to this cut because the liquid environment solves the dry-heat problem of achieving uniform heat penetration through a dense, fat-seamed muscle.
