# Beef Burger × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Ground beef burgers are formed patties of minced beef, most commonly from a blend of chuck (for flavour) and sirloin or brisket (for binding), with fat content typically 15–25 % by weight. Unlike whole-muscle cuts, a burger patty has no internal connective tissue structure — the grind has already disrupted muscle fibres — so the cooking challenge is heat penetration through the patty's cross-section rather than collagen conversion. The direct-heat grill creates a fierce Maillard crust on both flat faces, which is the primary flavour mechanism; the fat content keeps the interior moist even at higher internal temperatures. Carryover in a burger patty is minimal — the thin geometry dissipates heat quickly once removed from the grill — but genuine carryover occurs in thicker restaurant-style patties, making the pull-from-grill moment slightly earlier than the final target.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Smash Burger / Classic Grilled Burger
**Source**: https://www.seriouseats.com/the-burger-lab-how-to-make-perfect-thin-and-crispy-fast-food-style-burgers; also *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 382–395  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min resting after forming) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g ground chuck (80/20 fat ratio), freshly ground preferred
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 4 slices American or cheddar cheese (optional)
- 4 burger buns, split and toasted

**Method**
1. Gently divide beef into four 175 g portions without over-compacting. Form into loose balls, then flatten into 2 cm patties, pressing a slight indentation in the centre (prevents doming during cooking). Season both sides with salt and pepper immediately before grilling.
2. Preheat gas or charcoal grill to maximum heat (direct high heat, 230–260 °C grate temperature). Clean and oil the grates.
3. Place patties on the hottest part of the grill. Do not press down with a spatula — this squeezes out the juices.
4. Cook 3–4 minutes on the first side without moving. The patty is ready to flip when it releases naturally from the grate with no tearing.
5. Insert the MEATER probe into the patty from the side at the midpoint of the patty's thickness, ensuring the probe tip reaches the geometric centre. The probe must be level with the surface — enter from the edge, not the top.
6. Flip once. Add cheese if using. Cook a further 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 57–60 °C** (135–140 °F) for medium. The patty will rise 2–3 °C during the brief rest.
8. Rest on the toasted bun for 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (medium, pink centre with juicy texture).

### 2. Fergus Henderson — Classic Grilled Beef Patty (British tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson & Trevor Gulliver, *The Whole Beast: Nose to Tail Eating* (Ecco, 2004), pp. 47–49; stjohnrestaurant.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+1 hr chilling) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g beef mince — equal parts shin and rump, or short rib and brisket (ask your butcher to mince twice through a medium plate)
- 1 small shallot, very finely minced (raw, added to the mix)
- 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp Dijon mustard
- 1 tsp fine salt
- Generous grinding of black pepper
- 4 soft white rolls, lightly toasted

**Method**
1. Mix beef gently with shallot, Worcestershire, mustard, salt, and pepper. Do not over-work — mix until the ingredients are just combined. Over-working toughens the patty.
2. Form into four patties, each about 2.5 cm thick. Place on a tray, cover, and refrigerate 1 hour to help them hold their shape on the grill.
3. Heat a charcoal grill (or gas grill) to high. Clean and oil the grate.
4. Grill the patties over direct high heat. Cook 4 minutes without moving on the first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge of the patty, horizontally to the midpoint of the patty's height, ensuring the tip sits in the thickest mass of meat — not near the surface.
6. Flip. Cook a further 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 60–63 °C** (140–145 °F). British beef patty convention favours a pink, juicy centre without a fully red core.
8. Rest 3 minutes on the bun. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (medium, pink throughout with no raw centre).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Ultimate Burger (British modern tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011), pp. 78–83; hestonblumenthal.net  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+2 hr salting + 30 min freezing) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g beef (55% chuck, 45% short rib, coarsely ground once)
- 10 g fine salt (applied 2 hr ahead)
- 4 g freshly ground black pepper
- Neutral oil for grill

**Method**
1. Salt the ground beef 2 hours before cooking. This draws out proteins that improve binding. Form into four patties, 3 cm thick. Freeze on a tray for 30 minutes (Blumenthal's technique for grill stability — the frozen exterior gives the grill marks time to form before the patty cooks through).
2. Preheat grill to high. Oil the grate.
3. Place frozen-exterior patties directly on the grill. The cold exterior delays internal cooking, allowing maximum exterior Maillard reaction time.
4. Cook 5–6 minutes on the first side. Do not move.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side through the thickest edge into the geometric centre. The patty is now partially frozen at the centre — the probe tip must reach the uncooked mass.
6. Flip once. Cook 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 60–63 °C** (140–145 °F). Blumenthal recommends medium as the target for texture and flavour balance.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **63–66 °C / 145–151 °F** (medium, with the Maillard crust softened slightly during the rest).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Wagyu Burger on Charcoal (Japanese-Californian tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu the Cookbook* (Kodansha, 2001), pp. 134–136; noburestaurants.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g Wagyu ground beef (or Wagyu/Angus blend, min. 25% fat by weight)
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- ½ tsp toasted sesame oil
- 4 g fine sea salt
- Freshly ground black or white pepper
- Yuzu kosho (fermented chilli-yuzu paste), to serve
- 4 brioche buns, lightly grilled

**Method**
1. Gently mix Wagyu beef with soy sauce and sesame oil. Season with salt and pepper. Form into four patties, approximately 2 cm thick. Chill 15 minutes.
2. Heat a charcoal grill (*binchotan* charcoal preferred for consistent, radiant heat) to high. Place patties on the grill over direct heat.
3. Cook 2.5–3 minutes on the first side. The high fat content of Wagyu causes significant flare-ups — keep the patties moving if flames are excessive, or move them to the cooler side briefly.
4. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the edge of the patty into the centre, clear of the surface.
5. Flip. Cook 2–3 minutes on the second side.
6. **Pull at 54–57 °C** (129–135 °F). Nobu's approach to Wagyu honours the fat: medium-rare, so the intra-muscular fat remains liquid and coats the palate.
7. Rest 2 minutes. Spread a small amount of yuzu kosho on the bun before adding the patty. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (medium-rare, red/pink warm centre, fat fully liquefied).

### 5. Francis Mallmann — Gaucho-Style Grilled Burger (*hambúrguer gaucho*)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan, 2009), pp. 186–188  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min over wood embers · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g beef mince (a rough blend of *asado* (short rib) and *nalga* (topside), minced coarsely once)
- Coarse sea salt (*sal parrillera*) — the only seasoning, applied at the last moment before grilling
- 4 slices provolone cheese, to finish
- Chimichurri (parsley, garlic, red wine vinegar, chilli, olive oil), to serve

**Method**
1. Form beef into four patties, 2.5–3 cm thick, handling as minimally as possible. No mixing agents, no binders. The philosophy: *la carne habla por sí sola* (the meat speaks for itself).
2. Season with coarse salt on both faces immediately before placing on the grill. Season last — not first — to prevent moisture extraction.
3. Build a wood fire (*quebracho* hardwood for best flavour) and let it burn down to white-hot embers. The grill should sit 15–20 cm above the embers — this is a steady, deep heat, not a flame.
4. Place patties on the grill. Cook 5–6 minutes on the first side without touching. Argentine grilling virtue is patience.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge of the patty, entering horizontally to the centre.
6. Flip once. Lay a slice of provolone on each patty immediately after the flip. Cook 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 63–68 °C** (145–154 °F). The Argentine tradition for ground beef — *jugoso* in this context — means moist, fully cooked but not dry: a pink-grey interior with running juices.
8. Rest 3 minutes. Serve with chimichurri and *tuco* (tomato sauce) on the side. Final serving temperature: **65–70 °C / 149–158 °F** (Argentine medium-well, fully cooked, moist).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Serious Eats (Kenji) | Medium | 60–63 °C (140–145 °F) |
| British classic (Henderson) | Medium | 62–65 °C (144–149 °F) |
| British modern (Blumenthal) | Medium | 63–66 °C (145–151 °F) |
| Japanese-Californian (Nobu) | Medium-rare (Wagyu) | 57–60 °C (135–140 °F) |
| Argentine (Mallmann) | *Jugoso* / medium-well | 65–70 °C (149–158 °F) |

The observed range is **57–70 °C (135–158 °F)**. This breadth genuinely reflects cultural and food-safety conventions: Nobu's Wagyu medium-rare (57–60 °C) relies on high-quality single-source beef where whole-muscle safety norms are extended to ground beef by culinary tradition; the Argentine *jugoso* (65–70 °C) reflects a tradition where fully cooked but moist is the standard even for premium beef. The broadest common use — the American, British, and most European approach — targets medium at 60–65 °C, which maps to `medium` in the cooking_data.py doneness schema. Note that many food-safety guidelines (e.g., USDA) mandate 71 °C (160 °F) for ground beef, which maps to `well_done`; this standard is absent from the chef sources because experienced chefs explicitly work below it for quality reasons.

## What makes this method special
The direct-heat grill is the highest-heat cooking surface available to most cooks, producing intense Maillard browning on both flat faces of the patty simultaneously during the cook — no other method generates quite the same combination of charred exterior crust and smoky fat drip-flare flavour. The gaps in the grill grate allow fat to drip and ignite momentarily, creating brief flame contact that deposits aromatic pyrolysis products on the patty surface (the characteristic "grilled" flavour). The open-air environment means moisture escapes freely, concentrating exterior flavour. The patty geometry — thin relative to surface area — means both the Maillard crust and the internal temperature are reached in minutes, making the grill uniquely efficient for ground beef.

## Summary
Grilled beef burgers span the widest doneness range of any single cut-method combination — from Nobu's Wagyu medium-rare at 57 °C to Mallmann's Argentine *jugoso* at 70 °C — reflecting genuine cultural disagreement about whether premium ground beef should be treated like steak (rare/medium-rare) or like ground meat (well-done). The direct-heat grill delivers the defining charred Maillard crust and smoke-drip flavour that makes this the canonical burger method. The practical middle ground across the non-Wagyu traditions is medium at 60–65 °C, balancing flavour retention and food safety.
