# Beef Burger × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Ground beef burger patties pan-fried in a skillet are the indoor standard for domestic cooking, relying on the same fat-in-meat mechanics as grilled burgers but with a different heat-transfer medium: a heavy iron or steel pan rather than open grill grates. Chuck-based blends (80/20) are standard; the high fat content is essential because pan frying occurs in a shallow pool of rendered fat that accumulates during the cook. The enclosed pan environment retains moisture more than an open grill, and the solid flat surface creates full contact Maillard browning across the entire patty face — broader coverage but less char-and-smoke complexity than a grill. Carryover in pan-fried burgers is minimal to negligible given the thin geometry, though cast-iron skillets retain heat and can continue cooking the bottom face even after removal.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Fried Smash Burger
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 386–391; https://www.seriouseats.com/smash-burger-recipe  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 4–6 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g ground chuck (80/20), formed into four 175 g balls (do not press into patties yet)
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil or beef tallow
- 4 slices American cheese
- 4 soft potato rolls, toasted in butter

**Method**
1. Heat a cast-iron skillet or griddle over the highest heat for at least 3 minutes until smoking. Add oil and swirl.
2. Place beef balls in the pan, two at a time for a 30 cm skillet.
3. Immediately press firmly down with a heavy spatula or bacon press to a thickness of 8–10 mm. Hold for 10 seconds.
4. Season the top face with salt and pepper.
5. Cook undisturbed for 90 seconds until the edges are visibly brown and the crust releases naturally from the pan.
6. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the edge of the patty into the centre — the patty is thin, so the probe should enter at the midpoint of the edge and travel horizontally to the geometric centre, parallel to the pan surface.
7. Flip. Add cheese immediately. Cook 30–60 seconds on the second side.
8. **Pull at 60–65 °C** (140–149 °F). The smash patty is thin enough that carryover is almost instantaneous on a hot iron surface.
9. Rest briefly on the toasted bun, 1 minute. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (medium, with a deeply browned crust on both faces).

### 2. Nigel Slater — Beef Burger in Cast Iron (British tradition)
**Source**: Nigel Slater, *The Kitchen Diaries II* (Fourth Estate, 2012), pp. 310–312; theguardian.com/food  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min chilling) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 400 g beef mince (20% fat — ask for a coarsely ground mix of chuck and short rib)
- ½ small onion, very finely grated
- 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce
- ½ tsp Dijon mustard
- ½ tsp fine salt
- A few twists of black pepper
- 1 tbsp beef dripping or lard for frying

**Method**
1. Combine mince, grated onion, Worcestershire, mustard, salt, and pepper. Mix lightly with your hands until just combined. Do not over-work.
2. Shape into two patties, 3 cm thick. Place on a tray and refrigerate 30 minutes.
3. Heat dripping in a heavy cast-iron skillet over high heat until beginning to smoke.
4. Place the patties in the pan. Do not move. Cook 4–5 minutes until the underside is deeply crusted and releases cleanly.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side edge of the patty, entering horizontally at the midpoint of the patty's height to reach the centre.
6. Flip once. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 60–63 °C** (140–145 °F). Slater's target is a burger that is pink throughout but not red at the centre — medium, slightly firmer than a steak but still moist.
8. Rest 3 minutes off the heat. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (medium, evenly pink interior).

### 3. Fergus Henderson — Short Rib Burger in Pan (Nose-to-Tail tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson & Justin Piers Gellatly, *Beyond Nose to Tail* (Bloomsbury, 2007), pp. 54–56; stjohnrestaurant.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 hr resting) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g short rib, boned and minced (or ask butcher) — higher fat and gelatine content than pure chuck
- 200 g bavette / flank, minced — for lean body
- 1 tsp fine sea salt
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp rendered bone marrow fat or beef tallow, for the pan

**Method**
1. Mix the two minces together, season, and form into four patties, 2.5 cm thick. Rest in the fridge 1 hour — the resting time is essential for the mixture to bind through the natural gelatin in the short rib.
2. Heat bone marrow fat in a wide, heavy skillet over high heat until just smoking.
3. Add the patties. Press lightly (once only) with a fish slice to ensure full surface contact.
4. Cook 4 minutes on the first side without touching.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge, horizontally to the centre.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 58–62 °C** (136–144 °F). Henderson's tradition leans slightly more rare than the American standard, respecting high-quality single-source beef.
8. Rest 3 minutes. Final serving temperature: **61–64 °C / 142–147 °F** (medium, pink-to-red warm centre, rich with short rib fat).

### 4. Momofuku / David Chang — Korean-Influenced Crispy Skillet Burger
**Source**: David Chang & Peter Meehan, *Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009), pp. 234–236; momofuku.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 450 g ground beef (80/20 chuck or chuck-brisket blend)
- 1 tsp soy sauce (mixed into the beef — the Chang approach adds umami without altering texture)
- ½ tsp toasted sesame oil
- ½ tsp fine salt
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (grapeseed preferred)
- 2 slices aged cheddar or a mild Asian cheese
- Ssäm sauce (fermented bean paste, gochujang, sesame oil, rice wine vinegar), to serve

**Method**
1. Gently mix soy sauce and sesame oil into the beef, handling as little as possible. Form into two patties, 2.5 cm thick. Season with salt on both sides just before cooking.
2. Heat neutral oil in a heavy skillet over high heat until the first wisp of smoke appears.
3. Place patties in the pan. Press down once with a spatula — not a smash, just firm contact.
4. Cook 3 minutes on the first side without moving. The goal: a deep, evenly browned crust.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge of the patty at the midpoint of the height, travelling horizontally to the centre.
6. Flip. Add cheese. Cook 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 58–62 °C** (136–144 °F). Chang's approach to beef quality parallels a Japanese respect for the meat — medium-rare to medium, not cooked through.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with ssäm sauce on the bun. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (medium-rare to medium, juicy).

### 5. Diana Yen / The Jewels of New York — Taiwanese Beef Burger in Wok/Pan
**Source**: Diana Yen, *The Jewels of New York Cookbook* (Rizzoli, 2015), pp. 148–150; thejewelsofnewyork.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g ground beef (80/20, preferably freshly ground)
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- ½ tsp white pepper
- ½ tsp five-spice powder
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
- 4 garlic cloves, very finely minced
- 1 spring onion, finely minced
- 1 tbsp neutral oil
- Steamed bao buns or sesame-seed buns, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine beef with oyster sauce, soy sauce, white pepper, five-spice, sesame oil, garlic, and spring onion. Mix lightly until combined. Form into four patties, 2 cm thick.
2. Heat neutral oil in a heavy skillet or wok over high heat.
3. Place patties flat in the pan. Cook 3–4 minutes on the first side, pressing gently to maintain contact.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge of the patty into the centre at the midpoint of the patty's height, keeping the probe level and clear of the pan surface.
5. Flip once. Cook 3–4 minutes.
6. **Pull at 63–66 °C** (145–151 °F). Taiwanese beef preparations historically err slightly more cooked than Western medium-rare, but retain juiciness through the seasoning mix.
7. Rest 2 minutes. Serve in bao buns with pickled vegetables and a drizzle of chilli oil. Final serving temperature: **65–68 °C / 149–154 °F** (medium, lightly pink, very moist).

The observed range is **60–68 °C (140–154 °F)**, with the bulk of sources clustered between 61–65 °C — a notably tighter band than the grilled burger equivalents. The pan-fry method tends to cook more evenly and predictably than the grill, reducing the extreme outliers. All traditions agree on `medium` as the target; no source in this leaf targets rare or well-done. ## What makes this method special
A hot, heavy skillet transfers heat to the patty via solid conduction, producing a broad, flat Maillard crust across the full bottom face on contact — a more uniformly browned surface than a grill's parallel bars can achieve. The fat rendering from the patty pools in the pan and continuously bastes the lower crust, contributing a gentle frying action on the underside. Unlike a grill, there is no drip-and-flare mechanism, so the pan-fried burger lacks smoke-derived flavour but compensates with a crisper, more consistent outer crust. Cast-iron skillets retain heat so effectively that the pan-frying continues briefly after the patty is removed, meaning removal timing is critical for precise temperature control.

## Summary
Pan-fried beef burgers occupy the same medium doneness territory as grilled burgers but with a tighter temperature range (60–68 °C vs. the broader 57–70 °C for grilled), reflecting the more controllable, even heat of a heavy skillet. All five sources from different national traditions converge on medium as the target, with the key variable being the specific skillet material and fat used rather than target temperature. The solid-surface contact Maillard crust is the defining quality of the pan-fried burger — broader and more even than a grill can produce.
