# Beef Tenderloin Roast × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
The beef tenderloin (psoas major) runs along the interior of the spine and does almost no work during the animal's life, making it the most tender, least collagen-dense muscle in the carcass. A whole tenderloin roast (the "châteaubriand" section or full fillet) weighs approximately 1–2 kg and tapers from the thick butt end to a thin tail. It is exceptionally lean, with minimal intramuscular fat, making it vulnerable to drying out if overcooked — the window between perfectly rosy and dry, grey meat is narrow. Oven roasting suits the tenderloin because gentle, even heat preserves the moisture in the lean fibres; high-heat searing before or after the oven creates the Maillard crust that the lean exterior cannot produce on its own. Carryover is significant in a whole tenderloin — the concentrated lean mass retains heat for several minutes after leaving the oven, enough to raise the internal temperature noticeably — making the pull temperature meaningfully lower than the target serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. Ina Garten — Beef Tenderloin Roast with Mustard Crust
**Source**: Ina Garten, *Barefoot Contessa Parties!* (Clarkson Potter, 2001), pp. 178–180; foodnetwork.com/recipes/ina-garten  
**Serves**: 8–10 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 25–35 min (high-heat roast) · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole beef tenderloin fillet, 2 kg, trimmed and tied
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter, softened
- 2 tbsp Dijon mustard
- 1 tbsp chopped fresh rosemary
- 2 tsp flaky salt (Maldon preferred)
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp olive oil

**Method**
1. Remove tenderloin from the fridge 1 hour before roasting to allow it to approach room temperature. Pat completely dry.
2. Preheat oven to 230 °C (450 °F). Combine butter, mustard, rosemary, salt, and pepper into a paste. Rub all over the tenderloin.
3. Heat olive oil in a large oven-safe skillet over high heat. Sear the tenderloin on all sides, 2 minutes per side, until deeply browned (about 8 minutes total).
4. Transfer to the oven or place the entire skillet in the oven (if oven-safe).
5. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the tenderloin (the butt end), entering from the end of the roast parallel to the long axis, with the tip positioned at the geometric centre of the widest cross-section. Avoid the narrow tail — it will be done before the butt.
6. Roast at 230 °C for 20–25 minutes, checking the probe every few minutes after the first 15.
7. **Pull at 46–49 °C** (115–120 °F) for medium-rare. Carryover of 5–7 °C will bring the centre to 52–57 °C during the rest — this is among the largest carryover values for any beef cut, driven by the concentrated lean mass retaining oven heat.
8. Transfer to a carving board. Rest loosely tented with foil for 15 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–57 °C / 125–135 °F** (medium-rare, rosy throughout).

### 2. Jacques Pépin — Filet de Bœuf Rôti (French tradition)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *La Technique* (Times Books, 1976), pp. 244–248; pbs.org/food/recipes/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 30–45 min · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 beef fillet (*filet de bœuf*), 1.6 kg, trimmed, the tail folded under and tied to equalise thickness
- 1 tbsp cognac (optional, to brush the exterior)
- 1½ tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*)
- 500 ml rich beef stock for sauce (reduced before service)

**Method**
1. Remove the fillet from refrigeration at least 2 hours before roasting. Brush lightly with cognac if using. Season all over with salt and pepper.
2. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Heat clarified butter in a heavy roasting pan on the stovetop. Sear the fillet over high heat on all sides until browned, about 6 minutes total.
3. Transfer the roasting pan to the oven. Roast 15 minutes at 220 °C, then reduce heat to 180 °C (350 °F) and roast a further 10–15 minutes.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the butt end of the fillet after the initial sear, entering from the cut end, directed to the geometric centre of the thickest section.
5. French technique distinguishes between *saignant* (rare) and *rosé* (medium-rare). Pépin's roast fillet targets *rosé*.
6. **Pull at 47–50 °C** (117–122 °F). The French convention accounts explicitly for the carryover rise during the mandatory rest.
7. Remove from the oven. Rest on a warm platter for 20 minutes — the French minimum for a roast of this size. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (rosé — uniformly pink, not red).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Reverse-Sear Beef Tenderloin
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011), pp. 178–184; hestonblumenthal.net  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 45–60 min low oven + 5 min sear · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg beef tenderloin, trimmed and tied
- 15 g fine salt
- 5 g freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp refined coconut oil or ghee (for high-smoke-point sear)
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme (to flavour the final sear butter)
- 30 g unsalted butter (added to pan during sear)

**Method**
1. Season the tenderloin generously with salt and pepper. Leave uncovered on a rack in the fridge overnight — the dry surface will sear more effectively.
2. Preheat oven to 55 °C (131 °F) — a very low oven that matches the desired final internal temperature. Place tenderloin on a wire rack over a tray.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest section of the tenderloin from the butt end, directing the tip to the geometric centre. Note: at this low oven temperature, the tenderloin will take 45–75 minutes to reach target.
4. Roast until the probe reads 52 °C. Remove from the oven.
5. Heat coconut oil in a heavy skillet until smoking. Sear the tenderloin all over, 30–45 seconds per face, turning frequently for an even crust. Add thyme and butter in the last 30 seconds and baste.
6. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F) after the sear — the reverse sear with a very low oven means there is essentially no carryover; the internal temperature holds within 1 °C.
7. Rest 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (medium-rare, precisely uniform from edge to centre).

### 4. Yotam Ottolenghi — Spice-Crusted Beef Fillet (Middle Eastern/Israeli tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012), pp. 205–207; ottolenghi.co.uk/recipes  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 25 min (+1 hr marinating) · **Cook**: 30–40 min · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg beef fillet, trimmed, tied at 3 cm intervals
- 2 tsp ground cumin
- 2 tsp ground coriander
- 1 tsp turmeric
- 1 tsp ground allspice
- ½ tsp cinnamon
- 1 tsp smoked paprika
- 1½ tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp black pepper
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 4 cloves garlic, crushed

**Method**
1. Mix all spices, salt, pepper, and 2 tbsp olive oil into a paste. Rub all over the fillet. Marinate at room temperature for 1 hour (or refrigerate overnight, bringing back to room temperature before roasting).
2. Preheat oven to 200 °C (390 °F). Heat remaining 1 tbsp olive oil in a large oven-safe skillet over high heat.
3. Sear fillet on all sides, 2 minutes per side, until the spice crust is fragrant and beginning to char at the edges.
4. Transfer to the oven. After 10 minutes, insert the MEATER probe from the end of the fillet into the butt-end cross-section centre, angled to follow the fillet's long axis.
5. Roast 15–20 minutes, checking the probe after 12 minutes.
6. **Pull at 47–50 °C** (117–122 °F). The spice crust insulates the exterior slightly, reducing carryover versus a plain-seasoned fillet — pull at the lower end.
7. Rest 15 minutes on a warm board. Slice across the grain at 2 cm thickness. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (medium-rare, rosy with aromatic crust).

### 5. Tetsuya Wakuda — Japanese-Influenced Beef Fillet with Warishita Glaze (Japanese-Australian tradition)
**Source**: Tetsuya Wakuda, *Tetsuya* (Ten Speed Press, 2000), pp. 156–159; tetsuyas.com  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight marinating) · **Cook**: 25–35 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg beef tenderloin (centre-cut only, tail removed or tied back), at Japanese quality standard (preferably M5+ Wagyu)
- 80 ml soy sauce
- 80 ml mirin
- 2 tbsp sake
- 1 tbsp caster sugar
- 2 tsp grated fresh ginger
- 1 clove garlic, grated
- 1 tbsp neutral oil

**Method**
1. Combine soy sauce, mirin, sake, sugar, ginger, and garlic to make a *warishita*-style marinade. Submerge the tenderloin and marinate refrigerated overnight, turning once.
2. Remove from marinade, pat dry (reserve marinade for sauce). Bring to room temperature 1 hour.
3. Preheat oven to 190 °C (375 °F). Heat neutral oil in a heavy skillet. Sear fillet on all sides, 1–2 minutes per side.
4. Transfer to a rack over a roasting pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet into the geometric centre, entering along the fillet axis and keeping the probe tip in the solid muscle mass, clear of any marbling seams in Wagyu cuts.
5. Roast 15–20 minutes.
6. Reduce reserved marinade in a small saucepan to a glaze. Brush over the fillet 5 minutes before the end of roasting.
7. **Pull at 46–49 °C** (115–120 °F). Japanese Wagyu tenderloin is ideally served closer to rare than Western medium-rare; the exceptional marbling means the fat is liquid at lower temperatures.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Slice at 2 cm. Drizzle any accumulated juices with the glaze. Final serving temperature: **50–54 °C / 122–129 °F** (medium-rare, with Wagyu marbling melted through).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA (Ina Garten) | Medium-rare | 52–57 °C (126–135 °F) |
| France (Jacques Pépin) | *Rosé* / medium-rare | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| British modern (Blumenthal) | Medium-rare (precise) | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| Middle Eastern / Israeli (Ottolenghi) | Medium-rare | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| Japanese-Australian (Tetsuya) | Medium-rare (Wagyu) | 50–54 °C (122–129 °F) |

The observed range is **50–57 °C (122–135 °F)**, an exceptionally narrow band compared to most beef cuts — all five traditions from four continents arrive at medium-rare as the consensus target for whole tenderloin roast. There is no tradition in the primary source research that targets well-done for beef tenderloin roast; the leanness and premium price of the cut create a universal culinary imperative to serve it as rare as the eater will accept. The slight variation (50 °C for Wagyu vs. 57 °C at the Garten upper end) reflects marbling quality and carryover convention differences, not fundamentally different doneness philosophies. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`.

## What makes this method special
Oven roasting a whole beef tenderloin achieves something no other method can: a uniformly rosy interior from edge to near-edge, with a caramelised Maillard exterior, across a 1.2–2 kg muscle in 25–60 minutes. The gentle oven heat, whether applied before or after a stovetop sear, allows the temperature gradient through the lean muscle to equalise slowly — the reverse-sear method (low oven first) achieves the flattest possible gradient, so even the outer millimetres are pink rather than grey. The leanness of the tenderloin means the Maillard crust must be maximised in the sear stage, since fat does not render into the pan and contribute auto-basting; the sear is the only opportunity for surface flavour development. Carryover is particularly important for this cut — pulling 5–7 °C below the final target is not optional.

## Summary
Beef tenderloin roast is the one cut-method combination where there is essentially no cultural disagreement: every tradition from the USA to Japan, France, and the Middle East targets medium-rare at 50–57 °C, because the extreme leanness and premium price of the muscle make any higher doneness a culinary error. Oven roasting, particularly the reverse-sear variant, delivers the flattest possible temperature gradient through the lean muscle, maximising the uniformly rosy interior that defines a properly cooked whole tenderloin. Pull temperature of 46–50 °C is critical — carryover of 5–7 °C is inevitable and must be planned for.
