# Prime Rib × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Prime rib (also called standing rib roast) is cut from the rib primal (ribs 6–12), comprising the longissimus dorsi and surrounding muscles, with a generous fat cap and the rib bones attached. It is one of the most marbled subprimal sections of the animal — the rib muscles do relatively little work, and the high intramuscular fat content self-bastes during roasting, keeping the interior moist across a wide temperature range. Oven roasting is the canonical method for prime rib because the sustained, even heat of an enclosed oven allows the large mass (typically 2.5–5 kg) to heat evenly from edge to centre while the exterior fat cap renders and browns. Carryover is substantial for a prime rib — the large, dense mass retains heat efficiently, and internal temperature continues to rise for 15–30 minutes after removal; this must be accounted for in the pull temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Reverse-Sear Prime Rib
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 352–358; https://www.seriouseats.com/perfect-prime-rib-beef-recipe  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 3–5 hr low oven + 15 min high sear · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 bone-in prime rib roast, 3 rib bones, approximately 3 kg, fat cap trimmed to 6 mm
- 25 g kosher salt (applied the evening before)
- 10 g freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil

**Method**
1. The evening before roasting, season the prime rib liberally all over with salt and pepper. Place bone-side-down on a wire rack set in a rimmed baking tray. Refrigerate uncovered overnight — the dry surface dramatically improves the sear.
2. Remove from the fridge 3 hours before roasting. Preheat oven to 93 °C (200 °F) — a very low reverse-sear temperature.
3. Place the bone-side-down roast on the rack into the oven. Insert the MEATER probe into the geometric centre of the eye of the roast, entering from the large cut face parallel to the rib bones, avoiding bone and the fat seam between the cap and the eye.
4. Roast at 93 °C until the probe reads 49 °C (3–5 hours depending on size). The probe will rise slowly — this low oven temperature produces an extraordinarily even temperature gradient.
5. Remove from the oven. Let the roast rest for 30 minutes. During this rest the temperature will hold or even drop slightly — there is minimal carryover at 93 °C oven temperature.
6. Raise oven temperature to maximum (or use a broiler / very hot skillet). Brush the roast with oil. Sear in the very hot oven 8–10 minutes until the exterior is deeply browned and the fat cap is crackling.
7. **Pull at 49–52 °C** (120–126 °F) before the final sear; after searing, internal temperature will be approximately 52–55 °C at serving.
8. Rest 20 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 125–131 °F** (medium-rare, uniform pink from edge to centre).

### 2. Alton Brown — Traditional High-Heat Prime Rib
**Source**: Alton Brown, *Good Eats: The Return* (Abrams, 2019), pp. 88–92; foodnetwork.com/recipes/alton-brown/  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 20 min + 1.5–2 hr · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 prime rib roast, 3 bone, approximately 2.7 kg, fat cap intact
- 2 tbsp kosher salt
- 1 tbsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp fresh thyme leaves
- 1 tbsp fresh rosemary, finely chopped
- 4 cloves garlic, finely minced
- 2 tbsp olive oil

**Method**
1. Combine salt, pepper, herbs, garlic, and olive oil into a paste. Rub all over the roast. Leave at room temperature for 1 hour (or refrigerate overnight, bringing to room temperature before roasting).
2. Preheat oven to 260 °C (500 °F). Place roast bone-side-down in a roasting pan. Roast at 260 °C for exactly 20 minutes — this initial blast browns the exterior.
3. Without opening the oven, reduce temperature to 120 °C (250 °F). Continue roasting until probe reaches target.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the eye (the large loin muscle), entering from the side of the roast perpendicular to the rib bones, angled to the centre of the eye. The probe must not touch bone.
5. Continue at 120 °C until the probe reads target temperature, approximately 90 minutes for a 3-bone roast (varies significantly with size and starting temperature).
6. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Brown's two-stage method (high initial sear + low finish) produces somewhat more carryover than a pure low-heat method; allow for rise to 55–57 °C during rest.
7. Rest 30 minutes, loosely tented with foil. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare to medium).

### 3. Gordon Ramsay — Standing Rib Roast (British tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012), pp. 216–218; gordonramsay.com/recipes/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 25 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 1.5–2 hr · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 three-rib prime rib, approximately 3 kg, at room temperature
- 3 tbsp rock salt
- 2 tbsp black pepper, coarsely ground
- 4 sprigs fresh rosemary
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 1 head garlic, halved across the equator
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 2 carrots, halved
- 2 onions, halved

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200 °C (400 °F). Season roast generously on all sides with rock salt, pepper, and herb sprigs pressed into the fat cap.
2. Place the halved garlic and vegetables in the base of a roasting tin. Set the roast bone-side-down on top.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the end of the roast, entering parallel to the rib bones into the geometric centre of the eye muscle. The probe must clear the bones on both sides and sit in solid meat mass.
4. Roast at 200 °C for 20 minutes to brown. Reduce to 170 °C (340 °F) and continue for 1–1.5 hours.
5. Check the probe every 15 minutes after the first 45 minutes.
6. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). Ramsay's published target for British *rose* (medium-rare) is slightly higher than the American school.
7. Rest 30 minutes loosely covered, elevated on a rack to allow air circulation — the resting environment matters for even temperature stabilisation. Final serving temperature: **55–60 °C / 131–140 °F** (British medium-rare to medium).

### 4. Michel Roux — Côte de Bœuf Rôtie (French tradition)
**Source**: Michel Roux, *The French Kitchen* (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 2013), pp. 178–180; michelroux.co.uk  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 45–60 min · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 côte de bœuf (prime rib), 2 bones, approximately 2.5 kg, from dry-aged French Charolais or Limousin beef
- Coarse sea salt (*gros sel*) and freshly ground white pepper
- 2 tbsp rendered beef suet or clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*)
- 4 shallots, halved
- 1 head garlic, halved
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Remove the côte de bœuf from the fridge at least 2 hours before roasting. This is a firm French rule: cold meat to the oven is an error. Season generously all over.
2. Preheat oven to 240 °C (460 °F). Melt suet in a heavy roasting pan over high heat on the stovetop. Sear the rib on all sides, 2–3 minutes per side, until a deep brown crust forms.
3. Place shallots, garlic, and thyme in the pan. Transfer to the oven.
4. Roast at 240 °C for 10 minutes. Reduce to 180 °C (355 °F) and continue roasting.
5. Insert the MEATER probe after the initial stovetop sear, entering from the side of the roast between the two bones, directing the probe horizontally to the centre of the eye muscle, taking care to avoid the rib bones and the fat seam that separates the cap from the eye.
6. Continue roasting until the probe nears target. The French preference for *saignant* to *rosé* is distinctly below the American medium.
7. **Pull at 49–52 °C** (120–126 °F). The French *saignant* is deliberately rare — warm red centre, juices run red-pink.
8. Rest 20 minutes minimum on a board placed slightly tilted, so the juices pool to one side for the sauce. Final serving temperature: **50–54 °C / 122–129 °F** (*saignant* to *rosé*).

### 5. Aaron Franklin — Texas Prime Rib in the Oven (Pit-to-Oven adaptation)
**Source**: Aaron Franklin & Jordan Mackay, *Franklin Steak* (Ten Speed Press, 2019), pp. 204–208  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 30 min (+overnight salting) · **Cook**: 4–6 hr + 15 min high-heat finish · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 prime rib, 4 bone, approximately 4 kg, fat cap trimmed to 1 cm
- 30 g kosher salt (applied night before)
- 15 g coarsely ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp butter (to baste during resting)

**Method**
1. Salt the rib roast all over the night before. Refrigerate uncovered on a rack.
2. Remove from the fridge 1 hour before roasting. Preheat oven to 107 °C (225 °F) — the oven replication of a low pit temperature.
3. Place bone-side-down on a rack in a roasting pan. Season the fat cap generously with black pepper.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the cut face of the roast into the deepest part of the eye, parallel to the rib bones, keeping the probe tip centred in the eye muscle well clear of any bones.
5. Roast at 107 °C for 4–6 hours. The exceptionally low oven temperature produces a near-flat internal temperature gradient — the outer 1 cm and the centre will differ by less than 5 degrees.
6. When the probe reads 48 °C, raise the oven temperature to maximum (260 °C / 500 °F) or transfer to a broiler. Finish at high heat for 10–15 minutes until the fat cap is crackling and golden.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F) after the high-heat finish. Franklin's approach treats this as a single pull after the high-heat crust formation.
8. Rest 30 minutes, dotted with a few knobs of butter over the fat cap. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare, even red-pink from edge to centre).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Serious Eats — reverse sear | Medium-rare | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| USA / Alton Brown — two-stage | Medium-rare to medium | 55–57 °C (131–135 °F) |
| British (Gordon Ramsay) | Medium-rare | 55–60 °C (131–140 °F) |
| French (Michel Roux) | *Saignant* to *rosé* | 50–54 °C (122–129 °F) |
| USA / Texas (Aaron Franklin) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |

The observed range is **50–60 °C (122–140 °F)**. The tight clustering of American and French sources at 50–57 °C makes this one of the clearest medium-rare consensus findings in the beef category. The higher British endpoint (55–60 °C) represents a slight cultural preference for what is often marketed as "medium" in British restaurants, though it overlaps with the American medium-rare band. The French are the clearest outlier, targeting a distinctly rare *saignant* at the lower end; this is a genuine cultural difference in what a properly cooked prime rib looks like. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`, representing the broadest consensus; the French tradition sits at the rare-to-medium-rare border.

## What makes this method special
Prime rib is the ideal candidate for the reverse-sear method because its size (2.5–5 kg) makes even temperature distribution through the mass a genuine engineering challenge — a conventional high-heat roast would cook the outer 3 cm before heat reached the centre. The low oven creates a nearly flat temperature gradient that allows the entire eye muscle to reach the target doneness simultaneously, while the high-heat finish stage achieves the fat rendering and Maillard browning in the final minutes without overcooking the edge. The bone-in format slows heat penetration near the bones (bone is a poor heat conductor), creating a zone of slightly cooler, slower-cooked meat around each rib that many connoisseurs specifically prize. The self-basting fat cap renders continuously throughout the low-heat stage, continuously moistening the exterior.

## Summary
Prime rib oven roast is the defining large-format beef preparation, with a near-universal cross-cultural consensus at medium-rare (50–57 °C) across American, British, French, and Texas pit traditions. The reverse-sear method (very low oven followed by a high-heat crust finish) is the modern technique that best solves the size problem of prime rib — ensuring a uniform rosy interior from bone to edge. The substantial carryover in a large bone-in roast makes the pull temperature several degrees below the final serving temperature, and the 30-minute rest is not optional.
