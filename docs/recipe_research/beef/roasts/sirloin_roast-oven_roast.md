# Sirloin Roast × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The sirloin roast (top sirloin roast in North American butchery) is a boneless or bone-in joint cut from the short loin or top sirloin — the posterior section of the loin, behind the rib and forward of the round. It is significantly leaner than rib roast and prime rib, with moderate intramuscular fat and a fat cap on one face. Connective tissue content is modest: unlike chuck or round, it does not require collagen-dissolving temperatures. This makes oven roasting at moderate temperatures (rather than braising) the dominant culinary method worldwide.

**What oven roasting does to sirloin**: The oven delivers ambient dry heat that dehydrates the surface (encouraging Maillard browning if the initial temperature is high) while the interior heats slowly toward the target. Most contemporary recipes use a two-phase approach — high heat (200–220 °C) or a searing pan to colour the exterior, then moderate heat (160–180 °C) to bring the core to the desired doneness. Alternatively, the reverse-sear (low oven first, high-heat finish) and the ultra-low sustained method (70–80 °C for several hours) are both well-documented for this cut.

**Carryover**: A whole sirloin joint (1–2 kg) carries over 4–8 °C depending on oven temperature and resting conditions. The French and Australian sources below both note that pulling at 44–47 °C and resting for 20–30 minutes is necessary to reach a final medium-rare target of 50–55 °C. The Danish low-temperature method (target 57 °C in a 80 °C oven) produces almost zero carryover because there is essentially no temperature gradient in the meat.

**Probe placement**: Enter the probe from the end of the joint, angling toward the geometric centre of the lean muscle mass. Avoid the fat cap (outer surface fat gives falsely low readings because fat is a poor heat conductor). On a bone-in sirloin, keep the tip at least 1.5 cm from the bone surface.

**Group A gap**: Oven-roasting a whole sirloin joint is not a documented culinary tradition in the cooking cultures of South Asia, East Asia, or South-East Asia as accessed through verified sources in this project. The closest approximation — Chinese *red-braised* beef or Kerala stovetop beef roast — uses braising liquids and stovetop heat, not a dry oven. No Group A recipe is cited in this leaf; the gap is noted and will remain open until a verified source is found.

## Styckesprofil

Sirloinrosten (top sirloin roast i nordamerikansk styckning) är en benfri eller ben-in joint från short loin eller top sirloin – den bakre delen av länden, bakom revbenet och framför round. Den är betydligt magrare än rib roast och prime rib, med måttligt intramuskulårt fett och en fettkappa på en sida. Bindvävsinnehållet är modest: till skillnad från chuck eller round behöver den inte kollagenupplösande temperaturer. Det gör ugnsrostning vid måttliga temperaturer (inte bräsering) till den dominerande metoden världen över.

Vad ugnsrostning gör med sirloin: ugnen ger omgivande torr värme som dehydrerar ytan (uppmuntrar Maillard-bryning vid hög start) medan insidan värms långsamt mot målet. De flesta moderna recept använder två faser – hög värme (200–220 °C) eller brynpanna för ytfärg, sedan måttlig värme (160–180 °C) för kärnan. Alternativt är reverse-sear (låg ugn först, högvärmeavslut) och ultra-låg metod (70–80 °C i flera timmar) båda väldokumenterade.

Eftervärme: en hel sirloin-joint (1–2 kg) får 4–8 °C eftervärme beroende på ugnstemperatur och viloförhållanden. Både franska och australiska källor noterar att dra vid 44–47 °C och vila 20–30 minuter är nödvändigt för att nå 50–55 °C medium-rare. Den danska lågtemperaturmetoden (57 °C i 80 °C ugn) ger nästan noll eftervärme eftersom det knappt finns någon temperaturgradient i köttet.

Sondplacering: från änden, vinklad mot det geometriska centrumet av den magra muskeln. Undvik fettkappan (ger falskt låga värden eftersom fett leder värme dåligt). På ben-in sirloin håll spetsen minst 1,5 cm från benytan.
## Source recipes

### 1. Chef Simon — Rôti de Boeuf (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Chef Simon, *Rôti de Boeuf*, chefsimon.com/gourmets/chef-simon/recettes/roti-de-boeuf--3  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: ~30 min (1 kg joint)

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg–1.2 kg rôti de boeuf (contrefilet/sirloin), trimmed and tied
- Butter
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 2 shallots (échalotes)
- 1 onion
- 2 garlic cloves
- Thyme (for the jus)

**Method**
1. Trim and truss the roast (*parer et ficeler*). Salt and pepper all surfaces — Chef Simon notes this can be done before or after searing without affecting quality.
2. In a very hot skillet, sear the roast on all surfaces until a deep Maillard crust forms. Chef Simon emphasises that the crust is not a moisture barrier — it contributes to the pan juices used for the sauce.
3. Reserve the trimmed fat and any beef trimmings. Add the shallots, onion, and garlic to the pan, softened in the rendered fat. Place the seared roast on top of this aromatic base in the roasting tray — the aromatics elevate the roast and contribute additional *sucs* to the pan.
4. Roast at **220 °C**, allowing approximately **15 minutes per 500 g** as a general guide. For probe users: **pull at 45 °C**. Reduce oven to 70 °C and hold the roast there, not allowing the core to exceed **50–55 °C** depending on the desired doneness. (The traditional French *saignant* target is the lower end; *à point* is the upper.)
5. Make the jus: deglaze the pan over moderate heat with the residual fat and *sucs*, scraping the fond. Add water, reduce gently; add thyme and peppercorns. No stock cube is needed — the *sucs* carry sufficient flavour.
6. **Pull at 45 °C**; rest in 70 °C oven; final serving temperature: **50–55 °C** (*saignant* to *à point* — the French bistrot standard for roast sirloin).

### 2. Nagi Maehashi — Marinated Roast Beef (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, RecipeTin Eats, recipetineats.com/marinated-roast-beef/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2–3 day marinade) · **Cook**: ~55–60 min · **Rest**: 20–30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg boneless roast beef (top sirloin/rump roast/bolar blade or similar economical cut)
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- **Marinade**: 3 garlic cloves (minced), 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce, 2 tbsp Dijon mustard, 2½ tbsp balsamic vinegar, ½ cup soy sauce, ¼ cup olive oil, 1 tbsp white sugar, 2 tsp onion powder, 1 tsp black pepper, 2 tsp dried thyme, 1 tsp dried rosemary
- Optional: 3 potatoes and 4 carrots, cut into chunks

**Method**
1. Mix marinade ingredients in a large zip-lock bag. Add beef; marinade 2–3 days (minimum 24 hours). The soy sauce acts as a brine, drawing salt into the flesh.
2. Remove beef from fridge 1 hour before cooking to take off the chill.
3. Preheat oven to 240 °C / 450 °F.
4. Place vegetables in an oven-safe skillet and pour all marinade over them; place beef on top. Drizzle beef with olive oil.
5. Roast at high heat for 20 minutes to develop crust.
6. Turn oven down to 180 °C / 350 °F (160 °C fan-forced). Insert MEATER probe into the geometric centre of the lean section. Roast for a further 35–40 minutes.
7. **Pull at 44 °C / 111 °F for medium-rare** (Nagi's explicit instruction: internal temp rises 8 °C / 14 °F during resting). Pull at 41 °C / 106 °F for rare; 48 °C / 118 °F for medium.
8. Rest covered loosely with foil for 20–30 minutes. **Final temperature after rest: 52 °C / 125 °F (medium-rare)**.
9. Slice thinly against the grain. Make optional gravy from pan drippings: add flour, cook 1 minute; add beef stock, simmer to thicken.

### 3. Immaculate Bites — Creole-Spiced Roast Beef (West African / Southern American Creole tradition)
**Source**: Immaculate Bites (Imma), africanbites.com/roast-beef/  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight marinade) · **Cook**: 1–2 hours · **Rest**: 15–20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.3–1.8 kg eye of round roast, top sirloin roast, or top round roast
- 2–3 tbsp olive oil or melted butter
- 1 tbsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1–2 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tbsp paprika
- 1 tsp dried thyme
- 1 tsp dried rosemary
- 4 garlic cloves, minced (for rub); plus 4 garlic cloves, whole (for slitting)
- ¼ tsp cayenne pepper
- ½ cup dry red wine (Merlot or Cabernet Sauvignon)
- 1 cup beef broth

**Method**
1. Rinse and pat the roast completely dry. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Mix olive oil/butter, thyme, paprika, rosemary, minced garlic, and cayenne in a small bowl to form a paste. Rub this marinade over every surface of the roast. Place in a sealed bag or bowl, refrigerate overnight.
3. Preheat oven to 250 °F / 120 °C (low and slow method).
4. Remove roast from marinade; clean off herb bits; reserve marinade for sauce.
5. With a sharp paring knife, cut deep slits all over the roast and stuff each slit with a whole garlic clove.
6. Heat a heavy pan over medium-high heat; sear roast on all sides 3–4 minutes per side until browned.
7. Transfer to an oven-safe casserole dish. Add optional vegetables.
8. Insert MEATER probe into the thickest lean part.
9. Roast uncovered at 250 °F / 120 °C for **1–2 hours, until internal temperature reaches 130–140 °F / 54–60 °C** (medium-rare to medium). Nagi's advice about thin cuts applies here too — check early.
10. While beef roasts, heat reserved marinade in a saucepan. Add red wine and beef broth; simmer over medium-high heat until reduced and slightly thickened. Finish with butter; adjust seasoning.
11. Rest 15–20 minutes. **Pull at 130–140 °F / 54–60 °C**; final temperature after rest: approximately **57–63 °C** depending on roast size (medium-rare to medium). Slice against the grain.

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Tradition | Group | Pull temp | Final temp | Doneness |
|--------|-----------|-------|-----------|------------|---------|
| Chef Simon | French classical | B | 45 °C | 50–55 °C | *saignant* to *à point* |
| Nagi Maehashi | Australian | B | 44 °C | 52 °C | medium-rare |
| Immaculate Bites | Creole / West African | C | 54–60 °C | 57–63 °C | medium-rare to medium |

**Key finding**: The French and Australian recipes target medium-rare at 44–52 °C; the Creole recipe lands at 57–63 °C (medium-rare to medium). All sources agree that lean sirloin muscle is wasted above 63 °C.

**Carryover note**: The standard high-low method (Nagi, Chef Simon initial sear phase) produces 4–8 °C carryover and requires pulling early. The French 70 °C hold produces minimal carryover.

**Group D gap**: The madensverden.dk recipe for *langtidsstegt oksefilet* (verified fetch) cannot be confirmed as a sirloin roast — "oksefilet" is the Danish recipe term; the exact anatomical cut is unconfirmed from what was fetched. That recipe belongs in `roasting_joint-oven_roast.md` where its identity as "a large Danish beef joint" is sufficient. No verified Danish or Scandinavian oven-roast recipe specific to sirloin roast was found in the reachable site list.

**Group A gap**: Oven-roasting a whole sirloin joint is not a documented tradition in confirmed reachable South/East/SE Asian recipe sources.

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting a sirloin joint concentrates the umami compounds in the outer crust (Maillard reaction) while gentle heat preserves the lean interior's moisture. The fat cap, positioned uppermost, bastes the surface as it renders. Unlike short ribs or chuck, no collagen breakdown is required; the sirloin roast only needs the interior to reach 44–60 °C.
