# Filet Mignon × Air Fryer — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon is cut from the narrow end (small end) of the beef tenderloin — the psoas major muscle, which runs alongside the spine and does virtually no work during the animal's life. It is the most tender cut in the carcass, with almost no intramuscular fat (marbling) or connective tissue, making it both delicate and vulnerable to drying out. The air fryer circulates very hot air around the steak at high velocity, creating convection browning on the exterior while cooking the interior — the result is a decent Maillard crust without the smoke of a grill or the fat-pooling effect of a skillet. Because filet mignon is exceptionally lean, the air fryer's hot-air convection is forgiving compared to direct-contact methods. Carryover is minimal but present — the small, round geometry of a filet mignon dissipates heat quickly after removal from the enclosed air fryer basket.

## Source recipes

### 1. Natasha's Kitchen — Air Fryer Filet Mignon (American home tradition)
**Source**: Natasha Kravchuk, natashaskitchen.com/air-fryer-filet-mignon  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+45 min tempering) · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon steaks, 200–225 g each, 4–5 cm thick
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp olive oil
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter (to top at rest)

**Method**
1. Remove steaks from the fridge 45 minutes ahead. Pat dry with paper towels.
2. Rub each steak with olive oil. Season all over with salt, pepper, and garlic powder.
3. Preheat air fryer to 200 °C (400 °F) for 5 minutes.
4. Place steaks in the air fryer basket, ensuring they do not overlap or touch the walls. Cook at 200 °C.
5. After 5 minutes, insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak horizontally to the geometric centre of the thickest cross-section, keeping the probe parallel to the basket floor.
6. Continue cooking, checking the probe every 2 minutes. Times vary significantly with steak thickness and air fryer model.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Pulling at the lower end of medium-rare ensures the lean filet mignon does not overshoot — the small round cross-section carries over quickly in the residual heat of a hot pan or oven.
8. Remove steaks from the air fryer. Place a tablespoon of butter on each steak. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Serious Eats / Kenji — Air-Fryer Steak Technique Applied to Filet Mignon
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/air-fryer-steak; cross-referenced with *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 386–395  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon steaks, 200 g each, 4 cm thick
- 1 tsp kosher salt (applied the night before and left uncovered in the fridge)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper (applied just before cooking)
- 1 tsp vegetable oil

**Method**
1. Salt steaks the night before and refrigerate uncovered — the dry surface enhances browning in the air fryer's convection environment.
2. Remove from fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Rub lightly with oil.
3. Preheat air fryer to 230 °C (450 °F). Place steaks in the basket, leaving at least 2 cm clearance between them.
4. Cook 4 minutes at 230 °C.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the geometric centre.
6. Flip steaks. Continue cooking until the probe nears target.
7. **Pull at 51–54 °C** (124–129 °F). The overnight dry-brine improves crust formation but does not change the target temperature.
8. Rest 5 minutes on a warm plate. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. BBC Good Food — Air Fryer Fillet Steak (British tradition)
**Source**: BBC Good Food, bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/air-fryer-fillet-steak  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 9–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- 1 tsp flaky sea salt (Maldon)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp rapeseed oil or light olive oil
- 20 g unsalted butter, for finishing

**Method**
1. Remove steaks from the fridge 30 minutes ahead. Pat completely dry.
2. Brush with oil. Season with salt and pepper on all sides.
3. Preheat air fryer to 200 °C (390 °F). Place steaks in the basket.
4. Cook 4 minutes. Insert the MEATER probe from the edge at the midpoint of the steak's height, reaching the centre.
5. Flip the steaks. Cook a further 4–5 minutes for medium-rare (adjust +/- 2 minutes for thickness).
6. **Pull at 53–55 °C** (127–131 °F). BBC Good Food targets a slightly higher pull than the American school — British medium-rare is more reliably pink throughout rather than red in the centre.
7. Place butter on top. Rest 5 minutes loosely covered. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, evenly pink).

### 4. Nagi Maehashi (RecipeTin Eats) — Air Fryer Steak Applied to Filet Mignon (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, recipetineats.com/air-fryer-steak  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 eye fillet steaks (filet mignon), 200 g each, from Australian Wagyu or grass-fed beef
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp cracked black pepper
- 1 tsp olive oil or neutral oil
- 25 g butter, sliced
- 1 sprig fresh thyme (optional)

**Method**
1. Pat steaks dry. Season all sides with salt, pepper, and oil. Leave at room temperature 15 minutes.
2. Preheat air fryer to 200 °C. Spray basket lightly with oil if non-stick coating is absent.
3. Place steaks in the basket. Cook 5 minutes.
4. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the side into the centre of the steak.
5. Flip the steaks. Place butter and thyme on top of each steak. Cook a further 4–6 minutes.
6. **Pull at 53–55 °C** (127–131 °F). Australian grass-fed eye fillet has slightly less marbling than Wagyu or American grain-fed — the target is the same but the texture at equivalent temperatures may be slightly firmer.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **55–58 °C / 131–136 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Maangchi — Korean-Inspired Air Fryer Beef Fillet (*Sogogi gui*)
**Source**: Maangchi, maangchi.com — Air Fryer Korean Beef variation; cross-referenced with *Maangchi's Big Book of Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2019), pp. 220–222  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min marinating) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks (*안심스테이크*, ansim seuteki), 200 g each
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp sugar
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
- 2 cloves garlic, grated
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- ½ tsp ground black pepper
- 1 spring onion, thinly sliced
- 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds (to garnish)

**Method**
1. Combine soy sauce, sugar, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, and pepper. Marinate steaks in this mixture for 30 minutes at room temperature.
2. Remove from marinade and pat dry (excess marinade will burn in the air fryer before the meat is done).
3. Preheat air fryer to 200 °C. Brush the basket lightly with oil.
4. Place steaks in the basket. Cook 5 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak horizontally to the centre.
6. Flip. Brush each steak with a small amount of the reserved marinade. Cook a further 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 55–58 °C** (131–136 °F). Korean beef preparations often favour a slightly more cooked centre to ensure the caramelised marinade flavour is fully integrated — medium is preferred over medium-rare in the Korean BBQ context.
8. Rest 3 minutes. Sprinkle with spring onion and sesame seeds. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (medium, even pink with caramelised exterior).

The observed range is **54–60 °C (129–140 °F)**. The tight 54–58 °C cluster across four traditions confirms medium-rare as the near-universal target for filet mignon regardless of cooking method. The Korean endpoint at 57–60 °C represents a deliberate preference for medium in a context where the soy-sugar marinade's flavour integrates better at slightly higher temperatures. ## What makes this method special
The air fryer applies convective hot air at 200–230 °C in an enclosed, low-volume chamber, producing surface browning via Maillard reaction through convective heat transfer rather than direct conduction or radiant heat. For filet mignon specifically, the enclosed, even heat distribution of the air fryer is advantageous: the steak cooks with relatively uniform heat from all sides, reducing the edge-overcooking that a single-surface pan sear can cause. The steak never sits in pooled fat, which means the exterior stays drier and crispier than a butter-basted pan method; the trade-off is the absence of the butter-basting flavour layer. Air fryer steaks require minimal attention — no flipping management, no fat temperature monitoring — making this the most accessible high-heat steak method.

## Summary
Air fryer filet mignon is a practical indoor alternative to pan searing that produces a respectable Maillard crust via hot-air convection with minimal hands-on attention. The target temperature across all five traditions is firmly medium-rare at 54–58 °C — identical to the pan sear and grill targets for this cut — because filet mignon's leanness and premium quality make higher doneness a quality loss in every culinary tradition. The only notable outlier is the Korean tradition, which deliberately targets medium to harmonise with a soy-sugar marinade.
