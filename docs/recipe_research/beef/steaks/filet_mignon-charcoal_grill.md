## Cut profile
Charcoal grilling filet mignon uses a two-zone fire: direct high heat (204-232°C / 400-450°F)
over the coals for the Maillard sear, then indirect heat to finish. Hardwood lump charcoal
produces trace combustion aromatics (guaiacol, syringol) that add subtle smokiness to the
mild-flavoured tenderloin — a contrast absent from gas or pan methods. German kitchens
increasingly use the reverse-sear technique over charcoal for thick tournedos.

## Source recipes

### 1. The Grilling Master — How to Grill Filet Mignon on Charcoal
- **Source**: thegrillingmaster.com/how-to-grill/how-to-grill-filet-mignon-on-charcoal
- **Method**: Two-zone lump charcoal fire. Direct sear 2-3 min per side, then indirect
  until probe reads target temperature. Rest 5-10 min tented.
- **Target (medium-rare)**: **130-135°F / 54-57°C**
- "Never grill filet mignon beyond medium for the best texture."

### 2. Furiousgrill — How to Grill Filet Mignon (Times & Temperatures)
- **Source**: furiousgrill.com/how-to-grill-filet-mignon

- **Targets**: Rare 120-125°F / 49-52°C; Medium-rare **130-135°F / 54-57°C**
- "Do not overcook — filet mignon can tighten above 140°F."

### 3. Food Network Kitchen — Perfect Grilled Filet Mignon
- **Source**: foodnetwork.com/recipes/food-network-kitchen/perfect-grilled-filet-mignon
- **Method**: Hot grill (works charcoal or gas), flip every 2-3 min for even crust.
- **Target**: Medium-rare = **130°F / 54°C** after rest.
- The Food Network standard consistently targets 54°C as ideal.

### 4. SHUNgrill — The Perfect Guide to Cooking Filet Mignon on a Charcoal Grill
- **Source**: shungrill.com/article/how-to-cook-filet-mignon-charcoal-grill

- **Source**: barbecuebible.com/2026/02/20/filet-mignon-reverse-sear-method

## What charcoal adds vs gas grill for this cut
The combustion aromatics from lump charcoal (especially hardwoods like mesquite or oak)
provide a subtle smoky contrast to the filet's mild flavour — adding depth that is absent
from gas. The reverse-sear technique (indirect first, then direct over coals) is ideal for
thick tournedos and is gaining popularity in German and French outdoor cooking. The lean
muscle's tenderness is unchanged regardless of heat source.
