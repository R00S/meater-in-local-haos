# Filet Mignon × Charcoal Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon is cut from the narrow end of the beef tenderloin (psoas major), the least-used muscle in the animal, resulting in the most tender yet leanest steak in any carcass. Its near-total absence of intramuscular fat and connective tissue creates a delicate, buttery texture but also a vulnerability to drying out — the lean muscle fibres expel moisture rapidly above medium doneness. Charcoal grilling adds an irreplaceable dimension to filet mignon that no other cooking method provides: direct infrared radiation from glowing embers creates a Maillard crust at the surface level while the hardwood charcoal smoke deposits volatile aromatic compounds that complement the cut's mild flavour. Carryover is minimal for a small, round filet mignon — the geometry favours heat dissipation — but the intense radiant heat of charcoal means the final minutes of cooking are especially rapid.

## Source recipes

### 1. Aaron Franklin — Charcoal-Grilled Filet (Texas tradition)
**Source**: Aaron Franklin & Jordan Mackay, *Franklin Steak* (Ten Speed Press, 2019), pp. 154–158  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+45 min tempering) · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon steaks, 220–250 g each, 4–5 cm thick
- 15 g coarse kosher salt
- 8 g freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp neutral oil (for grate)

**Method**
1. Remove steaks from the fridge 45 minutes ahead. Pat dry. Season generously with salt and pepper on all sides.
2. Light a charcoal chimney. Use hardwood lump charcoal for the cleanest smoke. When coals are ashed over, bank them to one side of the grill — the two-zone fire allows both direct and indirect cooking.
3. Oil the grate. Begin on the direct (hot) side over the coals.
4. Place steaks on the hottest part of the grate. Cook 2–3 minutes on the first side without moving.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak at the midpoint of its height, entering horizontally to the geometric centre, clear of the grate.
6. Flip. Sear 2–3 minutes on the second side.
7. Move the steaks to the indirect (cool) side. Close the lid. Continue cooking until the probe reads near target.
8. **Pull at 50–52 °C** (122–126 °F). Franklin's approach targets the lower end of medium-rare, relying on residual grill-grate heat and carryover to finish at 54 °C.
9. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare, red-pink warm centre).

### 2. Adam Perry Lang — Two-Stage Charcoal Filet
**Source**: Adam Perry Lang, *BBQ 25* (Hyperion, 2010), pp. 62–66; adamperry lang.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 hr dry rub) · **Cook**: 12–16 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon, 225 g each
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- ½ tsp garlic powder
- ½ tsp onion powder
- 1 tsp vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter + 1 clove crushed garlic (compound butter, for finishing)

**Method**
1. Combine salt, pepper, garlic powder, and onion powder. Rub steaks on all sides. Let rest 1 hour at room temperature.
2. Build a charcoal fire. Set up for direct-heat grilling (all coals spread evenly under the cooking zone).
3. Oil the grates. Brush steaks with vegetable oil just before placing on the grill.
4. Place on the hot grate. Cook 4 minutes on the first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the side to the geometric centre.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. Move to a cooler spot or elevated rack. Finish until the probe nears target.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Lang's approach aims for a precisely medium-rare result with a structured sear on both faces.
9. Immediately top with compound butter. Rest 8 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 129–133 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Francis Mallmann — Filet on Charcoal Embers (*gaucho* tradition, Argentine)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan, 2009), pp. 98–104  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon (*lomo*), 220 g each, minimal trimming — the Argentine style leaves the natural membrane
- Coarse salt (*sal parrillera*) — applied immediately before cooking
- 1 tbsp wood fire coals or *quebracho* charcoal for the final minute (optional, ember placement)

**Method**
1. Allow steaks to come to room temperature for 1 hour. Season with coarse salt on both faces only at the last moment before placing on the grill.
2. Build a *quebracho* or hardwood charcoal fire. Allow to burn down to white-hot, glowing embers with minimal flame. Mallmann's philosophy: high, radiant ember heat, not flame.
3. Clean the grill. Do not oil — the Argentine tradition does not oil the grate.
4. Place steaks on the grill, 10–15 cm above the embers. Cook 4–5 minutes on the first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side horizontally to the centre of the steak.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes on the second side.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). Mallmann acknowledges that Argentine *lomo* is best at *jugoso* — warm, red-pink centre, juices flowing.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (*jugoso* — medium-rare to light medium, juicy).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Charcoal-Grilled Beef Fillet with Soy (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu the Cookbook* (Kodansha, 2001), pp. 138–142  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+15 min marinade) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks (*ヒレ肉*, hire-niku), 200 g each, 4 cm thick
- 3 tbsp soy sauce (*koikuchi shoyu*)
- 2 tbsp mirin
- 1 tbsp sake
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
- 1 garlic clove, grated
- Yuzu zest (optional)

**Method**
1. Combine soy sauce, mirin, sake, sesame oil, and garlic. Marinate steaks 15 minutes at room temperature.
2. Prepare a *binchotan* (white charcoal) grill or quality hardwood charcoal grill. The Japanese ideal is clean, consistent radiant heat from charcoal with minimal smoke or flavour contamination.
3. Remove steaks from marinade and pat very dry (residual marinade burns before the meat is cooked).
4. Grill 3–4 minutes on the first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side horizontally to the geometric centre.
6. Flip. Grill 3–4 minutes on the second side.
7. **Pull at 49–52 °C** (120–126 °F). Japanese beef fillet tradition leans closer to rare, where the Wagyu-influenced fat structure (even in non-Wagyu beef, Japanese cuts are typically well-trimmed) melts most pleasingly.
8. Rest 3 minutes. Drizzle with reserved marinade that has been briefly brought to a simmer. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (rare-to-medium-rare).

### 5. Yotam Ottolenghi — Middle Eastern Charcoal-Grilled Fillet
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Ramael Scully, *NOPI: The Cookbook* (Ebury Press, 2015), pp. 188–190  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min (+2 hr marinating) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 220 g each
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 2 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp ground allspice
- ½ tsp dried chilli flakes
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- Juice of ½ lemon
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, garlic, spices, salt, pepper, and lemon juice. Rub all over steaks. Marinate at room temperature for 2 hours.
2. Prepare a charcoal grill with good, hot coals. Clean and oil the grate.
3. Remove steaks from marinade, pat surface dry.
4. Grill over direct high heat for 4–5 minutes per side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side at the midpoint of the steak's height into the geometric centre.
6. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). Ottolenghi's Middle Eastern spice profile suits medium-rare, where the crust flavours complement a warm, pink interior.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Scatter parsley and drizzle with additional olive oil before serving. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

## What makes this method special
Charcoal grilling delivers two mechanisms that no other method combines: intense direct infrared radiation from glowing embers (which achieves faster Maillard browning than gas flames or pans) and hardwood smoke aromatic deposition. For filet mignon, the smoke dimension is particularly valuable because the cut's mild flavour absorbs the volatile aromatic compounds of the charcoal wood, adding depth that the lean muscle tissue cannot produce internally. The two-zone fire setup — direct heat for sear, indirect for gentle finish — is essential for a thick filet mignon to prevent the exterior from carbonising before the interior reaches medium-rare. The radiant heat from charcoal also produces a more evenly distributed crust than gas grill flame ports, which can leave ungrilled gaps between the bars.

## Summary
Filet mignon on a charcoal grill achieves the most flavourful expression of this lean, mild cut by adding smoky aromatic complexity from the charcoal wood to the Maillard crust. Every tradition in this leaf — American, Argentine, Japanese, and Middle Eastern — targets medium-rare at 52–57 °C, reflecting the universal understanding that the tenderloin's leanness cannot survive higher temperatures without flavour loss. The two-zone charcoal setup is essential technique for thick filet mignon to avoid charring the exterior while the centre catches up.
