# Filet Mignon × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon is the small, round, lean medallion cut from the narrow end of the psoas major (beef tenderloin). It has no marbling to speak of and no connective tissue, making it extraordinarily tender but simultaneously susceptible to drying at temperatures above medium. Oven roasting a filet mignon is typically a two-stage process: a stovetop sear creates the Maillard crust the lean exterior cannot achieve in the oven alone, followed by a transfer to the oven to gently bring the interior to the target temperature without burning the crust. The reverse-sear variant (low oven first, high-heat sear last) achieves the flattest possible temperature gradient through the small, round cross-section. Carryover is meaningful — a 4–5 cm filet mignon will rise after leaving the oven, and the pull temperature must account for this.

## Source recipes

### 1. Ina Garten — Pan-Seared Oven-Finished Filet Mignon
**Source**: Ina Garten, *Make It Ahead* (Clarkson Potter, 2014), pp. 166–168; foodnetwork.com/recipes/ina-garten  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+45 min tempering) · **Cook**: 5 min sear + 8–12 min oven · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 filet mignon steaks, 200 g each, 4 cm thick
- 1½ tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp good olive oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Remove steaks from the fridge 45 minutes ahead. Pat dry. Season all sides with salt and pepper.
2. Preheat oven to 230 °C (450 °F).
3. Heat olive oil in an oven-safe cast-iron or stainless steel skillet over the highest heat until the oil barely begins to smoke.
4. Add steaks. Sear on the first side 2–3 minutes without moving, until a deep brown crust forms. Flip. Sear 2 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of one steak horizontally to the centre while the steaks are still on the stovetop.
6. Add butter and thyme to the pan. Baste briefly for 30 seconds.
7. Transfer the skillet to the oven. Roast until the probe reads near target, approximately 8–12 minutes.
8. **Pull at 50–52 °C** (122–126 °F). Carryover of 4–5 °C during the 10-minute rest will bring the centre to 54–57 °C.
9. Rest on a warm plate for 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Gordon Ramsay — Filet Mignon with Roasted Garlic Butter (British tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012), pp. 204–206; gordonramsay.com/recipes  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 4 min sear + 6–10 min oven · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- Flaky sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 3 cloves garlic, skin on, lightly smashed
- 2 sprigs fresh rosemary

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200 °C (390 °F). Season steaks.
2. Heat oil in an oven-proof frying pan on high.
3. Sear steaks 2 minutes each side until well browned.
4. Add butter, garlic, and rosemary. Baste over medium-high heat for 1 minute.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side to the geometric centre.
6. Transfer pan to oven. Roast 5–8 minutes.
7. **Pull at 50–53 °C** (122–127 °F). Ramsay's technique accounts for carryover during the rest.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Jacques Pépin — Filet Mignon au Four (French tradition)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *Jacques Pépin: New Complete Techniques* (Black Dog & Leventhal, 2012), pp. 436–438  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 3 min sear + 8–12 min oven · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon (*médaillons de filet*), 200 g each
- Coarse salt (*gros sel*) and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*) or vegetable oil
- 15 g whole butter (for basting)
- Sauce béarnaise or *sauce au poivre*, to serve

**Method**
1. Bring fillets to room temperature 1 hour before cooking.
2. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Season steaks.
3. Heat clarified butter in an oven-safe sauté pan over high heat.
4. Sear fillets 1.5 minutes per side until browned. Add whole butter; baste briefly.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side at the midpoint of the steak's height to the centre.
6. Transfer to oven. Roast 6–10 minutes.
7. **Pull at 49–52 °C** (120–126 °F). French *rosé* (medium-rare) — the carryover will bring the steak to 52–55 °C during the mandatory rest.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Serve with béarnaise or pepper sauce. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (*rosé* — uniformly pink).

### 4. Heston Blumenthal — Reverse-Sear Filet Mignon
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011), pp. 156–160  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20–35 min low oven + 2 min sear · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon, 200 g each, 4 cm thick
- 1 tsp fine salt (applied 1 hr ahead)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp coconut oil or ghee (for sear)
- 20 g butter + 1 sprig thyme (sear baste)

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt 1 hour ahead. Preheat oven to 55 °C (131 °F).
2. Place steaks on a wire rack over a tray. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the geometric centre.
3. Place in the oven. The very low oven temperature will slowly bring the steak to the target internal temperature — this takes 25–40 minutes.
4. When the probe reads 52 °C, remove from the oven.
5. Heat coconut oil in a skillet over the highest possible heat until smoking. Sear the steak on all surfaces, 30–45 seconds per side, turning continuously.
6. Add butter and thyme in the last 30 seconds. Baste.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C** after sear (the oven pre-cooked to this temperature; the sear adds a negligible few degrees centrally but substantial surface browning).
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (precise medium-rare, uniform from edge to centre).

### 5. Tetsuya Wakuda — Oven-Roasted Wagyu Fillet (Japanese-Australian tradition)
**Source**: Tetsuya Wakuda, *Tetsuya* (Ten Speed Press, 2000), pp. 158–162; tetsuyas.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight marinating) · **Cook**: 3 min sear + 8–10 min oven · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 Wagyu beef fillet medallions, 200 g each
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp mirin
- 1 tbsp sake
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp neutral oil

**Method**
1. Marinate fillets overnight in soy, mirin, sake, and sugar. Pat completely dry before cooking.
2. Preheat oven to 190 °C (375 °F).
3. Heat neutral oil in an oven-safe skillet over high heat. Sear fillets 1.5 minutes each side.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side horizontally to the centre.
5. Transfer to oven. Roast 6–8 minutes.
6. **Pull at 46–49 °C** (115–120 °F). Wagyu fillet is ideally served closer to rare due to its exceptional marbling melting at lower temperatures.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **49–52 °C / 120–126 °F** (rare to medium-rare, with Wagyu fat fully liquefied).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA (Ina Garten) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| British (Gordon Ramsay) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| French (Jacques Pépin) | *Rosé* / medium-rare | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| British modern (Blumenthal) | Medium-rare (precise) | 53–56 °C (127–133 °F) |
| Japanese-Australian (Tetsuya) | Rare to medium-rare (Wagyu) | 49–52 °C (120–126 °F) |

The observed range is **49–57 °C (120–135 °F)**. The tight clustering of American, British, and French traditions at 52–57 °C demonstrates near-universal agreement on medium-rare. The Japanese-Australian outlier at 49–52 °C reflects the Wagyu premium: when fat content is exceptionally high, rare is the preferred doneness to ensure the fat is liquid without losing structural texture. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare` for the standard case.

## What makes this method special
The sear-then-oven method creates a deliberate two-stage heat transfer: a brief stovetop sear at very high temperature (above 180 °C pan surface) achieves the Maillard reaction in the outer 2–3 mm, while the oven provides gentle, even heat to bring the interior to the target without further external browning. This is the only method that precisely controls both surface flavour development (sear duration) and interior temperature (oven time) as independent variables. The reverse-sear variant eliminates carryover almost entirely — because the oven temperature matches the target internal temperature, there is no heat reservoir driving carryover once the steak is removed.

## Summary
Filet mignon oven roast combines a stovetop sear (for Maillard crust) with a gentle oven finish (for even interior temperature), solving the problem of cooking a very lean, delicate cut without burning the exterior before the interior reaches medium-rare. Every tradition from the USA to France, Britain, and Japan targets 49–57 °C as the final serving temperature — the narrowest possible range for any beef cut. The reverse-sear variant delivers the most precise temperature control and is especially suited to the small, uniform cross-section of a filet medallion.
