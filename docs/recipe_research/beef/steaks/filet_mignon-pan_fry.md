# Filet Mignon × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon (psoas major, narrow end) is the most tender, leanest steak in any carcass — virtually free of connective tissue and intramuscular fat. Pan frying in a small amount of oil or fat over high heat is a classic French bistro method for fillet steak, producing a full-contact Maillard crust on both faces while the lean interior heats gently from conductive transfer through the crust. Because filet mignon has no fat to render and self-baste, pan frying must rely on added fats (butter, oil, or bone marrow) to replicate the basting function. Carryover is minimal for a small, dense medallion on a pan-fry, where removing the steak from the hot metal surface stops the conductive heat transfer almost immediately.

## Source recipes

### 1. Julia Child — Pan-Fried Filet de Bœuf (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, Louisette Bertholle & Simone Beck, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961), pp. 296–300  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet medallions (*médaillons de filet*), 175–200 g each, 2.5–3 cm thick
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*)
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (to prevent burning)
- 15 g unsalted whole butter (added after sear)
- Optional: 2 tbsp Cognac, 120 ml beef stock (for a quick pan sauce)

**Method**
1. Remove fillets from the fridge 1 hour before cooking. Pat completely dry.
2. Season both sides and the edges with salt and pepper at the last moment — not earlier, to prevent moisture weeping.
3. In a large, heavy skillet, heat clarified butter and oil over medium-high heat until the fat begins to sizzle actively. The dual-fat approach: oil raises the smoke point of the butter.
4. Place fillets in the pan. Do not move. Sear 2–3 minutes on the first side — a moderately hot pan, not screaming-hot. Child favours even browning over aggressive char.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side horizontally to the geometric centre of the medallion.
6. Flip. Sear 2–3 minutes on the second side.
7. Add the whole butter. Baste the fillets briefly with the foaming butter.
8. **Pull at 49–52 °C** (120–126 °F) for *saignant* (bistro rare) to *rosé* (medium-rare).
9. Rest 3 minutes on a warm plate. Deglaze the pan with Cognac and stock for a quick sauce if desired. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (*rosé*).

### 2. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Fried Filet Mignon with Butter Baste
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 398–402; seriouseats.com/filet-mignon  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine optional) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon, 200 g each, 4 cm thick
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, lightly crushed
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Season steaks. Heat a cast-iron skillet over the highest heat for 3 minutes. Add oil.
2. Add steaks. Sear 30 seconds, flip. Continue flipping every 30 seconds — the frequent flip method creates even crust development.
3. After 2 flips, insert the MEATER probe from the side to the centre.
4. After 3–4 total minutes, add butter, garlic, and thyme. Tilt the pan and baste continuously with the foaming butter.
5. **Pull at 50–53 °C** (122–127 °F). The frequent flip produces a more even temperature gradient — carryover is only minimal.
6. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Marco Pierre White — Fillet Steak in the Pan (British tradition)
**Source**: Marco Pierre White, *White Heat* (Mitchell Beazley, 1990), pp. 88–92; originally demonstrated at Harveys  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 5–7 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- Sea salt and black pepper
- 2 tbsp clarified butter
- 30 g unsalted butter (for basting)
- 1 sprig thyme
- 2 cloves garlic, unpeeled

**Method**
1. Season steaks aggressively.
2. Heat clarified butter in a heavy pan until it begins to smoke — MPW prefers extremely high heat.
3. Place fillets. Sear 2 minutes without touching.
4. Flip. Add unsalted butter, thyme, garlic. Baste continuously.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side after the first flip.
6. **Pull at 48–51 °C** (118–124 °F). MPW's bistro tradition runs rarer than the American school — true bistro fillet is served *saignant*.
7. Rest 5 minutes in a warm place. Final serving temperature: **51–54 °C / 124–129 °F** (*saignant* to *rosé*).

### 4. Masaharu Morimoto — Iron Chef–Style Pan-Fried Beef Fillet (Japanese-American tradition)
**Source**: Masaharu Morimoto, *Mastering the Art of Japanese Home Cooking* (Ecco, 2016), pp. 188–190; morimotorestaurants.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min marinate) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- 2 tbsp ponzu sauce
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 1 tsp neutral oil (for pan)
- Wasabi and grated daikon, to serve

**Method**
1. Marinate steaks in ponzu, soy, sesame oil, and ginger for 30 minutes. Pat dry.
2. Heat neutral oil in a heavy pan over high heat.
3. Sear steaks 3 minutes first side.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side to the centre.
5. Flip. Cook 2–3 minutes.
6. **Pull at 50–53 °C** (122–127 °F). Japanese beef fillet — even non-Wagyu — is typically served at the rare end of medium-rare.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Serve with wasabi and grated daikon. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Nagi Maehashi (RecipeTin Eats) — Pan-Fried Eye Fillet Steak (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, *RecipeTin Eats: Dinner* (Macmillan Australia, 2022), pp. 224–226; recipetineats.com/eye-fillet-steak  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3–5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 eye fillet steaks, 200 g each (Australian premium grass-fed)
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp black pepper
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 30 g butter
- 2 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 sprigs thyme or rosemary

**Method**
1. Pat steaks dry, season.
2. Heat oil in a stainless steel or cast-iron pan over high until smoking.
3. Add steaks. Sear 2 minutes first side.
4. Flip. Add butter, garlic, herbs. Baste over medium-high heat.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side horizontally to the centre.
6. Continue 2–3 minutes, basting.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Australian eye fillet is typically served medium-rare; the grass-fed meat can be slightly firmer than grain-fed, making precise temperature critical.
8. Rest 3–5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| French bistro (Julia Child) | *Rosé* / medium-rare | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| USA / Serious Eats (Kenji) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| British bistro (Marco Pierre White) | *Saignant* to *rosé* | 51–54 °C (124–129 °F) |
| Japanese-American (Morimoto) | Medium-rare (rare end) | 53–56 °C (127–133 °F) |
| Australian (Nagi Maehashi) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |

The observed range is **51–57 °C (124–135 °F)**, with the French and British sources favouring the rare-to-medium-rare end (51–55 °C) and the American and Australian sources at the medium-rare target (54–57 °C). All traditions are within the cooking_data.py `medium_rare` band. The slight divergence reflects pan-frying tradition: French bistro pan frying runs somewhat rarer than American home cooking. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`.

## What makes this method special
Pan frying in clarified butter or oil produces full-surface conduction on both flat faces — the broadest possible Maillard crust for a medallion shape. Butter basting (adding whole butter during the cook and tilting the pan to pour it over the steak repeatedly) adds a flavour layer of dairy proteins and milk sugars browning at the pan surface that no other method replicates. For filet mignon, this butter layer is critical to flavour because the lean meat has no fat of its own to caramelise. The pan-fry method is also the most controllable for small, round medallions: the cook can monitor the probe, baste, and adjust heat as a single integrated action with complete visibility into the cooking process.

## Summary
Pan-fried filet mignon with butter basting is the classic French bistro preparation of the cut — it delivers a full-contact Maillard crust and the dairy-sweet flavour of browned butter on a cut that would otherwise taste mild without significant added fat. All five traditions from France, America, Britain, Japan, and Australia target medium-rare in the 51–57 °C range, with French and British sources favouring the rarer end. The butter basting step is not optional for full flavour — it compensates for the absence of intramuscular fat in the psoas major.
