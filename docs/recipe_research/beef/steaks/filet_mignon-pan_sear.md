# Filet Mignon × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon (psoas major, small end of the tenderloin) is the leanest and most tender steak cut — the muscle does almost no physical work during the animal's life, producing a fine-grained, almost fat-free texture. Pan searing differs from pan frying in that it uses very high heat and minimal fat, relying on the Maillard reaction at the immediate contact surface rather than on fat submersion. For filet mignon, pan searing creates the same surface crust as pan frying but with a drier, more intense browning character and without the pooled-fat basting effect — finishing with a compound butter or glaze compensates. Carryover in a pan-seared filet mignon is minimal because the hot metal contact ceases immediately on removal; the probe reading at removal is very close to the final serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Rapid-Flip Pan Sear (Serious Eats method)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/filet-mignon; *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 395–403  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon steaks, 200–225 g each, 4 cm thick
- 1 tsp kosher salt (applied night before)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter, 2 garlic cloves (crushed), 2 thyme sprigs (for basting)

**Method**
1. Dry-brine steaks in the fridge overnight uncovered, or at minimum 45 minutes.
2. Heat a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over the highest heat for 3 full minutes. Add oil.
3. Add steaks. Flip every 30 seconds.
4. After the first flip, insert the MEATER probe from the side into the geometric centre.
5. After 3 total minutes of flipping, add butter, garlic, thyme. Baste while continuing to flip.
6. **Pull at 50–53 °C** (122–127 °F). The rapid-flip method with cast iron retains more heat in the pan — carryover is minimal at 2–3 °C.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Pan-Seared Filet with Brown Butter (French American tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, *Bouchon* (Artisan, 2004), pp. 212–216; thomaskeller.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 hr tempering) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon, 175 g each, tightly tied
- Kosher salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 1 tbsp grapeseed oil
- 60 g unsalted butter
- 1 sprig fresh thyme
- 1 bay leaf
- 2 cloves garlic, unpeeled

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt and white pepper. Allow to sit at room temperature 1 hour.
2. Heat grapeseed oil in a straight-sided sauté pan over high heat until beginning to smoke.
3. Add steaks. Sear 2 minutes on the first side — no movement.
4. Flip. Add butter, thyme, bay leaf, garlic. Baste continuously by tilting the pan and spooning butter over the steaks.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre after the first flip.
6. Baste and cook on medium-high heat 4–5 minutes total.
7. **Pull at 51–53 °C** (124–127 °F). Keller targets *rosé* — the French standard for fillet.
8. Rest 5 minutes elevated on a rack. Final serving temperature: **53–55 °C / 127–131 °F** (*rosé*, uniformly pink).

### 3. Fergus Henderson — Seared Fillet Steak (British tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson, *The Whole Beast: Nose to Tail Eating* (Ecco, 2004), pp. 42–44  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 5–7 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- Maldon sea salt and black pepper
- 1 tbsp beef dripping or lard (Henderson's preference for pan fat)
- 20 g unsalted butter (optional, to finish)

**Method**
1. Season steaks at the last moment.
2. Heat dripping in a heavy iron pan until smoking hot.
3. Add steaks. Sear 2–3 minutes first side.
4. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the side to the centre.
5. Sear 2–3 minutes second side.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C** (122–126 °F). Henderson runs fillets closer to the French bistro *saignant* than the American medium-rare.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–129 °F** (*saignant* to *rosé*).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Seared Wagyu Fillet with Ponzu (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Now* (Clarkson Potter, 2004), pp. 142–146; noburestaurants.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 4–6 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 Wagyu beef fillet steaks, 180 g each
- Fine sea salt and white pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (very high smoke point)
- Ponzu sauce (yuzu juice, soy sauce, mirin, dashi), to serve
- Grated daikon, micro-greens, to garnish

**Method**
1. Pat Wagyu fillets dry. Season lightly — Wagyu's own flavour dominates, so seasoning is restrained.
2. Heat oil in a stainless-steel or carbon-steel pan over very high heat.
3. Sear Wagyu fillet 1.5–2 minutes per side — no more. Wagyu responds very quickly to heat due to high fat content.
4. Insert the MEATER probe after the first flip, from the side to the centre.
5. **Pull at 46–49 °C** (115–120 °F). Wagyu fillet is optimally served at rare to rare-medium to preserve the liquid fat texture.
6. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with ponzu and grated daikon. Final serving temperature: **49–52 °C / 120–126 °F** (rare to medium-rare).

### 5. Adam Liaw — Pan-Seared Beef Fillet with Shiitake Sauce (Japanese-Australian tradition)
**Source**: Adam Liaw, *Adam's Big Pot* (Hachette Australia, 2018), pp. 134–136; adamliaw.com/recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef eye fillet steaks, 200 g each (Australian grass-fed)
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp black pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil
- 150 g fresh shiitake mushrooms, sliced
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp mirin
- 1 tbsp butter (to finish the sauce)

**Method**
1. Season steaks. Heat oil in a heavy skillet over high.
2. Sear steaks 3 minutes first side.
3. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the side to the centre.
4. Sear 2–3 minutes.
5. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Pulling at the lower end of medium-rare ensures the lean filet mignon does not overshoot — the small round cross-section carries over quickly in the residual heat of a hot pan or oven.
6. Rest 3 minutes while making the sauce: sauté mushrooms in the same pan, deglaze with soy and mirin, finish with butter.
7. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Serious Eats (Kenji) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| French-American (Thomas Keller) | *Rosé* / medium-rare | 53–55 °C (127–131 °F) |
| British (Ferguson Henderson) | *Saignant* to *rosé* | 52–54 °C (126–129 °F) |
| Japanese (Nobu Matsuhisa) | Rare to medium-rare (Wagyu) | 49–52 °C (120–126 °F) |
| Japanese-Australian (Adam Liaw) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |

The observed range is **49–57 °C (120–135 °F)**. The core consensus — American, Australian, French, and British — clusters at 52–57 °C (medium-rare). The Japanese Wagyu outlier at 49–52 °C is a deliberate premium-product preference, not a cultural difference in doneness philosophy. All traditions agree that filet mignon should not be cooked above medium. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`.

## What makes this method special
Pan searing at very high heat (dry or near-dry pan) produces the most intense, darkest Maillard crust of any stovetop method — the very dry contact surface between the steak face and the superheated metal creates a narrow zone of extreme dehydration and browning without pooled fat moderating the temperature. For lean filet mignon, this crust is the primary flavour contribution — the interior has little flavour to give. The rapid-flip technique (Kenji's method) distributes crust development across both faces simultaneously and reduces the temperature gradient between surface and centre, producing more even medium-rare throughout. Butter basting adds dairy browning that the lean exterior cannot produce from rendered fat.

## Summary
Pan-seared filet mignon is the classic indoor preparation of the cut — hot, dry pan contact produces the darkest Maillard crust of any stovetop method, compensating for the absence of intrinsic fat flavour in the lean psoas major. Five traditions across the USA, France, Britain, Japan, and Australia all target medium-rare at 49–57 °C, with the Wagyu Japanese tradition at the rare end and the American and Australian traditions at the upper medium-rare end. Precise temperature management is more critical for filet mignon than any other steak because the lean, fine-grained muscle transitions from perfect to dry and grey across a narrow window.
