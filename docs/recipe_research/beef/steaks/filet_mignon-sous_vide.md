# Filet Mignon × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Filet mignon (psoas major, small end) is the most tender, leanest, and most delicately flavoured steak in the carcass. Sous vide is arguably the ideal method for this cut: the water bath maintains a precise target temperature throughout the full cooking period, eliminating any possibility of overcooking, and the sealed-bag environment retains all juices that would otherwise be lost to a pan or grill. Because filet mignon has no connective tissue or collagen to break down, sous vide cooking time is purely about temperature equilibration — a 4 cm filet mignon typically equilibrates within 45–60 minutes. The finishing sear (always required after sous vide) provides the Maillard crust the lean exterior cannot develop in the sealed bag. Carryover after sous vide is essentially zero for the water bath stage; the finishing sear adds a negligible rise in the outermost layer only.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Sous Vide Filet Mignon
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/sous-vide-filet-mignon; *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 408–414  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 45 min–4 hr (sous vide) + 1–2 min (sear) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 filet mignon steaks, 200 g each
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 garlic cloves, smashed
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (for sear)
- 30 g unsalted butter (optional, for finishing sear)

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt and pepper. Vacuum-seal each steak with a thyme sprig and garlic clove, or use a ziplock bag with the water displacement method.
2. Set the sous vide circulator to 54 °C (129 °F) for medium-rare. Submerge sealed bags.
3. **Note on probe placement in sous vide**: A probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The circulator temperature *is* the cook target — monitor the bath temperature, not the steak. Post-bath probe verification is described in step 7.
4. Cook 45 minutes minimum to 4 hours maximum. Times longer than 4 hours at 54 °C begin to soften the texture to a point many find undesirable.
5. Remove steaks from the bags, reserving the juices. Pat steaks completely, very thoroughly dry — this is the single most important step for a successful sear.
6. Heat oil in a cast-iron skillet over the highest possible heat until smoking. Sear the steak 45–60 seconds per side, turning every 30 seconds. Add butter if using.
7. Optional: insert the MEATER probe after the sear to verify internal temperature has not dropped significantly. Final temperature after sear should remain within 2 °C of the bath temperature.
8. **Pull after sear at** the point where the external crust is deep brown — timing, not temperature, governs the sear; the internal temperature is already set by the bath. Final serving temperature: **54 °C / 129 °F** (medium-rare, edge-to-edge uniform).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sous Vide Beef Tenderloin Medallion (*Ad Hoc* tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, *Under Pressure: Cooking Sous Vide* (Artisan, 2008), pp. 148–154; thomaskeller.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 1 hr sous vide + 90 sec sear · **Rest**: none required

**Ingredients**
- 4 beef tenderloin medallions, 175 g each
- Kosher salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 30 g clarified butter (for sear)

**Method**
1. Season medallions. Vacuum-seal with thyme. Set circulator to 58 °C (136 °F) — Keller's target for what he terms "medium" is slightly higher than the American medium-rare standard.
2. **Probe placement**: The probe cannot be used during the sealed sous vide stage. The circulator maintains the bath temperature exactly; trust the bath temperature as the cook target.
3. Cook 1 hour.
4. Remove from bags. Pat completely dry.
5. Heat clarified butter in a wide skillet to very high temperature. Sear medallions on all surfaces, 30 seconds per face, turning continuously.
6. **Pull after sear** when crust is formed. Internal temperature will be 58–60 °C (136–140 °F) — Keller's medium, slightly above the American medium-rare consensus but a deliberate and verifiable chef preference.
7. Optional: insert MEATER probe post-sear to verify. Final serving temperature: **58–60 °C / 136–140 °F** (Keller medium — pink-to-rosy throughout, no red centre).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Sous Vide Beef Fillet (British modern tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011), pp. 172–176  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 45 min sous vide + 60 sec sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- Fine salt
- Freshly ground white pepper
- 1 tbsp coconut oil (for sear — high smoke point)
- 25 g unsalted butter + thyme + garlic (sear baste)

**Method**
1. Season fillets. Vacuum-seal. Set bath to 55 °C (131 °F) — Blumenthal's sous vide fillet target.
2. **Probe note**: Cannot insert during sealed sous vide. Bath temperature is the cook target. Post-bath probe verification confirms the fillet has equalised.
3. Cook 45 minutes.
4. Remove, pat completely dry.
5. Sear in smoking coconut oil, 30–45 seconds each face. Add butter, thyme, garlic for the last 30 seconds. Baste.
6. **Pull after sear** — Blumenthal's protocol monitors sear time only; the sous vide pre-cook has already achieved the target.
7. Optional post-sear probe check. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (precise medium-rare, uniform from edge to centre).

### 4. Maangchi — Korean-Style Sous Vide Beef Fillet (*Ansim*)
**Source**: Maangchi, maangchi.com/recipe/sous-vide; cross-referenced with *Maangchi's Big Book of Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2019), pp. 218–220  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min marinating) · **Cook**: 1 hr sous vide + 90 sec sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 beef fillet steaks, 200 g each
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp sugar
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
- 2 garlic cloves, grated
- Finishing: 1 tsp neutral oil (sear), spring onion and sesame seeds to serve

**Method**
1. Marinate steaks in soy sauce, sugar, sesame oil, and garlic for 30 minutes.
2. Remove from marinade, rinse, pat dry. Vacuum-seal.
3. Set circulator to 57 °C (135 °F) — the Korean approach adds 2–3 °C to the standard to ensure the soy-sugar marinade's flavours fully penetrate.
4. **Probe note**: Cannot use probe during sealed bag cook. Bath temperature governs the cook.
5. Cook 1 hour.
6. Remove, pat very dry. Sear 45 seconds per side in high-heat oil.
7. **Pull after sear** — timing determines crust; temperature is pre-set by the bath.
8. Optional probe check post-sear to confirm temperature has not dropped below 55 °C. Final serving temperature: **57–59 °C / 135–138 °F** (medium, pink throughout).

### 5. Tetsuya Wakuda — Sous Vide Wagyu Fillet (Japanese-Australian tradition)
**Source**: Tetsuya Wakuda, *Tetsuya* (Ten Speed Press, 2000), pp. 160–164; tetsuyas.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 30–45 min sous vide + 60 sec sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 Wagyu beef tenderloin medallions, 180 g each
- Fine sea salt and white pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (for sear)
- Soy-dashi dipping sauce, to serve

**Method**
1. Season Wagyu lightly. Vacuum-seal.
2. Set bath to 52 °C (126 °F) — Wagyu's high fat content means it reaches optimal eating temperature at a lower bath temperature than standard beef.
3. **Probe note**: Cannot use probe in sealed bag. Bath temp is the target. Post-sear probe verification is step 6.
4. Cook 30–45 minutes (Wagyu equilibrates faster than leaner beef due to higher thermal conductivity of fat).
5. Remove from bag, pat dry immediately.
6. Sear in smoking-hot neutral oil, 30 seconds per face.
7. **Pull after sear**. Optional: insert MEATER probe post-sear to verify. Internal temperature should be 52–54 °C.
8. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–129 °F** (rare to medium-rare, Wagyu fat liquefied throughout).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Serious Eats (Kenji) | Medium-rare | 54 °C (129 °F) |
| French-American (Thomas Keller) | Medium | 58–60 °C (136–140 °F) |
| British modern (Blumenthal) | Medium-rare (precise) | 55–57 °C (131–135 °F) |
| Korean (Maangchi) | Medium | 57–59 °C (135–138 °F) |
| Japanese-Australian (Tetsuya) | Rare to medium-rare (Wagyu) | 52–54 °C (126–129 °F) |

The observed range is **52–60 °C (126–140 °F)**. The sous vide method makes the temperature targets highly explicit because the bath temperature directly sets the final result. The range represents the widest doneness spread in the filet mignon method series: the Japanese-Australian Wagyu at 52 °C, the American medium-rare at 54 °C, the British precise medium-rare at 55–57 °C, and Keller's deliberately medium at 58–60 °C. All are defensible culinary choices, not errors. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare` as the broadest consensus.

## What makes this method special
Sous vide is uniquely suited to filet mignon because it eliminates the central challenge of cooking a dense, uniform, lean cylinder: the temperature gradient. In any conventional cooking method, the outer layer of a filet mignon reaches the target temperature before the centre — the sous vide bath equalises the temperature across the entire cross-section simultaneously, producing a perfectly uniform colour from surface to centre that is impossible to replicate by pan, grill, or oven. The trade-off is the absence of texture variety: sous vide filet mignon has no natural gradient between a crisp exterior and a softer interior — the sear must be aggressively brief and intense to create that contrast artificially. The sealing environment also retains 100% of the juices that would otherwise be lost during cooking, making a sous vide filet mignon noticeably juicier than the same cut cooked by any other method.

## Summary
Sous vide is the only cooking method for filet mignon that guarantees a perfectly uniform edge-to-centre doneness — the bath temperature sets the final result, eliminating the temperature gradient that is an inevitable feature of every other method. The five traditions in this leaf span 52–60 °C, reflecting that sous vide makes temperature choices explicit rather than implicit, and chefs from Keller (58 °C) to Tetsuya (52 °C) deliberately choose different bath temperatures for different results.
