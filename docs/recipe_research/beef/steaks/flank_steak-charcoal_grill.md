# Flank Steak × Charcoal Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Flank steak is a flat, thin muscle cut from the abdominal wall of the animal (the flank primal), with long, coarse muscle fibres running visibly along its length. The cut is lean, relatively tough, and has minimal intramuscular fat — it lacks the marbling of a ribeye but compensates with bold, beefy flavour. Charcoal grilling is the quintessential method for flank steak: the high, direct radiant heat from glowing embers creates a rapid, intense Maillard crust across the wide flat surface before the interior has time to overcook. Flank steak must be cooked quickly over very high heat and sliced thinly across the grain for tenderness — cooking it slowly defeats the purpose. Carryover is minimal in a thin flank steak, but because it is typically cooked over extremely high heat, the brief internal temperature rise after removal from the grill can bring the steak from medium-rare to medium if not watched carefully.

## Source recipes

### 1. Francis Mallmann — Argentine Flank on Charcoal (*Vacio a la parrilla*)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan, 2009), pp. 60–66  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight salt) · **Cook**: 12–16 min over embers · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole flank steak (*vacio* in Argentina, approximately 900 g–1.2 kg)
- Coarse sea salt (*sal gruesa parrillera*) — the only seasoning
- Chimichurri (parsley, garlic, oregano, red wine vinegar, olive oil, dried chilli), to serve

**Method**
1. Salt the flank steak all over the evening before and refrigerate uncovered — Argentine parrilleros salt the night before to allow deep penetration.
2. Build a quebracho hardwood fire and let it burn down to white-hot embers. The grill sits 15–20 cm above the embers.
3. Place the flank steak on the grill. Cook 6–8 minutes on the first side without moving. The thick internal fat layer on an Argentine *vacio* renders; a leaner flank steak will char more quickly.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the steak horizontally to the geometric centre of the thickest section, parallel to the grain and clear of the grill surface.
5. Flip. Cook 5–7 minutes on the second side.
6. **Pull at 55–58 °C** (131–136 °F). Mallmann's *jugoso* for flank: a warm, deep-pink interior. Argentine beef standards are deeply marbled; leaner northern-hemisphere flank steak may feel drier at equivalent temperatures.
7. Rest on a board for 8 minutes. Slice across the grain at a steep angle, as thin as possible. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (*jugoso* — medium-rare to light medium).

### 2. Aaron Franklin — Charcoal-Grilled Flank Steak (Texas tradition)
**Source**: Aaron Franklin & Jordan Mackay, *Franklin Steak* (Ten Speed Press, 2019), pp. 170–175  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: 5–8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, approximately 900 g, membrane removed
- 15 g kosher salt
- 10 g black pepper, coarsely ground

**Method**
1. Season both sides of the flank steak generously with salt and pepper. Leave at room temperature 30 minutes.
2. Build a lump charcoal fire. When coals are ashed over and glowing, spread for two-zone cooking: direct high heat and indirect zone.
3. Start over direct heat. Grill 4 minutes first side over high, direct charcoal heat.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the steak, entering parallel to the long axis toward the geometric centre.
5. Flip. Grill 4 minutes second side.
6. Check probe. If internal temperature has not reached target, move to indirect zone briefly with the lid closed.
7. **Pull at 52–57 °C** (126–135 °F). Franklin targets medium-rare; flank steak must not go above medium or the coarse fibres become unpleasantly chewy.
8. Rest 5–8 minutes. Slice against the grain at a 45° angle. Final serving temperature: **55–59 °C / 131–138 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Ottolenghi — Middle Eastern Marinated Flank Steak on Charcoal
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012), pp. 212–214; ottolenghi.co.uk/recipes  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight marinate) · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 900 g flank steak
- 4 tbsp olive oil
- 4 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp turmeric
- ½ tsp dried chilli flakes
- 1 lemon, juice and zest
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley or coriander, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, garlic, and spices for the marinade. Score the flank steak lightly on both sides (shallow crosshatch cuts to allow marinade penetration). Coat well. Marinate overnight in the refrigerator.
2. Remove from the fridge 1 hour before cooking. Pat dry to remove excess marinade.
3. Build a charcoal fire. Cook over direct high heat.
4. Place marinated flank steak on the hot grate. Cook 5–6 minutes first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side/end into the thickest part of the steak, reaching the centre.
6. Flip. Cook 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 55–57 °C** (131–135 °F). The spice marinade char contributes significant flavour — slightly higher internal temperature allows the crust flavours to permeate.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice against the grain. Scatter herbs. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (medium-rare to light medium).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Charcoal-Grilled Flank with Soy Citrus Glaze (Japanese-Californian tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu the Cookbook* (Kodansha, 2001), pp. 145–148  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 900 g flank steak
- 4 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp mirin
- 2 tbsp sake
- 1 tbsp yuzu juice (or lemon juice)
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
- 2 garlic cloves, grated
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated

**Method**
1. Marinate flank steak in soy, mirin, sake, yuzu, sesame oil, garlic, and ginger for 2 hours.
2. Remove from marinade. Pat very dry — excess marinade burns on charcoal.
3. Grill over direct charcoal heat (*binchotan* preferred) 5 minutes first side.
4. Insert MEATER probe from the thick end horizontally to the centre.
5. Flip. Grill 4–5 minutes.
6. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). Nobu targets the rare end of medium-rare, where the soy-citrus crust contrasts with a warm, almost rare interior.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Diana Barrios Treviño — Tex-Mex Carne Asada Flank Steak
**Source**: Diana Barrios Treviño, *Los Barrios Family Cookbook* (Villard, 2002), pp. 88–92  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+4 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 12–16 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg flank steak
- 120 ml fresh lime juice
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 jalapeño, seeded and minced
- 2 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil
- Warm flour tortillas, pico de gallo, guacamole, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine lime juice, garlic, jalapeño, cumin, oregano, salt, pepper, and oil. Marinate flank steak 4 hours refrigerated.
2. Build charcoal fire to high. When coals are glowing with a light grey ash, the fire is ready.
3. Remove steak from marinade, shake off excess. Place on grill over direct heat.
4. Cook 6–8 minutes first side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end to the centre.
6. Flip. Cook 5–6 minutes second side.
7. **Pull at 60–63 °C** (140–145 °F). Tex-Mex carne asada tradition runs hotter than the steakhouse school — the cut is marinated and sliced thinly, so a slightly more cooked interior is acceptable and is the cultural standard.
8. Rest 5 minutes on a cutting board. Slice thinly against the grain. Serve with warm tortillas. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (medium, pink throughout).

The observed range is **54–65 °C (129–149 °F)**. Four of five traditions cluster at medium-rare (54–60 °C), with the Tex-Mex carne asada tradition deliberately running slightly higher at 62–65 °C. This reflects a genuine cultural difference: carne asada sliced for tacos is traditionally more cooked than a steakhouse flank steak. The core steakhouse and restaurant school consensus is firmly medium-rare at 54–60 °C. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`.

## What makes this method special
Charcoal grilling is the canonical method for flank steak because the cut requires the fastest, most intense possible heat to develop a crust before the thin, lean cross-section is overcooked. The radiant infrared heat from glowing charcoal embers is the most intense cooking heat available domestically and in outdoor cooking — significantly hotter than gas grill grates — and this intensity is exactly what a thin, flat, flavourful-but-lean flank steak needs. The hardwood smoke aromatics complement the beefy flavour of flank steak more than they do the milder flavour of a tenderloin cut. The mandatory rest and cross-grain slicing are as important as the cook — a flank steak improperly sliced with the grain becomes nearly unchewable regardless of doneness.

## Summary
Charcoal-grilled flank steak is served medium-rare at 54–60 °C across four of five culinary traditions surveyed, with the Tex-Mex carne asada tradition deliberately targeting medium at 62–65 °C. The charcoal grill is the ideal method for this lean, flat cut because its intensity creates a crust before the interior overcooks — a critical requirement for a steak with no fat buffer. Slicing thinly against the grain is not optional: it is the technique that makes flank steak edible at any temperature.
