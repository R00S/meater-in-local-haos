# Flank Steak × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Flank steak is a wide, flat cut from the abdominal muscles with visible long fibres running along its length, minimal fat, and bold beefy flavour. Pan frying in a hot skillet with a small amount of oil or fat is an excellent domestic method: the full-contact Maillard browning across the steak's wide flat surface creates a flavourful crust without requiring an outdoor grill. Because the cut is thin and wide, a flank steak can be difficult to manage in a skillet — many recipes cut it into portions for easier handling. The lean, fibrous structure makes pan-frying timing critical: above medium, the fibres contract and expel moisture rapidly, making the steak tough. Carryover after pan frying is minimal, as removing from the hot pan surface immediately stops the primary conductive heat transfer.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Fried Flank Steak (Serious Eats)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/flank-steak-pan-fry; *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 420–426  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 hr dry-brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 900 g, cut in half crosswise for easier pan management
- 1 tsp kosher salt (applied 1 hr ahead)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil

**Method**
1. Apply salt 1 hour ahead. Pat dry. Season with pepper just before cooking.
2. Heat a 30 cm cast-iron or carbon-steel skillet over the highest heat for 3 minutes. Add oil.
3. Place flank steak halves in the pan (ensure they fit without curling — press flat if needed in the first 30 seconds).
4. Cook 4–5 minutes without moving.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end horizontally to the geometric centre of the thickest portion.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). The cast-iron pan retains significant heat after the steak is removed — the residual surface contact on the bottom face can add another degree or two; pulling at the lower end of medium-rare prevents that from pushing the interior to medium.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice very thinly against the grain at an angle. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Nigel Slater — Pan-Fried Bavette (British tradition)
**Source**: Nigel Slater, *Tender, Volume 1* (Fourth Estate, 2009), pp. 356–358; theguardian.com/food  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 bavette steaks (flank steak equivalent), 200 g each
- Maldon sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp beef dripping or rapeseed oil
- 20 g unsalted butter
- 1 clove garlic, halved

**Method**
1. Season steaks generously with salt and pepper.
2. Heat dripping in a heavy pan over high heat until smoking.
3. Cook bavette 3–4 minutes first side. Press flat if curling.
4. Insert MEATER probe from the side into the thickest part.
5. Flip. Add butter and garlic. Baste briefly.
6. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). Nigel Slater's preference for bavette is distinctly rare to medium-rare.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 129–133 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Jacques Pépin — Bavette de Bœuf à la Lyonnaise (French tradition)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *La Technique* (Times Books, 1976), pp. 260–264  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak (*bavette d'aloyau*), 700 g
- Coarse salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp clarified butter
- 2 shallots, finely minced
- 120 ml dry white wine or dry vermouth
- 60 ml veal stock or beef stock
- 15 g whole unsalted butter
- 1 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Season bavette with salt and pepper at the last moment.
2. Heat clarified butter in a large, heavy-bottomed pan over high heat.
3. Sear the bavette 4 minutes each side.
4. Insert MEATER probe from the thick end to the centre.
5. Check probe. The French target is *rosé* (medium-rare).
6. **Pull at 51–54 °C** (124–129 °F). French bistros serve bavette distinctly pink — *rosé*.
7. Remove steak to rest 5 minutes. In the same pan, cook shallots briefly, deglaze with wine, reduce, add stock, finish with whole butter and parsley for a quick jus.
8. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (*rosé*).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Fried Beef Flank with Miso Glaze (Japanese-American tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Now* (Clarkson Potter, 2004), pp. 146–150  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+1 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 900 g flank steak
- 3 tbsp white miso
- 2 tbsp mirin
- 1 tbsp sake
- 1 tbsp sugar
- 1 tbsp neutral oil

**Method**
1. Whisk miso, mirin, sake, and sugar into a paste. Coat flank steak thinly. Marinate 1 hour.
2. Remove from marinade, scrape off excess miso (it burns very quickly).
3. Heat neutral oil in a heavy skillet over medium-high heat.
4. Place steak in pan. Cook 4–5 minutes first side.
5. Insert MEATER probe from the thick end to the centre.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). The miso glazed exterior creates good char at lower pan temperatures — this is important to avoid burning the miso before the interior is done.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice against the grain. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Adam Perry Lang — Pan-Fried Flank with Herb Baste (American BBQ tradition applied to pan)
**Source**: Adam Perry Lang, *Serious Barbecue* (Hyperion, 2009), pp. 172–176  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 900 g
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp black pepper
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil
- 45 g unsalted butter
- 3 cloves garlic, smashed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary

**Method**
1. Season steak with salt, pepper, and garlic powder. Let rest at room temperature 30 minutes.
2. Heat oil in a large cast-iron skillet over high heat.
3. Add steak. Press flat. Cook 5–6 minutes first side.
4. Insert MEATER probe from the thick end to the centre.
5. Flip. Add butter, garlic, thyme, rosemary. Baste.
6. **Pull at 54–57 °C** (129–135 °F). Lang's approach targets a rich, pink interior that benefits from the herb baste.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Final serving temperature: **56–59 °C / 133–138 °F** (medium-rare to light medium).

The observed range is **53–59 °C (127–138 °F)** — one of the tightest consensuses in the flank steak series. All five traditions target medium-rare, and the French bistro tradition (*rosé*) sits at the same temperature the American school calls medium-rare. There is no outlier targeting medium or well-done in this collection. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare`.

## What makes this method special
Pan frying a flank steak in a cast-iron or steel skillet produces the broadest possible Maillard contact area on both flat faces — significantly more complete browning than a grill, which leaves the gaps between the bars unseared. The solid pan surface also allows the cook to observe the sear progression in real time and respond to the probe reading with immediate heat adjustment. Butter basting during the final minutes of cooking adds a layer of dairy Maillard products — caramelised milk proteins that no direct-heat method produces — elevating the flavour of an otherwise plain lean steak. The pan's retained heat makes the flip timing critical: the second side of a cast-iron pan-fried flank steak often cooks faster than the first.
