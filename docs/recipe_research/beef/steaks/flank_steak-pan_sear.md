# Flank Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Flank steak (bavette d'aloyau in French bistro tradition, matambre in South America) is cut from the abdominal wall muscles — lean, intensely beefy, with long parallel muscle fibres that run the full length of the cut. Unlike marbled steaks, flank has almost no intramuscular fat, so high heat from a very hot pan creates crust through the Maillard reaction alone rather than through fat rendering. Pan searing suits flank well because the flat, wide cut covers a large contact surface at once, producing a fast, even sear on both faces. The cut's leanness means temperature discipline is critical: above 60 °C the long fibres tighten and the steak becomes dry and stringy regardless of resting. Carryover is minimal — 2–3 °C at most — because the thin cross-section loses heat quickly after leaving the pan.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Seared Flank Steak (Serious Eats)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/pan-seared-flank-steak; *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 432–436  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+45 min dry-brine) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 700–900 g
- 1 tsp kosher salt (applied 45 min before cooking)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (high smoke-point)

**Method**
1. Season both sides with salt 45 minutes before cooking. The brine draws out moisture that dissolves the salt and then reabsorbs — improving seasoning depth and surface browning.
2. Pat the steak completely dry with paper towels. A dry surface is essential for the Maillard reaction in a pan with no pooled fat.
3. Heat a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over the highest heat for 3–4 minutes until smoking.
4. Add oil and immediately place the steak in the pan.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes first side, pressing down with a spatula if the steak arches.
6. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end, angled through the long axis to reach the geometric centre. Avoid the thin tapered end — it will overshoot temperature fastest.
7. Flip. Cook 2–3 minutes second side.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C** (126–129 °F). "Flank steak has no fat to compensate — at 57 °C it noticeably loses moisture; at 130 °F you're at the top of the safe medium-rare zone before fibre tightening begins." — Kenji
9. Rest 5 minutes on a carving board. Slice thinly across the grain at a 45° angle. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Stéphane Reynaud — Bavette à l'échalote (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: Stéphane Reynaud, *Bistrot* (Marabout, 2012), pp. 114–115; stephanereynaud.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 4–5 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 bavette (flank) steaks, 180–220 g each, cut to serving portions
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter
- 3 shallots, finely sliced
- 100 ml dry red wine (Bordeaux preferred)
- 50 ml veal stock
- 1 tsp fine salt
- ½ tsp coarsely ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil

**Method**
1. Season bavette with salt and pepper.
2. Heat oil and 1 tbsp butter in a heavy iron pan (la poêle en fonte) until the butter foam subsides and the pan is very hot.
3. Lay bavette steaks flat. Sear 2 minutes first side undisturbed.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side edge into the thickest part. In French bistro cooking the bavette is cut thin to order — insert the probe parallel to the cutting board, aimed at the centre.
5. Flip. Sear 1.5–2 minutes second side.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C** (122–126 °F). *Saignante vif* (vivid rare) — "La bavette se mange saignante, point barre. Au-delà de 55 °C elle devient dure et filandreuse." ("Bavette is eaten rare, full stop. Above 55 °C it becomes tough and stringy.")
7. Rest 3 minutes.
8. In the same pan, sauté shallots in remaining butter 2 minutes. Deglaze with red wine, reduce by half. Add veal stock, reduce 1 minute. Spoon over bavette. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (*saignante*, rare to medium-rare).

### 3. Francis Mallmann — Plancha-Seared Matambre (Argentine tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan, 2009), pp. 168–172  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 matambre (flank steak), 1–1.2 kg
- 60 ml red wine vinegar
- 3 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp sweet smoked paprika
- 1 tsp coarse salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp olive oil

**Method**
1. Combine vinegar, garlic, oregano, paprika, salt, pepper, and oil. Score the flank steak surface lightly in a crosshatch. Marinate 2 hours at room temperature.
2. Remove from marinade, pat completely dry.
3. Heat a cast-iron griddle (*plancha*) until smoking hot. The very high surface temperature is what Mallmann calls *el fuego intenso* — intense fire.
4. Lay the matambre flat on the plancha. Sear 3–4 minutes first side without moving.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end toward the centre.
6. Flip. Sear 3 minutes second side.
7. **Pull at 55–57 °C** (131–135 °F). Argentine *a punto jugoso* — "Jugoso means the meat still releases juice when cut, not that it is raw in the centre. For matambre the upper ceiling is 58 °C."
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice against the grain. Serve with *chimichurri* and crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (medium).

### 4. Fergus Henderson — Pan-Fried Bavette with Anchovy Butter (British nose-to-tail tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson & Justin Piers Gellatly, *Beyond Nose to Tail* (Bloomsbury, 2007), pp. 48–50  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 5–6 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 bavette steak, 500–600 g
- 1 tbsp lard or dripping (Henderson's preferred fat for pan searing)
- Fleur de sel
- Cracked black pepper

**Anchovy butter**
- 50 g unsalted butter, softened
- 4 anchovy fillets (salted, rinsed)
- 1 tsp flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- ½ tsp lemon zest

**Method**
1. Prepare anchovy butter by mashing anchovies into softened butter with parsley and lemon zest. Roll in cling film, refrigerate.
2. Heat lard in a heavy iron pan until haze appears. Season bavette with salt and pepper just before cooking.
3. Place bavette in the pan. Press with a weight. Sear 3 minutes first side.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick edge, parallel to the cutting board, to the geometric centre.
5. Flip. Sear 2–3 minutes.
6. **Pull at 52–55 °C** (126–131 °F). Henderson's tradition: "Bavette must be pink throughout — if it is not, the cook has failed the animal and the diner."
7. Rest 5 minutes. Place a disc of anchovy butter on top. Slice against the grain. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Maangchi — Pan-Seared Bulgogi-Style Flank (Korean tradition)
**Source**: Maangchi, *Maangchi's Real Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2015), pp. 234–238; maangchi.com/recipe/bulgogi-whole-flank  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+2 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g flank steak
- 4 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 tbsp sugar
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
- 2 cloves garlic, grated
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 1 Asian pear, grated (enzyme tenderiser)
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (for pan)

**Method**
1. Combine soy sauce, sugar, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, pear, and pepper. Marinate flank steak 2 hours refrigerated.
2. Remove from marinade, pat dry. Do not rinse — the residual marinade caramelises in the pan.
3. Heat a cast-iron pan or heavy skillet over high heat with vegetable oil until smoking.
4. Lay the steak flat. Sear 3–4 minutes first side without moving — the sugar in the marinade will caramelise quickly.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end to the centre.
6. Flip. Sear 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 57–60 °C** (135–140 °F). Korean *bulgogi* pan-sear tradition targets medium doneness — the soy-pear caramelised crust carries the flavour; the interior provides contrast. "Serve pink inside, charred outside."
8. Rest 3 minutes. Slice against the grain and serve with steamed rice and *banchan*. Final serving temperature: **59–62 °C / 138–144 °F** (medium).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Kenji (ATK) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| French bistro (Reynaud) | *Saignante* (rare-plus) | 52–55 °C (126–131 °F) |
| Argentine (Mallmann) | *A punto jugoso* (medium) | 57–60 °C (135–140 °F) |
| British nose-to-tail (Henderson) | Medium-rare | 54–57 °C (129–135 °F) |
| Korean (Maangchi) | Medium | 59–62 °C (138–144 °F) |

The observed range spans **52–62 °C (126–144 °F)**. The French bistro tradition is the lowest, targeting vivid rare (52–55 °C) because French chefs argue the lean fibres are most palatable at that temperature. The American and British traditions cluster at 54–57 °C (medium-rare), while the Argentine and Korean traditions sit at 57–62 °C (medium) — both cultures serve this cut as part of a meal with accompaniments that carry sauce or seasoning, making a slightly warmer, firmer interior acceptable. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare` as the broadest English-language consensus; the French tradition sits a few degrees below it and the Argentine/Korean traditions a few degrees above.

## What makes this method special
Pan searing gives flank steak the fastest possible crust without additional fat pooling — a cast-iron pan at high heat transfers far more energy per second than a grill grate, and the full flat contact surface means the entire steak face browns simultaneously rather than just where it touches grill bars. For a thin, lean cut that is close to ruined at 60 °C, speed matters: the faster the crust forms, the less the interior has been cooked beyond the target. The lack of ambient heat (no oven, no grill surround) means removal from the pan halts cooking almost immediately, keeping carryover minimal — pull temperature and final serving temperature are very close. The absence of fat in the muscle also means the cook must provide fat in some other form — butter basting, anchovy butter, or marinade caramelisation — to achieve the richness that the muscle itself cannot supply.

## Summary
Pan-seared flank steak achieves the fastest, most even crust of any indoor method — the entire flat face browns simultaneously on a screaming-hot cast-iron surface. The French bistro tradition insists on vivid rare (52–55 °C) to keep the lean fibres tender, while American and Korean traditions accept medium (57–62 °C) because accompaniments carry the flavour. The critical discipline for all traditions is the same: do not exceed 60 °C, and always slice thinly across the grain.
