# Flank Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Flank steak (US), bavette d'aloyau (France), matambre (South America) is from the
abdominal wall — lean, long-fibred, intensely flavoured. No significant intramuscular
fat — marbling does NOT influence the optimal temperature the way it does for ribeye.
This cut goes tough and dry above 60°C regardless of method.

## Source recipes and what they actually say

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats — Pan-Seared Flank Steak (USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/pan-seared-flank-steak
- **Temperature cited**: **130°F / 54°C**
- Kenji uses the lower end of medium-rare. Quote: "flank steak is best at 130°F.
  At 135°F it begins losing moisture noticeably, because unlike ribeye there is no
  fat to compensate."
- No basting recommended — the lean muscle has nothing to render.

### 2. Stéphane Reynaud, Bistrot de Cuisine — Bavette à l'échalote (France)
- **Source**: stephanereynaud.com/bavette-echalote (published cookbook Bistrot)
- **Temperature cited**: **50-52°C** (saignant vif — vivid rare)
- Reynaud's bavette recipe is the French bistro classic. "La bavette se mange saignante,
  point barre. Au-delà de 55°C elle devient dure et filandreuse."
  ("Bavette is eaten rare, full stop. Above 55°C it becomes tough and stringy.")
- This is not a stylistic preference — Reynaud frames it as a quality requirement.

### 3. Bon Appétit — Flank Steak with Chimichurri (USA)
- **Source**: bonappetit.com/recipe/flank-steak-chimichurri
- **Temperature cited**: **130-135°F / 54-57°C**
- Standard US range. "Pull at 130°F for medium-rare."

### 4. BBC Good Food — Pan-fried bavette steak (UK)
- **Source**: bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/pan-fried-bavette-steak
- **Temperature cited**: **54°C** ("54°C for medium-rare, no higher for this cut")
- Note UK adopts the lower end of the US range. Explicitly warns: "don't go to 57°C
  or the fibres tighten."

### 5. Recetas de Argentina — Matambre a la plancha (Argentina)
- **Source**: recetasdeargentina.com.ar/matambre-plancha
- **Temperature cited**: **55-58°C** (a punto / jugoso)
- Argentine tradition uses slightly higher temp than French, but "jugoso" (juicy) still
  means pink — not the US "medium-well". 58°C is their upper ceiling.

## Genuine findings

**There is a real split:**
- French bistro tradition: **50-52°C** — Reynaud is not an outlier; this is the canonical
  French bavette temperature. The cut is specifically identified as one that must be rare.
- US/UK scientific-method cooks: **54°C** lower end, 57°C upper end
- Argentine: **55-58°C** — culturally accept a warmer pink

**The lower-end of medium-rare (54°C) is better supported than the upper end (57°C) for
this cut** — two independent sources (Kenji + BBC) specifically call out that 57°C causes
moisture loss, unlike a ribeye where fat compensates.

**Pan sear vs other methods**: No evidence that pan sear changes the target vs grill for
this lean cut. The heat source doesn't change the fibre behaviour.

## Verdict
- **Recommended target**: 54°C (lower end of medium-rare)
- **French tradition**: 50-52°C — genuinely valid, not an outlier
- **Upper ceiling**: 57°C — acceptable but noticeable moisture loss (confirmed by 2 sources)
- **Above 60°C**: universally condemned for this cut
