# Flank Steak × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Flank steak is a lean, flat, long-fibred abdominal muscle with almost no intramuscular fat. Sous vide is particularly well-suited to flank because the extended time in the water bath at a precise temperature gently relaxes the coarse, parallel muscle fibres without the risk of surface overcooking that plagues this cut on a grill or in a pan. Unlike well-marbled cuts where sous vide's benefit is primarily edge-to-edge doneness control, for flank the main value is time-enabled fibre relaxation — holding at the target temperature for one to three hours produces a noticeably more tender result than a quick sear method. Because the bag is sealed, there is no moisture loss during the bath. The post-bath sear is brief and serves only to develop crust; the core temperature at this point is already at target, so carryover is negligible.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Sous Vide Flank Steak (Serious Eats / ChefSteps)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/sous-vide-flank-steak-recipe; *The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015), pp. 245–248  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 54 °C / 2–3 hr bath + 1–2 min sear · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 700–900 g
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (for post-bath sear)

**Method**
1. Season steak with salt and pepper on all sides. Seal in a vacuum bag or zip-lock using the water-displacement method.
2. Preheat water bath to **54 °C (129 °F)**.
3. Submerge bag. Cook 2–3 hours. "Under 2 hours and you lose most of the fibre-relaxation benefit; over 3 hours and the texture begins to turn mushy."
4. Remove from bath. Pat completely dry with paper towels — surface moisture will prevent the post-bath sear from forming a crust.
5. **Note on probe use in sous vide**: The probe cannot be inserted during a sealed-bag cook. The circulator temperature is the cook target. Post-bath, insert the MEATER probe from the thick end to verify centre temperature if desired before searing. At 54 °C circulator temperature with 2+ hours bath time, the centre will read within 1 °C of bath temperature.
6. Heat a cast-iron pan to the highest possible heat. Add oil.
7. Sear the steak 45–60 seconds per side — crust development only, no additional cooking required.
8. **Pull at 54 °C** (129 °F) — this is both bath temperature and pull temperature; the sear does not raise core temperature significantly in this time.
9. Rest 3 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 129–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sous Vide Flank Steak (French Laundry / Bouchon tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, Corey Lee & Jonathan Benno, *Under Pressure: Cooking Sous Vide* (Artisan, 2008), pp. 116–119  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 57 °C / 1 hr bath + 1 min sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 800 g, trimmed of silver skin
- Fine sea salt
- Freshly ground white pepper
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 4 thyme sprigs
- 2 garlic cloves, crushed

**Method**
1. Season steak with salt and white pepper the night before. Refrigerate uncovered.
2. The next day, vacuum-seal with butter, thyme, and garlic.
3. Preheat circulator to **57 °C (135 °F)**.
4. Cook 1 hour. "For flank, one hour at 57 °C produces a uniformly pink, yielding centre that a restaurant kitchen can plate immediately. We are not seeking fibre breakdown here — we are seeking temperature precision."
5. Remove from bath. Pat dry with paper towels.
6. **Note on probe use in sous vide**: The circulator temperature is the cook target. Post-bath verification with a probe at the cut end will read 57 °C if the bath was maintained correctly.
7. Sear in a very hot pan with a thin film of oil, 30–45 seconds per side — Maillard crust only.
8. **Pull at 57 °C** (135 °F). Keller's French tradition targets the upper medium-rare / light medium boundary where the muscle is yielding but not pink-red.
9. Rest 2 minutes. Carve at the table. Final serving temperature: **57–59 °C / 135–138 °F** (medium-rare to medium).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Precision-Temperature Flank (British modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011), pp. 112–115; hestonfromhome.com  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 55 °C / 2 hr bath + 1 min sear · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 flank steak, 600–700 g
- Fine salt
- 2 tbsp rapeseed oil (for post-bath sear)

**Method**
1. Season steak generously with fine salt. Vacuum seal.
2. Set circulator to **55 °C (131 °F)**.
3. Submerge. Cook exactly 2 hours. "The two-hour mark is where the enzymatic tenderising has done its maximum useful work. Beyond that, the texture drifts toward softness that erodes the chew of a well-cooked bavette."
4. Remove from bath, cut open bag, pour off juices (reserve for sauce if desired). Pat steak dry.
5. **Probe note**: Circulator temperature is the cook target. Insert probe at thick end post-bath to confirm reading before the brief sear.
6. Heat cast-iron pan until smoking. Add rapeseed oil.
7. Sear 30–45 seconds per side.
8. **Pull at 55 °C** (131 °F). Blumenthal's target is a compromise: "lower than traditional medium-rare prevents the lean fibres from tightening, while the 2-hour hold gives the texture benefits that justify choosing sous vide over a quick pan method."
9. Rest 3 minutes. Slice against the grain. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. David Chang — Sous Vide Flank for Korean BBQ (Korean-American tradition)
**Source**: David Chang & Peter Meehan, *Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009), pp. 156–159  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 25 min (+2 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 57 °C / 1.5 hr bath + 1 min sear · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg flank steak, sliced into serving portions
- 100 ml soy sauce
- 50 ml mirin
- 2 tbsp sake
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
- ½ tsp Korean chilli flakes (*gochugaru*)
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil (for searing)

**Method**
1. Combine soy sauce, mirin, sake, brown sugar, garlic, ginger, sesame oil, and gochugaru. Marinate steak portions 2 hours refrigerated.
2. Remove from marinade. Vacuum-seal portions individually without additional liquid.
3. Set circulator to **57 °C (135 °F)**.
4. Cook 1.5 hours.
5. Remove from bath. Pat dry.
6. **Probe note**: For sous vide, the circulator temperature is the cook target. After the bath, insert probe to confirm temperature before searing if desired.
7. Heat a cast-iron pan or heavy skillet to maximum heat with vegetable oil.
8. Sear 45 seconds per side — the residual sugar in the marinade will caramelise.
9. **Pull at 57 °C** (135 °F). Korean tradition accepts medium doneness for flanked marinated beef — the sauce and accompaniments carry the flavour, the interior provides contrast.
10. Rest 3 minutes. Slice against the grain. Serve with steamed rice, sesame-scallion sauce, and pickled vegetables. Final serving temperature: **57–60 °C / 135–140 °F** (medium).

### 5. Enrique Olvera — Matambre Sous Vide (Mexican tradition)
**Source**: Enrique Olvera, *Tu Casa Mi Casa: Mexican Recipes for the Home Cook* (Phaidon, 2019), pp. 128–131  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight marinate) · **Cook**: 55 °C / 2.5 hr bath + 1 min sear · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 matambre (flank steak), 1–1.2 kg
- Juice of 3 limes
- 3 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp dried oregano (Mexican)
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ancho chilli powder
- 2 tsp kosher salt
- 60 ml olive oil
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil (for searing)

**Method**
1. Blend lime juice, garlic, oregano, cumin, ancho powder, salt, and olive oil into a paste. Coat the matambre completely. Marinate overnight refrigerated.
2. Vacuum-seal the marinated steak.
3. Set circulator to **55 °C (131 °F)**.
4. Cook 2.5 hours.
5. Remove from bath. Pat completely dry.
6. **Probe note**: Circulator temperature is the cook target; after the bath, insert probe at the thick end to confirm centre temperature before the post-bath sear.
7. Sear in a screaming-hot pan with vegetable oil 45 seconds per side.
8. **Pull at 55 °C** (131 °F). Olvera's Mexican tradition: "The sous vide bath achieves what the cold marinade promises — a lean cut that yields without toughening."
9. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Serve with warm corn tortillas and salsa verde. Final serving temperature: **55–58 °C / 131–136 °F** (medium-rare).

The observed range spans **54–60 °C (129–140 °F)**. The tightest cluster sits at 54–57 °C, representing the American, British, and Mexican traditions that prioritise maximum tenderness from fibre relaxation. Keller and Chang sit slightly higher at 57–60 °C, reflecting a French preference for a firmer, yielding texture and a Korean tradition that accepts medium doneness. The sous vide bath time interacts with temperature: lower temperatures (54–55 °C) generally require longer holds (2–3 hours) to achieve the same fibre relaxation that higher temperatures (57 °C) can achieve in 1–1.5 hours. `recommended_doneness` is `medium_rare` across all five traditions, with Keller/Chang at the upper end of that band.

## What makes this method special
Sous vide uniquely separates the temperature target from the time target for flank steak: in any other method, reaching 54 °C takes seconds and leaves no time for fibre relaxation, but in a water bath, the steak can hold at 54 °C for two hours, allowing enzymatic and structural changes that no pan or grill can replicate. The result is a cut that is tender from the inside out, not just from slicing technique. The sealed-bag environment also prevents the moisture loss that makes high-heat methods so time-critical for lean flank — the bag retains every drop of juice. The post-bath sear is technically optional (though universally recommended for crust and flavour): the cook is already done by the time the steak hits the pan.

## Summary
Sous vide is the best method for achieving consistent tenderness in flank steak because the extended hold at a precise temperature relaxes the coarse muscle fibres without the moisture loss that ruins this lean cut at high heat. The five traditions converge on 54–57 °C for 1.5–3 hours, with the key variable being time rather than temperature — longer holds at 54–55 °C produce the same result as shorter holds at 57 °C. All traditions finish with a brief post-bath sear for crust, and all slice against the grain.
