# Flat Iron Steak × Charcoal Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron steak, cut from the infraspinatus of the shoulder blade, is one of the most marbled non-loin cuts on the carcass, making it exceptionally well-suited to the aggressive dry heat of charcoal. Charcoal produces radiant heat in the 230–300 °C range alongside aromatic smoke from combusting wood volatiles and rendered fat dripping onto the embers, layering a mineral, smoky character onto the cut's natural beefiness. Individual flat iron steaks at 2–3 cm thickness are ideal for direct charcoal: thick enough to develop a deeply charred exterior while the centre remains rosy, yet thin enough to cook through in 8–12 minutes without a two-zone setup. Carryover over live coals is more pronounced than in an oven — expect a 3–5 °C rise — because dense radiant heat continues driving surface energy inward during the first minute of rest; pulling 4–5 °C below target is advisable.

## Source recipes

### 1. Francis Mallmann — Asado de Campo Flat Iron (Argentine tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann with Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan, 2009); direct-ember technique adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~300 g each
- 10 g coarse sea salt (flor de sal or Maldon)
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly cracked black pepper
- **Chimichurri**: 40 g flat-leaf parsley (finely chopped), 3 garlic cloves (minced), 15 g dried oregano, 30 ml red wine vinegar, 60 ml olive oil, 2 g dried red chilli flakes, salt to taste

**Method**
1. Prepare a bed of hardwood charcoal (quebracho preferred). Build a thick coal bed and allow to ash over completely — no flames, just glowing embers with a white-ash coating.
2. Make chimichurri 1 hour ahead: combine all ingredients, season, and allow to macerate at room temperature.
3. Season steaks liberally with coarse salt and cracked pepper immediately before grilling; Mallmann prescribes no advance salting so the surface remains dry for crust formation.
4. Insert probe into the geometric centre of one steak from its short end, with the tip at mid-height. Rest the probe wire to one side of the grill grate away from direct heat.
5. Place steaks directly over the coals, 8–10 cm above the embers.
6. Grill 4 minutes without moving — allow a dark, crackling crust to form. Flip and grill 4 minutes on the second side.
7. **Pull at 53 °C (127 °F).** Mallmann's asado tradition targets a pink-centred, deeply charred exterior; carryover brings the core to medium-rare during rest.
8. Rest 5 minutes on a wooden board. Spoon chimichurri generously over the top. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Jan Braai (Jan Scannell) — South African Braai Flat Iron with Monkey Gland Sauce
**Source**: Jan Braai (Jan Scannell), *Fireman: A Braai Book for Real Men* (Struik Lifestyle, 2014); direct-coal technique  
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g flat iron steak
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) sunflower oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) coarse salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- **Monkey Gland Sauce**: 30 ml tomato ketchup, 30 ml Worcestershire sauce, 15 ml mango chutney, 15 ml (1 tbsp) brown sugar, 10 ml (2 tsp) Dijon mustard, 2 garlic cloves (minced), combined and simmered 5 minutes

**Method**
1. Light a wood-and-charcoal braai fire; allow to burn to medium-hot coals (hold hand 10 cm above grid for 3–4 seconds before pulling away).
2. Simmer Monkey Gland Sauce ingredients in a small pot until slightly thickened. Keep warm.
3. Rub steak with oil, salt and pepper.
4. Insert probe into the thickest point of the steak from the side, with the tip centred at mid-depth. Place on braai grid.
5. Grill 5 minutes per side over medium-hot coals, basting with Monkey Gland Sauce in the final 2 minutes of each side.
6. **Pull at 53 °C (127 °F).** South African braai culture serves beef at medium-rare; the sauce's caramelised sugars add carryover beyond typical expectations.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice across the grain. Serve remaining sauce alongside. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Aaron Franklin — Direct-Coal Flat Iron (American BBQ/steakhouse tradition)
**Source**: Aaron Franklin and Jordan Mackay, *Franklin Steak* (Ten Speed Press, 2019); direct high-heat charcoal method for secondary cuts  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each, patted very dry
- 10 ml (2 tsp) neutral oil (rice bran or grapeseed)
- 5 g (1 tsp) Diamond Crystal kosher salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) coarse black pepper (16-mesh preferred)
- Maldon flake salt, to finish

**Method**
1. Fill a chimney starter with oak or post-oak lump charcoal; light and allow to fully ash over. Pour into a single-layer coal bed. Aim for a cooking surface temperature of around 260 °C (500 °F).
2. Coat steaks with a thin film of oil. Season with kosher salt and black pepper 30 minutes before cooking.
3. Insert probe from the side into the steak's geometric centre before placing on the grill — Franklin recommends probing before the steak hits the grill so you don't lose track of temperature during the fast cook.
4. Place steaks directly over coals. Do not move for 3 minutes; lift an edge to verify crust colour (deep mahogany, not black).
5. Flip. Cook a further 3–4 minutes, checking probe continuously.
6. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** High-radiant charcoal heat drives aggressive carryover; pulling here ensures the rested steak hits exactly medium-rare.
7. Rest on a wire rack for 5 minutes. Finish with Maldon flake salt. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. Maangchi — Korean Charcoal-Grilled Bulgogi Flat Iron (Korean tradition)
**Source**: Maangchi (Emily Kim), *Maangchi's Big Book of Korean Cooking* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2019); bulgogi marinade applied to whole flat iron on charcoal  
**Serves**: 3–4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+ 4 h marinade) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 4 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g flat iron steak, whole piece
- 60 ml (¼ cup) soy sauce
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) toasted sesame oil
- 20 g (1½ tbsp) sugar
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- ½ Asian pear, grated (natural tenderiser)
- 2 spring onions, finely chopped
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- Perilla leaves and steamed rice, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine marinade ingredients. Pierce steak all over with a fork to aid penetration. Submerge in marinade; refrigerate at least 4 hours.
2. Remove and allow to drain; do not pat completely dry — a thin marinade film on the surface adds caramelised flavour over the coals.
3. Build a hot charcoal fire; allow coals to ash over fully. Target grill surface around 250 °C (480 °F).
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak into its geometric centre before placing on the grill.
5. Grill 4 minutes per side, flipping once. Watch for flare-ups from the marinade sugar; brief flares add flavour but prolonged ones char the surface.
6. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F).** The sugary bulgogi marinade benefits from a slightly firmer interior so the sticky glaze sets properly.
7. Rest 4 minutes. Slice thin across the grain. Serve wrapped in perilla leaves with steamed rice. Final serving temperature: **60 °C / 140 °F** (medium).

### 5. Guga Foods — Brazilian Churrasco Flat Iron on Skewer (Brazilian tradition)
**Source**: Gustavo Tosta (Guga Foods), "Flat Iron Steak Churrasco Style," *Guga Foods* YouTube channel (2021); traditional gaucho churrasco skewer method  
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g flat iron steak, whole
- 30 g coarse rock salt (applied heavily, most shaken off after 5 min)

**Method**
1. Skewer the flat iron lengthwise through its centre on a large metal churrasco skewer so the meat hangs horizontally and both broad faces are exposed to the fire.
2. Apply rock salt heavily to all surfaces. Rest 5 minutes, then shake off the excess — the traditional gaucho technique leaves only a light seasoned coating.
3. Build a hardwood charcoal fire in a churrasco pit or large kettle grill. The cooking position is typically 30–40 cm above the coals (approximately 180–200 °C radiant surface temperature at meat level).
4. Because the skewer prevents standard probe placement during continuous rotation, insert a leave-in probe into the geometric centre of the steak before skewering, and secure the wire along the skewer shaft with heat-safe ties.
5. Rotate the skewer every 2–3 minutes over the fire to cook all sides evenly.
6. Monitor probe continuously as the steak cooks from all sides simultaneously.
7. **Pull at 53 °C (127 °F).** Churrasco targets medium-rare; carryover from the retained heat of the metal skewer adds approximately 3–4 °C during the rest.
8. Remove from skewer, rest 5 minutes. Slice thin across the grain. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

## What makes this method special

Charcoal imparts three simultaneous effects unavailable on a gas or electric grill: radiant heat from glowing embers drives fast Maillard browning; combustion volatiles from the charcoal and any added wood chunks deposit aromatic smoke compounds on the crust; and rendered intramuscular fat dripping onto the coals briefly flares, creating localised pyrolysis products that add the complex, char-forward flavour signature of asado, braai and churrasco. The flat iron's strong marbling provides a continuous supply of drip fat, ensuring repeated flavour-building flare cycles throughout the cook. The 2–3 cm thickness is the ideal range for a single-zone direct cook: thick enough to build a deeply charred exterior, thin enough that the centre reaches medium-rare before the crust burns. Compared to gas grilling, charcoal typically adds 10–15 minutes of setup but delivers a meaningfully more complex flavour profile.

## Summary

Charcoal grilling is an outstanding match for flat iron steak: the cut's rich marbling feeds consistent flare cycles that build complex char-and-smoke flavour, while the 2–3 cm thickness allows a thorough Maillard crust to form before the interior overcooks. Five traditions across South America, North America, East Asia and southern Africa all target medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F) as the final serving temperature, pulling 3–5 °C below that to account for charcoal's aggressive carryover. Probe monitoring is more critical here than for gentler methods because temperatures over live coals can rise from 45 °C to 60 °C in under two minutes.
