# Flat Iron Steak × Grill (Gas/Electric) — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron steak originates from the infraspinatus muscle of the shoulder blade, a position that requires little locomotive effort, allowing significant intramuscular fat to develop for a forequarter cut. Gas and electric grills provide consistent, controllable radiant and conductive heat in the 200–260 °C range, making them ideal for the flat iron's 2–3 cm thickness: high enough to produce a meaningful Maillard crust, controllable enough to avoid torching the exterior before the interior is cooked. Unlike charcoal, gas grills allow precise two-zone setups for thicker pieces or quick adjustments mid-cook. Carryover is moderate at 2–4 °C — less than over live charcoal because the radiant energy cuts off the moment the steak leaves the grate; pulling at 54 °C (130 °F) reliably delivers a 57 °C (135 °F) final serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. Bobby Flay — Grilled Flat Iron with Blue Cheese Butter (American tradition)
**Source**: Bobby Flay, *Bobby Flay's Grill It!* (Clarkson Potter, 2008)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 flat iron steaks, ~225 g each
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) canola oil
- 8 g (1½ tsp) kosher salt
- 4 g (¾ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 4 g (¾ tsp) garlic powder
- **Blue Cheese Butter**: 115 g unsalted butter (softened), 60 g blue cheese (crumbled), 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh chives (chopped), salt to taste

**Method**
1. Preheat gas grill to high (230–260 °C / 450–500 °F). Clean and oil grates.
2. Prepare blue cheese butter: beat together butter, blue cheese and chives until smooth. Roll in cling film into a log and refrigerate until firm.
3. Brush steaks on both sides with canola oil; season with salt, pepper and garlic powder.
4. Insert probe into the thickest part of one steak from the side, with the tip at the geometric centre. Place steaks on the hot grate.
5. Grill 4–5 minutes on the first side without pressing down. Rotate 45° at the 2-minute mark for crosshatch marks.
6. Flip and grill a further 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Rest 5 minutes.
8. Place a 1 cm slice of blue cheese butter on each steak immediately after pulling. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Jamie Oliver — Flat Iron Steak with Salsa Verde (British/Italian tradition)
**Source**: Jamie Oliver, *Jamie at Home* (Michael Joseph, 2007); gas grill application of Italian salsa verde treatment  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) black pepper
- **Salsa Verde**: 1 small bunch flat-leaf parsley, 1 small bunch fresh mint, 10 anchovy fillets, 30 g (2 tbsp) capers, 1 garlic clove, 15 ml (1 tbsp) red wine vinegar, 90 ml (6 tbsp) olive oil, salt and pepper

**Method**
1. Blitz all salsa verde ingredients in a food processor to a coarse paste; season and set aside at room temperature.
2. Preheat gas grill to medium-high (220 °C / 430 °F). Oil grates well.
3. Rub steaks with olive oil, salt and pepper.
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak at the thickest point, centring the tip at mid-depth.
5. Grill 4 minutes per side for 2–2.5 cm steaks.
6. Check probe. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).**
7. Rest 5 minutes. Spoon salsa verde generously over the top. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Claudia Roden — Chermoula-Grilled Flat Iron (North African/Moroccan tradition)
**Source**: Claudia Roden, *Arabesque: A Taste of Morocco, Turkey, and Lebanon* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2005); chermoula marinade applied to flat iron on gas grill  
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 20 min (+ 2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g flat iron steak
- **Chermoula**: 1 small bunch fresh coriander (finely chopped), 1 small bunch flat-leaf parsley (finely chopped), 4 garlic cloves (minced), 10 ml (2 tsp) ground cumin, 5 ml (1 tsp) sweet paprika, 2 g (½ tsp) ground ginger, pinch of cayenne, 60 ml (4 tbsp) olive oil, 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh lemon juice, 5 g (1 tsp) salt

**Method**
1. Combine all chermoula ingredients into a thick paste. Coat steak thoroughly on all sides. Cover and refrigerate for 2–4 hours; remove 30 minutes before cooking.
2. Preheat gas grill to medium-high (210–220 °C / 410–430 °F). Oil the grates.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, with tip at mid-depth.
4. Grill 5 minutes on the first side without disturbing. Flip and grill a further 4–5 minutes.
5. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** The herb crust should be deeply fragrant and slightly charred at the edges.
6. Rest 5 minutes, then slice across the grain. Serve with flatbread and harissa. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. José Pizarro — Spanish Plancha-Style Flat Iron with Romesco (Spanish tradition)
**Source**: José Pizarro, *Basque: Spanish Recipes from San Sebastián and Beyond* (Hardie Grant, 2016); high-heat gas grill application of Basque grilled-beef technique  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) extra virgin olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) fleur de sel
- 3 g (½ tsp) smoked pimentón (Spanish smoked paprika)
- **Quick Romesco**: 120 g roasted red peppers (jarred), 30 g blanched almonds (toasted), 1 garlic clove, 15 ml (1 tbsp) sherry vinegar, 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil, salt — blended smooth

**Method**
1. Make romesco: blend all romesco ingredients until smooth; season. Set aside.
2. Preheat gas grill to the highest setting (250–260 °C / 480–500 °F). Scrub grates clean and oil well.
3. Brush steaks with olive oil; season with fleur de sel and pimentón on all surfaces.
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, tip at geometric centre.
5. Grill 3–4 minutes on the first side, forming a strong dark crust. Flip and cook a further 3–4 minutes.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** In Basque grilling tradition the steak is served with a strong pink interior to showcase the quality of the beef.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice against the grain; spoon romesco alongside. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Yotam Ottolenghi — Za'atar and Lemon Grilled Flat Iron (Israeli/Levantine tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi and Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012); za'atar-rubbed grilled beef adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+ 1 h marinade) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 20 g (2 tbsp) za'atar spice blend
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- Zest of 1 lemon
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- Labneh or plain thick yoghurt, to serve
- Fresh mint leaves, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine za'atar, olive oil, lemon zest, lemon juice, garlic and salt to form a paste. Coat steaks thoroughly; marinate at room temperature for 1 hour.
2. Preheat gas grill to high (230 °C / 450 °F). Oil grates.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, centring the tip in the geometric middle.
4. Grill 4 minutes without moving. Flip and grill a further 3–4 minutes.
5. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** The za'atar crust should be fragrant and just beginning to char at the high points.
6. Rest 5 minutes. Serve sliced across the grain over labneh, scattered with fresh mint. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

Gas grilling's consistent heat produces tighter temperature control than charcoal, resulting in less variation in pull temperature across cultures. ## What makes this method special

Gas grilling provides the high-temperature dry heat needed for rapid Maillard browning — typically 220–260 °C at grate level — while offering instant adjustability unavailable with charcoal: the cook can throttle down to a gentler medium zone in seconds if the probe climbs too fast. The flat iron's 2–3 cm profile is perfectly sized for direct gas grilling: in roughly 8–10 minutes total the exterior browns deeply while the interior reaches medium-rare without requiring a two-zone cook. Fat rendering from the flat iron's marbling drips onto the burner flame-tamers, producing flavourful smoke and a mild char note that somewhat approximates charcoal character. Gas grilling is also forgiving enough that marinated or spice-rubbed steaks — as seen in the Chermoula, Za'atar and Romesco recipes — cook reliably without the flare-up risks of sugar-laden charcoal cooks.

## Summary

Gas grilling is one of the most reliable methods for flat iron steak: five traditions from four continents converge identically on medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F), pulled at 54 °C (130 °F). The cut's marbling tolerates the intense grate heat well, producing a flavourful crust without drying the interior; at 2–3 cm thickness it cooks through in 8–10 minutes, matching the natural window of a gas grill's direct-heat zone. The method's controllability makes it forgiving enough for marinated and spice-crusted preparations from diverse culinary traditions.
