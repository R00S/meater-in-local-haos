# Flat Iron Steak × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron steak is cut from the infraspinatus muscle of the shoulder blade, a section that develops pronounced intramuscular fat despite its forequarter origin. Oven roasting applied to individual flat iron steaks (200–350 g) typically takes one of two forms: a reverse-sear approach (gentle low oven to bring the interior to temperature, followed by a fierce stovetop or broiler sear) or a high-heat direct roast (220–230 °C oven, which simultaneously browns the exterior and cooks the interior). The reverse-sear method is particularly well-suited to the flat iron because it achieves edge-to-edge even doneness across the whole steak, minimising the grey band that a conventional high-heat approach produces. Carryover after oven cooking is mild (1–2 °C) compared to searing, but after a finishing sear it rises to 2–4 °C; the recommended pull temperature is therefore 3–4 °C below the intended serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. America's Test Kitchen — Reverse-Sear Flat Iron (American tradition)
**Source**: America's Test Kitchen, *The Cook's Illustrated Meat Book* (America's Test Kitchen, 2014); reverse-sear method applied to flat iron  
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 25–35 min (oven) + 4 min (sear) · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole flat iron steak (~400 g) or 2 individual steaks
- 10 g (1½ tsp) kosher salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) neutral high-heat oil (grapeseed or refined avocado)
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, smashed
- 4 fresh thyme sprigs

**Method**
1. Salt the steak liberally on all surfaces. Place uncovered on a wire rack set over a rimmed baking sheet; refrigerate uncovered for at least 1 hour (or up to overnight) to dry the surface.
2. Preheat oven to 120 °C (250 °F) fan or 135 °C (275 °F) conventional.
3. Insert probe into the geometric centre of the steak through the side, with the tip at mid-height. Place the rack-and-tray assembly in the oven.
4. Roast until probe reads 50 °C (122 °F), approximately 25–35 minutes depending on thickness.
5. Remove steak. Heat a cast-iron skillet over high heat until smoking. Add oil.
6. Sear steak 1½–2 minutes per side until a deep mahogany crust forms. Add butter, garlic and thyme in the final 30 seconds; baste continuously.
7. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F)** (probe will rise briefly during sear; monitor closely). Rest 5 minutes on a rack. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Heston Blumenthal — Low-and-Slow Oven Flat Iron (British modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *In Search of Perfection* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2006); precise low-temperature oven method  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min (+ overnight salting) · **Cook**: 45–60 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~270 g each
- 8 g (1¼ tsp) fine sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) ground white pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) rapeseed (canola) oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt and pepper. Leave uncovered on a rack in the fridge overnight to dry the exterior for optimal crust formation.
2. Remove from fridge 1 hour before cooking. Preheat oven to 100 °C (212 °F) — Blumenthal's research identified this as the temperature range that minimises moisture loss while bringing beef to serving temperature.
3. Insert probe into the thickest part of the steak from the side, with the tip at the geometric centre. Place steak directly on a wire rack over a tray in the oven.
4. Roast until probe reads 54 °C (130 °F) — approximately 45–60 minutes. The very low oven means virtually no carryover at this stage.
5. Remove. Heat a dry stainless or cast-iron pan over the highest flame possible until it begins to smoke lightly.
6. Add oil; sear steak 1 minute per side until a full dark crust forms. Add butter and rosemary; baste 30 seconds.
7. **Pull at 56 °C (133 °F)** (post-sear reading). Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Julia Child — Bifteck Rôti au Four (French tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, Louisette Bertholle, and Simone Beck, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961); high-heat oven steak method adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil or clarified butter
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 230 °C (450 °F) with a heavy ovenproof skillet inside. Allow skillet to heat for 10 minutes.
2. Pat steaks completely dry. Rub with oil and season with salt and pepper.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak into the geometric centre, with the tip at mid-depth.
4. Remove hot skillet from oven. Place on the stovetop over high heat; sear steaks 1 minute per side to start the crust.
5. Transfer skillet to the hot oven. Roast 8–10 minutes, checking probe at the 7-minute mark.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Remove from oven.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Deglaze the pan with lemon juice; swirl in cold butter and chopped parsley. Spoon beurre maître d'hôtel over steaks. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. Marcella Hazan — Bistecca al Forno con Erbe (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Marcella Hazan, *The Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1992); oven-roasted steak with herbs, method adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15–18 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) extra virgin olive oil
- 3 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 15 g fresh rosemary leaves
- 10 g fresh sage leaves
- 6 g (1 tsp) coarse sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) black pepper
- Additional olive oil and lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Place a cast-iron gratin dish or heavy baking tray in the oven for 10 minutes.
2. Combine olive oil, garlic, rosemary and sage. Rub thoroughly over steaks. Season with salt and pepper.
3. Insert probe through the short end of one steak, with the tip centred at mid-height.
4. Remove hot dish from oven. Place directly on the stovetop; sear steaks 1½ minutes per side on high heat.
5. Return to oven. Roast 10–12 minutes, checking probe after 8 minutes.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** The herbs on the surface should be fragrant and just beginning to crisp.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Drizzle with a little fresh olive oil and serve with lemon wedges. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Nigel Slater — Flat Iron with Anchovy and Mustard Crust, Roasted (British tradition)
**Source**: Nigel Slater, *Tender: A Cook and His Vegetable Patch* (Fourth Estate, 2009); anchovy-mustard crust applied to oven-roasted steak  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~260 g each
- 6 anchovy fillets in oil, drained and finely chopped
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) Dijon mustard
- 10 ml (2 tsp) Worcestershire sauce
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 10 ml (2 tsp) olive oil
- Watercress or rocket, to serve

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Set a wire rack in a roasting tin.
2. Mash anchovies into a paste; combine with mustard, Worcestershire sauce and black pepper.
3. Brush steaks with olive oil on all sides. Spread anchovy-mustard paste thickly over the top face of each steak.
4. Insert probe into the side of one steak at its thickest point, tip at mid-depth. Place on wire rack.
5. Roast 15–18 minutes, checking probe at 12 minutes; the crust will colour deeply.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** The anchovy crust should be caramelised and deeply savoury.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Serve over watercress or rocket. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

## Temperature consensus
| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|-------------------------|--------------------|
| American (ATK reverse sear) | Medium-rare | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| British modernist (Blumenthal) | Medium-rare | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| French (Julia Child) | Medium-rare | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| Italian (Marcella Hazan) | Medium-rare | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| British (Nigel Slater) | Medium-rare | 57 °C / 135 °F |

All five traditions converge on medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F) as the final serving temperature, with pull temperatures ranging from 50–56 °C (122–133 °F) depending on whether a finishing sear follows the oven stage. The two-stage reverse-sear recipes pull from the oven at 50–54 °C and read their final probe temperature after searing; single-stage high-heat oven recipes pull at 54 °C. ## What makes this method special

Oven roasting — particularly the reverse-sear approach — produces edge-to-edge even doneness that no other method matches: the gentle low heat (100–135 °C) brings the entire thickness of the steak uniformly to temperature before the crust is formed, eliminating the grey, overcooked band beneath the crust that high-heat methods produce. This is especially valuable for the flat iron because the cut's strong marbling can mask uneven cooking visually; the probe ensures the target is hit regardless. The high-heat direct oven method (220–230 °C) with a preliminary stovetop sear is the classical European approach and delivers a deep, caramelised crust with excellent flavour from the Maillard reaction, with the oven environment maintaining gentle heat throughout the cook. Both methods result in minimal moisture loss because the oven's humid environment — compared to a screaming-hot pan — limits evaporation while the interior sets.

## Summary

Oven roasting, whether by the reverse-sear or direct high-heat approach, is an excellent match for flat iron steak because both methods produce even, controllable doneness across the full thickness of the cut. Five traditions from the US, UK, France and Italy all target medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F), confirming the consensus across European and North American culinary cultures. A probe is essential because the oven's enclosed heat can mislead timing-based estimates, particularly at low temperatures where a few degrees' difference in the cabinet thermometer reading can shift cook time significantly.
