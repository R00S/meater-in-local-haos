# Flat Iron Steak × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron steak, taken from the infraspinatus of the shoulder blade, brings a rich buttery fat content that rewards pan frying: the marbling bastes the meat internally as it cooks while the fat in contact with a hot pan adds to the flavour-laden fond that forms the basis of classic sauces. Pan frying, in European culinary tradition, typically involves a moderately hot pan — lighter than a full sear — allowing the cook to baste repeatedly in butter, aromatics and wine as the interior approaches its target temperature. Individual flat iron steaks at 2–3 cm thickness cook through in 8–12 minutes in a pan, making them well-suited to à la minute bistro service. Carryover cooking after removing from the pan is moderate at 2–4 °C, calling for a pull temperature of 53–54 °C (127–130 °F) to land at medium-rare.

## Source recipes

### 1. Jacques Pépin — Steak à la Poêle, Sauce au Poivre (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *La Technique* (Pocket Books, 1976); pan-fried steak with classic pepper sauce  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 10 g (1½ tsp) whole black peppercorns, coarsely crushed
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) clarified butter or neutral oil
- 30 g unsalted whole butter
- 1 shallot, finely minced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) cognac or brandy
- 120 ml (½ cup) double cream
- 10 ml (2 tsp) Dijon mustard

**Method**
1. Press crushed peppercorns firmly onto both faces of each steak; season with salt.
2. Heat a heavy sauté pan (preferably carbon steel or cast iron) over medium-high heat. Add clarified butter; heat until shimmering but not smoking.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, with the tip centred at mid-depth.
4. Add steaks; cook 4 minutes on the first side without pressing. Reduce heat to medium; flip and cook a further 4–5 minutes, basting occasionally with pan fat.
5. Check probe. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Transfer steaks to a warm plate to rest 5 minutes.
6. In the same pan, sauté shallot 1 minute. Add cognac (stand back — flambé if desired). Add cream; reduce by half. Whisk in mustard; season.
7. Pour sauce over rested steaks. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Rowley Leigh — Flat Iron Steak with Watercress and Anchovy Dressing (British tradition)
**Source**: Rowley Leigh, *No Place Like Home* (Fourth Estate, 2000); pan-fried steak for bistro service  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 10 ml (2 tsp) groundnut (peanut) oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) coarse sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- 30 g unsalted butter
- **Anchovy dressing**: 4 anchovy fillets, 15 ml (1 tbsp) red wine vinegar, 45 ml (3 tbsp) olive oil, 1 tsp Dijon mustard — whisked together
- Large bunch watercress, to serve

**Method**
1. Bring steaks to room temperature; pat very dry. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a heavy frying pan over high heat until just smoking. Add groundnut oil.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak into the geometric centre.
4. Lay steaks in the pan away from you. Cook 3½ minutes on the first side — do not press or move. Flip.
5. Add butter to pan. Cook a further 3–4 minutes, tilting the pan and basting repeatedly with the foaming butter.
6. Check probe. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Rest on a warm plate for 5 minutes.
7. Whisk anchovy dressing; dress watercress. Slice steak across the grain; arrange over watercress. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Giorgio Locatelli — Tagliata di Ferro con Rucola e Parmigiano (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Giorgio Locatelli, *Made in Italy: Food and Stories* (Fourth Estate, 2006); tagliata (sliced steak) preparation for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~270 g each
- 20 ml (4 tsp) extra virgin olive oil
- 6 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) coarsely ground black pepper
- 3 garlic cloves, skin on, lightly crushed
- 3 sprigs fresh rosemary
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 60 g wild rocket (arugula)
- 30 g Parmigiano-Reggiano, shaved
- Lemon wedges and extra olive oil, to serve

**Method**
1. Bring steaks to room temperature. Season generously with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a heavy pan or plancha over high heat. Add olive oil; heat until just beginning to smoke.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, tip at mid-depth.
4. Add steaks, garlic and rosemary to the pan. Cook 3–4 minutes on the first side without moving.
5. Flip; add butter. Baste with the foaming butter-rosemary mixture for 3–4 minutes.
6. **Pull at 53 °C (127 °F).** The Italian tradition slightly favours a more aggressively red centre at the al sangue (rare-medium-rare) border.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thin at a 45° angle across the grain; fan over rocket. Top with Parmigiano shavings; drizzle with olive oil and a squeeze of lemon. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. Kurt Gutenbrunner — Wiener Steak aus der Pfanne (Austrian tradition)
**Source**: Kurt Gutenbrunner, *Neue Cuisine: The Elegant Tastes of Vienna* (Rizzoli, 2011); Viennese pan-fried steak technique  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~240 g each
- 10 ml (2 tsp) vegetable oil
- 6 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) white pepper, freshly ground
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 1 shallot, finely diced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine
- 60 ml (¼ cup) veal or beef stock
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) Dijon mustard
- 5 g fresh chives, finely cut

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt and white pepper. Leave at room temperature 30 minutes.
2. Heat a heavy stainless or copper pan over medium-high heat. Add vegetable oil.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak into the geometric centre.
4. Sear steaks 3–4 minutes per side, basting with pan butter in the final minute.
5. Check probe. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Rest on a warm plate.
6. In the same pan over medium heat, sweat shallot 2 minutes. Deglaze with white wine; reduce by half. Add stock; reduce by half. Whisk in mustard; season.
7. Spoon sauce over rested steaks; garnish with chives. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Stephanie Alexander — Pan-Fried Flat Iron with Herb and Caper Butter (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Stephanie Alexander, *The Cook's Companion* (Lantern, revised edition 2004); pan-fried steak with compound butter, method applied to flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) sea salt flakes
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- **Herb and caper butter**: 60 g unsalted butter (softened), 15 g flat-leaf parsley (finely chopped), 15 g capers (drained, roughly chopped), 5 ml (1 tsp) fresh lemon juice, 1 garlic clove (minced), salt and pepper

**Method**
1. Prepare compound butter: beat together butter, parsley, capers, lemon juice and garlic. Roll in baking paper into a 3 cm log; refrigerate until firm.
2. Bring steaks to room temperature. Pat dry; rub with olive oil, salt and pepper.
3. Heat a heavy cast-iron or stainless frying pan over medium-high heat until very hot. No additional fat required if steaks are well-oiled.
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak at the thickest point, with the tip at mid-depth.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes on the first side until a strong golden-brown crust forms. Flip and cook a further 3–4 minutes.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Rest 5 minutes on a warm plate.
7. Cut a 1 cm slice of compound butter per steak; place on top immediately after resting. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

All five traditions converge on medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F) as the preferred final serving temperature, pulled at 53–54 °C (127–130 °F). The Italian tagliata preparation pulls slightly earlier at 53 °C to preserve a more aggressively pink interior at the classic *al sangue* presentation. ## What makes this method special

Pan frying at moderate-to-high heat allows the cook to control crust development and internal temperature simultaneously, adjusting the flame in real time as the probe climbs. The butter-basting technique common to French, British and Austrian kitchens — tilting the pan and spooning hot aromatic butter over the face of the steak while it cooks — accelerates the Maillard reaction on the upper face without requiring a flip, and infuses herbs and garlic aromatics directly into the crust. The flat iron's natural fat content provides an internal source of moisture even as the surface caramelises. Unlike a high-heat sear, pan frying allows a moderate gradual temperature rise that produces less of a grey band beneath the crust, and the fond left in the pan forms the base for classic bistro sauces. This method produces a deeply caramelised, flavour-rich crust and a juicy interior — a combination that made flat iron steak a staple of European restaurant service.

## Summary

Pan frying is a classic European match for flat iron steak, allowing the cook to build sauce in the same pan while basting the steak in aromatic butter. Five traditions from France, Britain, Italy, Austria and Australia all target medium-rare (57 °C / 135 °F) with pull temperatures of 53–54 °C (127–130 °F), making this one of the most consistent method-cut pairings in the research set. The cut's marbling suits the moderately high pan heat and the basting technique, producing a juicy interior with a deeply flavoured brown crust.
