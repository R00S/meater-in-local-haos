# Flat Iron Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron, cut from the infraspinatus of the shoulder blade, is an excellent candidate for high-heat pan searing: its strong intramuscular fat resists drying at searing temperatures, its 2–3 cm thickness produces a favourable surface-to-volume ratio for crust formation, and its flat, even profile makes full-face contact with a hot pan straightforward. Pan searing at the highest achievable stovetop temperature (240–280 °C in a carbon steel or cast-iron pan) drives the Maillard reaction almost instantaneously, producing the characteristic dark, crackling crust that is the hallmark of restaurant steak cookery. Because of the extremely high surface temperature, carryover is among the most pronounced of any method — typically 4–5 °C — and pulling at 52–53 °C (126–127 °F) is required to land at a medium-rare serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Dry-Brined Pan-Seared Flat Iron with Butter Basting (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+ 1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each, 2.5 cm thick
- 6 g (1 tsp) kosher salt, applied 1 h ahead (or up to 24 h refrigerated)
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper (added immediately before searing)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) refined avocado oil or grapeseed oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, smashed
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Apply kosher salt to all surfaces of steaks at least 1 hour before cooking; refrigerate uncovered on a rack to dry the surface (Kenji's dry-brine method).
2. Remove steaks; apply black pepper. Bring to room temperature 30 minutes before cooking.
3. Heat a cast-iron or carbon steel pan over the highest possible heat for 5 minutes — until it begins to smoke. Add oil; heat until shimmering and just beginning to haze.
4. Insert probe from the side of the steak, with the tip at the geometric centre.
5. Add steaks to the pan. Sear 2 minutes undisturbed. Flip; sear 2 minutes. Continue flipping every minute until probe climbs to range.
6. Add butter, garlic and thyme to the pan; tilt pan and baste steaks continuously for 30–60 seconds.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** The frequent flipping and basting drive rapid carryover to medium-rare.
8. Rest on a wire rack for 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Gordon Ramsay — Restaurant-Style Butter-Basted Flat Iron (British/French restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) coarsely cracked black pepper
- 40 g unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, unpeeled, halved
- 3 fresh thyme sprigs
- 2 fresh rosemary sprigs

**Method**
1. Bring steaks to room temperature; pat completely dry. Season generously with salt and cracked pepper on all surfaces and edges.
2. Heat a heavy stainless or cast-iron pan over high heat until extremely hot — Ramsay describes it as "screaming hot." Add olive oil.
3. Insert probe into the steak from the side, centring the tip at mid-depth.
4. Lay steaks in the pan away from you. Press gently with tongs to ensure full surface contact. Sear 2½ minutes without moving.
5. Flip; add butter, garlic and herbs. Tilt the pan and baste continuously — Ramsay's technique layers caramelised butter fat into the crust.
6. Cook a further 2½ minutes while basting.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Transfer to a wire rack.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Nobu Matsuhisa — Wafu Flat Iron with Ponzu and Daikon (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa and Mark Edwards, *Nobu: The Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2001); wafu (Japanese-style) seared beef with ponzu dipping sauce, adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 4 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~240 g each
- 5 ml (1 tsp) sesame oil
- 5 ml (1 tsp) neutral oil (rice bran)
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) coarsely ground black pepper
- **Ponzu dipping sauce**: 60 ml (¼ cup) soy sauce, 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh yuzu or lemon juice, 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin, 5 ml (1 tsp) rice wine vinegar — combined
- Finely grated daikon and thinly sliced spring onion, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine sesame oil and neutral oil; brush over steaks. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a cast-iron or heavy stainless pan over very high heat until just beginning to smoke. No additional oil.
3. Insert probe through the side of the steak into its geometric centre.
4. Sear steaks 2–3 minutes per side; the Japanese wafu approach favours a clean crust without heavy butter basting — the aromatics are in the dipping sauce.
5. Baste with a small amount of the ponzu sauce in the final 30 seconds to glaze the exterior.
6. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** The wafu presentation slightly favours rare-to-medium-rare; the ponzu acidity brightens the richer end of the doneness range.
7. Rest 4 minutes. Slice thin across the grain; serve with ponzu, grated daikon and spring onion. Final serving temperature: **55 °C / 131 °F** (medium-rare, slightly leaning rare).

### 4. José Andrés — Flat Iron a la Plancha with Piquillo Peppers (Spanish tradition)
**Source**: José Andrés and Richard Wolffe, *Tapas: A Taste of Spain in America* (Clarkson Potter, 2005); plancha-seared steak, adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) extra virgin olive oil
- 6 g (1 tsp) fleur de sel
- 3 g (½ tsp) coarsely ground black pepper
- 4 piquillo peppers (jarred), halved lengthwise
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) sherry vinegar
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil (for peppers)
- 1 garlic clove, minced
- 5 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped

**Method**
1. Warm piquillo peppers in olive oil with garlic and a splash of sherry vinegar; keep warm.
2. Heat a plancha or heavy flat-bottomed pan over the highest heat until wisps of smoke appear. Brush with a thin film of olive oil.
3. Season steaks with fleur de sel and pepper immediately before cooking.
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak at its thickest point, tip at mid-height.
5. Place steaks on the plancha. Sear 3–4 minutes per side, pressing with a spatula for complete contact on the flat surface.
6. **Pull at 53 °C (127 °F).** The plancha retains heat in the steel, so carryover is aggressive.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Serve alongside warm piquillo peppers garnished with parsley. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. René Redzepi — Nordic Seared Flat Iron with Smoked Butter (Nordic tradition)
**Source**: René Redzepi, *Noma: Time and Place in Nordic Cuisine* (Phaidon Press, 2010); high-heat seared beef with smoked butter, technique adapted for flat iron  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 10 ml (2 tsp) cold-pressed rapeseed (canola) oil
- 6 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) coarsely ground black pepper
- **Smoked butter**: 60 g unsalted butter, briefly cold-smoked over hay or wood chips until smoke-flavoured — or substitute 60 g butter with 3 drops of liquid smoke and 1 tsp fresh thyme
- Fresh herbs (wood sorrel, dill tips, chives), to garnish

**Method**
1. Prepare smoked butter: cold-smoke or infuse butter; keep at room temperature until spreadable.
2. Bring steaks to room temperature; pat very dry. Rub with rapeseed oil; season with salt and pepper.
3. Heat a carbon steel or cast-iron pan over maximum heat for 5 minutes. The Nordic technique avoids additional pan fat to maximise Maillard browning from the steak's own surface.
4. Insert probe through the side of the steak into the geometric centre.
5. Add steaks; sear 2 minutes undisturbed. Flip; sear 2 minutes.
6. Add smoked butter; baste 30–60 seconds, tilting the pan to flow hot butter over the top face.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Rest 5 minutes on a rack.
8. Garnish with fresh Nordic herbs. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

## What makes this method special

Pan searing at maximum heat concentrates all the energy of the method in the first few minutes, driving the most intense Maillard reaction possible on a flat surface: caramelised proteins and browning sugars produce a crust with bitterness, depth and caramel notes that no gentler method achieves. The flat iron's fat content is a specific asset here — the intramuscular fat flows into the hot pan and joins the butter-basting cycle, creating a self-basting internal mechanism while the exterior crust forms. The cook must work precisely with a probe because the temperature window is narrow: at 260–280 °C pan surface temperatures the core can rise 10 °C in under 90 seconds. The frequent-flip technique (Kenji's method) reduces the grey band beneath the crust by distributing heat evenly, while the single-flip restaurant approach (Ramsay) prioritises crust depth on each face. Both are valid approaches for the flat iron's thickness.

## Summary

Pan searing is the pre-eminent restaurant method for flat iron steak: five traditions from the US, UK, Japan, Spain and Scandinavia all target medium-rare (55–57 °C / 131–135 °F), pulled at 52–53 °C (126–127 °F) to account for the aggressive carryover of a screaming-hot pan. The cut's marbling and even thickness make it ideal for the method — it sears confidently without curling, and the fat sustains the basting cycle. A probe is mandatory here because the cook window between perfect and overdone at maximum heat can be under two minutes.
