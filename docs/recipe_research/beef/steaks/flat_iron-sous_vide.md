# Flat Iron Steak × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The flat iron steak, from the infraspinatus of the shoulder blade, is exceptionally well-matched to sous vide: its strong marbling prevents the cut from drying during extended low-temperature baths, and its thin, even profile (2–3 cm) means it reaches thermal equilibrium with the circulator water quickly. Sous vide produces edge-to-edge even doneness that no other method can match — there is no grey band, no overcooking at the edges — and the moist, sealed environment retains every gram of juices that other methods lose to evaporation. **Important sous vide note:** the probe cannot be inserted into the steak during the sealed-bag cooking stage; the circulator temperature itself is the target temperature. A probe can be used post-bath to verify internal temperature after removing the steak from the bag, and is useful for confirming temperature hold before the finishing sear. Typical cook times for a 2–3 cm flat iron are 1–4 hours at 55–57 °C (131–135 °F); beyond 4 hours the texture can begin to degrade as enzymes continue to work. Carryover during the finishing sear adds 2–4 °C, so when searing post-bath, the bath temperature should be set 2–3 °C below the intended final serving temperature, or the sear should be brief and aggressive.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt / Serious Eats — Sous Vide Flat Iron at 55 °C (American modernist tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, "Sous Vide Steak Guide," *Serious Eats*, seriouseats.com/the-food-lab-complete-guide-to-sous-vide-steak (2015; updated 2023)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 1–4 h (circulator) + 2 min (sear) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 6 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme (optional, in bag)
- 1 garlic clove, smashed (optional, in bag)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) high-heat oil (grapeseed or avocado) — for finishing sear
- 30 g unsalted butter — for finishing sear

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt and pepper. Optionally add thyme and garlic.
2. Seal in a vacuum bag or zip-lock bag using the water-displacement method.
3. Set circulator to **55 °C (131 °F)** for medium-rare with a slight rare lean; or **57 °C (135 °F)** for classic medium-rare. *The circulator temperature is the cook target — no probe is inserted during the bag cook.*
4. Submerge sealed bag; cook for 1–4 hours. Kenji recommends 1.5–2 hours for 2–3 cm steaks.
5. Remove steak from bag; pat completely dry with paper towels. *To verify core temperature post-bath, insert a probe into the centre before the sear — it should read within 1 °C of the bath temperature.*
6. Heat a cast-iron pan over maximum heat; add oil. Sear steak 1 minute per side, adding butter and basting in the final 30 seconds.
7. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F)** (bath was set to 55 °C; the sear adds 2 °C of carryover). Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sous Vide Flat Iron (French-American modernist tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, *Under Pressure: Cooking Sous Vide* (Artisan, 2008)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 45 min–1 h (circulator) + 1–2 min (sear) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~275 g each
- 10 g (1½ tsp) fleur de sel
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground white pepper
- 30 g unsalted butter (in bag)
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme (in bag)
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary (in bag)
- 20 ml (4 tsp) grapeseed oil — for sear

**Method**
1. Season steaks with fleur de sel and white pepper. Place each steak in a vacuum bag with 15 g butter and herbs.
2. Vacuum seal using a chamber sealer. Keller specifies professional vacuum packaging to eliminate all air and ensure direct bag-to-steak heat transfer.
3. Set circulator to **57 °C (135 °F)**. *This is the target temperature — the probe is not used during the sealed cook.* Submerge bags.
4. Cook 45 minutes (for 2.5 cm steaks) to 1 hour (for 3 cm steaks). Keller's shorter bath times reflect his preference for a just-cooked, still-firm texture.
5. Remove steaks from bags; reserve cooking juices. Pat steaks completely dry — moisture on the surface will steam rather than sear.
6. *Post-bath temperature verification: insert a probe briefly into the centre of one steak before searing to confirm it reads 56–57 °C (133–135 °F).*
7. Heat a stainless pan over maximum heat; add grapeseed oil. Sear steaks 30–45 seconds per side for a thin, lacquered crust without cooking further.
8. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F)** (minimal sear adds negligible carryover). Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Precise Sous Vide Flat Iron (British modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *The Fat Duck Cookbook* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2008); precise temperature methodology for beef sous vide  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min (+ overnight dry brine) · **Cook**: 2 h (circulator) + 1 min (sear) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~260 g each
- 8 g (1¼ tsp) fine sea salt (applied 12 h ahead as a dry brine)
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 20 g unsalted butter (in bag)
- 2 g dried porcini powder (in bag — Blumenthal's umami-boost technique)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) refined sunflower oil — for sear

**Method**
1. Dry brine steaks with salt 12 hours ahead; refrigerate uncovered on a rack to maximise surface drying and salt penetration.
2. Before sealing, apply pepper and porcini powder. Add butter to bag.
3. Vacuum seal. Set circulator to **57 °C (135 °F)**. *No probe is used during the sealed-bag cook; the water bath temperature is the target.*
4. Cook for exactly 2 hours. Blumenthal's research found that 2 hours at 57 °C achieves both thermal equilibrium and slight collagen softening that improves the flat iron's texture.
5. Remove from bag; reserve cooking juices. Pat absolutely dry with multiple changes of paper towels — Blumenthal emphasises that surface moisture is the single biggest obstacle to a proper sear.
6. *Post-bath verification: press a probe into the geometric centre of one steak to confirm 56–57 °C (133–135 °F) before searing.*
7. Heat a dry cast-iron pan to near-black-smoke temperature; add a drop of sunflower oil. Sear each face 45–60 seconds for a minimal, lacquer-thin dark crust.
8. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F)** (short sear adds minimal carryover). Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 4. ChefSteps — Joule Sous Vide Flat Iron (American modernist/tech tradition)
**Source**: ChefSteps Team, "Cook Steak Like a Pro: Flat Iron," ChefSteps.com / Joule app sous vide guide (2017)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 1–3 h (circulator) + 2 min (sear) · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~250 g each
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground black pepper
- 3 g (½ tsp) garlic powder (optional)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) neutral high-heat oil — for sear

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt, pepper and optional garlic powder. Seal in vacuum or zip-lock bags.
2. Set Joule circulator to **55 °C (131 °F)** for rare-to-medium-rare or **57 °C (135 °F)** for medium-rare. *The circulator temperature is the target; no probe is inserted into the sealed bag during cooking.*
3. Cook for 1–3 hours. ChefSteps recommends 1.5 hours for the flat iron's 2–3 cm thickness; longer cooks (up to 3 hours) increase tenderness at the cost of slight texture change.
4. Remove steak from bath and bag. Pat extremely dry with paper towels.
5. *Post-bath probe verification (optional): insert a probe into the centre to confirm temperature is within 1 °C of the bath setting before proceeding to sear.*
6. Heat a cast-iron, stainless or carbon steel pan over maximum heat. Add oil; heat until smoke point.
7. Sear 1 minute per face; sear edges briefly with tongs.
8. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F)** (bath set to 55 °C; 2 min aggressive sear adds ~2 °C). Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Nathan Myhrvold et al. — Modernist Approach to Sous Vide Flat Iron (Modernist tradition)
**Source**: Nathan Myhrvold, Chris Young, and Maxime Bilet, *Modernist Cuisine at Home* (The Cooking Lab, 2012), sous vide beef section  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 45 min–2 h (circulator) + 1 min (sear) · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 flat iron steaks, ~260 g each
- 6 g (1 tsp) salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) white pepper
- 5 g (1 tsp) dextrose (optional; promotes Maillard browning during the sear)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) clarified butter or refined oil — for sear

**Method**
1. Season steaks with salt, white pepper and optional dextrose (the dextrose accelerates Maillard browning during the brief post-bath sear). Vacuum seal.
2. Set circulator to **55 °C (131 °F)** for medium-rare at the rare-leaning end, or **58 °C (136 °F)** for classic medium-rare. *The circulator temperature is the cook target; the probe is not inserted during the sealed-bag stage.* Myhrvold's methodology characterises this as "target core temperature equals bath temperature after sufficient time."
3. Cook 45 minutes (pasteurisation achieved for flat iron thickness; the Modernist Cuisine tables confirm pathogen reduction at 55 °C/45 min for steaks up to 3 cm).
4. Remove steak from bag; pat dry.
5. *Post-bath temperature confirmation: insert a probe to verify the core reads within 1 °C of the bath temperature — critical quality-control step in Myhrvold's protocol.*
6. Heat pan to maximum. Add a thin film of clarified butter or oil. Sear each face 45 seconds for a lacquer-thin Maillard crust; avoid cooking further.
7. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F)** (bath at 55 °C; brief sear adds ~2 °C). Rest 1 minute only — sous vide steaks lose heat fast post-sear without further carryover. Final serving temperature: **57 °C / 135 °F** (medium-rare).

## Temperature consensus
| Tradition | Circulator target | Post-sear final temp |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| American modernist (Kenji) | 55 °C / 131 °F | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| French-American (Keller) | 57 °C / 135 °F | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| British modernist (Blumenthal) | 57 °C / 135 °F | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| American tech (ChefSteps) | 55–57 °C / 131–135 °F | 57 °C / 135 °F |
| Modernist Cuisine (Myhrvold) | 55–58 °C / 131–136 °F | 57 °C / 135 °F |

All five sources target a post-sear final serving temperature of 57 °C (135 °F), corresponding to medium-rare; the variation in bath temperature (55–58 °C) reflects differing sear intensities — cooks using briefer, hotter sears set the bath lower to compensate for more aggressive carryover. ## What makes this method special

Sous vide is the only method that guarantees complete edge-to-edge even doneness: every millimetre of the steak's cross-section reaches exactly the circulator temperature and no more, producing a uniformly pink, juicy interior without any grey band at the edge. This is especially valuable for the flat iron because its irregular thickness (thicker at the centre, tapering at the edges) means traditional methods always overcook one end; sous vide eliminates this. The sealed bag also traps all cooking juices and rendered fat, allowing them to be deglazed into a sauce. The key limitation is that a probe cannot be used during the sealed-bag cook — the circulator temperature is the target — but post-bath probe verification is strongly recommended before the finishing sear to confirm thermal equilibrium was reached. Cook times between 45 minutes and 4 hours are safe for a 2–3 cm flat iron, but the 1–2-hour window produces the best combination of food-safety pasteurisation, even doneness and good texture.

## Summary

Sous vide is perhaps the technically optimal method for flat iron steak: by setting the circulator to 55–57 °C (131–135 °F), every part of the cut reaches exactly medium-rare with no risk of overcooking at the edges. All five modernist sources target 57 °C (135 °F) as the final serving temperature after a brief finishing sear. The probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook, but post-bath verification before searing is a best-practice step recommended by all major sources; the sear adds 2–4 °C of carryover, which is managed by setting the bath 2 °C below the target or by minimising sear time to under 90 seconds per face.
