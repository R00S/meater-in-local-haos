# Hanger Steak × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Pan-fried hanger steak (*onglet* in French) is the definitive method in French bistro cooking, where *onglet à l'échalote* — pan-fried hanger steak with a red-wine shallot sauce — is one of the most iconic brasserie dishes. The hanger's loose, open-grain structure sears quickly in a hot pan, developing a dark mahogany crust in 2–3 minutes per side. The rendered fat and juices left in the pan form the perfect base for a quick pan sauce — shallots, red wine, butter. This is a steak that demands medium-rare: the open grain releases all its flavour and juice when hot and pink but becomes tough and dry past 60 °C. The central sinew must be removed before or after cooking. Carryover from a hot pan is 3–4 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Julia Child — Onglet à l'Échalote (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking, Volume 1* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak (*onglet*), 500–600 g (sinew removed if possible)
- Fine salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*) or vegetable oil
- **Sauce à l'échalote**: 30 g (3 medium) shallots (*échalotes grises*), finely minced; 80 ml (⅓ cup) dry red wine; 30 ml (2 tbsp) cognac or Armagnac; 80 ml (⅓ cup) beef stock; 40 g cold unsalted butter, cubed; 15 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped; salt and pepper

**Method**
1. Season onglet generously with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a wide, heavy sauté pan over very high heat; add clarified butter; heat until almost smoking.
3. Lay onglet in the pan. Cook 3–4 minutes without moving (deep brown crust forms). Flip once.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak into its centre.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes more.
6. Remove steak to rest. Pour off excess fat from pan. Over medium heat, soften shallots 2–3 minutes; deglaze with cognac; add wine; reduce by half; add stock; reduce. Remove from heat; swirl in cold butter cubes; add parsley.
7. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Julia's French standard for onglet: *saignant* — bright red, very juicy. Pan carryover is modest (3–4 °C), so pull early.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thin against the grain; spoon sauce over. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (*saignant*, French bistro standard).

### 2. Fergus Henderson — Pan-Fried Onglet, Chips (British nose-to-tail tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson, *The Complete Nose to Tail* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak, 500 g (Henderson works with the sinew-on version — he removes the sinew post-cooking)
- Sea salt and black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) beef dripping or vegetable oil
- Thick-cut chips, watercress and Dijon mustard, to serve

**Method**
1. Season hanger steak.
2. Heat a heavy iron pan over high heat; add dripping; heat until smoking.
3. Lay the hanger steak in the pan; sear 3–4 minutes; flip.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the end of the steak into its thickest part.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes more.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Henderson's British standard: "blood-red and oozing." He is categorical that onglet must be served rare.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Remove central sinew; slice. Serve with chips, watercress and Dijon mustard. Final serving temperature: **53–55 °C / 127–131 °F** (rare, British nose-to-tail standard).

### 3. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Fried Hanger Steak (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/pan-seared-hanger-steak; *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak, 500 g (sinew removed)
- Kosher salt (applied 1 hour ahead)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 sprigs thyme

**Method**
1. Dry-brine 1 hour; pat dry; apply pepper.
2. Heat a cast-iron pan over absolute maximum heat. Add oil; heat until barely smoking.
3. Place hanger steak in the pan. Cook 2–3 minutes per side.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the end of the steak.
5. Reduce heat slightly; add butter, garlic and thyme; baste 1–2 minutes.
6. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Kenji's standard: medium-rare. He emphasises resting is critical for this coarse-grained cut to redistribute juices.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thin; serve. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, American standard).

### 4. David Tanis — Onglet with Anchovy Butter (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: David Tanis, *A Platter of Figs and Other Recipes* (Artisan Books, 2008)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak (*onglet*), 500 g (sinew removed)
- Fine salt and black pepper
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Anchovy butter**: 80 g softened unsalted butter; 4 anchovy fillets, finely mashed; 1 garlic clove, finely minced; 15 g flat-leaf parsley, chopped; 10 ml lemon juice; black pepper

**Method**
1. Mix anchovy butter; refrigerate.
2. Season onglet; heat a heavy pan over very high heat; add oil.
3. Sear 3–4 minutes per side. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre.
4. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Tanis's French bistro standard: *saignant*.
5. Rest 5 minutes. Slice; top with anchovy butter. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (*saignant*, French bistro standard).

### 5. April Bloomfield — Pan-Fried Hanger Steak, Red-Wine Sauce (American-British tradition)
**Source**: April Bloomfield, *A Girl and Her Pig* (Ecco Press, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak, 500–600 g (sinew removed)
- Kosher salt and freshly cracked black pepper
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) vegetable oil
- 20 g unsalted butter (in pan)
- **Quick red wine sauce**: 2 shallots, minced; 120 ml (½ cup) red wine; 30 ml red wine vinegar; 80 ml beef stock; 30 g cold butter; fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Dry-brine 1 hour; pat dry.
2. Heat a heavy pan over high heat; add oil. Sear 3–4 minutes per side. Add butter; baste briefly.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre.
4. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Bloomfield's standard: medium-rare.
5. Remove to rest. Sauté shallots in pan drippings; deglaze with wine and vinegar; add stock and thyme; reduce; swirl in cold butter.
6. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thin; pour red wine sauce over. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, American-British standard).
