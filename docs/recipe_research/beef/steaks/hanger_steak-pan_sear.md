# Hanger Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The hanger steak (onglet in French, lombatello in Italian, nierenzapfen in German) hangs from the diaphragm of the animal between the last rib and the loin, connected to the spine by a single thick central sinew that runs the length of the muscle and is typically removed by the butcher before sale. Because there is only one hanger per animal and the muscle performs continuous respiratory work, the fibres are coarse and loosely aligned, producing an open, yielding texture that carries seasoning exceptionally well and releases abundant juices when cut. The flavour is the most mineral and intensely beefy of all common quick-cook cuts. Pan searing is ideally suited to the hanger because the direct conductive heat of a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan drives the Maillard reaction the cut needs before its lean interior overcooks; the loose grain structure expels moisture rapidly above 60 °C, making temperature precision more important here than with a more forgiving ribeye. Carryover is moderate — typically 3–5 °C — and because the steak is often thinner and less regular than a ribeye, probe placement in the thickest section of lean muscle is critical.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Butter-Basted Pan-Seared Hanger Steak (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/hanger-steak-recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 h dry-brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5–8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 hanger steak, central sinew removed by butcher, approximately 450–680 g
- 12 g (2 tsp) kosher salt (for dry-brine)
- 4 g (¾ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or grapeseed oil
- 28 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly crushed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Season the steak generously with kosher salt on all surfaces at least 1 hour before cooking, or up to overnight; refrigerate uncovered on a wire rack. The dry-brine draws out surface moisture, which redissolves as seasoned brine and dries the exterior for a faster, deeper sear.
2. Remove from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Apply freshly ground black pepper immediately before searing.
3. Heat a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over the highest possible heat for at least 5 minutes until smoking. Add the vegetable oil.
4. Lay the steak in the pan. Flip every 30 seconds — Kenji's rapid-flip technique prevents a thick grey band building under any single face and produces a more uniformly cooked interior.
5. After 2 minutes of rapid flipping, insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak into the geometric centre of the thickest section, avoiding any remaining fat seam or the former sinew gap. The probe tip must sit in lean muscle.
6. Reduce heat to medium-high. Add the butter, garlic, and thyme. The butter will foam immediately.
7. Tilt the pan and baste the steak repeatedly with the foaming, browning butter, continuing to flip every 30 seconds. The baste layers caramelised milk-solid flavour into the crust.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Rapid flipping generates slightly less carryover than a static sear; the rest will bring the core to medium-rare without overshooting.
9. Rest on a wire rack 5–8 minutes. Slice firmly against the grain — with hanger steak this is non-negotiable; sliced with the grain the texture is unacceptably chewy. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 130–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 2. Stéphane Reynaud — Onglet à l'Échalote (French bistrot tradition)
**Source**: Stéphane Reynaud, *Ripailles: Traditional French Cuisine* (Flammarion, 2009)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 3–5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 onglet (hanger steak), central sinew removed, approximately 500 g
- 5 g (1 tsp) fleur de sel
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 10 ml (2 tsp) grapeseed oil
- 10 g (2 tsp) unsalted butter, for searing
- 4 échalotes grises (*grey shallots* — the small, elongated variety favoured in French bistrot cooking; common round shallots are an acceptable substitute), finely minced
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) red wine vinegar (*vinaigre de vin rouge*)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) dry red wine
- 15 g (1 tbsp) cold unsalted butter, cut into cubes, for finishing the sauce
- 10 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Season the onglet on both sides with fleur de sel and black pepper immediately before searing.
2. Heat a heavy steel or cast-iron pan over high heat until very hot. Add the grapeseed oil and the searing butter together — the butter browns rapidly and contributes flavour while the oil raises the smoke point.
3. Lay the onglet in the pan. Sear without moving for 2–3 minutes to build a dark-brown, aromatic crust on the first face.
4. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the steak, angling the tip to the centre of the thickest lean section, clear of the tapered end.
5. Sear the second face 2–3 minutes, again without moving. The French bistrot approach uses a static sear, not rapid flipping — the goal is a deeply coloured crust on each face and a warm red interior.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F)** (*saignant*). The French bistrot tradition serves onglet *saignant* — a warm, vivid red centre. Reynaud notes that pulling early is essential; the cut is unforgiving beyond *rosé*.
7. Rest the onglet on a warm plate, covered loosely with foil, 3–5 minutes.
8. While resting: add the minced shallots to the same pan over medium heat. Sweat 2 minutes until softened. Deglaze with the red wine vinegar and red wine — the liquid sizzles and lifts the fond (*sucs*) from the pan. Reduce by half, approximately 2 minutes.
9. Remove from heat. Whisk in the cold butter cubes one at a time to emulsify a light sauce. Stir in the parsley. Adjust seasoning with salt and pepper.
10. Slice the onglet against the grain on a board. Arrange on warmed plates. Spoon the shallot sauce around and over. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (*saignant* — vivid red centre, classic French bistrot presentation).

### 3. Hugh Fearnley-Whittingstall — Bavette / Onglet Seared in Dripping (British tradition)
**Source**: Hugh Fearnley-Whittingstall, *The River Cottage Meat Book* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2004)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+20–30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 bavette or onglet (hanger steak), approximately 400–500 g, sinew removed
- Maldon sea salt flakes
- Coarsely ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) beef dripping (rendered beef fat — the traditional British medium for steak; substitute cold-pressed rapeseed oil if unavailable)
- 20 g (1½ tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 small bunch watercress, to serve (optional)

**Method**
1. Remove the steak from the refrigerator 20–30 minutes before cooking. Pat completely dry with kitchen paper — surface moisture kills crust formation.
2. Season generously with Maldon sea salt flakes and coarsely ground black pepper on both sides and all edges.
3. Heat a heavy iron pan or griddle over very high heat until wisps of smoke rise. Add the beef dripping — Fearnley-Whittingstall notes that dripping is the correct traditional cooking medium for British steak and contributes a depth of flavour that neutral oils cannot replicate.
4. Lay the steak in the pan. Cook undisturbed on the first side for 3–4 minutes to build a good brown crust.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak at its thickest point, keeping the tip clear of the tapered thin end and any remaining connective tissue channels.
6. Flip once. Add the butter and allow it to foam and brown slightly. Cook the second side 2–3 minutes, spooning the foaming butter over the steak.
7. **Pull at 52–55 °C (126–131 °F).** Fearnley-Whittingstall emphasises that this cut must not be taken beyond medium — the loosely grained, lean muscle contracts sharply above 60 °C and becomes unpleasantly chewy. He favours the just-past-rare zone for maximum flavour and texture.
8. Rest on a warm board 5 minutes, loosely tented with foil. Slice across the grain. Serve with watercress if using. Final serving temperature: **55–58 °C / 131–136 °F** (medium-rare to medium).

### 4. Deuki Hong and Matt Rodbard — 안창살구이 Anchang-Sal Pan-Sear (Korean tradition)
**Source**: Deuki Hong and Matt Rodbard, *Koreatown: A Cookbook* (Clarkson Potter, 2016)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min marinating) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 450 g hanger steak (*anchang-sal*, 안창살), central sinew removed, sliced into two even pieces along the natural seam
- **Marinade:**
  - 45 ml (3 tbsp) soy sauce (*ganjang*)
  - 30 ml (2 tbsp) Asian pear juice or puréed Asian pear (the natural enzymes tenderise the coarse grain)
  - 20 ml (4 tsp) sesame oil (*chamgireum*)
  - 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin (*mirim*, rice wine sweetener)
  - 10 ml (2 tsp) sake or dry sherry
  - 3 cloves garlic, minced
  - 5 g (1 tsp) fresh ginger, grated
  - 2 spring onions (*pa*), thinly sliced
  - 5 g (1 tsp) sugar
  - 3 g (½ tsp) sesame seeds (*kkae*), lightly toasted
  - 2 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) neutral cooking oil (for the pan)
- Extra sesame seeds and sliced spring onion, to garnish
- **To serve**: steamed short-grain rice, *ssam* (crisp lettuce or perilla leaves for wrapping), *doenjang* (*된장*, fermented soybean paste) mixed with sesame oil as a condiment

**Method**
1. Combine all marinade ingredients in a bowl and whisk until the sugar dissolves.
2. Add the hanger steak pieces. Toss to coat. Cover and marinate at room temperature 30 minutes, or refrigerate 2–4 hours. The Asian pear enzymes begin to tenderise the coarse fibres of the anchang-sal within the first 30 minutes; do not marinate beyond 4 hours or the texture will become mushy.
3. Remove steak from marinade. Pat dry with kitchen paper to remove excess marinade — wet marinade burns in a hot pan. Reserve the marinade for glazing.
4. Heat a heavy cast-iron pan or well-seasoned wok over very high heat until smoking. Add the neutral oil.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the thicker steak piece, with the tip at the centre of the lean muscle, clear of any remaining fat channel.
6. Lay the steaks in the pan without crowding. Sear 2–3 minutes on the first side without moving — the goal is a dark, slightly charred caramelisation (*soegogi gui* char character) that is characteristic of Korean grilled beef.
7. Flip. Brush the cooked face lightly with reserved marinade. Cook the second side 2–3 minutes.
8. **Pull at 57–60 °C (135–140 °F).** Korean beef cookery tradition for anchang-sal targets medium rather than the Western medium-rare because the marinade and the thin cut format distribute heat more rapidly and the final texture with the open grain is preferred slightly firmer for wrapping in ssam. Hong and Rodbard note this pull point gives a moist, just-pink interior.
9. Rest 3 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain. Garnish with sesame seeds and spring onion. Serve with rice, ssam leaves, and doenjang condiment for wrapping. Final serving temperature: **59–62 °C / 138–144 °F** (medium — characteristic Korean *ssam* presentation).
