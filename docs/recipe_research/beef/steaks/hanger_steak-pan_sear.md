# Hanger Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Hanger steak (onglet in French, entraña in Spanish) hangs from the diaphragm between
the rib and the loin — one per animal, which makes it both rare and prized. It has an
assertively beefy, almost livery flavour that no other cut matches, with a coarse,
open grain that soaks up any marinade. French bistros made onglet famous because
it is a cook's cut — nobody but the butcher used to keep it — and pan searing over
very high heat is its canonical expression: a screaming-hot pan, a brief cook, and
the discipline to pull it rare when every instinct says wait longer.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Seared Hanger Steak (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/pan-seared-hanger-steak
- **Method**: Pat completely dry; season 45 min before. Ripping-hot cast iron; 2–3 min
  per side. One flip only. Butter baste final 45 seconds. Rest 5 min minimum.
- **Pull temp**: 125°F / **52°C**
- **Final after rest (5–8 min)**: 130°F / **54°C**
- **Why this method works**: The hanger's open grain and thick centre require a fast
  very-hot cook to prevent the exterior from going grey before the interior warms
  through; pulling at 52°C with a 5+ min rest produces the rare-to-medium-rare
  result that preserves the cut's distinctive mineral flavour.

### 2. Bistrot Paul Bert — Onglet à l'échalote (France / Paris)
- **Source**: bistrotpaulbert.fr (menu reference and technique — Paris 11ème)
- **Method**: Poêle en fonte à feu maximum; 2 min par face for a 300g onglet.
  Beurre noisette et échalotes confites versés sur le dessus. Repos 8 min.
- **Pull temp**: **50–52°C** (saignant — the only acceptable doneness for onglet)
- **Final after rest (8 min)**: **52–54°C**
- **Why this method works**: French bistro tradition is non-negotiable on this point:
  onglet served beyond saignant is considered a culinary error. The cut's fibres
  contract sharply above 55°C, making it chewy and losing the mineral juiciness
  that defines it. 50–52°C is the window of perfection.

### 3. Marcus Wareing — Hanger Steak with Café de Paris Butter (UK)
- **Source**: marcuswareing.com/recipes/hanger-steak-cafe-de-paris-butter
- **Method**: Bring to room temp 30 min; season generously. Very hot cast iron with
  clarified butter; sear 2 min per side for a 2.5 cm cut. Rest 5 min before slicing
  across the grain.
- **Pull temp**: **52–54°C** (medium-rare)
- **Final after rest (5 min)**: **54–56°C**
- **Why this method works**: Marcus Wareing's approach bridges the French saignant
  tradition with UK medium-rare preference; the compound butter adds richness that
  counterbalances the cut's mineral edge.

### 4. Matprat — Onglet / Hanger Steak i panne (Norway)
- **Source**: matprat.no/oppskrifter/kjott/hanger-steak-onglet
- **Method**: Varm jernpanne til røyking varm; olivenolje og smør. 2–3 min per side.
  Hvil 5 min. Skjær tynt mot fibrene.
- **Pull temp**: **54°C**
- **Final after rest (5 min)**: **56°C**
- **Why this method works**: Norwegian meat guides increasingly cover onglet/hanger as
  a butcher's cut; the pan-sear technique is recommended over grill for home cooks
  because the controlled indoor heat prevents the rapid overshoot this cut is
  vulnerable to.

### 5. Recetas de Argentina — Entraña a la sartén (Argentina)
- **Source**: recetasdeargentina.com.ar/entrana-sarten
- **Method**: Sal gruesa únicamente; sartén de hierro muy caliente; 2 min por lado.
  Reposo 5 min. Corte transversal y servir inmediatamente.
- **Pull temp**: **55–57°C** (jugoso / punto)
- **Final after rest (5 min)**: **57–60°C**
- **Why this method works**: Argentine parrilla tradition carries over to the sartén —
  entraña is eaten slightly more done than the French saignant; "jugoso" at 55–57°C
  is the Argentine reading of what makes this cut best, emphasising warmth
  throughout over the French preference for a cool rare centre.
