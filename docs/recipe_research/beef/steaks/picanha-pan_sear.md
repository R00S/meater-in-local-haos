# Picanha × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Picanha (*alcatra de capa* in Portuguese; *coulotte* in French; sirloin cap or rump cap in English) is cut from the top of the rump, comprising the *biceps femoris* muscle capped by a thick layer of subcutaneous fat. It is the most prized cut in Brazilian *churrasco* culture. The fat cap is extremely important — it renders during cooking and bastes the meat from above. Picanha has medium-to-high marbling for a rump-region cut and a bold, beefy flavour. Pan searing is less traditional than grilling for picanha, but produces excellent results: the fat cap renders in a hot pan to baste the lean meat below, the muscle crust Maillard-browns well, and the thin cut cooks quickly. Carryover is 4–5 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Francis Mallmann — Picanha in a Cast-Iron Pan (Argentine/Brazilian tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann and Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan Books, 2009); Mallmann addresses the *coulotte* cut as part of his broader treatment of premium rump cuts  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g picanha (*rump cap*), fat cap scored in a crosshatch at 2 cm intervals and left on
- Coarse sea salt (*sal gruesa*)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) beef dripping or vegetable oil (optional — the fat cap may render enough fat on its own)
- Chimichurri: 40 g flat-leaf parsley; 15 g fresh oregano; 4 garlic cloves; 60 ml olive oil; 30 ml red wine vinegar; red chilli flakes; fine salt

**Method**
1. Score the fat cap in a tight crosshatch 5 mm deep. Season all surfaces generously with coarse salt and pepper.
2. Heat a large cast-iron pan over the highest possible heat. Add a very small amount of oil (or none — the fat cap will begin rendering within 30 seconds).
3. Place picanha fat-cap-side down in the pan. Press firmly with a heavy lid or weight to ensure full fat-cap contact.
4. Render the fat cap 3–4 minutes until golden, deeply scored and rendered — the scored lines will open slightly.
5. Flip to the meat side. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the meat, below the fat cap.
6. Sear the meat side 3–4 minutes.
7. Return to fat-cap side; reduce heat to medium; continue cooking, basting the meat side with the rendered fat in the pan for 3–4 more minutes.
8. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Mallmann's preference for picanha: *jugoso* — a warm, vivid-red interior. The fat renders further during the rest and continues to add flavour.
9. Rest 5 minutes. Slice against the grain. Serve with chimichurri. Final serving temperature: **53–56 °C / 127–133 °F** (*jugoso*, Argentine/Brazilian traditional standard).

### 2. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Seared Coulotte (Sirloin Cap) (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, seriouseats.com/coulotte-sirloin-cap-steak-recipe; also *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 12–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g–1 kg picanha / sirloin cap, fat cap on, scored in a 2 cm crosshatch
- Kosher salt (1 h in advance on both sides)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil (optional)

**Method**
1. Score fat cap; apply kosher salt 1 hour in advance; refrigerate uncovered. Pat dry before cooking.
2. Season with black pepper.
3. Heat a heavy cast-iron pan over high heat. Add a small amount of oil, or cook fat-cap-side first to render fat.
4. Place fat-cap-side down; sear 3–4 minutes with weight pressed on top to ensure full contact.
5. Flip; insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the meat below the fat cap.
6. Continue cooking meat-side down 3 minutes; flip back to fat-cap side and reduce heat; cook 4–5 more minutes, basting.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Kenji's coulotte standard: medium-rare. He notes the fat cap provides continuous self-basting.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice 1 cm thick against the grain. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, American standard).

### 3. Gordon Ramsay — Pan-Seared Rump Cap (British restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012), rump cap preparation  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 12–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 900 g picanha (rump cap), fat cap scored
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 40 g unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, skin-on, smashed
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Score fat cap; season all surfaces.
2. Heat a heavy cast-iron pan over high heat. Add oil.
3. Place fat-cap-side down; press with a weight 3–4 minutes until deeply golden.
4. Flip; sear meat side 2–3 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the meat.
6. Add butter, garlic and thyme; baste continuously for 3–4 minutes on the meat side, then flip back to fat-cap side for final rendering.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Ramsay's target: medium-rare.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice; serve. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, restaurant standard).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Seared Picanha with Yuzu Kosho (Japanese-Brazilian fusion)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Miami: The Party Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2009)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 12–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g picanha, fat cap scored
- Fine salt and white pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Yuzu kosho sauce**: 15 g *yuzu kosho* (*yuzu kosho* — fermented Japanese paste of yuzu zest, chilli and salt); 30 ml (2 tbsp) soy sauce; 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin; 5 ml (1 tsp) sesame oil

**Method**
1. Mix yuzu kosho sauce; set aside.
2. Season picanha; heat a cast-iron pan over high heat; add oil.
3. Place fat-cap-side down with weight; render 3–4 minutes.
4. Flip; sear meat side 2–3 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre.
6. Reduce heat; continue cooking and basting with rendered fat 4–5 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Nobu's target: medium-rare.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice thin; drizzle with yuzu kosho sauce. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, Japanese-Brazilian fusion standard).

### 5. Donna Hay — Pan-Seared Rump Cap with Herb Dressing (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Donna Hay, *The Food I Love* (adapted; HarperCollins Australia)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min dry brine) · **Cook**: 12–14 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g picanha (rump cap), fat cap scored, skin-on
- Sea salt flakes and freshly cracked black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Herb dressing**: 30 g flat-leaf parsley; 15 g fresh mint; 1 garlic clove; 30 ml olive oil; 15 ml red wine vinegar; salt

**Method**
1. Dry-brine 30 minutes; pat dry. Blend herb dressing; set aside.
2. Heat a heavy pan over high heat; add oil.
3. Place fat-cap-side down with weight; render 3–4 minutes until crisp.
4. Flip; sear meat side 3 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre.
6. Reduce heat; continue cooking fat-cap-side up for 4–5 minutes, basting.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Hay's standard: medium-rare.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Slice; drizzle with herb dressing. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, Australian standard).
