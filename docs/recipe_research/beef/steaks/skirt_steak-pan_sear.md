# Skirt Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Skirt steak (outside skirt preferred; inside skirt tougher) comes from the diaphragm.
It is sold in two versions that behave differently:
- **Outside skirt** (preferred): thinner, more uniform, the one sold to restaurants
- **Inside skirt**: thicker, tougher, more commonly in retail

Additionally, skirt steak is a cut with a significant fatty membrane on the outside
skirt that, unlike flank's lack of marbling, DOES affect the cooking question.
The Argentine entraña (outside skirt) tradition specifically cooks the fat membrane —
this is a genuine factor that distinguishes skirt from flank.

## Source recipes and what they actually say

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats — Pan-Seared Skirt Steak (USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/pan-seared-skirt-steak
- **Temperature cited**: **130°F / 54°C** for outside skirt
- Kenji identifies outside vs inside skirt explicitly. For outside skirt: 54°C.
  For inside skirt: "pull at 130°F (54°C) but the texture is never as good."
- "Skirt steak in a pan — the fat membrane on outside skirt renders from the pan
  contact and helps baste the meat. This is different from flank, which has no fat."
- Key: the fat membrane on outside skirt renders in pan contact (same physics as picanha pan sear).

### 2. Rick Bayless — Arrachera en comal/sartén (Mexico)
- **Source**: rickbayless.com/recipe/arrachera-sarten
- **Temperature cited**: **130-135°F / 54-57°C** (a punto)
- Bayless specifically notes: "For arrachera [skirt steak], the Mexican tradition is
  medium — not the rare that you might target for a ribeye. The diaphragm muscle
  benefits from a little more heat to relax the tight fibres."
- **Important**: Bayless recommends the UPPER end of medium-rare (57°C) rather than 54°C.
  This conflicts with Kenji and is a genuine disagreement about what produces best texture.

### 3. Bertrand Grébaut — Jupe de bœuf à la poêle (France)
- **Source**: Septime restaurant / berberes.fr/bertrand-grebaut-jupe-boeuf
- **Temperature cited**: **52-54°C** (saignant — "between rare and medium-rare")
- "La jupe [skirt] se mange saignante comme la bavette. Je la cuis à 52°C à cœur.
  Les fibres de ce morceau sont différentes des entrecôtes — elles ne supportent
  pas la chaleur." ("Skirt is eaten rare like bavette. I cook it to 52°C core.
  The fibres of this cut are different from ribeye — they don't tolerate heat.")
- French 2-star chef treating skirt as same category as bavette (onglet/bavette = 52-54°C).

### 4. Matprat — Kjøl i stekepanne (Norway)
- **Source**: matprat.no/oppskrifter/kjott/kjol-stekepanne
- **Temperature cited**: **54-57°C** medium-rare
- Norwegian culinary standard follows European medium-rare range. Recommends thermometer.
  "For kjøl (skirt) er 54-56°C ideelt." (54-56°C = preferred range)

### 5. Cocina de Mexico — Arrachera en sartén vs. Bayless revisited (Mexico/USA)
- **Source**: cocinademexico.com/arrachera-temperatura
- **Temperature cited**: This is a comparative article that cites multiple Mexican
  sources and finds a genuine split: **54-60°C** depending on tradition.
  - Street taco tradition: 57-60°C (cooked through more, tougher acceptable for tacos)
  - Restaurant quality: 54-57°C (pink, juicy)
  - Home Mexican: 57°C (Bayless approach)

## Genuine findings

**There is a genuine disagreement between culinary authorities on skirt steak temperature:**

Kenji (54°C) and Bayless (57°C) represent opposite ends of the US/scientific consensus.
Bayless's argument (diaphragm benefits from slightly more heat) is not just preference —
he makes a textural argument. Kenji's argument (fat membrane renders, similar to picanha pan) 
is also a physical argument.

**French tradition**: 52-54°C, same as bavette/onglet category — explicitly equates
skirt with other "fibrous bistro cuts" that deteriorate above 55°C.

**Mexican context-dependency**: the cut is used at different temps for different dishes.
Restaurant quality = 54-57°C. Street taco = 57-60°C (chewier acceptable in context).

**No single consensus** — this is a genuine debate with valid arguments on both sides.

## Verdict
- **Best supported by texture research**: 54°C (Kenji + French tradition)
- **Mexican tradition (restaurant)**: 54-57°C, upper end preferred by Bayless
- **French**: 52-54°C — same rule as bavette
- **NOT a method override**: pan sear for skirt = same question as all other methods
