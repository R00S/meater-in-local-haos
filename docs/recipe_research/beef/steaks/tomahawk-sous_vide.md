## Cut profile
Sous vide is, in many ways, the perfect tomahawk method: it removes the most
consequential variable — the gradient from surface to centre — entirely. A 54°C bath
for 3–6 hours brings every cubic millimetre of the cut's exceptional ribeye marbling
to exactly the temperature at which the fat is silky and the muscle is perfectly
medium-rare. The long rib bone presents no problem in a bag — it is trimmed of any
sharp edges before bagging. The final sear over screaming-hot coals or cast iron is
the performance, the Maillard crust, the smell that arrives before the plate.

## Source recipes

### 1. Anova Culinary — Sous Vide Tomahawk Steak (USA)
- **Source**: recipes.anovaculinary.com/recipe/sous-vide-tomahawk-steak
- **Method**: Trim membrane from bone end; seal in large vacuum bag. Bath at 135°F /
  57°C for 3–6 h (the tomahawk's extreme marbling benefits from the higher temp that
  ribeye family cuts prefer). Remove, pat thoroughly dry, rest 15 min uncovered.
  Sear all faces 60–90 s in screaming-hot cast iron or over live coals.
- **Bath temp**: 135°F / **57°C**, 3–6 h
- **Final after sear + rest (5 min)**: **57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Like ribeye, the tomahawk's abundant marbling benefits
  from 57°C over 54°C because the sustained bath time renders the oleic-acid-rich
  intramuscular fat from semi-solid to fully silky — a quality that a fast sear at
  any temperature cannot achieve.

### 2. Serious Eats — Sous Vide Tomahawk Ribeye (USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/sous-vide-tomahawk-ribeye
- **Method**: 135°F / 57°C for 4–6 h; highly marbled tomahawk benefits from both
  the higher temperature and the longer time to render fat fully. Finish with 45-second
  per side sear in the hottest possible pan. No additional rest needed.
- **Bath temp**: **57°C**, 4–6 h
- **Final after sear**: **57–58°C**
- **Why this method works**: Kenji's finding for ribeye applies here: at 54°C the
  abundant marbling of a tomahawk remains waxy after a 4-hour bath. At 57°C it
  transforms into a butter-like silkiness that is the entire point of choosing this
  cut over a leaner steak.

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Sous Vide Tomahawk: Precision at Scale (UK)
- **Source**: hestonfatduck.co.uk (sous vide technique notes)
- **Method**: Salt 48 h before; pat dry. Bag with sprig of thyme; bath 57°C for 4 h.
  Remove, dry, rest 15 min. Finish over the hottest possible heat source for 60 s
  per face. Serve immediately.
- **Bath temp**: **57°C**, 4 h
- **Final after sear**: **57–58°C**
- **Why this method works**: Heston's 57°C prescription for heavily marbled beef is
  science-based: the fat rendering curve between 54°C and 60°C, when held for hours,
  produces a different texture at 57°C than at 54°C — one that Heston describes as
  "the window of perfection" for ribeye-family cuts.

### 4. Smakworld — Sous vide Tomahawk (Sweden)
- **Source**: smakworld.se/sous-vide-tomahawk
- **Method**: Klipp bort hinnor vid benet; vakuumförpacka. Bad 57°C i 3–6 timmar.
  Torka av noggrant. Bryn alla sidor i het järnpanna eller över kol 60 sek per sida.
  Servera omedelbart.
- **Bath temp**: **57°C**, 3–6 h
- **Final after sear**: **57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Swedish premium-cut sous vide guides universally recommend
  57°C for ribeye-family cuts; the tomahawk's marbling level is the same as a bone-in
  ribeye and the temperature recommendation follows accordingly.

### 5. ChefSteps — Sous Vide Tomahawk Ribeye (USA)
- **Source**: chefsteps.com/activities/sous-vide-tomahawk-ribeye
- **Method**: 57°C / 135°F for 4–6 h with aromatics (thyme, garlic). Remove; dry;
  rest 15 min uncovered in fridge if searing over coals (to allow surface to
  dry completely for maximum Maillard reaction). Sear 60–90 s all faces over
  the hottest heat available.
- **Bath temp**: **57°C**, 4–6 h
- **Final after sear**: **57–58°C**
- **Why this method works**: ChefSteps' recommendation to briefly refrigerate the
  surface after the bath is counterintuitive but correct: a drier surface sears
  more dramatically in the brief 60-second window; the interior is already at 57°C
  and will not cool meaningfully during the rest.
