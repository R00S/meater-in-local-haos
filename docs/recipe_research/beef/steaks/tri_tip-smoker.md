## Cut profile
Smoking a tri-tip sits at the crossroads of California BBQ tradition and the low-and-
slow world of Southern BBQ. The cut is not a collagen-rich braise candidate — it does
not need hours at 88°C the way a brisket does — but a longer smoke session at 110–130°C
impregnates the open-grained sirloin muscle with a smoke ring and bark character that
cannot be achieved any other way. The trick is knowing when to stop: tri-tip wants to be
pulled at medium-rare (54–57°C), not well-done, and a smoker cook must resist the
instinct to leave it on longer just because it still looks pale.

## Source recipes

### 1. Aaron Franklin — Smoked Tri-Tip (USA / Franklin Barbecue)
- **Source**: franklinbbq.com (tri-tip notes from Franklin's MasterClass)
- **Method**: Texas post oak smoke at 110–120°C ambient. Unwrapped for full smoke.
  Cook until probe reads 52°C (2–3 h depending on size). No wrap needed. Rest 15 min.
- **Pull temp**: **52°C** (at thickest point)
- **Final after rest (15 min)**: **54–57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Franklin argues that tri-tip's moderate fat content and
  lean sirloin muscle reach maximum smoke ring depth and bark formation by 52°C;
  taking it further destroys the texture that differentiates it from overcooked
  chuck. The low 110°C ambient temperature maximises smoke absorption time.

### 2. Serious Eats — Smoked Tri-Tip: California Meets Texas (USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/smoked-tri-tip
- **Method**: Pellet smoker or offset at 120°C / 250°F; hickory or red oak. Cook
  until probe reads 54°C (2–3 h). Wrap in butcher paper for last 30 min if colour
  is already set. Rest 15 min unwrapped.
- **Pull temp**: 130°F / **54°C**
- **Final after rest (15 min)**: **56–57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Unlike brisket, tri-tip does not benefit from a long
  stall and does not need to push through it; pulling at 54°C keeps the interior
  medium-rare while the bark and smoke ring are fully developed by 2–3 hours.

### 3. Meathead Goldwyn — Smoked Tri-Tip (USA / AmazingRibs.com)
- **Source**: amazingribs.com/recipes/beef/smoked-tri-tip
- **Method**: 225°F / 107°C smoker; oak or fruitwood. Cook until 130°F / 54°C,
  approximately 2–2.5 h. Rest 15 min. Serve sliced thin, cutting across grain in
  two directions from the point of the triangle.
- **Pull temp**: 130°F / **54°C**
- **Final after rest (15 min)**: **56–57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Meathead recommends fruitwood for a lighter smoke that
  complements the lean sirloin; the two-direction slice at the tip of the triangle
  is essential because the grain of a tri-tip runs in two planes that meet at a seam.

### 4. BBQ Pit Boys — Smoked Tri-Tip (USA / YouTube)
- **Source**: bbqpitboys.com (smoked tri-tip video)
- **Method**: Offset smoker with hickory at 225–250°F / 107–120°C. No wrap.
  Smoke 2–3 h until 57°C. Rest 15 min under foil.
- **Pull temp**: 135°F / **57°C**
- **Final after rest (15 min)**: **59–60°C**
- **Why this method works**: BBQ Pit Boys target the higher end of the medium-rare
  window because their audience prefers a warmer centre; hickory at 107–120°C gives
  a deeper smoke ring in the extra time before reaching 57°C vs. a hotter smoke.

### 5. Grillade.se — Rökt Tri-tip (Sweden)
- **Source**: grillade.se/rokt-tri-tip
- **Method**: Pellets-rökare eller offset med alträ (alder) vid 120°C. Rök tills
  kärntemperatur 54°C (ca 2–3 timmar). Vila 15 min under folie.
- **Pull temp**: **54°C**
- **Final after rest (15 min)**: **56–57°C**
- **Why this method works**: Swedish smoking guides recommend alder wood as a mild
  alternative to hickory; tri-tip is increasingly popular in the Nordic country's
  BBQ community, and the mild smoke suits the cleaner flavour preferences of the
  Swedish palate.
