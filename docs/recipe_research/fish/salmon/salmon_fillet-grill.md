# Salmon Fillet × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Salmon fillet on the grill is exposed to intense radiant and convective heat from below; if skin-on, the skin acts as a protective buffer between the direct flame and the delicate flesh. The fat content — particularly high in farmed Atlantic salmon — causes flare-ups when it renders and drips onto hot coals or burner elements, so heat management is critical. Wild salmon (sockeye, chinook) is leaner and more forgiving of moderate heat but more sensitive to overcooking. The key structural challenge of grilling salmon is the transition from firm, raw myotomes to the characteristic flake: this happens rapidly above 60 °C, so the cook window between underdone and overdone is narrow. Carryover is 2–3 °C from a screaming-hot grill surface; skin-side-down cooking provides a buffer and allows the cook to monitor visual cues (the translucency line moving up the fillet from below) without flipping.

## Source recipes

### 1. Steven Raichlen — Plank-Grilled Salmon (American tradition)
**Source**: Steven Raichlen, *BBQ USA* (Workman Publishing, 2003)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 h soaking) · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 cedar plank, 30 × 15 cm, soaked in water for at least 1 hour
- 1 centre-cut salmon fillet, skin-on, approximately 700 g, 3–4 cm thick
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 10 g (2 tsp) brown sugar
- 5 g (1 tsp) smoked paprika
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 2 g (¼ tsp) garlic powder
- Lemon wedges and fresh dill, to serve

**Method**
1. Soak cedar plank in cold water for a minimum of 1 hour (overnight is better). Weight it down with a heavy object to keep it submerged.
2. Mix brown sugar, smoked paprika, salt, pepper and garlic powder into a dry rub.
3. Rub the flesh side of the salmon generously with olive oil, then apply the dry rub over the entire flesh surface. Allow to sit at room temperature for 15 minutes.
4. Preheat gas or charcoal grill to medium-high (approximately 220 °C grate temperature). Place the soaked plank directly on the grate and heat it for 3 minutes until it begins to crackle and smoke.
5. Place the salmon skin-side down on the hot plank. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet into the centre of the flesh, parallel to the plank surface, avoiding the skin.
6. Close the grill lid. Cook 15–20 minutes without flipping — the plank conducts gentle heat from below and the closed grill creates convective heat from above. The plank smoke flavours the fish.
7. Monitor the probe and watch for the translucency line to migrate to the centre of the fillet.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Raichlen's target is medium — salmon should still be slightly translucent at the very centre when pulled; it will continue to firm up on the plank off the heat.
9. Rest 3 minutes on the plank. Serve with lemon wedges and dill. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium, firm but moist).

### 2. Yotam Ottolenghi — Chargrilled Salmon with Chermoula (North African / Middle Eastern tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi and Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 salmon fillets, skin-on, 160–180 g each
- **Chermoula**: 30 g fresh coriander (cilantro), roughly chopped; 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley; 4 garlic cloves; 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin; 3 g (½ tsp) sweet paprika; 2 g (¼ tsp) ground cinnamon; 1 g (pinch) cayenne; 60 ml (¼ cup) olive oil; juice of 1 lemon; 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- Extra olive oil, for brushing grill grates
- Lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Blend all chermoula ingredients to a rough paste in a food processor. Coat salmon fillets generously; marinate skin-up in the fridge for 2 hours.
2. Remove from fridge 20 minutes before grilling. Scrape excess marinade from the flesh — the herbs will burn on direct heat.
3. Preheat grill to high. Brush grates generously with olive oil.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet into the centre of the flesh before placing on the grill.
5. Place salmon skin-side down on the oiled grates. Grill 5–6 minutes without moving.
6. Flip carefully with a wide spatula. Grill flesh-side down for 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Ottolenghi's preference is for salmon that is barely cooked through — yielding, moist, with a warm interior.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with lemon wedges and any remaining chermoula. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium, moist).

### 3. René Redzepi — Grilled Wild Salmon with Burned Leek Ash (Scandinavian / New Nordic tradition)
**Source**: René Redzepi, *Noma: Time and Place in Nordic Cuisine* (Phaidon Press, 2010)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 wild Atlantic or Baltic salmon fillets, skin-on, 160–200 g each
- 2 leeks, outer leaves only (inner heart reserved for garnish)
- Sea salt flakes
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) cold-pressed rapeseed (*raps*) oil (*raps* — Scandinavian cold-pressed canola, nutty and grassy)
- 20 g unsalted butter
- Fresh dill fronds and green cucumber slices, to serve

**Method**
1. Grill or char the outer leek leaves directly over an open flame or under a grill until completely blackened. Grind in a mortar to fine ash. Season with a pinch of sea salt to make leek ash seasoning.
2. Season salmon flesh side with sea salt flakes and brush lightly with rapeseed oil.
3. Preheat grill to medium-high, approximately 200 °C at the grate. Clean and oil grates.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of each fillet into the centre of the flesh, keeping the probe below the skin line.
5. Place salmon skin-side down on the grill. Cook 7–9 minutes, lid open or closed depending on available heat — the New Nordic approach favours a slightly longer cook at moderate heat to develop gentle smoky flavour from the wood grill.
6. Flip once, briefly. Dot with butter and cook flesh-side down for 1 minute.
7. **Pull at 50 °C (122 °F).** Wild salmon has less fat than farmed; Redzepi pulls early to preserve the delicate flavour of the wild fish at the rare-to-medium-rare boundary.
8. Rest 3 minutes skin-side up. Dust flesh with leek ash. Serve with dill and cucumber. Final serving temperature: **52–53 °C / 126–128 °F** (medium-rare, warm rosy centre).

### 4. Tetsuya Wakuda — Grilled Ocean Trout with Konbu (Japanese-Australian fusion tradition)
**Source**: Tetsuya Wakuda, *Tetsuya* (Penguin Australia, 2000)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min (+30 min konbu cure) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 ocean trout (or Atlantic salmon) fillets, skin-on, 180–200 g each
- 2 sheets dried *konbu* (*konbu* — dried Japanese kelp, providing umami and light salt), large enough to wrap each fillet
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin
- 10 ml (2 tsp) soy sauce (*shoyu*)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- Warmed dashi broth and pickled ginger, to serve

**Method**
1. Lightly dampen each konbu sheet with a damp cloth. Brush the flesh side of the salmon with mirin and soy sauce.
2. Place each fillet flesh-side down on a konbu sheet. Wrap loosely; refrigerate for 30 minutes. The konbu imparts glutamate (umami) and gently seasons the flesh without adding salt.
3. Unwrap the salmon. Brush flesh side lightly with vegetable oil.
4. Preheat grill to medium (not high — the soy in the marinade will scorch at high heat). Clean and oil grates.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
6. Place salmon skin-side down. Grill 6–8 minutes.
7. Flip briefly; grill flesh-side down 1–2 minutes.
8. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Tetsuya's preference is for salmon that is barely set — translucent at the centre, with the delicate flavour of the konbu cure undistracted by overcooking.
9. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with warm dashi. Final serving temperature: **54–55 °C / 130–131 °F** (medium, just-cooked centre).

### 5. Rick Stein — Grilled Salmon with Salsa Verde (British / Mediterranean tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Rick Stein's Seafood* (BBC Books, 2001)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 skin-on salmon fillets, 160–180 g each
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- **Salsa verde**: 20 g flat-leaf parsley; 10 g fresh basil; 10 g fresh mint; 2 tbsp capers, rinsed; 4 anchovy fillets; 1 garlic clove; 15 ml (1 tbsp) red wine vinegar; 90 ml (6 tbsp) olive oil; salt and black pepper

**Method**
1. Blend or pound all salsa verde ingredients in a mortar — Stein prefers a slightly rough texture. Adjust acid and salt. Set aside.
2. Season salmon fillets on both sides with sea salt and black pepper; brush with olive oil.
3. Preheat grill to high. Brush grates well with oil.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet into the centre of the flesh.
5. Place salmon skin-side down. Grill 5–6 minutes, lid closed if possible.
6. Flip. Grill flesh-side down for 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Stein's seafood book targets fully cooked but still moist — he writes that salmon "should never be served dry" but notes that the British home cook prefers no translucency.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Spoon salsa verde generously over the top. Final serving temperature: **56–58 °C / 133–136 °F** (medium to medium-well, opaque throughout, moist).
