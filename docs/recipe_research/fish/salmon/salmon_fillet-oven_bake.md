# Salmon Fillet × Oven Bake — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Salmon fillet baked in the oven is cooked primarily by convective heat circulating around the fish, with radiant heat from the oven walls contributing secondary surface browning. Unlike pan searing, oven baking does not create a crust on the skin without an initial high-heat blast; the gentler environment means the flesh sets gradually from the outside inward, producing a more uniformly cooked texture. Baking is particularly well-suited to whole-side fillets and thicker portions where even heat penetration matters; it also permits wet preparations (baking in parchment or foil en papillote, or in a liquid bath) that are impractical on a pan. The carryover in an oven-baked salmon fillet at conventional oven temperatures (180–220 °C) is 3–5 °C, meaning pull timing is critical; at low-and-slow temperatures (100–120 °C) carryover is minimal.

## Source recipes

### 1. America's Test Kitchen — Slow-Roasted Salmon (American tradition)
**Source**: America's Test Kitchen, *The Complete Cookbook for Two* (America's Test Kitchen, 2014); americastestkitchen.com/recipes/7952-slow-roasted-salmon  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min rest) · **Cook**: 30–40 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets, 170–200 g each, 3–4 cm thick (centre-cut portions, even thickness)
- 5 g (1 tsp) olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- Lemon wedges and chopped fresh herbs, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat salmon dry. Rub flesh side with olive oil; season with salt and pepper. Let stand at room temperature 30 minutes.
2. Adjust oven rack to the middle position. Preheat oven to 120 °C (250 °F) — the ATK slow-roast method uses a drastically lower temperature than conventional baking to prevent the albumin (white protein) from coagulating and weeping onto the surface.
3. Place salmon skin-side down on a wire rack set over a rimmed baking sheet lined with foil.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet into the centre of the flesh, clear of the skin.
5. Roast at 120 °C for 30–40 minutes, depending on fillet thickness. The exterior will not brown in this method.
6. Check the probe — at the pull temperature, the centre will appear just barely opaque, with no resistance when pressed gently with a finger.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** ATK's slow-roast method targets 125 °F (52 °C); at this temperature the flesh is just barely set, with no albumin weeping, maximally moist throughout.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Serve immediately with lemon. Final serving temperature: **54–55 °C / 130–131 °F** (medium, silky and uniform throughout).

### 2. Julia Child — Saumon au Four en Papillote (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking, Volume 1* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 25–30 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 salmon fillets, 180–200 g each (skinless preferred for en papillote)
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 1 medium shallot, finely minced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine or dry vermouth
- 30 g unsalted butter, cut into small pieces
- 1 tbsp fresh tarragon or chervil, finely chopped
- 1 tsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- 2 large sheets parchment paper (baking paper), approximately 40 × 30 cm each

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200 °C (400 °F). Cut parchment into heart shapes — fold each sheet in half, cut a large half-heart so that when unfolded, the sheet is a heart roughly twice the size of the salmon fillet.
2. Place each fillet in the centre of one half of a parchment heart. Season with salt and white pepper.
3. Sprinkle shallot and herbs over each fillet. Dot with butter pieces. Pour 30 ml (2 tbsp) white wine over each.
4. Fold the other half of the heart over the fish. Starting from the top of the heart, crimp the edges together in a series of tight overlapping folds to seal the parcel completely.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet through the parchment — the probe wire will exit through the fold without breaking the steam seal if inserted at the outer crimp.
6. Place parcels on a baking sheet. Bake 25–30 minutes — the parcel will puff dramatically as the steam builds inside.
7. **Pull at 55–57 °C (131–135 °F).** En papillote cooking traps steam and raises the ambient temperature inside the parcel above the oven temperature; Child's target is a fully cooked fillet with no translucency, which she recommends for any salmon prepared this way.
8. Serve the parcel at the table; slit with scissors. Rest 3 minutes in the parcel. Final serving temperature: **57–59 °C / 135–138 °F** (fully cooked, tender and steamed through).

### 3. Ottolenghi — Baked Salmon with Preserved Lemon and Za'atar (Middle Eastern tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi and Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 15–18 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 side of salmon, skin-on, approximately 900 g–1.1 kg, pin-boned
- 2 preserved lemons, flesh discarded, rind finely chopped
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 10 g (2 tsp) za'atar (*za'atar* — dried wild thyme, sesame, sumac, salt blend)
- 5 g (1 tsp) sumac (*sumac* — ground dried red berry with tart, lemony flavour)
- Fine salt and freshly ground black pepper
- Fresh flat-leaf parsley and lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Mix preserved lemon rind, garlic, olive oil, za'atar, sumac, salt and pepper into a paste. Coat the flesh side of the salmon side. Marinate for 2 hours (refrigerated) or 30 minutes at room temperature.
2. Preheat oven to 200 °C (400 °F / Gas 6). Line a large rimmed baking tray with foil.
3. Place salmon skin-side down on the tray. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end (the head end) of the fillet into the centre of the flesh, below the marinade surface.
4. Roast for 15–18 minutes. The marinade will dry and caramelise slightly at the edges.
5. **Pull at 54–56 °C (130–133 °F).** Ottolenghi's baked salmon standard for a whole side is medium — opaque throughout but still yielding. The thin tail end will exceed this temperature; the probe reads the thickest central section.
6. Rest 3 minutes. Scatter fresh parsley. Serve with lemon. Final serving temperature: **56–58 °C / 133–136 °F** (medium, opaque, tender).

### 4. Gordon Ramsay — Baked Salmon with Mustard Herb Crust (British restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 salmon fillets, skin-on, 180–200 g each
- 15 g (1 tbsp) Dijon mustard
- 30 g fresh white breadcrumbs
- 15 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- 10 g (2 tsp) finely grated lemon zest
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 190 °C (375 °F). Line a baking tray with baking paper. Season salmon on both sides.
2. Mix breadcrumbs, parsley, lemon zest and olive oil in a small bowl.
3. Place salmon skin-side down on the tray. Brush flesh side with Dijon mustard. Press the breadcrumb mixture firmly over the mustard to form a crust.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet, through the crust, into the centre of the flesh, angling slightly to stay within the thickest part.
5. Bake 12–15 minutes — the crust should turn golden-brown and crisp while the flesh beneath cooks gently.
6. **Pull at 54 °C (130 °F).** Ramsay's oven salmon target is medium — fully opaque, moist, with the crust fully set and crisp. He notes that the crust acts as insulation, slowing the surface temperature rise.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Serve with new potatoes and a green salad. Final serving temperature: **56–57 °C / 133–135 °F** (medium, opaque with golden crust).

### 5. Paula Wolfert — Baked Salmon with Charmoula en Cocotte (Moroccan tradition)
**Source**: Paula Wolfert, *The Food of Morocco* (Ecco/HarperCollins, 2011)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 h marinade) · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 salmon portions, skinless, 150–180 g each
- **Charmoula marinade**: 30 g fresh coriander (cilantro); 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley; 4 garlic cloves; 10 ml (2 tsp) ground cumin; 5 ml (1 tsp) sweet paprika; 2 ml (½ tsp) ground turmeric; 1 preserved lemon quarter, rind only; 60 ml (¼ cup) olive oil; 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh lemon juice; 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 200 g cherry tomatoes, halved
- 1 green pepper (*charmoula* — Wolfert notes green peppers are traditional in Moroccan fish tagines), sliced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) water or fish stock

**Method**
1. Blend charmoula ingredients to a rough paste; taste for salt and acid. Coat salmon portions generously. Marinate 1 hour at room temperature (or up to 4 hours refrigerated).
2. Preheat oven to 180 °C (350 °F). In an ovenproof earthenware or cast-iron cocotte (*cocotte* — a lidded heavy casserole), arrange the marinated salmon in a single layer.
3. Scatter cherry tomatoes and green pepper around and over the fish. Pour in water or fish stock.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one salmon portion into its centre.
5. Cover with the lid and bake 20 minutes. Remove lid; bake a further 5 minutes to reduce the braising liquid slightly.
6. **Pull at 57–60 °C (135–140 °F).** Wolfert's Moroccan fish preparations target fully cooked — the braised cocotte method steams as much as it bakes, and the traditional Moroccan kitchen does not serve fish with a raw centre.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Serve directly from the cocotte with crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **60–62 °C / 140–144 °F** (well-done, flaking, fully cooked through).
