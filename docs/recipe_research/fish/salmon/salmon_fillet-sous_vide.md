# Salmon Fillet × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Salmon fillet cooked sous vide is sealed in a vacuum bag and submerged in a precision-temperature water bath for an extended period; the bath temperature is the cook temperature, and the fish will equilibrate to that temperature throughout its thickness over sufficient time. This method produces radically different textures compared to pan searing or roasting — at bath temperatures below 50 °C, the connective tissue between muscle segments (myocommata) softens but the proteins in the myotomes remain in a semi-liquid, custard-like state; at 52–54 °C the salmon fully sets to a firm but moist texture. The major structural advantage of sous vide is elimination of the hard-to-control temperature gradient from surface to core: the entire fillet is held at one temperature. Carryover is essentially zero — the fish exits the bath at the precise target temperature and the bag insulates it — but a quick sear or glaze step post-bath will add 2–3 °C at the surface.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Sous Vide Salmon (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/sous-vide-salmon-recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 40–60 min at bath temp · **Rest**: 0 min (serve immediately after optional sear)

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets, 170–200 g each, 3–4 cm thick at the centre
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 2 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp fresh dill or tarragon (optional, inside the bag)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil (for optional post-bath sear)
- Lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Season salmon fillets with salt and pepper on all sides. Place each fillet in a vacuum bag with fresh herbs if using. Seal using a vacuum sealer. Alternatively, use the water-displacement method with a zip-lock bag, pressing out air as you lower it into the water.
2. Set the sous vide circulator to **50 °C (122 °F)** for a translucent, silky, barely-set result; or **52 °C (126 °F)** for a medium texture. Kenji discusses the spectrum in detail: below 48 °C the flesh is nearly raw; 50–52 °C produces the custard-soft Kenji-preferred texture; above 54 °C produces a more conventional flaky texture.
3. Submerge the sealed bags. Cook for 40 minutes (thin fillets, 2 cm) to 60 minutes (thick portions, 4 cm). Longer times do not harm the fish at this temperature.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: A probe cannot be inserted during a sealed-bag sous vide cook. The circulator temperature is the cook target — set the bath to the target internal temperature. Post-bath, verify surface temperature after the optional sear if desired.
5. Remove bags from bath. Pat the fish dry if searing.
6. Optional post-bath sear: Heat oil in a pan over the highest possible heat. Sear skin-side down for 60 seconds only to crisp the skin — the interior is already at the target temperature.
7. **Pull from bath at bath temperature** (50–52 °C / 122–126 °F). After optional 60-second sear, the surface exceeds this, but the core remains at bath temperature. Final serving temperature: **50–52 °C / 122–126 °F** (the circulator temperature; medium-rare to medium, silky and translucent at the lower end).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sous Vide Wild King Salmon with Beurre Rouge (French haute cuisine tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, Michael Ruhlman and Sebastián Rouxel, *Ad Hoc at Home* (Artisan Books, 2009); and the sous vide techniques documented in *Under Pressure* (Artisan Books, 2008)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 17–20 min at 52 °C · **Rest**: 0 min (serve immediately)

**Ingredients**
- 4 wild king salmon portions, skinless, 150 g each, uniform thickness (skin removed to allow full surface contact with beurre rouge)
- Fine salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 20 ml (4 tsp) extra virgin olive oil (for bag)
- **Beurre rouge**: 2 shallots, minced; 120 ml (½ cup) red wine; 30 ml (2 tbsp) red wine vinegar; 150 g cold unsalted butter, cubed; salt and white pepper

**Method**
1. Season salmon portions with fine salt and white pepper. Place each in a vacuum bag with 5 ml olive oil. Vacuum-seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **52 °C (126 °F)**. Keller's published protocol for wild king salmon targets 17–20 minutes at 52 °C — thin, uniform-thickness portions require less time than thick irregular fillets.
3. Submerge the bags. Cook exactly 17 minutes for a 2 cm portion; 20 minutes for a 3 cm portion.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be used inside the sealed bag. The bath temperature (52 °C) is the cook target. For post-bath verification: after removing from the bag, a probe inserted into the thickest point should read within 1–2 °C of the bath temperature.
5. Meanwhile, prepare beurre rouge: sweat shallots with red wine and vinegar; reduce to a syrup. Over low heat, whisk in cold butter cube by cube to form an emulsified sauce. Season; keep warm.
6. Remove salmon from bags; pat dry. Serve immediately — do not sear (Keller's protocol for this preparation omits post-bath searing to preserve the silky texture).
7. **Bath temperature: 52 °C (126 °F).** Final serving temperature: **52 °C / 126 °F** (medium — just barely set, silky, translucent in the deepest centre with no albumin weeping, Keller's standard).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Low-Temperature Salmon (British Modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *The Fat Duck Cookbook* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2008)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min at 56 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 salmon portions, skinless, 180 g each, 4 cm thick
- Fine salt
- 10 ml (2 tsp) olive oil
- 1 sprig fresh dill (in bag, optional)

**Method**
1. Season salmon lightly with fine salt. Place in vacuum bags with olive oil and dill if using. Seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **56 °C (133 °F)**. Blumenthal's published temperature for salmon is 56 °C — considerably higher than Keller or Kenji — producing a firmer, more conventional texture while still retaining more moisture than conventional cooking.
3. Submerge and cook for 20–25 minutes.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The circulator temperature (56 °C) is the cook target. Post-bath, a probe may be used to verify temperature immediately after removing from bag.
5. Remove from bags. Pat dry. Serve immediately, or sear skin briefly if skin-on was used.
6. **Bath temperature: 56 °C (133 °F).** Final serving temperature: **56 °C / 133 °F** (medium-well, fully opaque and firm but moist, Blumenthal's stated preference for salmon at The Fat Duck).

### 4. Joël Robuchon — Saumon mi-cuit sous vide (French gastronomic tradition)
**Source**: Joël Robuchon, *The Complete Robuchon* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2008); and his published protocols at L'Atelier de Joël Robuchon  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 30–35 min at 48 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 Atlantic salmon portions, skinless and pin-boned, 150 g each
- Fine fleur de sel (*fleur de sel* — hand-harvested unrefined sea salt from France)
- Freshly ground white pepper
- 20 ml (4 tsp) high-quality olive oil (for the bags)
- **Serving**: beurre monté (60 g butter melted into 15 ml water, held at 75 °C), micro herbs

**Method**
1. Season salmon delicately with fleur de sel and white pepper. Place each in a vacuum bag with 5 ml olive oil. Seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **48 °C (118 °F)**. Robuchon's *mi-cuit* (*mi-cuit* — half-cooked, the French gastronomic term for salmon deliberately left translucent at the core) protocol uses 48 °C for 30 minutes.
3. Submerge and cook 30–35 minutes.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The bath temperature (48 °C) is the cook target. This temperature is below the conventional food-safety threshold of 54 °C for 15 seconds; Robuchon specifies this for sashimi-grade fish only.
5. Remove from bags. Brush with warm beurre monté.
6. **Bath temperature: 48 °C (118 °F).** This is *mi-cuit* — deliberately translucent at the core. For sashimi-grade farmed Atlantic salmon only. Final serving temperature: **48 °C / 118 °F** (*mi-cuit*, deep translucency at centre, fully tender, Robuchon's standard for the finest salmon).

### 5. Donna Hay — Sous Vide Salmon with Lemon and Capers (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Donna Hay, *Modern Baking* adapted with sous vide technique from *The Fast 400 Cookbook* (HarperCollins Australia, 2020)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 45 min at 52 °C · **Rest**: 0 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 salmon fillets, skinless, 180–200 g each
- Sea salt flakes and freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil (for the bags)
- 5 g (1 tsp) capers, rinsed
- 3 g (½ tsp) finely grated lemon zest
- Fresh dill, to serve
- Lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Season salmon fillets with sea salt and pepper. Place in vacuum bags with olive oil, capers and lemon zest. Seal.
2. Set the sous vide bath to **52 °C (126 °F)**.
3. Submerge and cook 45 minutes.
4. **Note for MEATER probe**: Probe cannot be inserted during the sealed-bag cook. The bath temperature (52 °C) is the cook target. Post-bath temperature can be verified with the probe immediately after removing from the bag.
5. Remove from bags. Pat dry. Serve immediately with fresh dill and lemon wedges. Hay's recipe does not include a post-bath sear.
6. **Bath temperature: 52 °C (126 °F).** Final serving temperature: **52 °C / 126 °F** (medium, Hay's Australian standard — moist, just-set, barely translucent at the thickest centre).
