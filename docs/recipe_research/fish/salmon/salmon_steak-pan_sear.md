# Salmon Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

A salmon steak is a cross-cut through the whole fish, perpendicular to the spine. Unlike a
fillet (which is cut from the side of the fish, parallel to the spine), the steak includes
the central backbone, the two belly flap lobes, and skin on all exposed outer surfaces. The
shape is a roughly horseshoe cross-section. The thickest portion is the back-flesh section
directly around and above the spine; the belly flaps are the thin curved portions.

**How pan searing differs from fillet**: The round cross-section means the steak can be seared
on both cut faces (the flat sides) plus the outer skin edge. Most pan-sear approaches stand the
steak on its cut faces rather than skin-side down, because the stable flat surfaces allow
controlled heat transfer. The central bone conducts heat toward the centre, which can slightly
accelerate cooking around the spine — conversely, the belly flap portions are very thin and
will overcook before the thickest back section is done if left flat.

**Practical approach**: Some cooks fold the belly flaps inward and secure with kitchen twine or
a toothpick, creating a more uniform thickness across the cross-section. This helps even cooking
and is common in French classical and fine-dining preparations. In simpler home preparations, the
steak is laid flat and the belly flaps are simply eaten first as they finish earlier.

**MEATER probe placement**: Insert from the outer edge of the back-flesh (the thickest section,
opposite the belly flap side), angling toward the geometric centre of the cross-section. Do not
insert from the belly flap side — too thin and the probe will be close to bone. The tip should
rest in the middle of the back-flesh section, at least 1 cm away from the central bone. The
spine will conduct heat slightly faster around the bone, so the meat 1 cm from the bone may read
2–3 °C higher than the true centre; the probe should not be in contact with the bone.

**Temperature targets** (same as salmon fillet):
- **Medium-rare**: 49 °C / 120 °F pull (cool, slightly translucent jewel-red centre)
- **Medium**: 54 °C / 130 °F pull (warm, moist, just-opaque centre) — recommended doneness
- **USDA safe / well-done**: 63 °C / 145 °F pull (fully opaque, flaky)
- Carryover: 2–3 °C due to the thin cross-section geometry

## Source recipes

### 1. Kelli Foster — Easy Pan-Seared Salmon (American / Western tradition)
**Source**: Kelli Foster (Culinary Producer, French Culinary Institute graduate), thekitchn.com/how-to-cook-perfect-salmon-fillets-230150  
**Published**: The Kitchn · **Makes**: 2 fillets/steaks · **Cook**: 10–15 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 (6–8 oz / 170–220 g) skin-on salmon fillets or steaks
- 1 tbsp canola or vegetable oil (high smoke-point)
- Kosher salt

**Method**
1. Remove salmon from refrigerator 15–20 minutes before cooking to bring closer to room temperature. (Cold fish seizes up in a hot pan, cooking unevenly.)
2. Pat each piece completely dry with paper towels on top and bottom. Moisture causes sticking and prevents crust formation.
3. Heat a medium stainless-steel or cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat for 2–3 minutes. Test: drop a few drops of water into the pan — they should sizzle and evaporate almost immediately.
4. Add 1 tbsp oil and tilt to coat; heat until ripples form across the surface but before it smokes.
5. Season the salmon with kosher salt just before adding to the pan.
6. Carefully place skin-side down (or flat-side down for steaks). Do not move once placed.
7. Cook undisturbed until the lighter-coloured flesh has moved about three-quarters of the way up the side: **6–7 min for thinner pieces, 8–9 min for thicker pieces**.
8. Flip using a fish spatula. Cook for **1–2 min more for thinner, 2 min for thicker**.
9. Transfer to a paper-towel-lined plate. Rest **3 minutes**.
10. **Temperature reference** (from thekitchn.com temperature guide): USDA safe minimum: **145 °F / 63 °C**. For moist, medium-rare salmon: **120 °F / 49 °C**. The recipe targets medium as described above using the visual three-quarters indicator.

### 2. Suzy Karadsheh — Mediterranean Pan-Seared Salmon (Egyptian-American / Southern tradition)
**Source**: Suzy Karadsheh (born Port Said, Egypt), themediterraneandish.com/pan-seared-salmon-recipe-with-orange-juice-wilted-spinach-and-arugula/  
**Makes**: 4 fillets/steaks · **Cook**: ~15 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 (4 oz / 115 g) skin-on salmon fillets or steaks
- 1½ tbsp dried oregano
- 1 tbsp garlic powder
- ¾ tsp paprika
- Kosher salt and black pepper
- Extra virgin olive oil (about 2 tbsp, to coat the pan)
- 2 lemons
- 5 oz (140 g) baby arugula, for serving (optional)

**Method**
1. In a small bowl, mix oregano, garlic powder, and paprika.
2. Pat the salmon very dry with paper towels on all sides.
3. Sprinkle all over with salt and black pepper, then rub the spice blend all over the flesh side(s).
4. Heat a large cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat. When hot, add enough oil to coat the bottom (~2 tbsp) and wait until the oil shimmers but does not smoke.
5. Reduce to medium-low heat. Place one salmon piece skin-side down; press top with a fish spatula for ~10 seconds to prevent curling. Repeat with remaining pieces.
6. Cook undisturbed until skin is golden and crispy, about **4 minutes**. If skin resists lifting, cook a little longer (up to 6 minutes total skin-side).
7. Flip. Cook for about **15 seconds** on the flesh side. Transfer to a rimmed plate.
8. Immediately zest one lemon over the fish, then squeeze the juice over.
9. **Pull temperatures** (from the recipe page):
   - Rare: **43 °C / 110 °F**
   - **Medium-rare: 49 °C / 120 °F** ✅
   - **Medium: 54 °C / 130 °F** ✅
   - "Salmon is ready when it flakes easily with a fork at the thickest part."
10. Optional: toss arugula with a pinch of salt, juice of ½ lemon, and drizzle of olive oil. Serve alongside.

### 3. Judy & Sarah Leung — Pan Fried Salmon (Chinese-American / Eastern tradition)
**Source**: Judy & Sarah Leung, thewoksoflife.com/pan-fried-salmon/  
**Makes**: 4 fillets/steaks · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: ~20 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 salmon fillets or steaks (6–8 oz / 170–220 g each, about 2.5 cm thick)
- Salt
- 2 tsp neutral oil (vegetable, canola, or avocado)

**Method**
1. Rinse salmon and pat dry with paper towels. Sprinkle both sides with salt.
2. Preheat a 10–12 inch skillet over medium-high heat. For stainless steel, carbon steel, or cast iron: heat until a splash of water beads and jumps around the pan (but not smoking vigorously). For nonstick: heat until the oil shimmers after adding.
3. Swirl the oil in the pan. Place salmon skin-side down (or flat-side down for steaks), about 1 inch apart.
4. Cook for **4 minutes** on the first side, without moving.
5. Flip and reduce heat to medium. Rotate the pan 180°. Cook the second side for **4–5 minutes**.
6. **Doneness check**: "As the salmon cooks, it will gradually turn opaque from bottom up. Cook until there is only a very thin line of translucent centre still visible — the fish will continue to cook off heat." For medium, the centre should be just-opaque. For medium-rare, a slightly translucent jewel-orange centre should remain.
7. **Note**: The Woks of Life targets what the Western culinary world calls "medium" — "just opaque inside" — which corresponds to approximately **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** internal. Their medium-rare has a "slightly translucent, jewel-like orange center."
8. If steaks are thicker than 2.5 cm, add 1–2 extra minutes per side.

### 4. Laleh Maghonaki — Stekt Laxfilé (Swedish / Northern tradition)
**Source**: Laleh Maghonaki (Swedish chef/TV personality), koket.se/laleh_maghonaki/smaratter_och_tillbehor/fisk_och_skaldjur/stekt_laxfile  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 4–6 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g (1.3 lbs) salmon fillet or steaks
- 2 dl (200 ml / ¾ cup) plain wheat flour (vetemjöl)
- 1 tbsp turmeric (gurkmeja)
- 1 tbsp salt
- 1 egg
- Oil or butter for frying

**Method**
1. Mix flour, salt, and turmeric in a shallow bowl. Spread on a plate.
2. Cut the salmon into 4–6 portions.
3. Whisk the egg with a pinch of salt.
4. Dip each salmon portion in the egg, then coat in the turmeric-flour mixture so the fish is evenly breaded (panerad).
5. Heat a frying pan over medium heat. Add oil or a knob of butter.
6. Fry the salmon for **a couple of minutes per side (ca 2–3 min each side)**. The salmon is done when it has developed a beautiful golden frying surface (fin stekyta) and is just cooked through.
7. **Temperature note**: No explicit thermometer target given. The Swedish culinary tradition for pan-fried salmon typically targets approximately **50 °C / 122 °F** internal for a moist, just-cooked result — achieved after the crisp golden crust has formed on both sides. The turmeric-flour breading creates a distinctive golden-yellow crust characteristic of Swedish home cooking.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Pull temp | Method notes |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| American (Western) | 49–63 °C (120–145 °F) | 120°F for medium-rare; 145°F USDA safe |
| Egyptian-American (Southern) | **49 °C / 120 °F** (medium-rare) · **54 °C / 130 °F** (medium) | Explicit in recipe ✅ |
| Chinese-American (Eastern) | ~52–54 °C (126–130 °F) | Visual: just opaque inside |
| Swedish (Northern) | ~50 °C (122 °F) | Visual: golden crust, just cooked through |

**Agreed culinary preferred targets:**
- **Medium-rare**: **49 °C / 120 °F** — translucent, jewel-orange centre (Egyptian-American, British modernist)
- **Medium**: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** — warm, just-opaque centre (American, Chinese-American)
- **USDA safe minimum**: **63 °C / 145 °F** — fully cooked, flaky (recommended for vulnerable populations)

**Carryover heating**: 2–3 °C after removing from pan. Pull 2 °C before the target.

**MEATER pull point for salmon steak pan-sear**:
- For medium-rare: pull at **47 °C / 117 °F**
- For medium: pull at **51 °C / 124 °F**
- For USDA safe: pull at **61 °C / 142 °F**

## What makes this method special

Pan searing is the fastest technique for salmon steak and produces the most texture contrast:
the direct conductive heat of a heavy pan creates a Maillard-browned crust on the flat cut
faces within 3–5 minutes, while the interior proteins set gently at culinary preferred
temperatures (49–54 °C). The cross-section shape means more cut surface area than a fillet,
which means more potential Maillard browning per portion. The belly flap portions will be
fully cooked (and slightly overdone) by the time the thickest back section reaches 49–54 °C —
this is unavoidable without folding the flaps.
