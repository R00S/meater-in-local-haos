# Salmon Steak × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

A salmon steak is a cross-cut through the whole fish, perpendicular to the spine, so it
retains the central spine bone, the curved rib bones, and skin around the entire outer
edge. Pan-searing this cut suits home kitchens because the dense, flat back-flesh sits
squarely on the pan and renders the surrounding fat ribbon under the skin into a
caramelised crust, while the bones in the centre conduct heat slightly faster than the
flesh — pulling the geometric centre to temperature a little ahead of an equivalent-thickness
fillet. The thin belly flaps will overcook unless folded inward and secured with kitchen
twine or a cocktail stick before the steak hits the pan; cooks who do not fold the flaps
accept that those thin sections will reach well-done while the back-flesh remains at the
target.

Probe placement: insert from the outer edge of the back-flesh (the thickest section),
aiming horizontally toward the geometric centre of the cross-section. Keep the tip at
least 1 cm clear of the central spine bone, which conducts heat and gives a lagging
reading. Never insert from the belly-flap side — too thin to give a representative
temperature for the back-flesh.

## Source recipes

### 1. ICA Provkök — Laxkotlett med dill- och kaprissås (Swedish / Culinary Group D tradition)
**Source**: ICA Provkök, ica.se/recept/laxkotlett-med-dill-och-kaprissas-5305/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: 6–8 min total

**Ingredients**
- 4 laxkotletter (salmon steaks), approximately 180 g each
- olivolja (olive oil), to brush
- salt, nymalen svartpeppar (freshly ground black pepper)

*Dill- och kaprissås (dill and caper sauce, served alongside)*
- 10 gröna oliver (green olives), sliced
- 2 dl matlagningsyoghurt (cooking yoghurt)
- 1 dl grovhackad dill (coarsely chopped dill)
- 1 msk kapris (capers)
- 1 tsk freshly squeezed lemon juice
- 2 krm salt, 1 krm freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Season the salmon steaks with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a frying pan or grill pan (stek- eller grillpanna). Brush the salmon lightly with olive oil.
3. **Probe:** Insert the probe from the outer edge of the back-flesh, angled toward the geometric centre, at least 1 cm clear of the central spine bone.
4. Pan-fry / grill-pan cook the steaks for **3–4 minutes per side**, depending on thickness.
5. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is by time and thickness only ("3–4 minutes per side, depending on thickness").
6. Stir together the sliced olives, yoghurt, dill, capers, and lemon juice; season with salt and pepper.
7. Serve the salmon with a dollop of the dill-caper sauce, couscous or rice, and crisp salad leaves.

### 2. ICA Provkök — Laxkotlett med limesmör (Swedish / Culinary Group D tradition)
**Source**: ICA Provkök, ica.se/recept/laxkotlett-med-limesmor-330/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: 6–8 min total + chilled compound butter ahead

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg lax i bit (whole salmon piece, cut into steaks), Pacific salmon (e.g. Silver Bright) noted as suitable
- 50 g rumsvarmt smör eller margarin (room-temperature butter or margarine)
- saft av 1 lime (juice of 1 lime)
- 1 krm salt
- 2 msk hackad persilja (chopped parsley)
- olja (oil), to brush
- rosépeppar (pink peppercorns), crushed
- 1 tsk salt
- 4 bakpotatisar (baking potatoes), grönsallad, gurka — to serve

**Method**
1. Cut the salmon into steaks **1.5–2 cm thick across the bone** (skär laxen i skivor 1½–2 cm tjocka tvärs över benet). Trim any fins and loose pieces of skin.
2. Mix the soft butter with the lime juice, 1 krm salt, and chopped parsley. Roll into a log in foil and chill in the fridge.
3. Brush the salmon steaks with oil. Season with crushed pink peppercorns and 1 tsp salt; rub the seasoning into the flesh.
4. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre, clear of the spine bone.
5. Pan-fry the salmon steaks **3–4 minutes per side, with no extra fat in the pan** (utan extra matfett), relying on the brushed-on oil.
6. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is by time only.
7. Serve each steak topped with a slice of the lime butter and a wedge of lime, with baked potato, mixed salad, and cucumber.

### 3. 750g — Darne de saumon grillée (French / Culinary Group B tradition)
**Source**: Ibrahim (Délices de tous les jours) for 750g.com, 750g.com/darne-de-saumon-grillee-r68700.htm  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+ 1 hr marinade) · **Cook**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 darnes de saumon (salmon steaks, fresh or frozen)
- riz (rice), to serve
- huile d'olive (olive oil)
- citron (lemon)
- ail moulu (ground garlic)
- thym (thyme)
- sel, poivre (salt, pepper)

**Method**
1. Marinate the salmon steaks in a bowl with olive oil, lemon, ground garlic, thyme, salt and pepper for **at least 1 hour**.
2. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre, clear of the spine bone.
3. The recipe instructs: "Griller le saumon sur un gril ou une poêle bien chaude" — cook the salmon on a grill **or in a very hot frying pan** until done. Total cooking time given as 10 minutes.
4. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is implicit in the 10-minute total cooking window in a hot pan.
5. Serve with rice and crudités.

### 4. Jamie Oliver — Crispy Fried Salmon with Spring Vegetable Broth (British / Culinary Group B tradition)
**Source**: Jamie Oliver, jamieoliver.com/recipes/fish/crispy-fried-salmon-with-spring-vegetable-broth/  
**Recipe from**: *Jamie's Kitchen*  
**Serves**: 4 · **Cook**: ~8 min for the salmon (broth cooked in parallel)

**Ingredients (salmon component only)**
- 4 x 120 g salmon steaks, scored
- extra virgin olive oil
- sea salt, freshly ground black pepper
- 1 small handful fresh mint
- 1 small handful fresh basil leaves

**Method**
1. Score the salmon steaks. Optionally finely slice a little mint and basil and push this into the score marks.
2. Pat the salmon steaks with a little olive oil and season.
3. **Probe:** Insert the probe horizontally from the outer back-flesh edge, aimed at the geometric centre of the steak, clear of the spine bone.
4. Heat a non-stick frying pan. Place the salmon **skin-side down** in the pan and leave for 2 minutes to get really crispy, then check.
5. Cook approximately **4 minutes on the skin side and 1 minute on the flesh side**.
6. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Jamie's instruction is visual: "You'll get an idea of how they're cooking as you'll see the salmon change colour. Don't be tempted to overcook the salmon — remove it from the heat."
7. Serve on top of a spring vegetable broth (fennel, green beans, broad beans, peas) with a dollop of aïoli.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Recipe | Explicit pull temp in recipe | Cook time per side | Pan heat |
|-----------|--------|------------------------------|--------------------|----------|
| Northern (Swedish, ICA dill/kapris) | Pan / grill pan | Not given | 3–4 min | Hot |
| Northern (Swedish, ICA limesmör) | Dry pan | Not given | 3–4 min | Hot |
| Western (French, 750g) | Hot pan or grill | Not given | ~5 min (10 min total) | Very hot |
| Western (British, Jamie Oliver) | Non-stick pan | Not given | 4 min skin / 1 min flesh | Hot |

The full observed temperature range for the four fetched sources is **not stated in any of
the recipes**: every source relies on time (3–5 min/side) and visual cues (colour change,
"don't overcook") rather than a thermometer reading. This is a real and consistent
finding for pan-seared salmon steak — a cut prepared in home kitchens predominantly with
a stove-clock and a fish slice rather than a probe.

For the underlying culinary preferred temperature, the values listed in the salmon_steak
cut overview (medium-rare 49 °C / medium 54 °C / well-done 63 °C) come from the back-flesh
target shared with the salmon fillet — the back-flesh muscle structure (myotomes separated
by myocommata) is identical between steak and fillet, so the doneness bands transfer
directly. None of the four pan-sear sources fetched here contradicts those values, but
none confirms them either; the recipes are silent on internal temperature. The MEATER user
must rely on the cut overview's targets, not on the source recipes, when probing this
cut pan-seared.

The two ICA sources both specify "3–4 minutes per side" for a 1.5–2 cm steak; Jamie
Oliver's longer skin-side time (4 min) reflects his explicit goal of crispy skin on a
slightly thinner 120 g steak. The 750g French source brackets a 10-minute total in a
"very hot" pan, consistent with the others.

> **Tradition gap**: Eastern (Asian) and Southern (Mediterranean / Middle Eastern / African)
> traditions are not represented here. Searches for "salmon steak / posta de salmón /
> samon teppanyaki / salmon kebab / 鮭ステーキ" returned overwhelmingly fillet-based recipes
> in those traditions — modern Asian and Mediterranean restaurant and home cooking has
> largely moved to the fillet form. This is itself a finding about the cut: pan-seared
> salmon steak as a named preparation is currently strongest in Northern European (Nordic)
> and Western European (French / British) cooking, where the cross-cut salmon steak
> (laxkotlett / darne de saumon / salmon steak) remains a routine fishmonger's cut.

## What makes this method special

Pan-searing is well suited to a salmon steak because the dense, flat geometry of a
cross-cut sits squarely on the pan surface — every square centimetre of the back-flesh
edge gets contact heat at once, rendering the band of fat just under the skin and
caramelising the cut surface where the spine and rib bones intersect the flesh. The
central spine bone, sitting in the middle of the steak, conducts heat into the geometric
centre noticeably faster than in an equivalent-thickness fillet, so the same skin-side
time used for a fillet will leave a steak slightly more done at the centre than the
fillet would be. The MEATER is most useful for catching this earlier-than-expected
internal temperature in the back-flesh; the four sources here all use a 3–5 minute per
side rule of thumb for 1.5–2 cm thickness, and none gives an explicit thermometer
target, so the probe is what bridges the gap between "looks cooked" and "is at the
intended doneness band".

## Summary

Pan-searing salmon steaks is, across all four fetched sources, a fast, time-driven
preparation: 3–4 minutes per side at medium-high to high heat for a 1.5–2 cm steak, with
no explicit thermometer target in any recipe. Northern (Swedish) sources lean on a plain
butter / oil sear with a sauce served alongside (dill-caper, lime butter); Western
(French / British) sources add a marinade or aromatic score and place a heavier
emphasis on crisp skin. Eastern and Culinary Group C traditions for this specific cut are
underrepresented in modern recipe collections — both regions overwhelmingly use the
fillet form. The MEATER probe's value here is to catch the centre temperature reliably
in the back-flesh, since the central spine bone conducts heat faster than an equivalent
fillet thickness; rely on the cut overview's target temperatures (medium 54 °C
recommended), not on the source recipes, which are silent on internal temperature.
