# Shrimp × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Shrimp (called prawns in British, Australian, and much of Asian-English usage) are the most widely consumed shellfish globally; the species most commonly used for pan frying include tiger prawns (*Penaeus monodon*), white leg shrimp (*Litopenaeus vannamei*), spot prawns (*Pandalus platyceros*), and Gulf shrimp (*Farfantepenaeus aztecus*). The edible flesh is the tail muscle, which contracts rapidly under heat and curls the tail from the characteristic C-shape of a live shrimp to a tighter O-shape when overcooked. Pan frying over high heat produces a caramelised exterior on the natural sugars in the shell or flesh, and in shell-on preparations the shell acts as a protective layer. The protein in shrimp begins to denature around 50 °C and is fully set at 60 °C; the culinary window for moist, tender shrimp is extremely narrow, typically 3–4 minutes total cook time for medium prawns in a very hot pan. Carryover is 2–3 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Julia Child — Sautéed Shrimp in Garlic Butter (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, *The Way to Cook* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1989)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 4–5 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g (1½ lb) large shrimp (16–20 count per pound), peeled and deveined, tails on
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 60 g (4 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 4 garlic cloves, finely minced
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) dry white wine or dry vermouth
- 30 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Juice of 1 lemon
- Crusty French bread, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat shrimp completely dry with paper towels. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a large, wide sauté pan (*sauteuse*) over high heat. Add olive oil; heat until shimmering.
3. Add shrimp in a single layer — do not crowd the pan. Work in batches if necessary; crowding creates steam rather than searing. Cook undisturbed for 90 seconds.
4. Note: Due to the small size of shrimp, inserting a MEATER probe into each shrimp is impractical. Monitor doneness by visual colour change — shrimp turn from translucent grey-blue to opaque pink-white, and the tail curls from a C-shape to a partial curl. A straight-line "I" or tight "O" indicates overcooking. For large shrimp (16–20 count), insert the probe into the thickest shrimp in the batch at the start, pushing from the head end into the tail muscle.
5. Flip shrimp. Add butter and garlic. Sauté 60–90 seconds while tossing constantly.
6. Deglaze with white wine; cook 30 seconds.
7. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Julia's target is fully cooked shrimp — opaque throughout, just barely firm, with a snap when bitten. She notes that shrimp "done in this manner should never be chewy — if they are, they are overdone."
8. Add lemon juice and parsley. Toss briefly. Serve immediately with crusty bread. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, opaque, just firm, French standard).

### 2. Nobu Matsuhisa — Spicy Garlic Sautéed Shrimp (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa and Mark Edwards, *Nobu: The Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2001)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 3–4 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 400 g large tiger prawns or spot prawns, shell-on, heads optional
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 10 g (2 tsp) freshly grated fresh ginger
- 2 g (¼ tsp) *togarashi* (*togarashi* — Japanese chilli flakes or shichimi blend)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) sake
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) soy sauce (*shoyu*)
- 10 ml (2 tsp) mirin
- 10 g (2 tsp) caster sugar
- 5 ml (1 tsp) sesame oil
- Thinly sliced spring onions and toasted sesame seeds, to garnish

**Method**
1. If using shell-on prawns, split the shells along the back with scissors and remove the vein; leave shells on. Patting dry is important even with shell-on prawns.
2. Heat wok or large frying pan over very high heat. Add vegetable oil.
3. Add prawns; cook 1 minute without stirring.
4. For large shell-on prawns, insert the MEATER probe into the largest prawn, inserting from the tail end through the shell into the flesh of the tail muscle.
5. Add garlic and ginger; stir-fry 30 seconds.
6. Add sake; let sizzle. Add soy, mirin and sugar; toss to coat.
7. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Nobu's target for stir-fried prawns is fully cooked — the shells should be bright orange-red, the flesh white and firm, the glaze caramelised. The Japanese does not serve pan-fried shrimp undercooked.
8. Toss in sesame oil off the heat. Serve immediately scattered with spring onions and sesame. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, bright, glazed).

### 3. Yotam Ottolenghi — Prawns with Harissa and Preserved Lemon (Middle Eastern / North African tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi and Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+1 h marinade) · **Cook**: 4–5 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g large raw prawns (king prawns), peeled and deveined, tails on
- 30 g (2 tbsp) *harissa* paste (*harissa* — North African chilli and spice paste)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 1 preserved lemon quarter, flesh discarded, rind finely chopped
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin
- 3 g (½ tsp) ground coriander
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 20 g fresh coriander (cilantro), chopped, to serve

**Method**
1. Mix harissa, olive oil, preserved lemon, garlic, cumin and ground coriander into a marinade. Coat prawns; marinate 1 hour refrigerated.
2. Remove from fridge 15 minutes before cooking.
3. Heat a large frying pan over high heat. Add a small amount of oil.
4. Cook prawns in a single layer, without crowding. For the largest prawn in the batch, insert the MEATER probe from the tail end into the tail muscle.
5. Sear 2 minutes per side — the harissa will caramelise and char slightly at the edges.
6. **Pull at 60 °C (140 °F).** Ottolenghi's standard for pan-fried prawns is fully cooked — bright orange-pink, just firm to the touch, the harissa glaze caramelised.
7. Serve immediately, scattered with fresh coriander. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, fragrant with spice).

### 4. Rick Stein — Pan-Fried King Prawns with Ginger and Spring Onion (Cantonese / Chinese tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Far Eastern Odyssey* (BBC Books, 2009)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 4–5 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g large king prawns, shell-on, heads removed
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 4 spring onions (*scallions*), cut into 5 cm lengths
- 30 g fresh ginger, cut into fine julienne
- 3 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) Shaoxing rice wine (*Shaoxing* — Chinese fermented rice wine with a nutty, slightly sweet character)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) soy sauce (*shoyu*)
- 5 ml (1 tsp) sesame oil
- Steamed rice, to serve

**Method**
1. Split prawn shells along the back; remove vein. Pat dry.
2. Heat wok over highest heat. Add oil.
3. Add prawns; stir-fry 1–2 minutes until shells are bright orange.
4. For the largest prawn, insert the MEATER probe from the head end (where shell was removed) into the tail muscle.
5. Add ginger, garlic and spring onions; stir-fry 30 seconds.
6. Add Shaoxing wine; let sizzle. Add soy sauce. Toss.
7. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** The Cantonese standard is fully cooked prawns — bright, snappy, aromatic. Stein notes that Chinese wok cooking at maximum heat means the cook time is short and the pull temperature determines everything.
8. Toss in sesame oil. Serve immediately with steamed rice. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, Cantonese wok standard).

### 5. Donna Hay — Chilli and Lime Sautéed Prawns (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Donna Hay, *Simple Dinners* (HarperCollins Australia, 2011)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 3–4 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g large raw prawns, peeled and deveined, tails on (Australian school or tiger prawns)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 1–2 long red chillies, finely sliced
- Finely grated zest and juice of 2 limes
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) fish sauce (*nam pla*)
- 15 g fresh coriander (cilantro), to serve
- Steamed jasmine rice, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat prawns dry. Combine garlic, chilli, lime zest and fish sauce in a small bowl.
2. Heat a large non-stick or carbon-steel frying pan over high heat. Add olive oil.
3. Add prawns in a single layer. For large prawns, insert the MEATER probe into the largest from the tail end.
4. Cook undisturbed 90 seconds; flip.
5. Add garlic-chilli mixture; toss well. Cook 60–90 seconds.
6. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Hay's standard is fully cooked — bright pink-orange, just firm, fragrant with chilli and lime.
7. Squeeze lime juice over; toss. Scatter fresh coriander. Serve immediately. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, bright and fragrant).
