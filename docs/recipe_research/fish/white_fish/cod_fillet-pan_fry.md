# Cod Fillet × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Atlantic cod (*Gadus morhua*) fillet is cut from the sides of the fish; the flesh is firm, bright white when raw and milky white when cooked, with large moist flakes separated by thin layers of connective tissue. Cod is lean (less than 1% fat), which means it has no internal fat to provide self-basting protection during high-heat cooking; the natural moisture and gelatin in the connective tissue are the primary sources of perceived juiciness. Pan frying is a very common preparation — the high heat of the pan produces a golden-brown crust on the thin, flour- or breadcrumb-coated exterior while the interior cooks gently. Battered cod (fish and chips) uses the batter as insulation; simple pan-fried cod relies on a light coating of seasoned flour or the naked flesh. Carryover is minimal (2–3 °C) given the lean composition; the flesh transitions from moist-and-flaky to dry-and-crumbly rapidly above 65 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Heston Blumenthal — Fish and Chips at Home (British tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 30 min (+30 min rest for batter) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 cod fillets, skin removed, 200–250 g each, approximately 3 cm thick
- **Beer batter**: 120 g plain flour; 120 g cornflour; 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt; 5 g (1 tsp) baking powder; 330 ml cold lager beer
- Plain flour, for dusting
- Vegetable oil for deep frying (or shallow pan frying — see method)
- Malt vinegar, thick-cut chips (*chips* — British fried potatoes) and mushy peas, to serve

**Method**
1. Make batter: whisk plain flour, cornflour, salt and baking powder together. Whisk in cold lager until smooth — the batter should coat the back of a spoon thickly. Rest 30 minutes.
2. Pat cod fillets dry. Season lightly with salt. Dust in plain flour, shaking off excess.
3. For shallow pan-fry adaptation: heat a depth of 3–4 cm of vegetable oil in a heavy pan to 180 °C (350 °F) — use a thermometer to confirm.
4. Dip each floured fillet in batter; let excess drip off.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet before dipping in batter — the probe will pass through the batter and remain in the flesh during the fry.
6. Carefully lower battered fillets into the oil away from you. Fry 3–4 minutes per side until the batter is deep golden-brown and fully puffed.
7. **Pull at 63–65 °C (145–149 °F).** Blumenthal's standard for battered cod: the fish should be just cooked through — moist, large flakes — while the batter is shatteringly crisp.
8. Drain on a wire rack (not paper towels — paper traps steam and softens the batter). Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **65–68 °C / 149–154 °F** (fully cooked, moist large flakes, British fish-and-chip standard).

### 2. Julia Child — Filets de Poisson Sautés au Beurre (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking, Volume 1* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1961)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 cod fillets (or any firm white fish — *cabillaud* in French), 180–200 g each, skin removed
- Fine salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 50 g plain flour (for dredging)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*) or vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted whole butter (for finishing)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice
- 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Lemon wedges, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat cod fillets completely dry. Season with fine salt and white pepper on both sides immediately before cooking.
2. Dredge in flour, shaking off excess — a very thin, even coat is the target. Excess flour burns.
3. Heat a wide sauté pan (*sauteuse à bords évasés*) over medium-high heat. Add clarified butter; heat until it begins to foam.
4. Lay fillets in the pan. Do NOT move them. Cook 2–3 minutes until the first side is golden.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet into the centre of the flesh.
6. Flip gently with a fish spatula. Cook second side 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Julia's standard is fully cooked but moist — the flesh should flake under gentle pressure but not fall apart. She writes: "the test of doneness is whether the flesh separates easily when pierced with a fork."
8. Remove to warm plates. Add whole butter to the hot pan; heat until golden-brown (*beurre noisette*). Add lemon juice; swirl. Pour over fillets. Scatter parsley. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, moist flakes, beurre noisette finished).

### 3. Rick Stein — Pan-Fried Cod with Lemon and Capers (British seafood tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Rick Stein's Seafood* (BBC Books, 2001)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on cod fillets, 200–230 g each (skin scores slightly to prevent curling)
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 30 g small capers, rinsed
- 20 g flat-leaf parsley, roughly chopped
- Juice of ½ lemon
- Mashed or new potatoes, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat cod dry. Score the skin every 2 cm with a sharp knife to prevent the fillet from curling in the pan. Season on both sides.
2. Heat a heavy frying pan (stainless or cast iron) over high heat. Add olive oil until shimmering.
3. Place cod skin-side down. Press briefly with a spatula.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet, at an angle, into the centre of the flesh above the skin.
5. Cook skin-side down 4–5 minutes without moving until the skin is crisp and golden.
6. Flip. Cook flesh-side down 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 62–64 °C (144–147 °F).** Stein's standard: cod should be "just cooked — the large flakes should be visible and the fish should be moist." He notes that cod overcooked past 65 °C becomes dry and stringy.
8. Rest 2 minutes. In the hot pan, add butter, capers and parsley; cook 30 seconds. Squeeze in lemon. Spoon over cod. Final serving temperature: **64–66 °C / 147–151 °F** (fully cooked, large moist flakes).

### 4. Tetsuya Wakuda — Pan-Fried Blue Cod with Yuzu Butter (Japanese-Australian tradition)
**Source**: Tetsuya Wakuda, *Tetsuya* (Penguin Australia, 2000)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 h dry cure) · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 blue cod or sea perch fillets (Tetsuya uses Australian species; Atlantic cod or haddock are closest equivalents), 180–200 g each, skin-on
- 10 g (2 tsp) fine salt (for dry cure)
- 3 g (½ tsp) caster sugar (for dry cure)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Yuzu butter**: 60 g cold unsalted butter; 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh yuzu juice (or half lemon, half lime); fine salt; white pepper
- Watercress or micro herbs, to serve

**Method**
1. Mix salt and sugar; rub over both sides of the fillets. Refrigerate uncovered on a wire rack 1 hour. Rinse and pat dry — the dry cure extracts surface moisture and season the flesh through.
2. Make yuzu butter: warm butter and yuzu in a small pan; keep just barely melted, not broken.
3. Heat a non-stick or carbon-steel pan over medium-high heat. Add oil.
4. Place fillets skin-side down. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
5. Press lightly with a spatula. Cook 5 minutes, skin-side down, until skin is very crisp.
6. Flip. Cook 2 minutes flesh-side down.
7. **Pull at 60 °C (140 °F).** Tetsuya's preference is for white fish that is just cooked through — large moist flakes, no translucency, but never dry.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with yuzu butter spooned over; garnish with watercress. Final serving temperature: **62–63 °C / 144–145 °F** (just cooked, moist, yuzu-fragrant).

### 5. Jacques Pépin — Sole Meunière (French classical tradition, applied to cod)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *New Complete Techniques* (Black Dog & Leventhal Publishers, 2012), based on *La Technique* (1976)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 8 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 cod fillets, skinless, 180–200 g each (or Dover sole if available — Pépin's original uses sole, but he explicitly demonstrates the technique with any white fillet in his TV series)
- Fine salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 60 g plain flour
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) clarified butter or olive oil (for the pan)
- 60 g whole unsalted butter (for *meunière* sauce)
- Juice of 1 lemon
- 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Season fillets on both sides with fine salt and white pepper.
2. Dredge in flour, pressing lightly to adhere; shake off all excess.
3. Heat a large sauté pan over medium-high heat. Add clarified butter; heat until foam subsides and butter begins to brown.
4. Add fillets. Cook without moving 3 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet into the centre of the flesh.
6. Flip gently. Cook second side 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Pépin's standard for *meunière* is fully cooked cod — opaque throughout, moist, with a thin golden crust. He notes: "the fish should just barely separate at the bone when tested — one flake lifts cleanly."
8. Remove to warm plates. Wipe the pan clean. Add whole butter; heat until nut-brown and fragrant. Add lemon juice; it will sizzle. Scatter parsley; pour *beurre meunière* over fillets. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, French classical standard).
