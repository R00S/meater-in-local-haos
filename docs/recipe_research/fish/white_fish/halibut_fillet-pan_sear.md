# Halibut Fillet × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Pacific halibut (*Hippoglossus stenolepis*) and Atlantic halibut (*Hippoglossus hippoglossus*) are the largest flatfish; the fillet — cut from the meaty upper or lower side of the fish — is thick, firm, brilliant white, and lean with large, tight muscle flakes. The flesh has little connective tissue relative to its density, which gives a satisfying firm bite when properly cooked but transitions quickly to a dry, chalky texture above 60 °C. Pan searing is one of the most technically demanding preparations for halibut because the thick, lean flesh must be cooked through to approximately 58–62 °C without surface scorching; a common technique is to sear in a screaming-hot pan to set the crust, then finish in a moderate oven or with basting, treating it more like a thick beef steak than most fish. Carryover is significant — 4–5 °C for a thick fillet — so pulling early is essential.

## Source recipes

### 1. Thomas Keller — Pan-Roasted Halibut with Chanterelles (American / French haute cuisine tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, *Ad Hoc at Home* (Artisan Books, 2009); keller's pan-roasted halibut technique documented at bouchonbakery.com and in the book  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 halibut fillets, 170–200 g each, 3–4 cm thick (skin-on or skinless)
- Fine salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) grapeseed oil
- 60 g (4 tbsp) unsalted butter, divided
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly smashed
- 250 g fresh chanterelle mushrooms, cleaned and halved
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice

**Method**
1. Season halibut fillets generously with fine salt and white pepper on all sides. Let stand at room temperature 15 minutes.
2. Heat a large oven-safe sauté pan over very high heat. Add grapeseed oil; heat until smoking.
3. Place halibut fillets in the pan, skin-side down if skin-on. They should sizzle loudly on contact.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet into the centre of the flesh, avoiding any bone along the lateral line.
5. Sear undisturbed 3–4 minutes until a golden-brown crust forms on the bottom.
6. Flip fillets. Add half the butter, thyme and garlic. Baste continuously — Keller's technique for thick, lean white fish is to baste constantly with the foaming butter for the remainder of the cook.
7. Continue cooking and basting over medium-high heat for 3–4 minutes.
8. In the same pan, quickly sauté chanterelles in remaining butter; season.
9. **Pull at 57–58 °C (135–136 °F).** Keller's published target for halibut: barely opaque at the deepest centre, very moist, with the large firm flakes just beginning to separate.
10. Rest 3 minutes. Serve over chanterelles; finish with lemon juice. Final serving temperature: **60–62 °C / 140–144 °F** (just cooked, firm and moist, Keller standard).

### 2. Gordon Ramsay — Pan-Seared Halibut with Sauce Vierge (British-French restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay Makes It Easy* (Quadrille Publishing, 2005)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 halibut steaks or thick fillets, 200–250 g each, 3–4 cm thick
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- 40 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter
- **Sauce vierge**: 4 plum tomatoes, blanched, peeled, deseeded, finely diced; 2 tbsp capers, rinsed; 10 g basil leaves, torn; 60 ml (¼ cup) extra virgin olive oil; 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice; fine salt and black pepper; pinch of sugar

**Method**
1. Make sauce vierge: warm olive oil gently in a small saucepan with the capers; add tomatoes and basil. Season with lemon, salt, pepper and sugar. Keep warm — do not boil.
2. Season halibut on both sides with salt and pepper.
3. Heat a heavy sauté pan over high heat. Add olive oil; heat until smoking.
4. Place halibut in the pan. Do NOT move it.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
6. Sear 3–4 minutes without moving. The crust should be deep golden.
7. Flip. Add butter; baste continuously for 3–4 minutes.
8. **Pull at 58–60 °C (136–140 °F).** Ramsay's published target: "white and just set — it should offer slight resistance when pressed, not spring back firmly."
9. Rest 3 minutes. Serve with sauce vierge spooned around and over. Final serving temperature: **60–63 °C / 140–145 °F** (just cooked, firm but moist).

### 3. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Seared Halibut with Pepper Sauce (Japanese-Peruvian fusion tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Miami: The Party Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2009)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 halibut fillets, 170–200 g each, skinless
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 2 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground white pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Jalapeño pepper sauce**: 3 jalapeño peppers, seeded; 60 ml (¼ cup) soy sauce; 15 ml (1 tbsp) rice wine vinegar; 15 ml (1 tbsp) mirin; 10 ml (2 tsp) sesame oil; 5 g (1 tsp) caster sugar
- Micro cress, to garnish

**Method**
1. Blend jalapeño pepper sauce ingredients until smooth; adjust seasoning. Reserve.
2. Season halibut fillets with salt and white pepper.
3. Heat a carbon-steel or stainless pan over high heat. Add oil; heat to smoking.
4. Place halibut in pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
5. Sear 3–4 minutes without moving.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes, basting with any pooled fat.
7. **Pull at 58 °C (136 °F).** Nobu's Peruvian-fusion target for halibut: barely set, moist, with the large firm flakes just separating under gentle pressure — the Japanese preference for barely-cooked white fish applied to a Peruvian-inspired preparation.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Spoon pepper sauce around the fillet; garnish with micro cress. Final serving temperature: **60–62 °C / 140–144 °F** (just cooked, firm, fragrant with jalapeño and soy).

### 4. Rick Stein — Pan-Fried Halibut with Brown Butter (Scandinavian / British tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Rick Stein's Long Weekends* (BBC Books, 2016)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 thick halibut steaks (bone-in or fillet), 250–300 g each
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) sunflower or vegetable oil
- 80 g (5 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 2 tbsp small capers, rinsed
- 20 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Juice of 1 lemon
- New potatoes and watercress, to serve

**Method**
1. Season halibut on both sides with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a wide, heavy frying pan over high heat. Add oil; heat until smoking.
3. Add halibut. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
4. Sear undisturbed 4–5 minutes. The crust should be deep golden.
5. Flip carefully. Sear second side 3–4 minutes.
6. **Pull at 60 °C (140 °F).** Stein's target: "the thick flakes should be just set and still moist — halibut dries out faster than almost any other fish."
7. Rest 3 minutes. Wipe the pan; add butter and heat to golden-brown *beurre noisette*. Add capers and parsley; sizzle 30 seconds. Squeeze in lemon; pour over halibut. Final serving temperature: **62–64 °C / 144–147 °F** (just cooked, moist, brown-butter finished).

### 5. Feran Adrià — Pan-Seared Halibut with Olive Oil and Sea Salt (Spanish / Catalan tradition)
**Source**: Ferran Adrià, Albert Adrià and Juli Soler, *El Bulli 2003–2004* (Ecco/HarperCollins, 2006); the minimalist halibut preparation is documented in restaurant notes  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 halibut fillets, 200–220 g each, skin-on
- Fine salt
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) high-quality Arbequina or Picual extra virgin olive oil
- Fleur de sel (*fleur de sel* — hand-harvested French sea salt), to finish
- 2 thin lemon slices, to garnish

**Method**
1. Season halibut lightly with fine salt on both sides. Let stand 5 minutes.
2. Heat a non-stick or stainless pan over medium-high heat. Add olive oil.
3. Place halibut skin-side down. Press briefly with a spatula.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
5. Cook skin-side down, with a moderate heat that allows the skin to crisp without the flesh surface browning — approximately 5 minutes.
6. Flip. Cook flesh-side down 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 57 °C (135 °F).** Adrià's minimalist interpretation targets a barely-set halibut — the large firm flakes remain moist and the olive oil's flavour is the focus. The Spanish Mediterranean tradition does not push fish to the fully-cooked American standard.
8. Rest 3 minutes. Finish with a small drizzle of extra virgin olive oil and fleur de sel. Garnish with lemon. Final serving temperature: **59–61 °C / 138–142 °F** (barely cooked, moist, Spanish standard).
