# Mahi-Mahi × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Mahi-mahi (*Coryphaena hippurus*), also called dolphinfish or dorado, is a large tropical and subtropical pelagic fish; the fillet is firm, moist, and moderately lean with a sweet, mild flavour and a distinctive large-flake texture. The skin is tough and not typically eaten — most preparations use skinless fillets. Mahi-mahi is popular across the Caribbean, Hawaiian, and American Gulf Coast cuisines, and is widely available as a sustainable fish choice. Pan searing produces a beautifully golden crust on the firm flesh; the natural moisture and slight fat content prevent drying out at moderate pull temperatures. The flesh transitions from moist-and-flaky to dry and stringy above 65 °C, so pulling at 60–62 °C is the target. Carryover is 3–4 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Emeril Lagasse — Blackened Mahi-Mahi (American / Louisiana Creole tradition)
**Source**: Emeril Lagasse, *Louisiana Real and Rustic* (William Morrow, 1996)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 mahi-mahi fillets, skinless, 200–250 g each, approximately 3 cm thick
- **Blackening spice**: 10 g (2 tsp) sweet paprika; 5 g (1 tsp) dried thyme; 5 g (1 tsp) dried oregano; 3 g (½ tsp) garlic powder; 3 g (½ tsp) onion powder; 3 g (½ tsp) cayenne pepper; 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt; 3 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter, melted (for coating the fish before searing)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) vegetable oil (for the pan)
- Lemon wedges and remoulade sauce, to serve

**Method**
1. Mix all blackening spice ingredients together.
2. Brush both sides of mahi-mahi fillets with melted butter. Press the blackening spice mixture firmly onto both flat faces.
3. Heat a cast-iron skillet over the highest heat for 5–7 minutes until extremely hot and beginning to smoke — Emeril's blackening technique requires a very high-temperature pan.
4. Add vegetable oil; it will smoke immediately.
5. Place spiced fillets in the pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
6. Cook undisturbed 3–4 minutes. The spice coating will blacken dramatically — this is correct (blackening, not burning).
7. Flip carefully. Cook 3–4 minutes on the second side.
8. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Emeril's standard: fully cooked through, the blackened crust deep and fragrant, the interior moist and large-flaked.
9. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with lemon and remoulade. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, boldly blackened, Louisiana Creole standard).

### 2. Rick Stein — Pan-Fried Mahi-Mahi with Mango Salsa (Caribbean tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Far Eastern Odyssey* (BBC Books, 2009)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 mahi-mahi fillets, skinless, 200 g each
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- **Mango salsa**: 1 ripe mango, finely diced; ½ red onion, finely diced; 1 jalapeño, seeded and finely diced; 30 g fresh coriander (cilantro), chopped; juice of 2 limes; sea salt

**Method**
1. Mix mango salsa ingredients; season and set aside.
2. Season fillets with salt and pepper.
3. Heat a heavy frying pan over high heat. Add olive oil.
4. Place mahi-mahi in the pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the flesh.
5. Sear undisturbed 3–4 minutes.
6. Flip. Cook 3–4 minutes.
7. **Pull at 62 °C (144 °F).** Stein's Caribbean standard: fully cooked, large moist flakes, the mild sweet flavour of the mahi-mahi complemented by the vivid salsa.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with mango salsa spooned over. Final serving temperature: **64–66 °C / 147–151 °F** (fully cooked, Caribbean standard).

### 3. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Seared Mahi-Mahi with Dried Miso (Japanese-Hawaiian tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Miami: The Party Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2009)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 6–7 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 mahi-mahi fillets, skinless, 180–200 g each
- **Miso marinade**: 60 g white *shiro miso*; 30 ml (2 tbsp) mirin; 30 ml (2 tbsp) sake; 15 g (1 tbsp) caster sugar
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- Pickled ginger and micro shiso, to serve

**Method**
1. Combine miso, mirin, sake and sugar over low heat; stir until dissolved. Cool. Coat mahi-mahi; marinate 2 hours refrigerated.
2. Scrape excess marinade; heat a non-stick pan over medium heat (not high — miso sugars burn).
3. Place fillets in the pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre.
4. Cook 3–4 minutes per side, watching the caramelising glaze.
5. **Pull at 60 °C (140 °F).** Nobu's miso-glazed mahi standard: fully cooked, with the golden-brown miso lacquer and a moist large-flaked interior.
6. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with pickled ginger. Final serving temperature: **62–64 °C / 144–147 °F** (fully cooked, miso-glazed, Japanese-Hawaiian standard).

### 4. Yotam Ottolenghi — Pan-Fried Mahi-Mahi with Preserved Lemon (Middle Eastern tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi, *Plenty More* (Ebury Press, 2014)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 mahi-mahi fillets, skinless, 200 g each
- 1 preserved lemon quarter, flesh discarded, rind finely chopped
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground cumin
- 5 g (1 tsp) ground coriander
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) olive oil
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- Fresh coriander (cilantro), to serve

**Method**
1. Mix preserved lemon, garlic, cumin, coriander and 15 ml olive oil into a paste. Coat mahi-mahi; marinate 20 minutes at room temperature.
2. Heat a heavy frying pan over high heat. Add remaining olive oil.
3. Place mahi-mahi in the pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre.
4. Sear 3–4 minutes per side.
5. **Pull at 60–62 °C (140–144 °F).** Ottolenghi's standard: fully cooked, with the preserved lemon and cumin crust caramelised.
6. Rest 2 minutes. Scatter fresh coriander. Final serving temperature: **62–65 °C / 144–149 °F** (fully cooked, Levantine spiced).

### 5. Donna Hay — Seared Mahi-Mahi with Lime Butter (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Donna Hay, *Simple Dinners* (HarperCollins Australia, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 7–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 mahi-mahi fillets, skinless, 200 g each
- Sea salt and freshly cracked black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil
- 40 g unsalted butter, softened
- 5 g (1 tsp) finely grated lime zest
- 10 ml (2 tsp) fresh lime juice
- 10 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Mix softened butter, lime zest, lime juice and parsley into a lime butter. Refrigerate.
2. Season mahi-mahi with salt and pepper.
3. Heat a heavy pan over high heat. Add olive oil.
4. Place fillets in the pan. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre.
5. Cook 3–4 minutes per side.
6. **Pull at 62 °C (144 °F).** Hay's standard: fully cooked, moist large flakes.
7. Rest 2 minutes. Place a disc of lime butter on each fillet to melt. Final serving temperature: **64–66 °C / 147–151 °F** (fully cooked, Australian standard).
