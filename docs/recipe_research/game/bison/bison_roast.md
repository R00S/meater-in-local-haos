# Bison Roast — Cut Overview

## Cut profile

Bison roast cuts are drawn from the same primals as beef roasting joints — most commonly the
shoulder (chuck/Schulter), topside (Oberschale), silverside (Unterschale), or loin (Rücken).
The fillet roast (whole tenderloin) is also used in high-end German preparations. Like bison
steak, bison roast is substantially leaner than equivalent beef cuts: the marbling is sparse
and the outer fat layer thin, which has two important consequences. First, a bison roast dries
out faster in a hot oven than a comparably sized beef joint, making temperature monitoring with
a probe essential. Second, without intramuscular fat to buffer heat and provide moisture, the
off-flavour threshold at which overcooked meat tastes "liver-like" is reached earlier — roughly
65–68 °C for a roast compared to around 63 °C for a steak.

The two dominant German cooking traditions for bison roast diverge sharply in oven temperature
but agree on a pull temperature of approximately 60–63 °C (medium). The high-heat tradition
(200 °C, 45–60 minutes for a 1 kg joint) produces a crisper exterior but requires careful
attention because the meat approaches the pull temperature quickly. The low-heat tradition
(80–90 °C, 90–120 minutes) relies on the oven temperature being close to the desired internal
temperature, preventing overshoot and producing an evenly pink interior across the full
cross-section. Both traditions use an initial sharp sear to develop a Maillard crust before
the oven phase, since at low oven temperatures browning will not occur.

Unlike bison steak, a large roast does carry some collagen from the connective tissue that
surrounds muscle groups — particularly in shoulder or topside cuts. However, bison roast is
not a braise candidate: the collagen content is insufficient to benefit from the 85–95 °C
sustained-hold window required for full collagen conversion, and the lean muscle would be
ruined at those temperatures. The correct window is medium (60–65 °C), where the muscle is
still moist and pink throughout. Resting is critical for roasts: a minimum of 10 minutes
allows the thermal gradient within the joint to equalise and the muscle fibres to reabsorb
juices. A mozzarella-mustard herb crust, applied before the oven phase, is a distinctive
German preparation that acts as an insulating layer and baste, partially compensating for
the low fat content.
