## Cut profile

Rabbit legs roasted in a dry oven occupy a narrower culinary niche than braised
legs: the dry heat browns the surface and renders the small amount of subcutaneous
fat, but without the moisture-retaining effect of braising liquid, the lean muscle
fibres dry out faster as temperature rises. Oven-roasted rabbit legs therefore
require either a fat-based marinade or basting, a covered roasting period followed
by uncovered browning, or a flavourful liquid in the pan to create steam. The
technique is especially prominent in Spanish, Catalan, and Provençal cooking, where
the rabbit is marinated in olive oil and herbs before a hot, relatively short roast.
Carryover in oven-roasted legs is approximately 3–4°C owing to the legs' higher
mass than the saddle; pull temperature is therefore set 3–4°C below the intended
serving temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. Penelope Casas — Conejo al Ajillo al Horno (Spanish Garlic Rabbit)
**Source**: *The Foods and Wines of Spain* (Knopf, 1982); consistent with
traditional Spanish *conejo al ajillo* oven-roast technique  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 1 h 15 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 hind rabbit legs (approximately 700 g)
- 1 whole head of garlic, cloves peeled and left whole
- 100 ml olive oil (*aceite de oliva virgen extra*)
- 100 ml dry sherry (*Jerez seco*) or dry white wine
- 1 tbsp sherry vinegar (*vinagre de Jerez*)
- 1 tsp sweet smoked paprika (*pimentón de la Vera dulce*)
- 1 bay leaf, crumbled
- 1 sprig fresh thyme
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, sherry, vinegar, paprika, garlic, bay, thyme, rosemary, salt,
   and pepper to make a marinade. Coat the rabbit legs completely. Marinate
   refrigerated for at least 2 hours (overnight is better for Casas's full flavour
   development).
2. Preheat oven to 325°F / 165°C. Transfer the legs and all marinade to a heavy
   earthenware or cast-iron roasting dish, legs in a single layer, garlic cloves
   distributed around them.
3. Cover with foil or a lid and roast at 165°C for 50 minutes — the covered phase
   creates a gentle steam-roast environment that keeps the legs moist while the
   garlic softens to a confit texture.
4. Insert the MEATER probe before uncovering: push the tip from the top of the
   thickest hind leg into the centre of the thigh muscle, parallel to the femur,
   stopping when the tip is 1–2 mm from the bone without contacting it.
5. Remove cover. Increase oven to 400°F / 200°C. Roast uncovered for a further
   20–25 minutes, basting every 10 minutes with pan juices, until the legs are
   golden brown and the garlic has caramelised.
6. **Pull at 74°C** (165°F). Casas's *conejo al ajillo* targets "deeply coloured,
   tender, and fragrant with garlic" — fully cooked through, USDA-safe, and just
   short of the drier texture above 77°C.
7. Rest 10 minutes in the covered dish. Final serving temperature: **77–80°C /
   171–176°F** (well done). Serve directly from the roasting dish with crusty bread
   to mop up the garlic-oil-sherry pan juices.

### 2. Hank Shaw — Rabbit Legs Roasted with Herbs (Wild Rabbit al Forno)
**Source**: honest-food.net; consistent with Mediterranean-influenced wild-game
technique in *Hunt, Gather, Cook: Finding the Forgotten Feast* (Rodale, 2011)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight marinade) · **Cook**: 1 h · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 rabbit legs (wild or farmed, approximately 600–800 g)
- 60 ml olive oil
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tbsp fresh rosemary, finely chopped
- 1 tbsp fresh thyme leaves
- 1 tsp salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- Zest of 1 lemon
- 80 ml white wine or chicken stock (for the roasting pan)

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, garlic, rosemary, thyme, salt, pepper, and lemon zest. Coat
   the rabbit legs thoroughly. Refrigerate overnight; wild rabbit benefits
   significantly from overnight marinating to allow herb penetration.
2. Remove from refrigerator 30 minutes before roasting. Preheat oven to 375°F /
   190°C.
3. Place legs in a single layer in a heavy roasting pan, skin side up if present.
   Pour wine or stock into the pan to a depth of 5 mm — this prevents the
   marinade from scorching and provides steam.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the top of the thickest leg into the thigh muscle
   before placing in the oven, directing the tip alongside the femur, probe cable
   running over the pan edge.
5. Roast uncovered at 190°C for 55–60 minutes, basting at 20 and 40 minutes with
   pan juices. Wild rabbit legs are drier than farmed; basting is critical.
6. **Pull at 73–75°C** (163–167°F). Shaw's target for oven-roasted wild rabbit is
   "cooked through and juicy, never stringy" — pulling before 77°C preserves the
   small amount of moisture in the lean wild muscle.
7. Rest 10 minutes covered loosely. Final serving temperature: **76–78°C /
   169–172°F** (well done). Squeeze lemon over before serving.

### 3. Good Food Middle East (BBC Good Food) — Oven-Roasted Rabbit Legs with Thyme and Lemon
**Source**: bbcgoodfoodme.com; representative of the British home-cooking
approach to rabbit, adapted for oven-roasting legs  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 55–60 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 rabbit hind legs
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
- 1 lemon, sliced into rounds
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- ½ tsp flaky sea salt
- 100 ml chicken stock (for roasting pan)

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 180°C / 355°F. Pat rabbit legs dry.
2. Rub the legs with olive oil, salt, and pepper. Place in a single layer in a
   roasting tin. Tuck thyme sprigs and garlic slices alongside. Lay lemon rounds
   over and around the legs.
3. Pour chicken stock into the tin. The lemon slices act as an aromatic steam source
   and prevent direct contact between the legs and the hot tin base.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the top of the thickest leg, tip in the deepest
   thigh muscle alongside the femur.
5. Cover the tin tightly with foil. Roast covered at 180°C for 40 minutes.
6. Remove foil. Increase oven to 200°C / 390°F. Roast uncovered for 15–20 minutes
   until the surface is golden and the stock has reduced to a thin glaze.
7. **Pull at 75°C** (167°F). BBC Good Food's home-cooking standard for rabbit legs
   is "fully cooked through, golden, not pink at the bone" — 75°C satisfies all
   three criteria.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **78–80°C / 172–176°F** (well done).

### 4. Adina Beck — Roasted Rabbit (Central European / German home cooking)
**Source**: Adina Beck, *Where Is My Spoon* (whereismyspoon.co); Central European
home-cooking approach for marinated rabbit legs roasted at high heat  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 35–40 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 rabbit legs (approximately 1.5 kg total)
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 150 ml dry white wine
- 4 garlic cloves, grated (plus extra whole cloves for the roasting tin)
- 2 tbsp fresh rosemary, finely chopped
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- A splash of water for the roasting tin

**Method**
1. Mix olive oil, white wine, grated garlic, rosemary, salt, and pepper. Coat
   the rabbit legs thoroughly, cover, and marinate for at least 2 hours (or
   overnight) in the refrigerator. Remove from the fridge 30 minutes before
   roasting to bring to room temperature.
2. Preheat oven to 200°C / 400°F. Arrange the legs in a single layer in a
   roasting tin. Add the extra garlic cloves and a splash of water to the tin.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest hind leg from the top, tip seated
   in the thigh muscle alongside the femur, clear of the bone.
4. Roast uncovered for 35–40 minutes, flipping the legs every 10 minutes and
   basting with the pan juices. The legs are done when deeply golden on all
   sides and the probe reads the target temperature.
5. **Pull at 74–75°C** (165–167°F) — the USDA-safe threshold for rabbit (treated
   as poultry). The Central European home-cooking tradition aims for meat that is
   fully opaque, pulls easily from the bone, and shows no pink at the joint.
6. Rest 10 minutes loosely covered with foil. Final serving temperature:
   **77–79°C / 171–174°F** (well done). Serve straight from the roasting tin
   with the golden garlic cloves and pan juices as a sauce.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| Spanish classic (Casas) | well done | 77–80°C / 171–176°F |
| USA / Wild game (Shaw) | well done | 76–78°C / 169–172°F |
| British home cooking (BBC Good Food) | well done | 78–80°C / 172–176°F |
| Central European / German home cooking (Beck) | well done | 77–79°C / 171–174°F |

**Full observed range: 76–80°C at the oven-roasted leg.** All four traditions
converge tightly on the "well done" band: 76–80°C. This is notably warmer than
the French braised leg (75–78°C) because the dry oven environment forces more
moisture loss from the lean muscle as temperature rises — the target is set high
enough to tenderise the connective tissue but pulled before the muscle fibres
become dry and stringy. No tradition oven-roasts rabbit legs below 74°C (the
USDA-safe threshold for poultry, the nearest food-safety analogue), and no
tradition pushes beyond 81°C in the oven-roast context (unlike braising, where
collagen conversion justifies higher temperatures). The consensus `recommended_
doneness` of `"dark_meat_optimal"` (77°C) sits at the midpoint of the observed
range and represents the broadest common result.

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting rabbit legs in a two-phase approach — covered for moisture retention
followed by uncovered for surface browning — is the only dry-heat method that can
simultaneously tenderise the connective tissue in the thigh while creating the
caramelised, golden surface that distinguishes a roasted leg from a braised or
poached one. The covered phase acts as a partial braise (the steam from the pan
liquid moistens the meat), while the uncovered phase concentrates pan juices and
develops Maillard browning on the exterior. This two-phase technique is used by
every tradition in this leaf: Casas, Shaw, BBC Good Food, and Paulina Cocina all
cover first and uncover to finish, which is why oven-roasted legs achieve a result
closer to braise than pan-sear in texture, while retaining the roasted flavour of
direct dry heat.

## Summary

Oven-roasting rabbit legs in a covered-then-uncovered two-phase cook delivers
the golden, caramelised exterior and just-tender connective tissue that no other
dry-heat method can achieve: the covered phase keeps the lean meat moist while
the uncovered phase browns the surface in the final 15–20 minutes. All four
traditions examined — Spanish, American, British, and Mediterranean home-cooking —
converge on 76–81°C (well done) as the correct finishing range, making this a
reliable, forgiving method as long as the two-phase approach is respected and the
legs are not left uncovered for the full roasting time.
