# Rabbit Saddle × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The rabbit saddle is the backbone section of the rabbit bearing two boneless loins
and, when left bone-in, thin belly flaps that protect the loins during roasting.
Oven roasting suits the saddle particularly when it is stuffed, tied, or cooked
bone-in as a whole piece: the oven's dry, even heat browns the exterior more gently
and uniformly than a stovetop pan, and the belly flaps — which would burn in a pan
— insulate the loins and add a thin layer of fat that bastes the meat from the
inside. The saddle is lean enough that it needs a hot oven (190–200°C) to brown
before the interior overcooks; conversely, finishing times are short (10–15 minutes)
and carryover is 2–3°C, so the cut demands close monitoring. Stuffed or rolled
saddle (farce or ballotine style) can tolerate a slightly lower oven and longer
time because the stuffing moderates heat transfer into the loin.

## Source recipes

### 1. Emeril Lagasse — Roasted Saddle of Rabbit
**Source**: emerils.com (Emeril's New Orleans Kitchen); consistent with technique
in *Emeril's Creole Christmas* (Morrow, 1997)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 15–18 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 whole bone-in rabbit saddles (approximately 450 g total)
- 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tbsp fresh thyme leaves
- 1 tbsp fresh rosemary, chopped
- 1 tsp Creole seasoning (*assaisonnement créole*) or smoked paprika + salt
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 120 ml chicken stock (for deglazing the roasting pan)

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 425°F / 220°C. Remove saddles from refrigerator 20 minutes
   before roasting.
2. Combine olive oil, garlic, thyme, rosemary, and Creole seasoning. Rub this
   mixture all over the saddles, getting under any belly flaps.
3. Season with additional salt and pepper. Place the saddles bone-side down on a
   rack set in a small roasting pan.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the wider (rear) end of one saddle, guiding the tip
   into the thickest part of the loin, parallel to the backbone, stopping just short
   of the bone. Keep the probe clear of the belly flap.
5. Roast at 220°C/425°F for 15–18 minutes. The high oven temperature creates a
   golden exterior on the loins without prolonged exposure that would dry them.
6. **Pull at 63°C** (145°F). Lagasse's target for rabbit saddle is "145°F internal
   for juicy, just-done meat" — well within USDA-safe parameters when combined
   with a 5-minute rest.
7. Rest 5 minutes tented loosely with foil. Deglaze pan with chicken stock, reduce
   to a light jus. Final serving temperature: **65–67°C / 149–153°F** (medium).

### 2. Pardon Your French (Audrey Le Goff) — Stuffed Rabbit Saddle with Chestnuts and Mushrooms
**Source**: pardonyourfrench.com; consistent with traditional French *selle de
lapin farcie* preparation from Normandy  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole bone-in rabbit saddle (approximately 400 g)
- **Stuffing**: 80 g cooked chestnuts (*châtaignes*), roughly chopped; 60 g button
  mushrooms, finely diced and sautéed; 1 shallot, sautéed; 1 tbsp flat-leaf
  parsley, chopped; salt and pepper
- 2 slices thin-cut streaky bacon (*lardons plats*), to wrap
- 2 tbsp clarified butter
- 100 ml dry white wine (*Muscadet* or *Bourgogne Aligoté*)
- 150 ml chicken or rabbit stock

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200°C / 390°F. Prepare stuffing by combining chestnuts,
   sautéed mushrooms and shallot, parsley, and seasoning.
2. Open the saddle flat, belly flaps extended. Spread stuffing along the centre
   of the loins. Fold belly flaps over to encase the stuffing. Wrap with bacon
   and tie with kitchen twine at 2 cm intervals.
3. Melt clarified butter in an oven-safe sauté pan over high heat. Brown the
   rolled saddle on all sides, about 3–4 minutes total.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the rear end of the roll, pushing the tip to the
   geometric centre of the farce-and-loin bundle. Avoid piercing through to the pan.
5. Transfer pan to oven. Roast at 200°C for 15–20 minutes (the stuffing and bacon
   wrap extend cooking time by 5 minutes vs. plain saddle).
6. Remove saddle. Deglaze pan with white wine, add stock, reduce by half to a
   light jus.
7. **Pull at 65–68°C** (149–154°F). The bacon wrap and stuffing insulate the loin,
   reducing the risk of the outer surface drying before the interior reaches
   temperature; pull before 70°C to preserve moisture.
8. Rest 5 minutes, twine removed. Final serving temperature: **67–70°C /
   153–158°F** (medium-well). Slice into medallions to reveal the stuffing cross-
   section.

### 3. Dairy Farmers of Canada — Saddle of Rabbit with Celery Sauce and Ricotta
**Source**: dairyfarmersofcanada.ca; representative of the Canadian-French
culinary tradition integrating dairy into classical French preparations  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 bone-in rabbit saddles
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter, softened
- 1 tbsp fresh tarragon (*estragon*), finely chopped
- 1 tbsp fresh chervil (*cerfeuil*), finely chopped
- Salt and white pepper
- **Celery sauce**: 2 stalks celery, thinly sliced; 1 shallot; 120 ml heavy cream
  (35%); 60 ml chicken stock; 30 g ricotta; salt and white pepper

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 375°F / 190°C. Combine softened butter with tarragon and
   chervil to make a compound herb butter.
2. Loosen the belly flaps of each saddle. Spread herb butter under the flaps and
   along the loins. Fold flaps back and truss lightly.
3. Melt a thin film of butter in an oven-safe pan over medium-high heat. Sear
   saddles on all sides, 2 minutes total.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the rear end of one saddle into the deepest part
   of the loin, tip parallel to the backbone, clear of bone.
5. Roast at 190°C/375°F for 12–15 minutes. Meanwhile, make celery sauce: sweat
   celery and shallot, add stock and cream, simmer until reduced by a third, stir
   in ricotta, season.
6. **Pull at 63–65°C** (145–149°F). The herb butter compound keeps the loin moist
   at this slightly lower temperature, preserving the fresh tarragon character.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **65–67°C / 149–153°F** (medium).
   Pool the celery-ricotta sauce on each plate and rest the sliced saddle on top.

### 4. Good Food Middle East (BBC Good Food) — Roast Rabbit with Thyme
**Source**: bbcgoodfoodme.com; consistent with BBC Good Food's classic British
roasting style adapted for Middle Eastern home cooks  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight marinade) · **Cook**: 15 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 whole rabbit saddles (bone-in)
- 3 tbsp olive oil
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
- Zest of 1 lemon
- 1 tsp black pepper, coarsely ground
- ½ tsp sea salt
- 50 ml white wine or water (for roasting tin)

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, thyme leaves, garlic, lemon zest, pepper, and salt. Rub all
   over the saddles. Refrigerate overnight for full flavour penetration.
2. Remove from refrigerator 30 minutes before roasting. Preheat oven to 400°F / 204°C.
3. Place saddles in a roasting tin, belly-flap side up. Pour wine or water into the
   bottom of the tin to prevent fat from scorching.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the rear end of the larger saddle, tip in the deepest
   loin muscle, parallel to the backbone, probe resting over the edge of the tin so
   the cable is clear of the oven door seal.
5. Roast at 204°C for 12–15 minutes until the loin surface is golden.
6. **Pull at 65°C** (149°F). The BBC Good Food standard for rabbit is "no pink, moist"
   — slightly more conservative than the French standard but consistent with home
   cooking guidance for Britain and Australia.
7. Rest 5 minutes loosely tented. Final serving temperature: **67–70°C / 153–158°F**
   (medium-well). Baste once with pan juices before resting.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / New Orleans (Lagasse) | medium | 65–67°C / 149–153°F |
| French / Normandy (Le Goff) | medium-well (stuffed) | 67–70°C / 153–158°F |
| Canadian-French (Dairy Farmers of Canada) | medium (*rosé*) | 65–67°C / 149–153°F |
| British / Middle East (BBC Good Food) | medium-well | 67–70°C / 153–158°F |

**Full observed range: 65–70°C at the saddle.** Oven roasting narrows the
temperature spread compared with pan searing: the gentle, even heat of the oven
does not permit the very-low-pull approach of wild-game pan searing (57–60°C),
because the oven surface cannot create an instantaneous crust without some time
in the heat. All four traditions here cluster between 63–70°C — a *rosé* to
medium-well window — which corresponds to `"medium"` (63°C) and `"well_done"`
(71°C) in the data model. No source targets below 63°C for oven-roasted saddle.
The stuffed/rolled preparations naturally run slightly warmer (68–70°C) because
the stuffing delays heat penetration, requiring longer oven time. The broadest
consensus for `recommended_doneness` is `"well_done"` (71°C) for plain saddle;
stuffed preparations settle at 67–70°C (medium-well, approaching well done).

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting is the only practical way to cook a stuffed or bone-in rabbit saddle:
the oven's enveloping heat reaches the curved underside of the backbone while the
loins roast from above, something a flat pan cannot achieve without turning the
saddle repeatedly and unevenly. The belly flaps — which would burn in a pan before
the thicker loin centres were done — become a natural basting membrane in the oven,
self-basting the loins with their thin layer of subcutaneous fat. Short roasting
times (10–15 minutes) and a hot oven (190–220°C) are essential to brown the surface
before the lean interior dries out; long, slow roasting is contraindicated for this
cut. For stuffed preparations, oven roasting is the default method in every
European tradition precisely because the stuffing's moisture moderates the interior
temperature while the oven browns the exterior uniformly.

## Summary

Oven roasting gives the rabbit saddle a uniformly golden exterior without the
moment-to-moment attention that pan searing demands, making it the preferred
choice for stuffed or bone-in preparations where a flat pan cannot reach all
surfaces. The heat is gentle enough for the belly flaps to self-baste the loins
throughout the cook, but the oven must still be hot (190–220°C) to brown the
exterior before the lean meat dries — long, slow roasting has no place here.
