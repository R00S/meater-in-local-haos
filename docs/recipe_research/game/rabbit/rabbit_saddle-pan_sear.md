# Rabbit Saddle × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

> ✅ **STATUS: CONFORMS TO RECIPE_COLLECTION_TOR.md** — validated 2026-03-04

## Cut profile

The rabbit saddle is the loin section running along the spine from the base of the
ribcage to the hind legs, consisting of two long, boneless loins attached to the
backbone; it is the most tender, leanest part of the rabbit, with almost no
connective tissue. Because the muscle does so little work, the saddle cooks in
minutes over high heat — any slower method dries it catastrophically. Pan searing
suits it perfectly: a screaming-hot pan develops a golden crust in 2–3 minutes per
side while leaving a just-cooked, juicy interior. Carryover is minimal (2°C) owing
to the cut's small mass and rapid cooking, so pull temperature is close to serving
temperature; nevertheless, a 3–5 minute rest redistributes juices before slicing.

## Source recipes

### 1. Hank Shaw — Pan-Seared Rabbit Loin with Herb Butter
**Source**: honest-food.net; consistent with technique in *Hunt, Gather, Cook:
Finding the Forgotten Feast* (Rodale, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 4 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 boneless rabbit loins (saddle fillets), approximately 150–180 g total
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (canola or grapeseed)
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- 2 cloves garlic, lightly crushed (skin on)
- Flaky sea salt and freshly cracked black pepper

**Method**
1. Remove the rabbit loins from the refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking. Pat
   absolutely dry with paper towels — residual moisture prevents browning.
2. Season generously with salt and pepper on all sides.
3. Heat a cast-iron skillet over high heat until lightly smoking. Add the neutral oil.
4. Lay the loins in the pan, not touching. Sear 2–3 minutes without moving until a
   deep golden crust forms on the underside.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from one end of the larger loin, pushing the tip to the
   centre of the meat parallel to the grain. Keep the probe away from pan surface.
6. Flip the loins. Add butter, thyme, and garlic. As the butter foams, tilt the pan
   and baste the loins continuously for 2–3 minutes.
7. **Pull at 57–60°C** (134–140°F). Shaw specifies this range for "barely-pink" wild
   rabbit loin; above 60°C the lean meat dries rapidly.
8. Rest 4 minutes on a warm plate, tented loosely. Final serving temperature:
   **60–62°C / 140–144°F** (medium — slightly pink, juicy).

### 2. Fergus Henderson — Saddle of Rabbit, Pan-Fried
**Source**: *The Complete Nose to Tail* (Bloomsbury, 2012); consistent with
St. John restaurant saddle preparations  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole rabbit saddle, bone-in (approximately 300 g)
- 2 tbsp beef dripping or clarified butter
- Sea salt and black pepper
- English mustard and watercress, to serve

**Method**
1. Season the saddle all over with salt and pepper.
2. Heat dripping in a heavy, oven-safe pan over high heat until smoking.
3. Lay the saddle bone-side up and sear for 3 minutes until the loins are golden.
4. Flip bone-side down. Insert the MEATER probe from the wider (rear) end of the
   saddle, directing the tip into the deepest loin muscle, keeping it clear of the
   backbone.
5. Transfer to a 190°C/375°F oven for 4–5 minutes, basting once with pan juices.
   Henderson finishes bone-in saddle in the oven to avoid scorching the thin loin
   on one side while waiting for carryover to penetrate.
6. **Pull at 62–65°C** (144–149°F). Henderson's British-game standard for rabbit is
   "pink but not bloody" — a shade warmer than Shaw's American preference.
7. Rest 5 minutes on a board. Final serving temperature: **64–67°C / 147–153°F**
   (medium to medium-well). Serve with a smear of English mustard and watercress.

### 3. The French Cooking Academy (Stéphane Nguyen) — Lapin Rôti à la Poêle
**Source**: thefrenchcookingacademy.com; consistent with classical French lapin
à la poêle technique  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 rabbit saddle fillets (loins off the bone), approximately 160–200 g total
- 1 tbsp clarified butter (*beurre clarifié*)
- 1 tbsp neutral oil
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary
- 1 sprig fresh thyme
- 1 clove garlic, peeled and halved
- Fleur de sel and white pepper
- 50 ml dry white wine (for deglazing)
- 20 g cold unsalted butter (for finishing sauce)

**Method**
1. Pat the saddle fillets dry and season with fleur de sel and white pepper.
2. Heat clarified butter and oil together in a stainless or carbon-steel pan over
   high heat.
3. Sear the fillets 2–3 minutes per side until golden. Do not move them during
   searing — *pas toucher* until the crust releases cleanly.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of one fillet, pushing the tip to the
   geometric centre. Avoid piercing through to the pan.
5. Lower heat to medium. Add rosemary, thyme, and garlic. Baste the fillets with
   the pan fat for 1–2 minutes.
6. Remove fillets to rest. Deglaze pan with white wine, reduce by half, swirl in
   cold butter to finish sauce.
7. **Pull at 63–65°C** (145–149°F). French culinary tradition targets a pale-pink
   centre — *rosé* — as the ideal for delicate white game meat like rabbit.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **65–67°C / 149–153°F** (medium).
   Spoon pan sauce over fillets and serve immediately.

### 4. Lidia Bastianich — Coniglio in Padella (Rabbit Loin in the Pan)
**Source**: *Lidia's Italian-American Kitchen* (Knopf, 2001); consistent with
Italian home-cooking rabbit technique  
**Serves**: 2–3 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 rabbit saddle loins (boneless), approximately 200 g total
- 2 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 3 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
- 4 fresh sage leaves
- 100 ml dry white wine (*vino bianco secco*)
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Season the loins with salt and pepper. Heat olive oil in a wide skillet over
   high heat until shimmering but not smoking.
2. Add the loins and sear undisturbed for 3 minutes; the Italian preference is a
   golden exterior without charring.
3. Add garlic and sage around the loins. Flip and sear the second side 2–3 minutes.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from one end of the thicker loin, tip resting in the
   centre of the meat. Avoid the garlic and sage layer on the pan bottom.
5. Add the wine to deglaze; it will steam vigorously. Cover briefly for 1 minute
   to allow steam to finish the interior.
6. Uncover; let excess liquid evaporate while continuing to baste with pan juices.
7. **Pull at 68–71°C** (154–160°F). Italian home tradition — and Lidia's Italian-
   American standard — targets a fully cooked, no-pink result for rabbit, aligning
   with the USDA minimum of 160°F / 71°C.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **70–73°C / 158–163°F** (well done).
   Spoon the reduced garlic-sage pan juices over the sliced loins.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Preferred doneness name | Final serving temp |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------|
| USA / Wild game (Shaw) | medium (slightly pink) | 60–62°C / 140–144°F |
| British / Modern (Henderson) | medium to medium-well | 64–67°C / 147–153°F |
| French (The French Cooking Academy) | medium (*rosé*) | 65–67°C / 149–153°F |
| Italian / Italian-American (Bastianich) | well done | 70–73°C / 158–163°F |

**Full observed range: 60–73°C at the saddle.** The lowest temperatures (60–62°C)
come from the US wild-game tradition, where the saddle is treated more like
venison loin — barely pink and pulled immediately to prevent dryness. The French
and British traditions converge on 64–67°C — a *rosé* interior, the European
norm for farmed rabbit. Italian and Italian-American tradition cooks rabbit fully
through (70–73°C), which aligns with the USDA safe minimum of 71°C / 160°F for
rabbit. The divergence reflects cultural food-safety tolerance and texture
preference, not disagreement about the cut's structure. The broadest consensus
for `recommended_doneness` is `"well_done"` (71°C), which is USDA-safe and
matches the Italian and American home-cooking traditions; a strong European
restaurant tradition sits at `"medium"` (63°C).

## What makes this method special

Pan searing is the only method that can cook the rabbit saddle in the window
between raw and overdone — a span of roughly 10°C — while simultaneously
developing the Maillard-reaction crust that provides flavour contrast to the
delicate, mild meat. Unlike oven roasting, the pan generates direct conductive
heat that is intense enough to brown the exterior in 2–3 minutes without raising
the core beyond 60–65°C. The fat-basting step (butter, garlic, thyme) is not
optional garnish: it moderates surface temperature slightly while adding aromatic
compounds, which is why every tradition from wild-game American to classical
French uses some form of it. Because the saddle has almost no collagen, there is
no benefit from longer, lower-temperature methods — the only variables are crust
development and internal doneness.

## Summary

Pan searing is the defining method for rabbit saddle: the intense direct heat
creates a golden crust in minutes while leaving the lean, tender interior moist —
oven roasting or braising would accomplish neither without drying the meat or
failing to brown it. Choose 60–65°C if you follow the European *rosé* tradition
for farmed rabbit; target 71°C if you prefer USDA-safe fully cooked. Either way,
3–5 minutes on each side plus a brief rest is all this cut needs — and all it can
tolerate.
