## Cut profile

Venison steak most commonly refers to cuts from the haunch (hindleg), loin, or backstrap of deer — typically roe deer (*Capreolus capreolus*), red deer (*Cervus elaphus*), or fallow deer (*Dama dama*). The backstrap and loin medallions are prized for pan searing: they are fine-grained, lean, and quick-cooking. The meat is deep-red and intensely flavoured, with very little intramuscular fat.

Because venison is so lean, it must be cooked hot and fast to prevent drying. Pan searing in butter or oil over high heat for 2–5 minutes per side achieves a caramelised crust while keeping the centre rare to medium-rare. Resting is critical — at least 5 minutes — to allow juice redistribution.

The European tradition across the UK, Germany, Scandinavia and Italy pairs venison with acidic or fruity sauces (blackberry, lingonberry, cloudberry, redcurrant, balsamic) that cut the richness of the meat. Nordic cooks often finish fillets in the oven at low heat (125°C) after searing, using a thermometer to hit exactly 60°C. UK and North American cooks tend to pan-fry direct.

Target internal temperature: pull at **54–57°C (130–135°F)** for medium-rare. Venison dries out rapidly above 65°C and should never be cooked to well-done.

## Source recipes

### 1. BBC Good Food — Pan-Fried Venison with Blackberry Sauce (UK tradition)
**Source**: Good Food Magazine, November 2007; https://www.bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/pan-fried-venison-blackberry-sauce (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 2 thick venison steaks, or 4 medallions (approx. 200 g each)
- 1 tbsp balsamic vinegar
- 150 ml beef stock (made with 2 tsp Knorr beef concentrate)
- 2 tbsp redcurrant jelly
- 1 garlic clove, crushed
- 85 g fresh or frozen blackberries
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**
1. Remove venison from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking to bring to room temperature. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Heat the olive oil in a heavy frying pan over high heat until the oil just starts to smoke.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of one steak, ensuring the tip is centred in the meat.
4. Lay the steaks into the hot pan and cook for 5 minutes on the first side without moving.
5. Turn and cook for a further 3–5 minutes depending on thickness and preferred doneness.
6. **Pull at 54°C (130°F)** for medium-rare, 58°C (136°F) for medium. Lift meat from pan and rest on a warm plate for 5 minutes.
7. Add the balsamic vinegar to the pan, then pour in the beef stock, redcurrant jelly and crushed garlic. Stir vigorously over high heat to combine, scraping up any fond.
8. Add the blackberries and cook until they soften into the sauce, about 3–4 minutes.
9. Serve steaks with the blackberry sauce, celeriac mash and broccoli.
*Final resting temperature: 57–60°C (135–140°F).*

### 2. Matprat — Hjortefilet med snittebønnestuing (Norwegian tradition)
**Source**: Matprat.no; https://www.matprat.no/oppskrifter/gjester/hjortefilet-med-snittebonnestuing/ (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 35 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g ytrefilet av hjort (outer fillet/loin of deer)
- 1 tsp salt
- ½ tsp ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp butter

*Snittebønnestuing (French bean stew):*
- 400 g French beans (snittebønner)
- 100 ml single cream
- 1 tbsp plain flour
- 200 ml water
- 1 tsp salt · ½ tsp pepper

*Side:*
- 16 small potatoes
- 1 tbsp butter
- 1 tbsp chopped fresh herbs (parsley/thyme)
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Remove the venison fillet from the fridge 30 minutes before cooking (tempering). Carefully trim any sinew.
2. Season all over with salt and pepper.
3. Heat butter in an oven-safe frying pan over high heat until foaming subsides. Sear the fillet on all sides until well browned, about 2–3 minutes per side.
4. Transfer the fillet to an ovenproof dish. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into the centre of the thickest part.
5. Roast in the oven at 125°C (255°F) until the probe reads the target temperature.
6. **Pull at 60°C (140°F)** for medium; pull at 55°C (131°F) for medium-rare. Rest for at least 5 minutes before slicing.
7. Meanwhile, wash and slice the French beans into thin strips. Boil in lightly salted water until just tender, about 4–5 minutes. Drain and return to the pan.
8. Whisk cream and flour together until smooth; pour into the bean pan. Simmer for 5 minutes, stirring, until the sauce thickens. Season with salt and pepper.
9. Cook potatoes in salted water until tender. Drain and toss with butter and chopped herbs.
10. Slice the venison fillet and serve with the bean stew and potatoes. Pass redcurrant or rowan-berry jelly on the side.
*Final resting temperature: 63–65°C (145–149°F).*

### 3. Tasteline — Hjortstek (Swedish tradition)
**Source**: Tasteline.com; https://www.tasteline.com/recept/hjortstek/ (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 60 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g hjortstek (whole venison joint, loin or haunch)
- 2 tbsp margarine or butter
- 2 carrots, peeled and roughly chopped
- 1 yellow onion, quartered
- Water (enough to cover base of pot)
- 1 tsp salt
- 6–8 juniper berries, lightly crushed
- ½ tsp black peppercorns
- 2 garlic cloves (optional)

*Sauce:*
- Pan drippings
- 1–2 tbsp plain flour
- 100 ml cream or white wine (optional)

**Method**
1. Heat margarine in a heavy-based pot or casserole dish over high heat until very hot.
2. Brown the venison joint on all sides until deep golden, about 8–10 minutes total.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the thickest part of the joint.
4. Lift the joint temporarily and add a splash of water to the pan, scraping up the browned bits. Return the joint to the pot.
5. Add the carrots, onion, juniper berries, peppercorns, garlic and salt. Add enough water to come 2–3 cm up the sides.
6. Reduce heat to low, cover and braise on the stovetop for a minimum of 45 minutes per kg, until the internal temperature reaches the target.
7. **Pull at 60°C (140°F)** for medium-rare to medium. Rest the joint on a board for 10 minutes.
8. Strain the pan juices into a small saucepan. Whisk in flour and bring to a simmer; stir in cream or white wine if desired. Adjust seasoning.
9. Slice the venison and serve with boiled potatoes and the pan sauce.
*Final resting temperature: 63–65°C (145–149°F).*

### 4. Chefkoch — Hirschmedaillons in Preiselbeer-Rahmsauce (German tradition)
**Source**: Chefkoch.de; https://www.chefkoch.de/rezepte/1696211277979587/Hirschmedaillons-in-pfiffiger-Preiselbeer-Rahmsauce.html (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 30 min (plus overnight thaw)

**Ingredients**
- 400 g Hirschfleisch (Medaillons) — venison medallions, thawed
- 1 tsp rapeseed oil (Rapsöl)
- 100 ml Sahne (single/double cream)
- 2 tbsp Schmand (soured cream)
- 1 tbsp Preiselbeeren (lingonberry/cranberry jam)
- 1 splash white rum
- Salt and pepper to taste
- Dark sauce thickener (Saucenbinder dunkel), optional

**Method**
1. Thaw the venison medallions overnight in the refrigerator. Pat very dry with kitchen paper and trim all sinew using a small knife.
2. Heat the rapeseed oil in a heavy pan over very high heat.
3. Sear the medallions sharply on each side, about 2 minutes per side. Season with salt and pepper. Remove to a plate and set aside.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest medallion and return them all to the pan.
5. Reduce heat to medium-low. Add cream, soured cream and lingonberry jam to the pan. Deglaze with a splash of white rum.
6. Let the medallions finish gently in the sauce until the internal temperature reaches the target.
7. **Pull at 56°C (133°F)** for medium-rare, or 60°C (140°F) for medium. Rest 3–4 minutes.
8. If needed, thicken the sauce with a little dark sauce thickener.
9. Serve with red cabbage (Blaukraut) and Schupfnudeln (potato dumplings) or pasta.
*Final resting temperature: 59–62°C (138–144°F).*

### 5. GialloZafferano — Arrosto di Cervo (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Chef Stefano Croce, Agritur El Mas di Moena; https://ricette.giallozafferano.it/Arrosto-di-cervo.html (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 60 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg filetto di cervo (venison fillet/loin), trimmed and tied
- 200 g butter, divided (150 g + 50 g)
- 4 garlic cloves, halved
- 4 sprigs fresh sage
- 2 sprigs fresh rosemary
- 10 juniper berries (bacche di ginepro)
- 2 bay leaves (alloro)
- 300 ml dry red wine
- 1 tbsp tomato concentrate
- Salt and black pepper

*Stock from trimmings:*
- Venison trimmings and bones
- 1 onion, roughly chopped
- 3 garlic cloves
- A little olive oil

**Method**
1. Tie the venison fillet into a neat, even cylinder. Reserve any trimmings.
2. To make the stock: heat olive oil in a saucepan, brown trimmings and onion over high heat for 5–7 minutes. Add wine, tomato concentrate, juniper berries, bay, rosemary and sage. Cover with water, season and simmer 30 minutes. Strain and reduce.
3. Melt 150 g butter in a wide, heavy frying pan over high heat until foaming.
4. Add the venison roast and sear on all sides until golden, basting continuously with the foaming butter. Season with salt and pepper.
5. Add garlic, sage, rosemary and juniper berries to the pan.
6. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the fillet through the end.
7. Transfer to an ovenproof tray and roast in a static oven at 80°C (175°F) for 10–15 minutes.
8. **Pull at 48°C (118°F)** for rare-medium-rare as the Italian chefs specify; the carry-over in a warm tray will bring it to 54–56°C (129–133°F). Rest 10 minutes.
9. Return to the pan with remaining 50 g butter; sear at high heat for 1 minute per side to finish the crust.
10. Slice and serve with the reduced pan jus, roasted carrots and asparagus.
*Final resting temperature after second sear: 57–60°C (135–140°F).*

### 6. The Rustic Elk — Marinated Venison Steak (North American tradition)
**Source**: The Rustic Elk; https://www.therusticelk.com/marinated-venison-steak-recipe/ (fetched 2025-07-13)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+ 3 hr marinate) · **Cook**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 venison steaks, about 2.5 cm (1 inch) thick (backstrap or hindquarter)
- 120 ml (½ cup) olive oil
- 60 ml (¼ cup) lemon juice
- 2 tbsp red wine vinegar
- 60 ml (¼ cup) Worcestershire sauce
- 80 ml (⅓ cup) soy sauce
- 1 tbsp minced garlic
- 2 tbsp Italian seasoning
- ½ tsp black pepper
- 1 tsp salt
- ¼ tsp smoked paprika
- 2 tbsp butter (to finish)

**Method**
1. Combine olive oil, lemon juice, red wine vinegar, Worcestershire sauce, soy sauce, garlic, Italian seasoning, black pepper, salt and smoked paprika in a bowl to form the marinade.
2. Add venison steaks, toss to coat. Cover and refrigerate for at least 3 hours, or overnight.
3. Remove steaks 30 minutes before cooking. Pat thoroughly dry with paper towels.
4. Preheat a cast iron skillet over medium-high heat until very hot (approximately 230°C/450°F surface temperature).
5. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into the thickest steak.
6. Sear steaks 4–5 minutes per side, flipping only once. Add butter in the last minute and baste.
7. **Pull at 52°C (126°F)** for medium-rare (the recipe targets 117°F/47°C pull; carry-over brings it to 125–130°F/52–54°C). Rest 10 minutes.
8. Slice against the grain and serve with the pan juices, roasted vegetables or mashed potatoes.
*Final resting temperature: 54–57°C (130–135°F).*

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Pull temperature | Method |
|--------|----------------|--------|
| BBC Good Food (UK) | 54°C (130°F) | Pan fry, no thermometer specified |
| Matprat (Norway) | 55–60°C (131–140°F) | Sear + low oven |
| Tasteline (Sweden) | 60°C (140°F) | Stovetop braise |
| Chefkoch (Germany) | 56°C (133°F) | Sear + sauce finish |
| GialloZafferano (Italy) | 48°C pull → 57°C rested | Pan sear + low oven |
| The Rustic Elk (USA) | 47–52°C pull → 54°C rested | Cast iron pan sear |

**Consensus pull**: 54–57°C (130–135°F) for medium-rare. Nordic tradition runs slightly higher (60°C/140°F). Italian tradition pulls lower with longer rest.

**Representative coding value**: 55°C (131°F)
