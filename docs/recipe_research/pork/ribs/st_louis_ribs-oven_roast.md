# St. Louis Style Ribs × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

St. Louis ribs oven-roasted low-and-slow is functionally indistinguishable from the smoker
method in terms of the collagen-dissolution chemistry, but without the smoke. The oven method
exposes the rib rack to dry, even, radiant heat rather than smoke-laden convective heat — the
result is ribs that are tender and juicy but carry no smoke ring, no charred bark, and no
smoke flavour unless a smoked paprika rub is used.

**What makes this method work**: connective tissue (primarily collagen in the intercostal
muscles between the bones and the periosteum lining the bones) converts to gelatin at
approximately 74 °C / 165 °F but the process is time-dependent. A quick blast of heat to
74 °C does not produce tender ribs; the meat must be held at or above that temperature for
an extended period (2–4 hours depending on thickness) for full collagen dissolution. This is
why every oven rib recipe uses 150–225 °C / 300–440 °F for 2.5–4 hours rather than the
short high-heat approach used for pork tenderloin or loin chops.

**Doneness for oven ribs is assessed by the bend test**, not the thermometer, in most
traditions. The rack is lifted by tongs at its centre point — if it bends into a "U" shape
(also described as an inverted rainbow or "rainbow bend") and the surface crust cracks,
the ribs are done. At this stage the internal temperature of the thickest lean section will
typically read 88–96 °C / 190–205 °F. The foil-wrap stage (first 1.5–2.5 hours) holds
moisture; the unwrapped stage (final 30–60 minutes) dries the surface and sets the bark or
glaze.

**MEATER probe placement**: insert lengthwise along the widest bone, keeping the tip in
the thickest continuous lean section of the intercostal meat, clear of any bone surface.
The probe should run roughly parallel to the bone. Do not insert at the end of the rack
where bones are thinner — use the centre or widest third of the rack.

## Source recipes

### 1. Patty Catalano — St. Louis-Style Ribs (American / Western tradition)
**Source**: Patty Catalano, thekitchn.com/oven-baked-st-louis-ribs-recipe-23535500  
**Published**: The Kitchn, May 2023 · **Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 30 min (+ 8–24 hr rest) · **Cook**: 4 hr 15 min

**Ingredients**
*Spice rub*
- 2 tbsp packed light brown sugar
- 2 tbsp smoked paprika
- 1 tbsp kosher salt
- 2 tsp garlic powder
- 2 tsp onion powder
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp ground coriander

*Ribs*
- 2 racks St. Louis-style pork ribs (2¼ to 4¼ lbs each)
- ⅓ cup spicy brown or Dijon mustard
- 1 cup tomato-based barbecue sauce, divided

**Method**
1. Combine all spice rub ingredients.
2. Place ribs meaty-side down. Slide a butter knife under the membrane, then grasp with a dry paper towel and pull to remove the membrane completely.
3. Brush mustard all over both racks. Sprinkle spice rub all over and pat to adhere. Refrigerate uncovered **8–24 hours**.
4. Let ribs sit at room temperature 30 minutes. Heat oven to 225 °F / 107 °C.
5. Wrap each rack completely in heavy-duty aluminium foil. Place on separate rimmed baking sheets, meaty side up. Bake, rotating after 1 hour, for **2 hours total**.
6. Unwrap ribs. Place directly on baking sheets, meaty side up. Return to oven and bake, rotating after 1 hour, for a further **2 hours**.
7. Check using **bend test**: lift rack from centre with tongs — ribs should bend into a wide "U" shape and the spice crust should crack. At this stage, internal temperature will be approximately **88–96 °C / 190–205 °F**.
8. Switch oven to broil. Flip racks; brush bone side with ¼ cup sauce. Broil one rack at a time on the upper rack until sauce bubbles, 2–3 minutes. Flip; brush meaty side with remaining ¾ cup sauce. Broil 2–3 minutes until bubbly and darkened.
9. Rest before cutting into individual or multi-bone portions.

### 2. Nagi Maehashi — Oven Pork Ribs with Barbecue Sauce (Australian / Western tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, recipetineats.com/oven-baked-barbecue-pork-ribs/  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: ~2 hr

**Ingredients**

*Barbecue rub*
- 2½ tsp paprika powder
- 2 tsp garlic powder
- 1½ tsp onion powder
- 1 tsp cayenne pepper (omit for mild)
- 1½ tsp dried thyme
- 1½ tsp dried oregano
- 1½ tsp salt
- ½ tsp black pepper

*Ribs*
- 2–2.5 kg (4–5 lbs) pork ribs, 2 racks (Baby Back or St. Louis style)
- ¾ cup sweet alcoholic apple cider (or apple juice)
- 1 tbsp olive oil

*Homemade barbecue sauce*
- ½ cup apple cider vinegar
- 1½ cups tomato ketchup
- ½ cup water
- 1½ tbsp molasses (not blackstrap)
- ⅓ cup brown sugar
- 2 tsp mustard powder
- 1½ tsp garlic powder
- 2 tsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp salt
- 1 tsp black pepper

**Method**
1. Rub the spice mixture onto both sides of the ribs (mostly on the meaty side). Rest 20 minutes (or overnight).
2. Preheat oven to 160 °C / 320 °F (all oven types).
3. Place ribs in a single layer in a roasting tray. Pour apple cider under the ribs, cover tightly with foil. Bake **1 hour 30 minutes** until meat is pretty tender.
4. Remove foil. Increase temperature to 180 °C / 350 °F. Drizzle with olive oil. Return to oven for **15 minutes** until rub forms a crust.
5. Make barbecue sauce: combine all ingredients in a saucepan, simmer stirring occasionally for 45 minutes until thickened.
6. Transfer ribs to a foil-lined, parchment-covered tray. Pour tray juices over. Flip so bony side faces up. Slather with barbecue sauce, bake 10 minutes.
7. Flip meaty side up. Slather with sauce, bake 5 minutes. Repeat basting 2–3 more times until a thick glaze builds up.
8. Serve with remaining barbecue sauce.
9. **Temperature note**: Doneness is assessed visually/tactilely (meat tender, bark formed). Internal temperature at the stated doneness point will typically be **88–96 °C / 190–205 °F**.

### 3. Judy & Bill Leung — Char Siu-Style Oven Baked Ribs (Chinese-American / Eastern tradition)
**Source**: Judy & Bill Leung, thewoksoflife.com/oven-baked-ribs-char-siu/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 2 hr (including marinating) · **Cook**: 1 hr

**Ingredients**
- 3 lbs (1.4 kg) baby back or spare ribs
- ¼ cup sugar (50 g)
- 2 tsp salt
- ½ tsp five-spice powder
- ¼ tsp white pepper
- ½ tsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine (or dry sherry)
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp hoisin sauce
- 2 tsp molasses
- ⅛ tsp red food colouring (optional — for traditional char siu colour)
- 3 cloves garlic, finely minced

**Method**
1. Rinse ribs and pat dry thoroughly.
2. Combine all remaining ingredients in a bowl to make the marinade.
3. Reserve about ⅓ of the marinade. Rub ribs with the rest. Marinate at least **2 hours**, overnight for best flavour.
4. Preheat oven to 400 °F / 204 °C. Place ribs on a rack over a baking sheet. Pour 1 cup water into the baking sheet.
5. Roast **30 minutes**.
6. Remove from oven, baste with reserved marinade. Return to oven and roast **30 more minutes** (1 hour total).
7. Rest 5 minutes. Slice between bones and serve.
8. **Temperature note**: Oven at 204 °C with 1 hour total cooking time. No internal temp specified; the sugars in the marinade create the signature caramelised char siu crust at this temperature. Internal temp at this stage typically exceeds USDA minimum 63 °C / 145 °F.

### 4. Holger Rørby Madsen — Langtidsstegte Ribben i Ovn (Danish / Northern tradition)
**Source**: Holger Rørby Madsen, madensverden.dk/langtidsstegte-ribben-i-ovn/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 3 hr

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg (3.3 lbs) ribben (spareribs / meaty pork ribs)

*Tør rub (dry rub)*
- 3 tsp salt
- 3 tsp paprika
- 1 tsp spidskommen (cumin)
- 1 tsp hvidløgspulver (garlic powder)
- 1 tsk løgpulver (onion powder)
- Freshly ground black pepper

*Plus: BBQ-marinade (linked separately at madensverden.dk/bbq-marinade-opskrift/)*

**Method**
1. Remove the membrane (hinde) from the bone side of the ribs. Ask the butcher to do this if preferred.
2. Combine all dry rub ingredients.
3. Place ribs on a rack in a deep roasting tin, bone side up. Sprinkle 1 tsp dry rub on each side, massage in well. Turn and repeat on the top side. Use all remaining dry rub. Rest 30 minutes (or refrigerate overnight for deeper flavour).
4. Preheat oven to **150 °C / 302 °F** (conventional top/bottom heat — not fan).
5. Pour ½ to 1 litre of water into the base of the roasting tin below the rack. Slide into centre of oven.
6. Roast for **2.5 hours** — no basting or attention needed.
7. Make homemade BBQ marinade (see linked recipe).
8. Remove ribs after 2.5 hours. Increase oven to **200 °C / 392 °F**.
9. Brush BBQ marinade generously on both sides of the ribs.
10. Return ribs to oven on the rack, uncovered. Roast a further **30 minutes**.
11. Rest 5 minutes before cutting into portions.
12. **Temperature note**: 2.5 hours at 150 °C followed by 30 min at 200 °C is the classic slow-roast approach. Internal temperature at completion will typically be **88–96 °C / 190–205 °F** — the collagen-dissolution range required for fall-off-the-bone tender Danish spare ribs.

### 5. Immaculate Bites — Caribbean Jerk Barbecue Ribs (Caribbean / Southern tradition)
**Source**: Immaculate Bites (Imma Adamu), africanbites.com/caribbean-jerk-barbecue-ribs-2/  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: overnight (including resting) · **Cook**: ~1 hr + broil

**Ingredients**
- 3 lbs (1.4 kg) pork spare ribs or baby back/loin ribs
- Salt: approximately 1 tsp per pound (2½ tsp for 3 lbs)
- Jerk dry rub: approximately 1 tbsp per pound (~3 tbsp for 3 lbs) — see africanbites.com/jamaican-jerk-seasoning/ for jerk spice blend
- Barbecue sauce (to taste, for finishing glaze)

**Method**
1. Salt the ribs at 1 tsp per pound. Apply jerk dry rub at approximately 1 tbsp per pound.
2. Wrap in aluminium foil. Refrigerate overnight.
3. Remove from refrigerator and let sit at room temperature for 1 hour before cooking.
4. Bake at **350 °F / 177 °C**:
   - Baby back or loin ribs: a little over 1 hour
   - Spare ribs: 1.5 hours or more
5. Remove from oven. Baste with barbecue sauce. Broil uncovered, checking every 2 minutes, until sauce caramelises. Watch closely to prevent burning.
6. **Temperature note**: No explicit thermometer temperature given. The Caribbean jerk rib tradition (a fusion of West African dry spicing and British colonial BBQ methods, brought together in Jamaican and broader Caribbean cuisine) focuses on the jerk flavour profile. The ribs are cooked until the meat pulls back from the bone tips.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Internal temp target | Oven temp | Total time |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------|------------|
| American (Western) | ~88–96 °C / 190–205 °F (bend test) | 107 °C (225 °F) | ~4 hr |
| Australian (Western) | ~88–96 °C / 190–205 °F (tactile) | 160–180 °C (320–350 °F) | ~2 hr |
| Chinese-American (Eastern) | >63 °C / 145 °F (USDA minimum) | 204 °C (400 °F) | 1 hr |
| Danish (Northern) | ~88–96 °C / 190–205 °F (implied) | 150–200 °C (302–392 °F) | 3 hr |
| Caribbean (Southern) | Not explicit | 177 °C (350 °F) | 1–1.5 hr |

**Key findings:**

1. **USDA minimum**: 63 °C / 145 °F — this is safe but NOT the culinary target. At 63 °C ribs are still tough (collagen intact). The Chinese-American char siu approach (204 °C for 1 hour) reaches USDA minimum but does not aim for collagen-dissolved tenderness — it aims for a caramelised, charred exterior with still-chewy interior meat, which is characteristic of char siu pork.

2. **Culinary preferred** (tender, pull-back ribs): **88–96 °C / 190–205 °F**. This is the temperature at which collagen in the intercostal muscles has dissolved into gelatin, leaving fall-from-the-bone to slightly-chewy-but-tender meat. The MEATER app's `pulled` doneness target for pork ribs (93 °C / 200 °F) is well within this window.

3. **Oven temperature trade-off**: Lower oven temperatures (107–160 °C) require 2.5–4 hours to bring ribs to 88–96 °C internal; higher temperatures (177–204 °C) take 1–1.5 hours but with higher risk of the exterior drying out before the interior reaches the collagen-dissolution range. The foil-wrap technique (first phase) solves this by creating a steam environment around the ribs.

4. **No explicit thermometer readings** in most oven rib recipes: the bend test is the universal doneness indicator for oven ribs in Western, Nordic, and Caribbean traditions. A MEATER probe inserted along the ribs will typically read **88–96 °C when the bend test passes**.

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting St. Louis ribs is the home-cook alternative to smoking when a dedicated smoker or outdoor grill is unavailable. The four structural differences from the smoker method:

1. **No smoke flavour** — compensated for by heavy dry rubs with smoked paprika, cumin, or other warm spices
2. **Moister interior** — the oven's enclosed humidity, especially during the foil phase, prevents the surface moisture loss that defines true smoked ribs' "bark"
3. **Glaze-forward finish** — most oven recipes finish with a BBQ sauce glaze baked or broiled onto the surface, creating a sticky lacquer that is the visual hallmark of oven ribs
4. **More control** — the oven environment allows precise temperature management in a way that charcoal smokers do not

## Summary

St. Louis ribs oven-roasted require 2–4 hours at 150–225 °C depending on whether low-and-slow or higher-heat methods are used. The universal doneness indicator is the bend test (rack bends into a "U" shape when lifted at centre, crust cracks) which corresponds to approximately 88–96 °C / 190–205 °F internal — the collagen-dissolution range. USDA minimum (63 °C) is safe but produces tough ribs. A MEATER probe inserted lengthwise along the widest bone, tip in the centre of the thickest lean intercostal section, will read 88–96 °C when the bend test passes.
