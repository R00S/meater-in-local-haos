# Ham × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Ham is the hind leg of the pig, cured with salt, smoke, or both before being sold. Unlike
any other pork cut it arrives at the oven already transformed: curing has tightened the
proteins, expelled moisture, and built a dense, savoury structure that raw pork leg does
not have. Oven roasting suits this cut because the enclosed radiant heat caramelises the
scored fat surface and sets the glazed, lacquered crust that defines baked ham; the low,
steady oven temperature then heats the large dense mass to serving temperature without
drying out the firm interior. Carryover is modest — typically 2–4 °C — because the dense
mass equalises heat slowly.

Probe placement: insert from the thick cut end (butt end), angling the tip to the geometric
centre of the largest lean muscle section, at least 1 cm clear of the central bone. Ham has
multiple muscle groups separated by fat seams — the probe tip must not rest in a fat seam,
which gives a lagging reading.

## Source recipes

### 1. Anna och Fanny Bergenström — Ugnsbakad Julskinka med Senapsgriljering (Swedish / Northern tradition)
**Source**: Anna och Fanny Bergenström, koket.se/anna-och-fanny-bergenstrom/varmratter/korv-chark/ugnsbakad-julskinka-med-senapsgriljering  
**Serves**: 8–10 · **Prep**: 10 min + 2–4 hr soaking · **Cook**: 60–70 min/kg + 15 min · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 3–4 kg skinka (Swedish Christmas ham, cured/lightly salted pork leg)

*Griljering (mustard glaze)*
- 3 msk (tbsp) svensk färdiglagad senap (Swedish prepared mustard)
- 1 ägg (egg)
- 3 msk (tbsp) ströbröd (dried breadcrumbs)

**Method**
1. Soak the ham in cold water for 2–4 hours to reduce saltiness. Remove from water and pat dry.
2. **Probe:** Insert the thermometer diagonally from the thicker end of the ham. On a flat ham, insert from the short end. Push the tip to the exact centre of the thickest muscle, away from the bone.
3. Wrap ham tightly in aluminium foil. Place in a roasting tin. Bake at 175 °C / 347 °F for **60–70 minutes per kilogram**.
4. **Pull at 72 °C (162 °F).** This is the Swedish food safety standard for cured pork leg (julskinka); it sits above the USDA minimum of 63 °C / 145 °F as an additional safety margin for a large whole joint.
5. Rest wrapped in foil 15 minutes. Open and remove the rind (svål) while slightly warm.
6. *Griljering:* Increase oven to 225 °C / 437 °F. Whisk 1 egg with 3 tbsp mustard. Brush mixture over fat surface. Sift 3 tbsp breadcrumbs over. Return to oven for approximately 15 minutes until glaze browns. Watch closely.
7. Final serving temperature: **72 °C / 162 °F** minimum (fully cooked/well done).

### 2. Angela Lawrence — Jamaican Glazed Baked Ham (Jamaican / Southern tradition)
**Source**: Angela Lawrence (Cook Like a Jamaican), 196flavors.com/jamaica-glazed-baked-ham/  
**Serves**: 12 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 2 hr

**Ingredients**
- 4 lb (1.8 kg) ready-to-serve ham (fully cooked)
- A few whole cloves

*Glaze*
- 1 cup cranberry sauce (or strawberry or raspberry jam)
- ¼ cup orange juice
- ¼ cup pineapple juice
- 1 tablespoon lemon juice
- ½ cup brown sugar (if using cranberry sauce rather than sweet jam)
- 1 teaspoon mustard

**Method**
1. Remove wrapping from ham. Score fat in a diamond pattern and stud with cloves.
2. **Probe:** Insert probe from the thicker cut end, angling tip to the geometric centre of the largest lean section, clear of the central bone.
3. Bake at **325 °F / 165 °C** for 2 hours, or until a meat thermometer registers the target temperature.
4. In a saucepan, combine cranberry sauce (or jam), orange juice, pineapple juice, lemon juice, brown sugar if using, and mustard. Simmer uncovered 5 minutes to thicken.
5. During the last 30 minutes of baking, spoon glaze over the ham every 10 minutes.
6. **Pull at 130 °F / 55 °C.** This is for fully cooked, ready-to-serve ham where the oven is heating through to serving temperature only — not cooking raw pork. The holiday tradition in Jamaica uses fully cooked imported ham.
7. Final serving temperature: **55 °C / 130 °F** (reheated, glazed — warm slicing temperature).

### 3. John Torode — Baked Glazed Ham (British / Western tradition)
**Source**: John Torode, bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/baked-glazed-ham  
**Published**: *Good Food* magazine, November 2016  
**Serves**: 8 generously · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 5 hr 45 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 raw smoked gammon on the bone, approximately 5–6 kg, rind left on
- 100 ml maple syrup
- 200 g apricot jam
- 300 g light brown soft sugar
- 12 whole cloves
- 50 ml water

**Method**
1. Heat oven to 180 °C / 160 °C fan / gas 4. Place ham on a wire rack over a large roasting tin. Pour in 480 ml warm water. Cover tightly with foil. Put the ham in the oven, immediately reduce heat to 160 °C / 140 °C fan / gas 3. Cook for **5 hours**.
2. Meanwhile, combine maple syrup, apricot jam, and brown sugar in a heavy-based pan with 50 ml water. Bring to the boil, remove from heat and allow to cool.
3. Remove ham from oven. Increase temperature to 200 °C / 180 °C fan / gas 6. Carefully remove foil (watch for steam). Remove the rind by inserting a sharp knife at the knuckle end and gently lifting it away from the fat.
4. Score the fat into large diamond shapes approximately 4 cm apart. Stud each diamond corner with a clove.
5. Brush generously with the sugar glaze, pushing it into the scored fat.
6. **Probe:** Insert probe from the thick cut end, to the centre of the largest lean muscle mass, clear of the bone.
7. Return to oven on the rack over water. Bake a further **45 minutes** until deeply coloured and glazed.
8. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** Doneness is assessed visually: deeply coloured, caramelised glaze set over the fat. This is a raw smoked gammon (approximately 5–6 kg), so full cooking through to a safe internal temperature is required; the 5 hr 45 min total cook time at 160–200 °C is sufficient. UK Food Standards Agency safe minimum for whole cuts of pork: 75 °C / 167 °F — but this temperature is not stated in Torode's recipe.
9. Serve hot or cold; keeps refrigerated up to 1 week.

### 4. Nagi Maehashi — Maple Glazed Ham (Australian / Western tradition)
**Source**: Nagi Maehashi, recipetineats.com/easy-maple-sticky-glazed-ham/  
**Serves**: 30–40 as part of a spread · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 1.5–2 hr · **Rest**: 20 min+

**Ingredients**
- 5 kg / 10 lb leg ham, bone in, skin on (fully cooked, smoked)
- 30 cloves (optional, for studding)
- 2 oranges, cut into quarters
- 1 cup (250 ml) water

*Glaze*
- ¾ cup (185 ml) maple syrup
- ¾ cup (165 g) brown sugar, packed
- 3 tbsp Dijon mustard
- ¾ tsp ground cinnamon
- ½ tsp allspice (or nutmeg)

**Method**
1. Remove ham from fridge 1 hour before cooking.
2. Preheat oven to 160 °C / 320 °F (140 °C fan). Arrange shelf in lower third so the ham sits in the centre of the oven.
3. Whisk all glaze ingredients together in a bowl.
4. Remove rind: run a small knife around the bone handle, down each side, and under the rind on the cut face. Slide fingers under to peel back the rind; leave as much fat as possible.
5. Lightly score the fat surface in 2.5 cm diamonds, about 75 % of the way into the fat. Optionally stud each intersection with a clove.
6. **Probe:** Insert from the thick cut end, angling tip to the geometric centre of the largest lean muscle mass, clear of the bone and any fat seams.
7. Place ham in a large baking dish. Squeeze juice of 1 orange over ham; place all orange quarters in the dish. Brush or spoon half the glaze over the surface. Pour water into the dish.
8. Bake 1.5–2 hours, basting generously every 30 minutes with remaining glaze and pan juices. Cover any sections browning too fast with small foil patches.
9. **No explicit pull temperature given in this recipe.** This is a fully cooked/smoked ham where the oven is used to heat through and caramelise the glaze; Nagi's visual indicator is "sticky and golden." USDA guideline for reheated cooked ham is 60 °C / 140 °F — but this temperature is not stated in the recipe.
10. Rest at least 20 minutes. Baste generously with thickened pan juices before serving.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Ham type | Explicit pull temp in recipe | Oven temp |
|-----------|----------|------------------------------|-----------|
| Swedish (Northern) | Cured, lightly salted (raw) | **72 °C / 162 °F** | 175 °C / 347 °F |
| Jamaican (Southern) | Fully cooked, ready-to-serve | **55 °C / 130 °F** | 165 °C / 325 °F |
| British (Western) | Raw smoked gammon | Not given (visual doneness) | 160–200 °C / 320–392 °F |
| Australian (Western) | Fully cooked, smoked | Not given (visual doneness) | 160 °C / 320 °F |

Two of four sources give explicit pull temperatures. The split is entirely by ham type, not by tradition: fully cooked ham is heated to 55–60 °C (serving temperature only); raw/cured ham that must be fully cooked to a safe temperature targets 72–75 °C. The two Western sources (British and Australian) do not give explicit thermometer targets — both assess doneness visually by glaze colour and set.

## What makes this method special

Oven roasting is the universal method for ham because ham's cured structure does not require the collagen-dissolving extended moist braise that raw braising cuts need — the curing has already done that work. What the oven provides is: (1) caramelisation of the sweet glaze at 180–225 °C, converting sugars and building the mahogany crust that defines baked ham visually; (2) rendering of the scored surface fat into the glaze, basting the surface from within through the score lines; and (3) controlled, even heat that brings a 3–6 kg joint to the correct internal temperature without over-drying the already-firm interior. The MEATER probe is particularly useful here because the difference between a good result (55–72 °C internal) and a dried-out one (85 °C+) can be as little as 10–15 minutes in a hot oven.

## Summary

Baked ham divides into two categories with different temperature targets: fully cooked (ready-to-serve) ham, which needs only to be heated to serving temperature (55–60 °C) while the glaze caramelises; and raw or lightly cured ham (UK gammon, Swedish julskinka), which must reach 63–72 °C to be safe and fully cooked. In both cases the glaze — always sweet (maple, sugar, fruit jam) with a tangy counterpoint (mustard, citrus, cloves) — is applied in the final 15–45 minutes at elevated heat. Two of four fetched sources give explicit thermometer targets; two rely on visual doneness. The probe is most valuable for raw gammon where undercooking is a safety risk.
