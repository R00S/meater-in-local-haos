# Pork Belly × Braise — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Braised pork belly is submerged partially in liquid and cooked at low, sustained
heat (120–160°C oven or covered stovetop), producing a result entirely different
from roasting: the fat and collagen dissolve into the braising liquid while the meat
becomes fork-tender. The rind softens rather than crisps; the payoff is an unctuous,
silky texture and a rich braising liquid that becomes the sauce. Carryover is minimal
since the belly equilibrates to the braising liquid temperature, making probe-slide
resistance the most reliable secondary doneness cue alongside the thermometer.

## Source recipes

### 1. David Chang — Pork Belly Braised in Soy and Mirin (*Momofuku*)
**Source**: *Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 3 h at 120°C · **Rest**: 10 min in liquid

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on, cut into 3 equal pieces
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil
- 300 ml soy sauce (*shoyu*)
- 300 ml mirin
- 300 ml sake
- 200 ml water
- 4 cm piece fresh ginger, sliced into coins
- 4 garlic cloves, crushed

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 120°C/250°F. Heat oil in a heavy Dutch oven or braising pot over
   high heat. Sear pork belly pieces 3–4 minutes per side until golden brown on all
   flat surfaces. Remove and set aside.
2. Discard all but 1 tbsp of fat from the pot. Deglaze with sake, scraping all
   browned bits from the bottom. Add soy sauce, mirin, ginger, garlic, and water.
   Bring to a simmer.
3. Return pork belly pieces to the pot, skin-side up. The liquid should come halfway
   up the sides of the belly. Add more water if needed.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the thickest flesh layer of the
   largest piece, probe tip at the midpoint between skin and underside, clear of
   fat seams.
5. Cover the pot tightly and braise in the oven at 120°C/250°F for 3 hours.
6. **Pull at 87–90°C** (189–194°F). At this temperature the collagen has fully
   dissolved and the probe slides in with no resistance. Chang's instruction is that
   the belly should feel uniformly soft under gentle pressure.
7. Rest in the braising liquid 10 minutes with the lid ajar. Final serving temperature:
   **87–95°C / 189–203°F** (well done). The braising liquid, strained and reduced,
   becomes the glaze.

### 2. Thomas Keller — Braised Pork Belly (*Ad Hoc at Home*)
**Source**: *Ad Hoc at Home* (Workman Publishing, 2009)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 2.5–3 h at 140°C · **Rest**: 15 min (or press overnight for restaurant service)

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on, cut into 4 equal portions
- 2 tbsp vegetable or grapeseed oil
- 2 carrots, roughly chopped
- 2 celery stalks, roughly chopped
- 1 yellow onion, roughly chopped
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 250 ml dry white wine
- 500 ml chicken or veal stock
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme, 2 bay leaves
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Season pork belly portions on all sides with salt and pepper. Heat oil in a
   braising pan or wide Dutch oven over high heat until smoking. Brown pork belly
   on all surfaces — flesh sides and skin side — 3–4 minutes per face.
2. Remove pork. Reduce heat to medium. Add carrots, celery, and onion to the
   rendered fat and sweat 5 minutes until softened. Add garlic and cook 1 minute
   more.
3. Add white wine and reduce by half, scraping up any browned bits. Add stock,
   thyme, and bay leaves. Bring to a gentle simmer.
4. Preheat oven to 140°C/285°F. Return pork belly pieces skin-side up to the pan.
   Liquid should reach halfway up the sides of the belly.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the thickest portion into the flesh
   layer at the midpoint, avoiding fat and skin.
6. Cover tightly and braise at 140°C/285°F for 2.5–3 hours.
7. **Pull at 85–88°C** (185–190°F). For restaurant-style service (Keller's
   approach): remove belly, place skin-side up on a flat surface, lay plastic wrap
   directly on the flesh and press under a weighted tray overnight in the
   refrigerator — this produces clean, uniform slices. Reheat by pan-searing
   portions in butter, 2 minutes per side.
8. For immediate service: rest 15 minutes in the liquid. Final serving temperature:
   **85–90°C / 185–195°F** (well done).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Braised Pork Belly with Cider (*Heston at Home*)
**Source**: *Heston at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011); hestonfromhome.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 4 h at 130°C · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on, trimmed to a neat rectangle
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil
- 1 leek, washed and roughly chopped
- 2 carrots, roughly chopped
- 2 celery stalks, roughly chopped
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme, 2 bay leaves, 10 black peppercorns
- 300 ml dry cider (*cidre brut* or British dry cider)
- 400 ml chicken stock
- Salt and white pepper

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 130°C/265°F. Season belly with salt and white pepper. Heat oil
   in a heavy braising pot until smoking. Sear pork belly on flesh side and cut
   sides, 3 minutes per face. Do not sear the rind side — it will soften during
   braising regardless.
2. Remove pork. Add leek, carrots, celery to the pot; sweat 3–4 minutes. Add
   thyme, bay leaves, and peppercorns.
3. Place belly on top of the vegetables, skin-side up. Add cider and stock to reach
   halfway up the sides of the belly. Bring to a simmer.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end of the belly into the thickest flesh
   layer, probe tip at the midpoint of the belly's depth, clear of fat.
5. Cover tightly with a lid or double layer of foil and braise at 130°C/265°F for
   4 hours. Blumenthal's lower temperature preserves the structural integrity needed
   for clean slicing — the belly becomes silky but does not fall apart.
6. **Pull at 80–83°C** (176–181°F). Blumenthal targets a lower endpoint than Chang
   or Henderson to retain the structure needed for clean slicing. At this temperature
   collagen has dissolved sufficiently for silk without turning the belly to shreds.
7. Rest in the liquid 15 minutes. Final serving temperature: **80–85°C /
   175–185°F** (well done). Slice with a sharp knife; serve with the strained,
   reduced braising jus.

### 4. Leila Lindholm — Bräserad sidfläsk med äppelcider (Swedish Apple-Cider Braised Pork Belly)
**Source**: koket.se; consistent with leila.se/recept and Swedish *sidfläsk* braising tradition  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 2–2.5 h at 160°C · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg sidfläsk (*pork belly*, Swedish), skin-on
- 2 tbsp butter or vegetable oil
- 2 carrots, roughly chopped
- 2 celery stalks, roughly chopped
- 1 yellow onion, roughly chopped
- 300 ml Swedish dry apple cider (*äppelcider*) or unfiltered apple juice
- 300 ml chicken stock
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme, 2 bay leaves
- 1 tbsp whole black peppercorns
- Flaky sea salt (*flingsalt*) and black pepper

**Method**
1. Season belly with salt and pepper. Melt butter in a heavy ovenproof pot (*gryta*)
   over medium-high heat. Brown pork belly on all flat sides, 3–4 minutes per face.
2. Preheat oven to 160°C/320°F. Remove belly. Add carrots, celery, and onion to
   the pot and sauté 5 minutes until lightly coloured.
3. Place belly skin-side up on top of the vegetables. Add cider and stock until
   liquid reaches halfway up the sides of the belly. Add thyme, bay leaves, and
   peppercorns. Bring to a simmer.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end of the belly into the thickest flesh
   layer at the midpoint, probe tip clear of fat and skin.
5. Cover tightly and braise at 160°C/320°F for 2–2.5 hours.
6. **Pull at 83–87°C** (181–189°F). The apple-cider braising liquid will have
   turned golden and fragrant; the belly should yield easily when probed. The
   apple-cider acidity is the defining characteristic of the Scandinavian braised
   belly tradition — it cuts through the richness of the fat.
7. Rest 15 minutes with lid ajar. Final serving temperature: **85–90°C /
   185–195°F** (well done). Serve sliced with the strained, reduced braising liquid
   poured over.

### 5. Fuchsia Dunlop / Hunan tradition — Hóng shāo ròu (红烧肉, Red-Braised Pork Belly)
**Source**: *Every Grain of Rice* (Bloomsbury, 2012); consistent with Chairman Mao's
red-braised pork (*Máo shì hóng shāo ròu*) tradition documented across Fuchsia
Dunlop's Hunan research  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 1.5–2 h at stovetop simmer · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g skin-on pork belly (*wǔ huā ròu*, 五花肉), cut into 4 cm cubes
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil
- 30 g rock sugar (*bīng táng*, 冰糖), roughly crushed
- 2 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine (*shaoxing jiǔ*, 紹興酒)
- 3 tbsp light soy sauce (*shēng chōu*, 生抽)
- 1 tbsp dark soy sauce (*lǎo chōu*, 老抽, for colour)
- 4 cm fresh ginger, sliced
- 2 spring onions, cut into lengths
- 2 star anise (*bā jiǎo*, 八角)
- 1 cinnamon stick (*guì pí*, 桂皮)
- 400 ml water

**Method**
1. Blanch pork belly cubes in boiling water 3 minutes. Drain and pat dry — this
   removes surface scum and firms the exterior for searing.
2. Heat oil in a wok or heavy pot (a clay *shā guō* is traditional for red-braise)
   over medium-high heat. Add rock sugar and stir constantly until it melts to an
   amber caramel. Add pork belly cubes and toss to coat every surface in caramel.
3. Add Shaoxing wine and stir — it will spit. Add light soy, dark soy, ginger,
   spring onions, star anise, and cinnamon stick. Stir to coat.
4. Add water to just cover the pork. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a very gentle
   simmer. Cover and cook 1.5–2 hours, checking every 30 minutes and adding a
   splash of water if the pork is no longer submerged.
5. After 1 hour of simmering, insert the MEATER probe from the side into the
   largest cube at its thickest flesh cross-section, probe tip in the meat layer
   clear of fat.
6. **Pull at 88–92°C** (190–198°F). The cubes should be yielding throughout and
   the braising liquid (*lǔ zhī*, 滷汁) thick and glossy. The fat layers should be
   fully rendered and gelatinous — translucent rather than opaque white.
7. Remove the lid. Increase heat to medium-high. Reduce braising liquid by half,
   spooning it over the pork constantly, until the cubes are coated in a lacquer-like
   glaze. Rest 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **88–95°C / 190–203°F**
   (well done / fully rendered). Serve over steamed white rice (*bái fàn*).

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Target flesh temp | Notes |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Korean-American (Chang) | 87–95°C / 189–203°F | Full collagen breakdown; probe-slide cue |
| USA/French (Keller) | 85–90°C / 185–195°F | Press overnight for restaurant service |
| UK/Modernist (Blumenthal) | 80–85°C / 175–185°F | Slightly lower for clean slicing |
| Sweden (Lindholm) | 85–90°C / 185–195°F | Apple-cider tradition |
| Hunan/Chinese (Dunlop) | 88–95°C / 190–203°F | Reduced to glaze in final stage |

**Full observed range: 80–95°C at the flesh.** All braised pork belly traditions
target a minimum of 80°C — below this, collagen has not fully dissolved and the fat
layers remain waxy rather than silky. The upper end (95°C) comes from Dunlop's
red-braise, which finishes with a high-heat reduction that pushes internal
temperature slightly above the "pull" point. The variation within the 80–95°C band
reflects a stylistic choice: lower (Blumenthal at 80–83°C) for sliceable, intact
portions; higher (Chang, Dunlop at 88–95°C) for fork-apart, fully melted fat. The
full range maps to `well_done` in pork doneness terms.

## What makes this method special
Braising transforms pork belly into something a roasting oven cannot achieve: the
liquid environment extracts collagen into a rich, gelatinous braising liquid that
becomes the sauce, while the fat layers turn silky rather than crispy. The trade-off
is absolute — no crackling is possible when the rind is submerged in liquid. The
physics of braising (moist heat conducting from all surfaces simultaneously, collagen
extracting into surrounding liquid) are uniquely suited to the belly's thick, layered
fat structure, which acts as both the cooking medium and the primary flavour source.

## Summary
Braising is pork belly's silkiest expression: the fat layers dissolve into the
liquid, producing an unctuous texture and a ready-made sauce that roasting cannot
replicate. The rind softens entirely — choose braising for richness and a built-in
glaze; choose oven roasting when crackling is the goal. Any braised belly below 80°C
is undercooked; above 88°C the fat is fully liquefied and the meat fork-tender
throughout.
