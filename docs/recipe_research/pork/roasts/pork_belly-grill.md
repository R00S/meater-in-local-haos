# Pork Belly × Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Grilled pork belly is almost universally done in thin slices (5–8 mm) placed
directly over heat — the same physical logic as pan frying, but on a grill grate
over live fire, charcoal, or gas. The direct heat renders the fat rapidly and
creates char lines across the slices, adding smoke and bitterness that a pan cannot
provide. Korean *samgyeopsal* at a restaurant charcoal or gas table grill is the
most iconic form, but grilled pork belly appears in Filipino *lechon kawali* prep,
Australian backyard BBQ, and various South-East Asian street-food traditions. Whole
slabs are occasionally grilled (Australian and American style) but the slab method
requires indirect heat and effectively merges with smoker technique; the true
grill method for pork belly is the thin slice. Carryover is minimal given the thin
cut and fast cook; pull promptly.

## Source recipes

### 1. Korean BBQ tradition — 삼겹살 구이 (Samgyeopsal Gui, Grilled Pork Belly)
**Source**: *My Korean Kitchen* (Sue Pressey, mykoreankitchen.com); consistent
with traditional Korean BBQ table-grill technique documented by the Korea Tourism
Organization and numerous Korean culinary authorities  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: none (eat at the table)

**Ingredients**
- 600 g fresh pork belly, sliced 5–7 mm thick
- To serve: butter lettuce (*ssam yachae*, 쌈야채), perilla leaves (*kkaennip*,
  깻잎), garlic cloves (whole, grilled alongside), sliced green and red chilli,
  *ssamjang* (*쌈장*), kimchi, sesame oil + salt dipping sauce

**Method**
1. Preheat the grill to high heat (gas grill) or allow the charcoal to reach
   a white-ash, glowing stage. The Korean table grill (*sangseon guyi*, 상선구이)
   is set in the centre of the table — diners cook their own portions.
2. Lay pork belly slices directly on the grate. No oil, no marinade — this is
   unseasoned *samgyeopsal*, the pure flavour of the belly.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the short side into the thickest slice, probe tip
   in the meat layer at the midpoint of the slice depth. Hold the probe clip to
   keep it from touching the grate.
4. Grill 3–4 minutes on the first side until char lines form and fat begins to
   render visibly at the edges.
5. Flip with tongs. Grill 3–4 minutes on the second side. Use kitchen scissors to
   cut portions into bite-sized pieces on the grill — the standard Korean table technique.
6. **Pull at 72–75°C** (162–167°F). The fat should be rendered and golden; some
   char is desirable and expected. Do not pull early to preserve pink colour — pork
   belly is a fatty cut traditionally served well done on the grill.
7. No rest. Final serving temperature: **72–75°C / 162–167°F** (well done). Eat
   immediately wrapped in lettuce with garlic, chilli, and *ssamjang*.

### 2. Australian BBQ tradition — Grilled Pork Belly Slices
**Source**: taste.com.au (Australia's largest recipe platform); consistent with
Australian backyard barbecue conventions for pork belly strips  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+1 h marinade) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g pork belly, skin on or off, cut into 1.5 cm thick slices
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp honey
- 1 tbsp hoisin sauce
- 1 tbsp rice vinegar
- 2 cloves garlic, crushed
- 1 tsp grated fresh ginger
- 1 tsp sesame oil

**Method**
1. Mix marinade ingredients. Toss belly slices to coat. Marinate refrigerated for
   1 hour minimum, or up to 4 hours.
2. Remove slices from marinade and shake off excess (excess sugar will burn on the grill).
   Reserve the marinade.
3. Preheat the grill to high. Clean and oil the grates well.
4. Place slices on the grill. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest slice from
   the short side, probe tip in the meat layer, keeping clear of fat seams and any skin.
5. Grill 4–5 minutes per side. Brush with reserved marinade in the final minute on
   each side for a sticky glaze.
6. **Pull at 73–76°C** (163–169°F). The marinade's sugar will be deeply caramelised
   and slightly charred at the edges — this is correct for Australian-style grilled belly.
7. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **73–76°C / 163–169°F** (well done).
   Serve with steamed rice, Asian slaw, or sweet potato.

### 3. Filipino tradition — Inihaw na Liempo (Grilled Pork Belly)
**Source**: Panlasang Pinoy (panlasangpinoy.com, Vanjo Merano) — the leading
Filipino recipe authority; technique consistent with traditional charcoal-grilled
*liempo* preparation across the Philippines  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+3 h marinade or overnight) · **Cook**: 14–18 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg pork belly (*liempo*), skin on, sliced 2–2.5 cm thick
- 4 tbsp soy sauce
- 4 tbsp calamansi juice or lime juice (calamansi is traditional; lime is the closest substitute)
- 1 tbsp fish sauce (*patis*)
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 6 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp black pepper
- Banana ketchup (for basting, the Filipino standard — not tomato ketchup)

**Method**
1. Mix soy sauce, calamansi juice, fish sauce, brown sugar, garlic, and pepper.
   Score the skin of each slice at 1 cm intervals. Marinate the belly slices for
   at least 3 hours; overnight is traditional for festive *liempo*.
2. Prepare charcoal grill. *Inihaw* (*iniihaw*) means "grilled over live fire" —
   charcoal is traditional, though gas grills are acceptable for everyday cooking.
   Allow charcoal to reach a red-hot glowing stage.
3. Remove slices from marinade, shaking off excess. Reserve marinade for basting.
4. Place skin-side down first on the grill. Insert the MEATER probe from the short
   side of the thickest slice into the meat layer, probe tip clear of the skin and
   clear of the bone (if bone-in is used).
5. Grill skin-side down 6–8 minutes. Baste with banana ketchup when flipped.
6. Flip. Baste generously. Grill flesh-side down 6–8 minutes more. Baste once more
   in the final 2 minutes.
7. **Pull at 75–78°C** (167–172°F). *Liempo* is always cooked until the fat is
   completely rendered and the skin has blistered and charred slightly — a lower
   temperature would leave the thick slices underrendered. The charring on the skin
   is considered part of the dish's character.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **75–78°C / 167–172°F** (well done).
   Serve with steamed white rice and spiced vinegar (*sukang maasim*) as a dipping sauce.

### 4. Steven Raichlen — Grilled Pork Belly Slices (*Planet Barbecue*)
**Source**: *Planet Barbecue!* (Workman Publishing, 2010); barbecuebible.com  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min (+4 h dry-rub rest) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g pork belly, skinless, sliced 1 cm thick
- 1 tbsp smoked salt
- 1 tsp brown sugar
- 1 tsp smoked paprika (*pimentón de la Vera*)
- ½ tsp garlic powder
- ½ tsp black pepper
- ½ tsp cayenne (optional)
- Finishing: 60 ml apple-cider glaze (equal parts apple cider vinegar and honey,
  reduced to a syrup)

**Method**
1. Mix dry rub ingredients. Apply evenly to both sides of each slice. Refrigerate
   uncovered at least 4 hours (overnight preferred) to let the rub dry-brine the
   surface and form a crust.
2. Set up a two-zone grill: high direct heat on one side, no-heat zone on the other.
3. Start the slices on high heat. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest slice
   from the short side, probe tip in the centre of the meat layer.
4. Grill on direct high heat 4–5 minutes per side, developing a char crust. If
   flare-ups occur (pork belly fat drips readily), move temporarily to the indirect zone.
5. In the final 2 minutes, brush the top of each slice with the apple-cider glaze.
   Flip and brush the second side. The glaze will caramelise quickly.
6. **Pull at 74–76°C** (165–169°F). The crust should be well-caramelised and
   slightly charred at the edges.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Final serving temperature: **74–76°C / 165–169°F** (well done).
   Slice or serve whole with coleslaw or pickled vegetables.

### 5. Vietnamese street-food tradition — Thịt Ba Chỉ Nướng (Grilled Pork Belly)
**Source**: Vietnam Coracle (tomcoracle.com, Tom Coracle); documented in Vietnamese
street-food literature; technique consistent with Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City
market-stall grilling tradition  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 700 g pork belly (*ba chỉ*, three-layer pork), skin on, sliced 1.5 cm thick
- 2 tbsp fish sauce (*nước mắm*)
- 1 tbsp lemongrass, finely minced
- 1 tbsp shallots, minced
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- ½ tsp black pepper
- To serve: vermicelli rice noodles, fresh herbs (mint, perilla, bean sprouts),
  *nước chấm* dipping sauce

**Method**
1. Combine fish sauce, lemongrass, shallots, sugar, sesame oil, and pepper. Score
   the skin of each slice at 1 cm intervals to allow the marinade to penetrate.
   Marinate refrigerated 2 hours minimum.
2. Prepare a charcoal grill (*bếp than*) or gas grill. Vietnamese street grilling
   uses small charcoal braziers; charcoal gives the characteristic smoky-sweet
   flavour of *thịt nướng*.
3. Remove slices from marinade. Grill on direct heat 4–5 minutes skin-side down first.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the short side of the thickest slice into the meat
   layer, probe tip clear of skin and fat seam.
5. Flip. Grill flesh-side down 4–5 minutes more. Baste once with remaining marinade
   in the final 2 minutes.
6. **Pull at 72–74°C** (162–165°F). Vietnamese grilled pork belly is sliced thin
   and eaten quickly, so a slightly lower final temperature than Filipino-style
   *liempo* is appropriate.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Final serving temperature: **72–74°C / 162–165°F** (well done).
   Slice thinly and serve over vermicelli with fresh herbs and *nước chấm*.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Final serving temp | Style |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Korean BBQ (*samgyeopsal*) | 72–75°C / 162–167°F | Thin slices, table grill |
| Australian BBQ | 73–76°C / 163–169°F | Marinated strips |
| Filipino (*inihaw na liempo*) | 75–78°C / 167–172°F | Thick slices, charcoal |
| American / Raichlen | 74–76°C / 165–169°F | Dry-rubbed, glazed |
| Vietnamese (*thịt ba chỉ nướng*) | 72–74°C / 162–165°F | Lemongrass-marinated |

The full observed range is **72–78°C** — a tight consensus. All traditions grill
pork belly until well done (no pink), because thin sliced belly requires the fat to
render visibly before the exterior is palatably crisp; this happens only at 70°C and
above. The Filipino tradition targets the high end of the range (75–78°C) because
thicker slices (2–2.5 cm) are used and full rendering of the thicker fat layers
requires slightly more time and heat. Korean tradition targets the low end (72–75°C)
for the thinnest slices (5–7 mm). All traditions map to `well_done` (71–84°C).

## What makes this method special
Grilling pork belly slices adds something neither pan frying nor oven roasting can
provide: live-fire char, smoke, and the flavour compounds produced by fat dripping
onto hot coals or grates and flaring back up onto the meat. The high, direct heat
and grill grates also produce distinct crosshatch marks that carry a concentrated
Maillard flavour. Korean *samgyeopsal* on a charcoal table grill is the world's
most recognisable form of this method — the act of cooking at the table, cutting
with scissors, and wrapping in leaves is a complete cultural ritual. Filipino
*inihaw na liempo* extends the method to thicker slices with a charred, deeply
caramelised skin. Across all traditions, the grill is chosen for its heat intensity,
smoke contribution, and char character — not for any unique temperature effect.

## Summary
Grilling pork belly in thin slices delivers char, smoke, and rendered fat in under
fifteen minutes — the fastest possible path to a deeply flavoured, fully rendered
pork belly. Korean *samgyeopsal* has made this the most iconic global form of the
cut, and Filipino *inihaw na liempo* extends it to thicker slices with spectacular
charred skin. The method requires no equipment beyond a hot grill; the challenge is
managing fat flare-ups. All traditions target the same well-done range (72–78°C),
because the whole point of the method is visible fat rendering and caramelised surface
texture, both of which require reaching that temperature.
