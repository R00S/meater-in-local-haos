## Cut profile
Pork belly is the uncured, skin-on slab from the underside of the pig, made up of
alternating thick layers of fat and lean muscle running parallel to the skin. Oven
roasting suits it uniquely: sustained low heat renders the thick fat layers and
dissolves the collagen between muscle sheets, while a high-temperature blast at the
end creates the crackling crust that defines the cut's most celebrated preparations.
No other method achieves both the silky-rendered interior and the shatteringly crisp
rind simultaneously. Carryover during the low-temperature phase is negligible (the
oven temperature is close to the target), but the final high-blast phase can add
2–3°C in the minutes after removal.

## Source recipes

### 1. Heston Blumenthal — Perfect Roast Pork Belly
**Source**: hestonfromhome.com; consistent with technique documented in *Heston at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011)  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight drying) · **Cook**: 6 h at 90°C + 30 min at 230°C · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on, bone-out
- 2 tbsp flaky sea salt (for rind)
- Freshly ground black pepper (for flesh side)

**Method**
1. Using a sharp knife or box cutter, score the rind in parallel lines 1 cm apart,
   cutting just through the rind and the first 2–3 mm of fat. Pat the belly
   completely dry with paper towels.
2. Rub flaky sea salt generously into the scored rind, pressing into the cuts. Place
   the belly skin-side up, uncovered, on a wire rack set over a tray. Refrigerate
   overnight (minimum 8 hours). The rind must be bone-dry before roasting — any
   retained moisture will steam and prevent crackling.
3. Remove the belly 1 hour before cooking. Preheat oven to 90°C/195°F.
4. Season the flesh underside with black pepper. Place belly skin-side up on a rack
   in a roasting tray.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end of the belly, angling it into the
   thickest flesh layer at the midpoint of the belly's length. Probe tip must be in
   the meat layer — not in fat. Keep the probe clear of the rind surface.
6. Roast at 90°C/195°F for 6 hours. The low temperature confit-renders the fat
   without heating the flesh beyond 85°C — the precise mechanism behind
   Blumenthal's "Rolls-Royce" texture claim.
7. When the probe reaches 80°C, increase oven temperature to 230°C/445°F. This is
   the crackling phase.
8. **Pull at 85–87°C** (185–189°F), typically 20–30 minutes into the blast phase.
   The rind should be puffed, blistered, and shatteringly crisp. If not fully
   crisped, extend the blast 5–10 minutes more.
9. Rest 20 minutes, rind uncovered — do not tent with foil; steam destroys
   crackling. Final serving temperature: **85–90°C / 185–195°F** (crispy / well done).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Slow-Roasted Pork Belly
**Source**: *Ad Hoc at Home* (Workman Publishing, 2009)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight brine) · **Cook**: 8 h at 80°C + 20 min at 220°C · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on, bone-out
- **Brine**: 2 litres water, 150 g / ½ cup sea salt, 100 g / ½ cup sugar, 2 bay
  leaves, 1 tbsp black peppercorns, 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- Kosher salt and black pepper (for seasoning before roasting)

**Method**
1. Dissolve salt and sugar in the water. Add bay leaves, peppercorns, and thyme.
   Cool brine completely. Submerge pork belly and refrigerate overnight (12–16 hours).
2. Remove belly from brine. Pat thoroughly dry with paper towels. Score the rind at
   1 cm intervals. Season the flesh side with salt and pepper.
3. Preheat oven to 80°C/175°F (fan-assisted if available). Place belly skin-side up
   on a rack in a roasting tray.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end of the belly into the thickest flesh
   section, probe tip clear of fat layers and rind.
5. Roast at 80°C/175°F for 8 hours. Keller's extremely low temperature produces a
   near-confit texture — the fat becomes silky and translucent rather than rendered
   in the conventional sense.
6. When the internal temperature reaches 72°C, increase oven to 220°C/425°F for
   the crackling phase.
7. **Pull at 74–78°C** (165–172°F). The high-blast phase is for the rind only — the
   flesh temperature is already at target. After 20 minutes at 220°C the rind should
   be golden and crispy.
8. Rest 15 minutes on a rack, rind uncovered. Final serving temperature:
   **74–79°C / 165–175°F** (well done). Slice thickly to reveal the rendered layers.

### 3. David Chang — Slow-Roasted Pork Belly (*Momofuku*)
**Source**: *Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009); consistent with Momofuku Ssäm Bar
pork belly service preparation  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight cure) · **Cook**: 6 h at 150°C · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg pork belly, skin-on
- 50 g / 3 tbsp sea salt
- 50 g / 3 tbsp granulated sugar

**Method**
1. Combine salt and sugar. Rub all surfaces of the pork belly — rind, flesh side,
   and cut sides. Place uncovered in a container, refrigerate overnight (minimum
   12 hours). The sugar-salt cure seasons and dries the surface simultaneously.
2. Preheat oven to 300°F/150°C. Remove belly from the refrigerator and pat dry;
   the cure will have partially dissolved into the meat surface.
3. Place skin-side up on a rack in a roasting tray. No additional seasoning needed —
   the overnight cure provides all salt and surface development.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the belly into the thickest flesh
   portion, probe tip midway into the meat, clear of fat seams and rind.
5. Roast uncovered at 150°C/300°F for approximately 6 hours. Unlike Blumenthal's
   and Keller's dual-temperature methods, Chang uses a single constant temperature
   with no blast phase. The result is a jammy, rendered texture with a glossy
   exterior rather than shatteringly crisp skin.
6. **Pull at 74–77°C** (165–171°F). The fat layers should be completely rendered
   and glossy; the belly should yield noticeably when pressed.
7. Rest 15 minutes. Final serving temperature: **74–79°C / 165–175°F** (well done).
   Slice and use for ssäm (*쌈*), steamed buns, or ramen.

### 4. Leila Lindholm — Ugnsrostad sidfläsk med krispig svål (Swedish Oven-Roasted Pork Belly with Crackling)
**Source**: leila.se/recept; consistent with arla.se and koket.se Swedish pork belly guides  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight drying) · **Cook**: 2 h at 160°C + 20 min at 240°C · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2–1.5 kg sidfläsk (*pork belly*, Swedish), skin-on
- 2 tbsp coarse salt (*grovt salt*)
- 1 tsp white pepper (*vitpeppar*)
- 1 tsp dried thyme or caraway seeds (*timjan eller kummin*) — optional; caraway
  is a traditional Swedish companion to pork
- 100 ml water (for the roasting tray, to prevent fat smoking)

**Method**
1. Score the rind at 5 mm intervals with a very sharp knife, cutting through the
   rind into the fat layer. Pat the belly completely dry with paper towels.
2. Mix salt, pepper, and optional seasoning. Rub generously into the scored rind,
   pressing into the cuts. Refrigerate uncovered for at least 1 hour to further
   dry the salted rind.
3. Preheat oven to 160°C/320°F. Place belly skin-side up on a rack in a roasting
   tin. Add 100 ml water to the tin below the rack to prevent fat from smoking.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end of the belly into the thickest flesh
   layer at the midpoint, probe tip clear of fat pockets and rind.
5. Roast at 160°C for approximately 2 hours. Swedish pork guidance targets 70–75°C
   at the flesh (*kärntemperatur*, core temperature).
6. When the internal temperature approaches 68°C, increase oven to 240°C/460°F.
   The 240°C blast is characteristic of the Swedish tradition — higher than most
   British approaches and correspondingly faster.
7. **Pull at 70–73°C** (158–163°F). After 15–20 minutes at 240°C, the rind should
   have puffed and crisped to crackling (*svålen*). If crackling is not fully set,
   hold a further 5–10 minutes.
8. Rest 20 minutes, rind uncovered. Final serving temperature: **70–75°C /
   158–167°F** (Swedish well done, *genomstekt*). Serve with pickled red cabbage
   (*rödkål*) and lingonberry jam (*lingonsylt*).

### 5. The Woks of Life (Leung family) — Cantonese Oven-Roasted Crispy Pork Belly (脆皮燒肉, Siu Yuk)
**Source**: thewoksoflife.com (Bill, Judy, Sarah, and Kaitlin Leung); consistent
with Cantonese roast-meat (*siu mei*, 燒味) tradition  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 30 min (+overnight dry-cure) · **Cook**: 30 min at 180°C + 30 min at 230°C · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg pork belly (*wu hua rou*, 五花肉), skin-on
- **Rind preparation**: 1 tbsp white rice vinegar + 1 tbsp coarse salt (applied
  to rind surface; brushed off before roasting)
- **Flesh marinade**: 1½ tsp fine salt, 1 tsp five-spice powder (*wǔ xiāng fěn*,
  五香粉), 1 tsp sugar, ½ tsp white pepper, 1 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine (*shaoxing
  jiǔ*, 紹興酒)

**Method**
1. Blanch the pork belly skin-side down in boiling water for 3–4 minutes. This
   opens the rind pores and helps the skin dry properly. Pat completely dry.
2. Score the rind at 5–6 mm intervals. Mix the flesh marinade. Rub it onto the
   flesh side and all cut sides — **do not allow any marinade to contact the rind**,
   as it will prevent uniform crackling.
3. Brush the rind with rice vinegar. Pat dry. Apply coarse salt evenly to the rind.
   Place the belly skin-side up, uncovered, on a rack in the refrigerator overnight.
   The salt draws out surface moisture; overnight drying is essential for
   Cantonese-style crackling (*zha pi*, 炸皮 — exploded skin).
4. Next day: brush away all residual salt from the rind. Pat the rind absolutely
   dry with paper towels. This step cannot be skipped.
5. Preheat oven to 180°C/355°F. Place belly skin-side up on a rack in a foil-lined
   roasting tin. Insert the MEATER probe from one short end into the thickest flesh
   section, probe tip clear of fat, rind, and marinade-penetrated surface tissue.
6. Roast at 180°C for 30 minutes to cook the meat through.
7. Increase oven to 230°C/445°F. Roast for a further 25–30 minutes until the rind
   blisters and crackles all across the surface.
8. **Pull at 72–75°C** (162–167°F). The rind should be deeply blistered and
   shatteringly crispy — the Cantonese standard for *siu yuk* is uniform rind
   eruption, not partial crispness.
9. Rest 10 minutes skin-side up. Final serving temperature: **72–77°C / 162–171°F**
   (well done). Slice each piece against the grain to show crackling, fat, and meat
   in every slice. Serve with steamed jasmine rice and hoisin dipping sauce.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Target flesh temp | Method approach |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| UK/Modernist (Blumenthal) | 85–90°C / 185–195°F | 6 h at 90°C + blast — confit texture |
| USA (Keller) | 74–79°C / 165–175°F | 8 h at 80°C + blast — near-confit |
| Korean-American (Chang) | 74–79°C / 165–175°F | 6 h at 150°C, no blast — jammy |
| Sweden (Lindholm) | 70–75°C / 158–167°F | 2 h at 160°C + 240°C blast |
| Cantonese (Woks of Life) | 72–77°C / 162–171°F | 30 min at 180°C + 230°C blast |

**Full observed range: 70–90°C at the flesh.** The lowest temperatures (70–75°C)
come from the Swedish and Cantonese traditions, where crackling quality is the
primary measure and the flesh target is simply "safely cooked through." The highest
end (85–90°C) is Blumenthal's confit approach, which deliberately drives collagen
and fat into near-complete breakdown. Chang's single-temperature method and Keller's
low-oven method both land in the 74–79°C band — fully rendered, not maximally
broken down. All traditions map to either `well_done` (74°C, the broadest consensus)
or `crispy` (85°C, Blumenthal's confit style). No tradition targets below well done
for oven-roasted pork belly.

## What makes this method special
Oven roasting is the only method that can simultaneously render pork belly's thick
fat layers AND create shatteringly crisp crackling, because the oven's dry heat
desiccates the rind while the interior steams in its own rendered fat. The
dual-temperature approach (low render, high blast) exploits this by separating the
two objectives into sequential phases — a physics trick no other cooking method
can replicate. Single-temperature methods (Chang at 150°C) sacrifice the crackling
to focus entirely on the jammy interior texture, which is a deliberate culinary
choice rather than a limitation.

## Summary
Oven roasting is the defining method for pork belly: it is the only approach that
can render the thick fat layers and produce shatteringly crisp crackling in the same
cook. Choose the low-and-slow approach (Keller, Chang) for silky, jammy belly
without crackling; choose dual-temperature (Blumenthal, Lindholm, Cantonese) for
full crackling with a well-rendered interior. Either way, a well-dried rind and
2–8 hours of patience are non-negotiable — the result cannot be achieved by any
other method.
