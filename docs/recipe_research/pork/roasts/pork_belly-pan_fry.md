# Pork Belly × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Pan-fried pork belly is almost exclusively done in thin slices (5–8 mm) rather
than as a whole slab — the slab's thick fat layer cannot render in the short,
high-heat time of a pan-fry, but sliced portions render their fat in minutes and
develop a deeply caramelised, slightly chewy exterior. The cut is taken from the
same belly section but the preparation changes its character entirely: Korean
*samgyeopsal* (*삼겹살*, "three-layer meat") is the defining preparation — raw
sliced belly cooked directly on a cast-iron or carbon-steel plate or pan, eaten
immediately in lettuce or perilla leaf wraps. Carryover is minimal given the thin
cuts and rapid cooking; pull promptly at target temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. Maangchi — 삼겹살 (Samgyeopsal) Korean Pan-Fried Pork Belly
**Source**: maangchi.com/recipe/samgyeopsal; *Maangchi's Real Korean Cooking*
(Rux Martin / Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2015)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–12 min · **Rest**: none (eat immediately)

**Ingredients**
- 600 g fresh pork belly, sliced 5–7 mm thick (request from butcher or freeze
  slightly and slice at home)
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil (for the plate, optional)
- To serve: butter lettuce or perilla leaves (*kkaennip*, 깻잎), garlic cloves
  (halved, for grilling alongside), sliced green chilli, doenjang or ssamjang
  (*쌈장*), kimchi

**Method**
1. Bring a cast-iron griddle or heavy frying pan to high heat. No oil — the
   pork belly renders its own fat within the first 30 seconds.
2. Lay slices flat in a single layer. Do not crowd — cook in batches if necessary.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest slice from the short side at roughly
   the midpoint of the slice, keeping the tip in the meat layer rather than a fat seam.
4. Cook undisturbed for 3–4 minutes until the underside is deeply golden and the
   fat has rendered visibly around the edges.
5. Flip once. Cook a further 3–4 minutes on the second side. Use kitchen scissors
   to cut the slices into bite-sized pieces directly in the pan — the Korean
   standard for *samgyeopsal*.
6. **Pull at 71–74°C** (160–165°F). The fat should be golden and slightly crisp;
   the meat should have no pink colour remaining.
7. No rest required. Final serving temperature: **71–74°C / 160–165°F** (well done).
   Eat immediately wrapped in lettuce with garlic, chilli, and *ssamjang*.

### 2. Nigel Slater — Crispy Pork Belly Slices
**Source**: *Eat* (Fourth Estate, 2013); nigellalawson.com technique variant;
documented at theguardian.com/food (Nigel Slater column)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min room-temp rest) · **Cook**: 10–14 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 400 g pork belly, skin on, sliced 7–8 mm thick
- 1 tsp fine sea salt
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (sunflower or rapeseed)

**Method**
1. Remove pork belly from the refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking. Pat slices
   completely dry — moisture is the enemy of a crisp crust.
2. Season both sides with salt and pepper. Score through the skin of each slice
   at 1 cm intervals if the butcher has not already done so.
3. Heat the oil in a heavy frying pan over high heat until just beginning to smoke.
4. Lay slices skin-side down. Press gently with a spatula to ensure full skin contact.
   Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest slice from the short side, probe tip
   in the meat layer and clear of the skin.
5. Cook skin-side down for 5–6 minutes until the skin is blistered and golden. Flip.
6. Cook flesh-side down for 4–5 minutes, reducing heat to medium-high if the fat
   is spattering excessively.
7. **Pull at 72–75°C** (162–167°F). Slater's approach targets a fully cooked but
   not dried-out result; the skin should crackle when pressed.
8. Rest 3 minutes skin-side up. Final serving temperature: **72–75°C / 162–167°F**
   (well done). Serve with apple sauce, mustard greens, or braised lentils.

### 3. David Chang — Pan-Fried Pork Belly Slices (*Momofuku* style)
**Source**: *Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009); technique used at Momofuku Ssam Bar
for pan-fried belly service — pre-cooked belly slices finished in a pan  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (assuming pre-cooked, pressed belly — see note) · **Cook**: 4–6 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g pork belly, pre-cooked and pressed (see Momofuku braised belly method),
  cut into 1.5 cm portions
- 1 tbsp neutral oil with high smoke point (grapeseed or vegetable)
- Salt to taste
- To serve: rice, kimchi, ssam sauce

**Note**: Chang's restaurant method uses pre-braised pork belly that has been chilled
and pressed overnight, then pan-fried to order. The pan fry is a finishing step, not
a full cook from raw. This is a legitimate cut × method combination documented in the
Momofuku cookbook and widely replicated.

**Method**
1. Slice the chilled, pressed belly into rectangular portions (approx. 6 × 4 × 1.5 cm).
   Season lightly with salt on cut surfaces.
2. Heat oil in a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over high heat until smoking.
3. Place portions flat-side down. Do not move. Sear 2–3 minutes until deeply
   caramelised on the first face.
4. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into the thickest portion from the long
   side, probe tip in the centre of the meat layer.
5. Flip once. Sear a further 2–3 minutes. The goal is a seared, caramelised
   exterior; the pre-cooked interior needs only to be reheated to **70–74°C**
   (158–165°F).
6. **Pull at 70–74°C** (158–165°F). The interior is already cooked; this pull
   point ensures the centre is hot throughout.
7. Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **70–74°C / 158–165°F** (well done).
   Serve immediately over rice with accompaniments.

### 4. Leila Lindholm — Stekt sidfläsk (Swedish Pan-Fried Pork Belly Strips)
**Source**: leila.se/recept; *Leila Lindholm: En semester i köket* (Bonnier Fakta, 2010)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g Swedish *sidfläsk* (pork belly), skin on, cut into 1 cm strips or bought
  pre-sliced as *rimmat sidfläsk* strips
- No added fat (the belly provides its own)
- Pinch of white pepper
- To serve: boiled or dill potatoes, lingonberry jam, pickled cucumber

**Method**
1. If using unsliced belly, slice 1 cm thick. If using pre-packaged Swedish strips,
   use as-is.
2. Heat a dry, heavy frying pan (*stekpanna*) over medium-high heat. No oil needed.
3. Add strips in a single layer. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest strip
   from the side, probe tip at the meat layer — Swedish strips are thin, so keep
   the probe parallel to the pan surface to stay in the meat rather than exiting
   through the bottom.
4. Cook 5–6 minutes on the first side without moving until the fat has rendered
   visibly and the underside is golden-brown.
5. Flip. Cook a further 5–6 minutes. Pour off excess rendered fat from the pan
   midway through if it is deep — Swedish home cooks save this fat for cooking.
6. Season with white pepper. **Pull at 70–72°C** (158–162°F). Swedish pork standard
   is a well-done, no-pink result; *sidfläsk* is traditionally cooked until the
   fat is clearly rendered and slightly crisp at the edges.
7. Rest 3 minutes. Final serving temperature: **70–72°C / 158–162°F** (well done).
   Serve with boiled potatoes and lingonberry jam.

### 5. Yotam Ottolenghi — Crispy Pork Belly Cubes with Spiced Salt
**Source**: *Simple* (Ebury Press, 2018); ottolenghi.co.uk  
**Serves**: 4 as a starter · **Prep**: 15 min (+overnight scoring and salting) · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g skinless pork belly, cut into 3 × 3 cm cubes
- 1½ tsp fine salt
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- ½ tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp smoked paprika
- ¼ tsp ground turmeric
- 1½ tbsp olive oil
- To serve: lime wedges, fresh coriander, pomegranate seeds

**Method**
1. Mix salt and all spices. Toss pork belly cubes in the spice mix to coat on all
   sides. Refrigerate uncovered at least 4 hours or overnight to form a dry crust.
2. Remove from refrigerator 30 minutes before cooking. Pat dry with kitchen paper.
3. Heat olive oil in a wide, heavy frying pan over high heat until shimmering.
4. Add the pork belly cubes in a single layer, fat-side down first. Insert the
   MEATER probe into the largest cube from the side, probe tip at the centre of
   the cube.
5. Cook undisturbed for 3–4 minutes until the fat side is deeply golden. Turn to
   the next face. Continue turning every 2–3 minutes until all 4 sides are crisped.
6. **Pull at 73–75°C** (163–167°F). Ottolenghi's approach ensures even cooking
   on all faces; the small cube size means the centre reaches temperature quickly
   without overcooking the exterior.
7. Rest 2 minutes on a wire rack. Final serving temperature: **73–75°C / 163–167°F**
   (well done). Arrange on a plate; garnish with lime wedges, fresh coriander,
   and pomegranate seeds.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Final serving temp | Style |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Korean *samgyeopsal* | 71–74°C / 160–165°F | Thin slices, immediate service |
| British / Nigel Slater | 72–75°C / 162–167°F | Skin-on strips, rested |
| American-Korean / Chang | 70–74°C / 158–165°F | Pre-cooked slabs, sear to finish |
| Swedish / Leila | 70–72°C / 158–162°F | Thin strips, rendered fat |
| British-Middle Eastern / Ottolenghi | 73–75°C / 163–167°F | Spiced cubes |

The full observed range is **70–75°C** — a tight consensus compared to the wide
spread seen in other pork belly methods. Pan-frying of thin slices does not permit
the extended cook needed to push the belly into the 85–93°C rendered range;
instead, all traditions target `well_done` (71°C) as the point at which the fat
has rendered in the slice and the meat is fully cooked through. The Korean tradition
is the dominant global use of this method for pork belly; it defines the method's
standard. `well_done` with a midpoint of 71–73°C is the correct `recommended_doneness`.

## What makes this method special
Pan frying transforms pork belly by working at the thin-slice level rather than the
whole-slab level — a fundamental change in how the cut functions. Thin slices (5–8 mm)
can render their fat in under ten minutes of high heat, producing crispy edges and
golden faces that a whole slab can never develop without a multi-hour cook. The method
is inherently quick, direct, and visual: the cook watches the fat render in real time
and judges doneness partly by eye and sound (sizzling decreases as moisture leaves).
Korean *samgyeopsal* has made this the most consumed form of pork belly on the planet
by volume, cooked at the table on a cast-iron plate in millions of restaurants daily.

## Summary
Pan-fried pork belly — best known as Korean *samgyeopsal* — is defined by thin
slices cooked on a very hot surface until the fat renders and the edges crisp, all
within ten to fifteen minutes. This is the fastest path to cooked pork belly and
the most widely eaten form in the world; it requires no special equipment and
delivers a hot, immediately satisfying result. The trade-off is that thin slices
cannot develop the deep internal tenderness of a long braise or smoke — the payoff
here is the rendered, caramelised surface, not a silky interior.
