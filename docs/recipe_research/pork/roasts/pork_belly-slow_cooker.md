# Pork Belly × Slow Cooker — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Pork belly in the slow cooker undergoes the same collagen-to-gelatin and
fat-rendering transformation as braising, but at a gentler, hands-off pace over
six to ten hours. The enclosed, moist environment of a slow cooker keeps the
temperature locked at 85–95°C on the Low setting, slowly dissolving the thick
connective tissue between the fat and muscle layers. No crackling is possible; the
payoff is extraordinary fork-tenderness and a concentrated, syrupy cooking liquid
that doubles as a sauce. Carryover is negligible — the slow cooker's gentle, uniform
heat means the internal temperature at the end of cooking equals the target temperature.

## Source recipes

### 1. Momofuku / David Chang tradition — Slow Cooker Soy-Braised Pork Belly
**Source**: Inspired by the Momofuku bossam and belly preparations documented in
*Momofuku* (Clarkson Potter, 2009); slow cooker adaptation documented at
seriouseats.com and bonappetit.com  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+8 h brine overnight) · **Cook**: 8 h on Low · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg skinless pork belly
- 120 ml soy sauce
- 60 ml mirin
- 60 ml sake or dry sherry
- 2 tbsp brown sugar
- 4 cloves garlic, smashed
- 1 thumb-sized piece fresh ginger, sliced
- 2 spring onions, roughly chopped
- 250 ml water or light chicken stock

**Method**
1. Bring a large pot of water to the boil. Blanch the pork belly 3 minutes, drain,
   and rinse under cold water. This removes surface impurities and firms the exterior
   slightly. Pat dry.
2. Combine soy sauce, mirin, sake, brown sugar, garlic, ginger, spring onions, and
   water in the slow cooker. Stir to dissolve the sugar.
3. Add the pork belly, fat-side up. The liquid should come about halfway up the
   sides of the belly. If more liquid is needed, add water.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the short end horizontally into the thickest meat
   layer, keeping the probe tip clear of the slow cooker wall and the fat cap.
5. Cook on Low for 7–8 hours. The belly is done when it is completely fork-tender
   and the fat layers yield when pressed.
6. **Pull at 88–90°C** (190–195°F). At this temperature the connective tissue
   between the fat and muscle has fully dissolved and the meat will separate into
   tender slices without any resistance.
7. Rest 15 minutes in the liquid before removing. Final serving temperature:
   **88–90°C / 190–195°F** (well done / rendered). Slice or shred; strain and
   reduce cooking liquid to a glaze for serving.

### 2. Nigella Lawson — Slow Cooker Sticky Pork Belly
**Source**: nigella.com (Nigella.com recipe collection); documented technique for
slow cooker pork belly with Asian-inflected glaze  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 6–8 h on Low + 20 min oven finish · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg skin-on pork belly, cut into thick strips approx. 5 cm wide
- 3 tbsp hoisin sauce
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp rice vinegar
- 1 tbsp honey
- 2 tsp Chinese five-spice powder
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 125 ml water

**Method**
1. Mix hoisin, soy, rice vinegar, honey, five-spice, garlic, and ginger together.
   Reserve 2 tablespoons of this mixture for glazing at the end.
2. Toss the pork belly strips in the marinade in the slow cooker. Add water.
   Arrange strips in a single layer, skin-side up.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest strip from the short end, penetrating
   the meat layer and stopping clear of the skin. Ensure the probe fits inside the
   slow cooker lid.
4. Cook on Low for 6–8 hours until the fat is completely rendered and the meat pulls
   apart easily with two forks.
5. **Pull at 85–88°C** (185–190°F). Transfer strips skin-side up to a foil-lined
   baking tray.
6. Brush generously with the reserved marinade. Place under a hot grill (230°C /
   450°F) for 10–15 minutes to caramelise and crisp the surface. Watch closely —
   the sugar burns fast. Rest 10 minutes.
7. Final serving temperature: **85–88°C / 185–190°F** (well done). The oven finish
   does not significantly raise internal temperature; it affects only the surface.

### 3. Korean tradition — 수육 (Suyuk) Slow-Simmered Pork Belly
**Source**: Maangchi (maangchi.com); documented in Korean home cooking tradition;
also covered in *Korean Bapsang* (Hyosun Ro, koreanbapsang.com)  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 h on Low · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg skin-on pork belly
- 1 litre water
- 1 onion, halved
- 8 cloves garlic, whole
- 1 thumb fresh ginger, sliced
- 2 tbsp doenjang (*된장*, Korean fermented soybean paste — absorbs pork odour)
- 1 tbsp whole black peppercorns
- 2 dried chilli peppers
- 2 spring onions, whole

**Method**
1. Rinse the belly under cold water. No browning — *suyuk* (*수육*) is a clean-tasting
   dish; searing would add unwanted Maillard bitterness.
2. Place the belly fat-side up in the slow cooker. Add water, onion, garlic, ginger,
   doenjang, peppercorns, dried chillies, and spring onions. The liquid should
   mostly cover the belly.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the short end into the thickest meat layer.
4. Cook on Low for 8–10 hours. *Suyuk* requires a longer, slower cook than Western
   braised pork belly — the Korean tradition prizes a very clean, silky texture
   with almost no residual chewiness.
5. **Pull at 88–92°C** (190–198°F). The belly is ready when a chopstick passes
   through the meat layers with no resistance.
6. Rest 20 minutes in the cooking liquid before removing. Final serving temperature:
   **88–92°C / 190–198°F** (well done). Slice 5–7 mm thick across the grain.
   Serve with ssam jang (*쌈장*) dipping sauce and kimchi.

### 4. Italian tradition — Pancetta cotta in pentola lenta (Slow Cooker Cooked Pancetta)
**Source**: GialloZafferano (giallozafferano.it), Italy's leading recipe publication;
technique consistent with regional Umbrian and Tuscan preparations for fresh belly  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 6–7 h on Low · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg fresh pork belly (*pancetta fresca*), skin on
- 200 ml dry white wine (*vino bianco secco*)
- 200 ml light vegetable or chicken broth
- 1 sprig fresh rosemary
- 4 sage leaves
- 3 cloves garlic, whole
- 1 tsp fennel seeds, lightly crushed
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**
1. Score the skin in 1 cm crosshatch cuts. Season the flesh side generously with
   salt, pepper, and fennel seeds.
2. Place the belly skin-side up in the slow cooker. Add wine, broth, rosemary,
   sage, and garlic. The herbs and aromatics should surround, not cover, the skin.
3. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest meat layer from the short side,
   angled to avoid the skin and the fat cap. Keep the probe within the lean
   muscle layer.
4. Cook on Low for 6–7 hours. The skin will soften and the fat will render into
   the cooking liquid, creating a gelatinous broth.
5. **Pull at 85–88°C** (185–190°F). Italian tradition for cooked fresh belly
   targets a firm-but-yielding texture — not fully pull-apart.
6. Rest 20 minutes before slicing. Reduce the cooking liquid in a small pan until
   syrupy and pour over the sliced belly. Final serving temperature:
   **85–88°C / 185–190°F** (well done).

### 5. American Southern tradition — Low-and-Slow Glazed Pork Belly
**Source**: Southern Living magazine (southernliving.com); technique consistent with
Appalachian and Deep South pork belly preparations  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight brine) · **Cook**: 8 h on Low · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.5 kg skinless pork belly
- For brine: 1 litre water, 60 g salt, 30 g brown sugar, 1 tbsp black pepper,
  1 tsp red pepper flakes, 3 bay leaves
- For glaze: 80 ml apple cider vinegar, 60 ml honey, 2 tbsp Worcestershire sauce,
  1 tsp smoked paprika, pinch of cayenne
- 250 ml apple cider (for the slow cooker liquid)

**Method**
1. Bring brine ingredients to the boil, stir to dissolve, and cool completely.
   Submerge the belly in the brine and refrigerate overnight (8–12 hours).
2. Remove belly, rinse, and pat dry. Place in slow cooker. Add apple cider.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the short end into the thickest lean meat layer,
   avoiding any fat seams that run through the slab.
4. Cook on Low for 8 hours. At the 6-hour mark, mix the glaze ingredients.
5. At 7 hours, spoon 2 tablespoons of glaze over the top of the belly.
   Repeat once more at 7.5 hours.
6. **Pull at 88–90°C** (190–195°F). The meat will be extremely tender and the glaze
   should have caramelised on the exposed surface.
7. Rest 20 minutes. Final serving temperature: **88–90°C / 190–195°F** (well done).
   Slice thickly (2 cm) and serve with the reduced cooking juices.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Final serving temp | Texture goal |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Japanese / Momofuku-style | 88–90°C / 190–195°F | Fork-tender, slice or shred |
| UK / Nigella | 85–88°C / 185–190°F | Tender strips, oven finish |
| Korean *suyuk* | 88–92°C / 190–198°F | Silky, clean-tasting slices |
| Italian | 85–88°C / 185–190°F | Firm-yielding, sliceable |
| American Southern | 88–90°C / 190–195°F | Glazed, fork-tender |

The full observed range is **85–92°C**. All traditions target the `well_done` band
(71–84°C) or the `crispy` / rendered band (85–95°C). No tradition slow-cooks pork
belly to a lower temperature — the long cook time is the point, not an option to
accelerate. The Korean *suyuk* tradition targets the high end (88–92°C) for a
deliberately silky, fully dissolved connective tissue; Western traditions land in the
middle of the range. `well_done` is the closest `cooking_data.py` key, though the
practical target sits near the boundary with `crispy`. `well_done` is appropriate
for `recommended_doneness` for this method.

## What makes this method special
The slow cooker delivers braising conditions — moist heat, gentle temperature, long
time — with near-zero supervision. For pork belly the long, low cook (85–95°C for
six to ten hours) is ideal: it gives the thick fat layers and connective tissue time
to fully dissolve without the risk of the liquid boiling dry or the exterior
overcoking. Unlike stovetop or oven braising, the slow cooker's sealed environment
concentrates the cooking liquid into a natural glaze by the end of the cook, and the
thermal mass of the appliance makes stall behaviour during the long cook almost
imperceptible. The one thing slow cooking cannot do is create crackling — for that,
an oven blast or grill finish is required after the slow cook.

## Summary
The slow cooker is an ideal vessel for pork belly: the fat-rich, collagen-heavy cut
needs exactly the long, gentle heat this appliance provides, and the hands-off nature
of the method means a six-to-ten-hour cook requires no attention. The result is
silky, deeply flavoured, fork-tender belly with no risk of overcooking — though
crackling is not possible without a short finishing step in a hot oven or under a
grill.
