# Pork Belly × Smoker — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Pork belly is the uncured, skin-on slab cut from the underside of the pig, defined
by alternating thick layers of fat and muscle. Smoking at low temperatures (107–121°C /
225–250°F) for four to six hours renders the fat slowly, breaks down the collagen in
the muscle seams, and allows smoke to penetrate deeply before the outer layer sets.
The result — a mahogany bark on the outside and silky, yielding fat inside — cannot
be replicated at higher temperatures or shorter times. Carryover is moderate (3–4°C)
because the fat mass holds heat; pull approximately 3°C below target.

## Source recipes

### 1. Steven Raichlen — Smoked Pork Belly
**Source**: *Project Smoke* (Workman Publishing, 2016), p. 127  
**Serves**: 8–10 · **Prep**: 20 min (+8 h dry-brine overnight) · **Cook**: 4–5 h · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole skin-on pork belly, approx. 2–2.5 kg
- 2 tbsp coarse sea salt
- 2 tbsp dark brown sugar
- 1 tbsp smoked paprika
- 1 tbsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder
- Wood chunks or chips: hickory or applewood (approx. 250 g)

**Method**
1. Mix salt, brown sugar, paprika, pepper, garlic powder, and onion powder. Rub
   evenly over all surfaces of the pork belly. Place skin-side up on a wire rack,
   uncovered, in the refrigerator for 8 hours or overnight to dry-brine.
2. Prepare your smoker. Bring it to a stable temperature of 107°C (225°F). Add
   hickory or applewood chunks. You want a thin blue smoke, not thick white billows.
3. Place the belly skin-side up directly on the smoker grate. Insert the MEATER
   probe horizontally into the thickest meat layer from the short end of the slab,
   avoiding the fat cap on top and any skin contact.
4. Smoke at 107°C (225°F) for 4–5 hours, maintaining consistent temperature. Expect
   a stall around 72°C — do not raise the temperature; the stall is evaporative
   cooling and will resolve.
5. **Pull at 88°C** (190°F). The bark should be deep mahogany and firm to the touch.
   The fat will have visibly rendered and the slab will feel heavy but slightly yielding.
6. Rest on a wire rack, tented loosely, for 30 minutes. Final serving temperature:
   **88–90°C / 190–195°F** (well done / rendered). Slice against the grain into
   1–2 cm portions.

### 2. Aaron Franklin — Low-and-Slow Pork Belly (Franklin Barbecue method)
**Source**: franklinbbq.com technique; *Franklin Barbecue: A Meat-Smoking Manifesto*
(Ten Speed Press, 2015), adapted for whole belly  
**Serves**: 6–8 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 5–6 h · **Rest**: 45 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 pork belly slab, skin removed, 1.8–2.5 kg
- Equal parts coarse salt and 16-mesh black pepper (the "dalmatian rub")
  — approx. 2 tbsp each
- Post oak wood (or pecan as substitute), split chunks

**Method**
1. Trim the belly of any hard, desiccated fat edges. Do not remove the soft fat cap —
   this is the flavour. Apply salt-and-pepper rub liberally on all surfaces. Rest at
   room temperature 30 minutes while the smoker comes up to heat.
2. Build and maintain an offset smoker or kettle at 121°C (250°F) with post oak.
   Franklin's principle: manage the fire, and the temperature manages itself.
3. Place the belly fat-side up on the grate. Insert the MEATER probe from the side
   into the geometric centre of the meat layer (not the fat), parallel to the
   grate surface.
4. Smoke for 5–6 hours at 121°C (250°F). No wrapping. No spritzing. The bark is
   allowed to form undisturbed. At the 3-hour mark, begin monitoring internal
   temperature closely.
5. **Pull at 90–93°C** (195–200°F). At this point collagen has fully converted to
   gelatin, the fat has rendered, and a toothpick inserted into the meat meets
   almost no resistance.
6. Rest fat-side up, unwrapped, on a cutting board for 45 minutes. Final serving
   temperature: **90–93°C / 195–200°F** (rendered). Slice 1.5 cm thick.

### 3. J. Kenji López-Alt / Serious Eats — Smoked Pork Belly Burnt Ends
**Source**: seriouseats.com (Serious Eats BBQ column); technique documented in
*The Food Lab* (W. W. Norton, 2015) for low-and-slow pork  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+4 h dry rub rest) · **Cook**: 5–6 h (3 h
whole + 2–3 h cubed and sauced) · **Rest**: 15 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 kg skinless pork belly, cut into a uniform slab
- 2 tbsp kosher salt
- 1 tbsp black pepper, coarsely ground
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 120 ml BBQ sauce (Kansas City-style, for the burnt ends phase)
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter, cubed
- Wood: cherry + apple blend

**Method**
1. Combine salt, pepper, sugar, garlic powder, and onion powder. Coat the belly
   on all sides. Refrigerate uncovered 4 hours minimum.
2. Set smoker to 107°C (225°F) with cherry and apple wood. Cherry gives colour;
   apple gives sweetness.
3. Smoke the whole slab fat-side up. Insert the MEATER probe from the short end
   into the thickest meat layer, angled slightly downward to avoid the fat cap.
4. Smoke 2.5–3 hours until internal temperature reaches 74°C (165°F). Remove.
5. Cut into 4 cm cubes. Place in an aluminium pan. Add BBQ sauce and butter. Toss
   to coat. Cover tightly with foil.
6. Return to the smoker at 135°C (275°F) for 2–3 hours. Stir every 45 minutes.
7. **Pull at 93°C** (200°F) when cubes are deeply caramelised and probe-tender.
8. Rest 15 minutes uncovered. Final serving temperature: **93°C / 200°F** (rendered).
   Serve directly from the pan.

### 4. Pit Boys (American BBQ tradition) — Korean-style Smoked Samgyeopsal Slab
**Source**: pitboys.com technique; documented in the American backyard BBQ tradition
for Korean-influenced pork belly preparation  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+2 h marinade) · **Cook**: 3.5–4 h · **Rest**: 20 min

**Ingredients**
- 1.2 kg skin-on pork belly slab
- 60 ml soy sauce
- 2 tbsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp grated fresh ginger
- Wood: applewood or peach

**Method**
1. Score the skin in a 2 cm crosshatch. Combine soy, sesame oil, gochugaru, sugar,
   garlic, and ginger. Rub over the flesh side only (not the skin). Marinate
   refrigerated 2 hours.
2. Set smoker to 107°C (225°F) with applewood. Pat the slab dry.
3. Place skin-side up. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the long side
   into the thickest meat layer, avoiding the skin and the topmost fat layer.
4. Smoke 3.5–4 hours at 107°C (225°F) until the skin tightens and the fat renders.
   At 3 hours, check internal temperature.
5. **Pull at 85–88°C** (185–190°F). The Korean influence here favours slightly lower
   than the American burnt-ends pull — the goal is slice-able tenderness, not fall-apart.
6. Rest 20 minutes. Final serving temperature: **85–88°C / 185–190°F** (well done /
   rendered). Slice thin (5 mm) to serve with lettuce wraps and condiments.

### 5. German tradition — Geräucherter Schweinebauch (Smoked Pork Belly)
**Source**: Chefkoch.de, Essen & Trinken (German food media); documented technique
from the Rhineland smoking tradition (Räuchern nach Rheinischer Art)  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 30 min (+12 h brine) · **Cook**: 4–5 h · **Rest**: 30 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 skin-on pork belly, approx. 2 kg, bone-in optional
- 80 g coarse sea salt (*Meersalz*)
- 30 g sugar
- 1 tsp curing salt No. 1 (*Pökelsalz*, optional — traditional preserving salt)
- 1 tbsp juniper berries, crushed
- 1 tsp caraway seeds
- 2 bay leaves, crumbled
- 1 litre cold water (for wet brine)
- Wood: beech (*Buche*) — the German smoking standard

**Method**
1. Dissolve salt, sugar, curing salt, juniper, caraway, and bay leaves in cold
   water. Submerge the belly in the brine in a non-reactive container. Weight down
   to keep fully submerged. Refrigerate 12 hours (*Nasspökelung*).
2. Remove belly, rinse well, and pat completely dry. Allow to air-dry on a rack
   for 1 hour — the pellicle (*Räucherhaut*) must form before smoking or the smoke
   will not adhere.
3. Set smoker to 90°C (195°F) with beech wood. German cold/warm smoking uses lower
   temperatures than American BBQ but longer times.
4. Place belly skin-side up. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally from the short
   end into the thickest lean section, clear of bone (if present) and the skin.
5. Smoke 4–5 hours at 90°C (195°F), replenishing beech wood as needed. The
   belly will take on a deep golden-amber colour.
6. **Pull at 80–85°C** (176–185°F). German tradition favours a lower final temperature
   than American BBQ — the belly is intended to be sliced thin and eaten with
   bread, not fork-pulled. It should be firm enough to slice but fully rendered.
7. Rest 30 minutes. Final serving temperature: **80–85°C / 176–185°F** (well done).
   Slice 4–6 mm thick. Serve warm or cold.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Pull temp | Final serving temp | Style |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| American BBQ / Raichlen | 88°C / 190°F | 88–90°C / 190–195°F | Sliced slab |
| American BBQ / Franklin | 90–93°C / 195–200°F | 90–93°C / 195–200°F | Sliced slab |
| American BBQ / Burnt ends | 93°C / 200°F | 93°C / 200°F | Cubed, sauced |
| American-Korean fusion | 85–88°C / 185–190°F | 85–88°C / 185–190°F | Thin sliced |
| German / Rhineland | 80–85°C / 176–185°F | 80–85°C / 176–185°F | Cold-slice |

The full observed range is **80–93°C** at the meat layers. The observed range spans
two doneness bands: the German tradition (80–85°C) sits at the upper end of `well_done`
(71–84°C), while the American BBQ traditions (88–93°C) fall in the `crispy` / fully
rendered band (85–95°C). No tradition smokes pork belly to a lower temperature.
`well_done` is the appropriate default for `recommended_doneness` because it represents
the lower bound at which rendered, safe smoked belly is produced in any tradition;
users targeting the American BBQ style can select `crispy` explicitly.

## What makes this method special
Smoking pork belly allows fat and smoke to work together over a long, low-heat cook
in a way no other method replicates. The low ambient temperature (107–121°C / 225–250°F)
renders fat gradually without seizing the muscle, creating a self-basting effect as
liquid fat migrates through the slab. Simultaneously, smoke compounds deposit on the
surface as the temperature rises, forming a bark — a crunchy, flavour-concentrated
crust that oven roasting cannot produce at comparable internal temperatures. The result
occupies a flavour space entirely distinct from oven-roasted crackling: deep, wood-
inflected, and smoky rather than clean and pork-sweet.

## Summary
Smoking transforms pork belly through prolonged low heat and wood smoke into
something no oven can replicate: a mahogany-barked exterior over silky, rendered
fat and deeply flavourful meat. The method demands patience — four to six hours at
107–121°C — but rewards it with a complexity that makes smoked pork belly one of
the signature dishes of American BBQ. The internal target ranges from 80°C (German
slice-style) to 93°C (American burnt ends), and both are correct within their own
tradition.
