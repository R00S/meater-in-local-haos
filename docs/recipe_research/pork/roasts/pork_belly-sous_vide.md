# Pork Belly × Sous Vide — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Pork belly is perhaps the single best cut for sous vide because its two structural
challenges — rendering the thick fat layers and breaking down the collagen between
muscle seams — are precisely what prolonged, precise low-temperature cooking solves
best. The sealed bag prevents evaporative moisture loss, keeps the belly in its
own rendered fat throughout the cook, and allows the cook to choose between two
very different textural outcomes: 68°C for 36 hours produces a silky, custard-like
fat with firm but yielding meat; 80°C for 8–12 hours produces a more rendered,
pull-apart result closer to a braise. Carryover after sous vide is negligible —
the core and surface temperatures equalise during the long bath — but a hot sear
after the bath can add 3–4°C briefly.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt / Serious Eats — Sous Vide Pork Belly
**Source**: seriouseats.com/sous-vide-pork-belly-recipe  
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+1 h seasoning) · **Cook**: 36 h at 68°C · **Rest**: 10 min (ice bath cool-down if storing)

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg skinless pork belly, cut into two equal pieces
- 1½ tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp freshly ground black pepper
- Optional finish: 1 tbsp vegetable oil for searing

**Method**
1. Season the pork belly pieces on all surfaces with salt and pepper. Place each
   piece in its own vacuum bag (or use a high-quality zip-lock bag, water-displacement
   method). Seal.
2. Set the sous vide circulator to 68°C (154°F). Submerge the bags. Ensure they
   are fully weighed down — pork belly contains enough fat to make bags float.
3. Cook for 36 hours. The extended time at this precise temperature fully renders
   the fat while keeping the meat extremely moist. **Note on probe use:** a
   temperature probe cannot be inserted into a sealed sous vide bag during the
   cook. Trust the circulator's temperature display; the bath temperature is the
   internal temperature after sufficient time. On removal from the bath, a probe
   inserted into the centre of the thickest piece will confirm the core has reached
   the target 68°C before serving if verification is desired.
4. If serving with a sear: remove from the bag, pat completely dry, and sear in
   a ripping-hot oiled pan for 60–90 seconds per side. This is a surface-only
   step; the internal temperature should remain at **68°C** (154°F).
5. **Pull at 68°C** (154°F) — this IS the final serving temperature for the
   sous vide portion. The sear does not raise the core.
6. Rest 10 minutes. Final serving temperature: **68°C / 154°F** (well done
   for pork, silky texture). Slice in 1.5 cm portions.

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sous Vide Pork Belly (*Under Pressure*)
**Source**: *Under Pressure: Cooking Sous Vide* (Artisan Books, 2008), p. 130  
**Serves**: 8 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 8 h at 82°C · **Rest**: 20 min (chill) + 5 min (pan sear to finish)

**Ingredients**
- 1 skin-on pork belly, approx. 1.8 kg, skin scored
- 12 g kosher salt
- 4 g white pepper
- 2 tbsp fresh thyme leaves
- 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, chopped
- Vacuum seal bags (commercial chamber sealer recommended by Keller)

**Method**
1. Score the skin in a 2 cm crosshatch pattern. Season the belly on all sides
   with salt and white pepper. Distribute thyme and parsley evenly over the flesh.
2. Vacuum seal with a chamber sealer. If using a home zipper bag, ensure all air
   is removed — trapped air causes uneven cooking at higher temperatures.
3. Set circulator to 82°C (180°F). Submerge and cook for 8 hours. Keller's
   higher temperature targets a result closer to a braise — fully rendered fat
   and very soft meat suitable for pressing and chilling overnight, then slicing
   and pan-searing for service.
4. After 8 hours: remove from the bath. The circulator maintained 82°C (180°F)
   throughout; a probe inserted into the centre on removal should confirm **82°C**
   (180°F). The belly should feel uniformly soft and yield to finger pressure.
5. For Keller's restaurant finish: cool in an ice bath 1 hour, refrigerate
   overnight under weights to press into a uniform block. Slice into portions.
   Sear in a very hot pan 2 minutes per side before serving.
6. **Pull temperature (sous vide bath)**: **82°C** (180°F). Final serving
   temperature after sear: **80–82°C / 176–180°F** (well done / rendered).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Low-Temperature Pork Belly (*Heston at Home*)
**Source**: *Heston at Home* (Bloomsbury, 2011); hestonfromhome.com  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 20 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 24 h at 64°C · **Rest**: sear 2 min each side

**Ingredients**
- 1 pork belly slab, 1.5 kg, skin on, skin scored deeply
- 2 tbsp fine sea salt
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp white pepper
- 2 sprigs thyme
- 1 clove garlic, thinly sliced

**Method**
1. Rub the flesh side with salt, sugar, and pepper. Press thyme and garlic onto
   the flesh. Place skin-side up and refrigerate overnight uncovered to dry-brine
   the skin (critical for crackling finish).
2. Vacuum seal the belly skin-side up so the skin remains flat. Use a chamber
   sealer; the fat renders into liquid during cooking and the sealed environment
   keeps it in contact with the meat.
3. Set circulator to 64°C (147°F). Submerge and cook for 24 hours. Blumenthal's
   lower temperature (versus Keller's 82°C) produces a much silkier, almost
   molten fat texture — the result he describes as "the Rolls-Royce of pork belly".
4. After the bath: remove the belly skin-side up. Pat the skin completely dry.
   Confirm core temperature at **64°C** (147°F) by inserting a probe.
5. Place under a grill preheated to maximum (or use a kitchen blowtorch) until
   the skin blisters and crackles — approximately 10–15 minutes under the grill.
   The fat beneath will not significantly change temperature during this short blast.
6. **Pull at 64°C** (147°F) from the bath. Final serving temperature after
   crackling finish: **64–68°C / 147–155°F** (USDA safe for pork; well done
   in texture). Slice at the table.

### 4. ChefSteps / Modernist tradition — Two-Stage Sous Vide Pork Belly
**Source**: chefsteps.com sous vide pork belly protocol; technique consistent with
Modernist Cuisine (Nathan Myhrvold, Cooking Lab, 2011) pork belly approach  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 12 h at 80°C · **Rest**: ice bath + sear

**Ingredients**
- 800 g skinless pork belly, one piece
- 1 tsp smoked salt
- ½ tsp black pepper
- ½ tsp garlic powder
- Finishing: 1 tbsp neutral oil, 1 tsp honey (for a glazed sear)

**Method**
1. Season the belly on all sides. Vacuum seal with no additional liquid.
2. Set circulator to 80°C (176°F). Submerge and cook 12 hours. At this
   temperature the collagen dissolves rapidly and the fat renders fully within
   12 hours — no need for the 36-hour extended cook required at lower temperatures.
3. Remove bag. Plunge into an ice bath for 15 minutes to firm the belly for
   slicing. This optional step (common in restaurant mise-en-place) allows the
   belly to be portioned cleanly and stored.
4. When ready to serve, dry the belly portions completely. Mix honey with a drop
   of water. Heat oil in a heavy pan until smoking.
5. Sear the portions 90 seconds per side until deeply caramelised. Brush with
   honey glaze in the final 30 seconds.
6. **Pull temperature from the bath**: **80°C** (176°F). Post-sear internal
   temperature rises negligibly (1–2°C) given the short sear time.
7. Final serving temperature: **80°C / 176°F** (well done / fully rendered).

### 5. Japanese tradition — 低温調理豚バラ (Low-Temperature Sous Vide Pork Belly)
**Source**: Ryuichi Sakamoto culinary documentation at cookpad.com (Japan's largest
recipe platform); technique consistent with Japanese precision cooking tradition
(*teitonchousei*, 低温調理)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min (+6 h marinade) · **Cook**: 24 h at 70°C · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g skinless pork belly (*buta no bara*, 豚のバラ)
- 2 tbsp soy sauce (*shoyu*)
- 1 tbsp mirin (*mirin*)
- 1 tbsp sake
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 clove garlic, grated
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated

**Method**
1. Mix soy, mirin, sake, sesame oil, garlic, and ginger. Marinate the pork belly
   for at least 6 hours refrigerated.
2. Vacuum seal the belly with the marinade. Set circulator to 70°C (158°F).
   Japanese home practitioners typically use 70°C rather than Western 68°C to
   stay clearly within food-safety convention for pork.
3. Cook 24 hours. The marinade infuses throughout the long bath, producing a
   lightly glazed, very tender result.
4. Remove from the bag. Reserve the bag liquid (it is rich with rendered fat and
   concentrated marinade) and reduce it in a small pan as a serving sauce.
5. Insert probe to confirm **70°C** (158°F) at the core. Pat dry.
6. **Pull at 70°C** (158°F). Optional: sear briefly in a dry, very hot pan,
   30 seconds per side, to add colour without altering the texture.
7. Final serving temperature: **70°C / 158°F** (well done). Slice 1 cm thick.
   Serve with the reduced sauce and steamed rice.

## Temperature consensus

| Tradition | Bath temperature | Bath duration | Final serving temp | Texture goal |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------|-------------------|--------------|
| American / Serious Eats (Kenji) | 68°C / 154°F | 36 h | 68°C / 154°F | Silky-custard fat, very moist meat |
| French / Thomas Keller | 82°C / 180°F | 8 h | 80–82°C / 176–180°F | Braise-like, firm, pressable |
| British / Blumenthal | 64°C / 147°F | 24 h | 64–68°C / 147–155°F | "Molten" fat, crackling finish |
| Modernist / ChefSteps | 80°C / 176°F | 12 h | 80°C / 176°F | Fully rendered, restaurant-portionable |
| Japanese | 70°C / 158°F | 24 h | 70°C / 158°F | Tender, glaze-infused slices |

The full observed range is **64–82°C** — by far the widest range of any pork belly
cooking method. This reflects a genuine textural choice: 64–68°C produces a silky,
barely-set fat (almost molten) that many find revelatory and some find unpleasant;
80–82°C produces a texture indistinguishable from a long braise. The lower temperatures
map to `well_done` (71°C minimum by USDA for pork, though Blumenthal's 64°C is a
choice some cooks make at their own discretion). The higher temperatures sit in the
`crispy` / fully rendered band. For `recommended_doneness`, `well_done` (71°C)
is the appropriate default — it is the USDA safe minimum for pork and the lowest temperature at which all five source traditions overlap as clearly safe.
Note that the lower-temperature approaches (64–68°C) require the cook to explicitly
accept sub-USDA temperatures as a personal decision.

## What makes this method special
Sous vide is the only method that gives the cook precise control over the fat texture
in pork belly. At 64–68°C the fat layers turn almost custard-like — a texture
impossible to achieve by any other means. At 80–82°C the method reproduces braise
conditions without the risk of the liquid boiling or the meat drying. The sealed
environment keeps all the rendered fat in contact with the meat throughout the cook,
self-basting continuously. The trade-off: no bark, no crackling. Sous vide pork belly
always requires a post-bath sear or grill blast to add surface texture and colour,
and those finishing steps are surface treatments only — they do not change the
internal result.

## Summary
Sous vide gives the cook something no other method can: the ability to choose the
exact texture of the fat in pork belly, from silky and barely set at 64–68°C to
fully braise-like at 80–82°C. The trade-off is that the sealed bath cannot produce
crackling or bark — a hot sear or grill blast is always needed after the bath for
surface texture. For cooks willing to invest the time (8–36 hours), sous vide pork
belly is technically the most controllable and consistent approach of any method.
