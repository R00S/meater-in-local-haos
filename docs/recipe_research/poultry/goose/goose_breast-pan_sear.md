# Goose Breast × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Goose breast (*Anser anser domesticus*) is a large, dense, deeply-coloured red-meat breast — closer in character to duck breast than to chicken or turkey, but darker, leaner in intramuscular fat and with a thicker fat-and-skin layer over the magret. Properly scored, the skin renders down at moderate heat to leave a crisp, deep-mahogany crust over a wine-coloured magret with a clear pink centre. Pan-searing is the classical European preparation: skin-side down in a cold pan, brought up gently to render the fat over 8–12 minutes, then flipped and finished on the meat side. Like duck, goose breast is best served at a frank rosé internal temperature (54–58 °C) for tenderness; pulling above 62 °C produces livery, dry meat. Carryover is significant — 4–6 °C — given the dense meat and fat layer.

## Source recipes

### 1. Hugh Fearnley-Whittingstall — Pan-Roasted Wild Goose Breast (British River Cottage tradition)
**Source**: Hugh Fearnley-Whittingstall, *The River Cottage Meat Book* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2004); rivercottage.net  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 goose-breast fillets, 200 g each, skin on, silver skin removed
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 6 sprigs fresh thyme
- 4 garlic cloves, lightly crushed
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter, to baste
- 60 ml (¼ cup) ruby port, to deglaze
- 5 g chopped fresh thyme leaves, to finish

**Method**
1. Score the skin in a 1 cm crosshatch through the fat to (but not into) the meat. Pat very dry. Season generously with salt and pepper.
2. Place the breasts skin-side-down in a cold heavy stainless or cast-iron pan. Set over low-medium heat.
3. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into one breast through the side, parallel to the pan, with the tip at the geometric centre of the muscle.
4. Render the fat 8–10 minutes — the skin should turn deep mahogany and crisp; pour off accumulated fat once or twice (reserve for roast potatoes).
5. Flip the breasts skin-side-up. Add thyme, garlic and butter; cook 3–4 minutes more, basting the meat side.
6. **Pull at 54–57 °C (130–135 °F).** Fearnley-Whittingstall's standard for goose magret is "rosé through" — between rare and medium-rare. Carryover takes the centre to 58–61 °C.
7. Add port to the pan; let it bubble and reduce to a glaze. Pour over the rested breasts.
8. Rest skin-side-up 8 minutes loosely tented. Slice 5 mm thick on the diagonal. Final serving temperature: **58–61 °C / 136–142 °F** (rosé centre, crisp-skinned, port-glazed).

### 2. Magnus Nilsson — Stekt Gåsbröst med Lingon (Nordic / Swedish tradition)
**Source**: Magnus Nilsson, *The Nordic Cookbook* (Phaidon, 2015)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 goose-breast fillets, 200 g each, skin on, scored
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt (Falksalt)
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground white pepper
- 1 g (¼ tsp) ground kryddpeppar (allspice)
- 30 g cultured unsalted butter
- 4 sprigs fresh thyme
- 80 g (½ cup) raw stirred lingonberries (rårörda lingon)
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) cider vinegar, to deglaze
- 15 g chopped fresh dill, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat breasts very dry. Season with salt, white pepper and allspice.
2. Place skin-side-down in a cold heavy pan. Set over low-medium heat.
3. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into one breast.
4. Render fat 9–11 minutes until deep mahogany.
5. Flip; add butter and thyme; cook 3 minutes basting.
6. **Pull at 54–56 °C (130–133 °F).** Nilsson targets a cool-rosé centre — "blank rosa" — that he says is mandatory for goose; any further cooking turns it livery.
7. Remove breasts. Add vinegar to the pan; reduce 30 seconds. Pour over the breasts.
8. Rest skin-side-up 10 minutes loosely tented. Slice 5 mm thick on the diagonal. Top each portion with a spoon of lingonberries and dill. Final serving temperature: **58–61 °C / 136–142 °F** (cool-rosé, allspice-perfumed, sweet-tart counterpoint).

### 3. Anthony Bourdain — Magret d'Oie au Poivre (French bistro tradition)
**Source**: Anthony Bourdain & José de Meirelles, *Les Halles Cookbook* (Bloomsbury, 2004) — the goose variant of his canonical *magret de canard au poivre*  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 goose-breast fillets, 200 g each, skin on, scored
- 5 g (1 tsp) sea salt
- 30 g (2 tbsp) coarsely-cracked black peppercorns, pressed into the meat side
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 60 ml (¼ cup) Cognac
- 120 ml (½ cup) veal demi-glace or rich brown chicken stock
- 60 ml (¼ cup) double cream
- 5 g chopped fresh chervil, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat breasts dry. Season meat side with salt and the cracked pepper, pressing to adhere.
2. Place skin-side-down in a cold heavy pan. Set over low-medium heat.
3. Insert MEATER probe horizontally into one breast.
4. Render fat 9–11 minutes until deep mahogany; flip; add butter; cook 3 minutes basting.
5. **Pull at 54–57 °C (130–135 °F).** Bourdain's "*saignant à rosé*" — bleeding to rosé.
6. Remove breasts. Pour off all but 15 ml of fat. Off heat add Cognac (away from the flame); return to heat to flame off. Add demi-glace; reduce by half. Off heat swirl in cream.
7. Rest breasts skin-side-up 8 minutes loosely tented. Slice 5 mm thick on the diagonal. Spoon sauce; sprinkle chervil. Final serving temperature: **58–61 °C / 136–142 °F** (peppery, rosé, cream-sauced).

### 4. Diana Henry — Pan-Seared Goose Breast with Quince & Star Anise (British modern tradition)
**Source**: Diana Henry, *Crazy Water, Pickled Lemons* (Mitchell Beazley, 2002) and her *Telegraph* recipe column at telegraph.co.uk/food-and-drink — the autumn goose section  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 14 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 goose-breast fillets, 200 g each, skin on, scored
- 5 g (1 tsp) flaked sea salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 1 small ripe quince, peeled, cored and cut in 6 wedges
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 2 star anise pods
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 60 ml (¼ cup) Pedro Ximénez sherry
- 120 ml (½ cup) chicken stock
- 5 g chopped fresh flat-leaf parsley, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat breasts dry. Season with salt and pepper.
2. Place skin-side-down in a cold heavy pan. Set over low-medium heat. Render 9–10 minutes.
3. Insert MEATER probe horizontally into one breast. Flip; add butter, quince wedges, star anise and cinnamon. Cook 3–4 minutes, basting.
4. **Pull at 54–57 °C (130–135 °F).** Henry: rose-pink through the slice.
5. Remove breasts and quince. Pour off excess fat; add sherry and stock to the pan; reduce by half (3 minutes). Discard whole spices.
6. Rest breasts skin-side-up 8 minutes loosely tented. Slice 5 mm thick on the diagonal. Spoon sauce and the quince wedges; sprinkle parsley. Final serving temperature: **58–61 °C / 136–142 °F** (sweet-spiced, fruit-warmed, rosé centre).
