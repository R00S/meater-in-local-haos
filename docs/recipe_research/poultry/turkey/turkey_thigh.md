# Turkey Thigh — Cut Overview

## Cut profile

The turkey thigh is the upper-leg dark-meat section of the turkey, running from the hip to the knee joint (the biceps femoris and associated muscles). It is sold bone-in skin-on or boneless skinless. Compared to turkey breast, the thigh has significantly higher fat content (≈ 5–8 % vs ≈ 1–2 % for breast) and more connective tissue (collagen-rich). This makes the thigh forgiving — it stays moist at a wider temperature range and benefits from cooking above the USDA minimum so collagen converts to gelatin, improving texture and mouthfeel.

The thigh is larger and denser than the drumstick (≈ 600–900 g bone-in per thigh), with estimated thickness around 60 mm at the thickest point. It requires longer cooking than the breast at the same oven temperature.

USDA minimum safe temperature is **74 °C (165 °F)** for all poultry. Most culinary authorities recommend pulling turkey thigh at **77–82 °C (170–180 °F)** for optimal texture — this allows full collagen conversion and eliminates any pink tinge from myoglobin. Carryover during rest is approximately 3–5 °C.

Turkey thigh is primarily cooked by oven roasting in Western culinary traditions, especially around Thanksgiving and Christmas. It appears as a holiday substitute for whole turkey in smaller households. Culinary research found almost no dedicated turkey thigh recipes outside North America — the cut is typically cooked as part of a whole bird rather than as a standalone product in most global traditions.

Target internal temperature: **165 °F (74 °C)** minimum — **170–175 °F (77–79 °C)** preferred for dark meat
