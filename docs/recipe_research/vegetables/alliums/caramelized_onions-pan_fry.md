# Caramelized Onions × Pan Fry — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Caramelised onions via pan-frying refers to the style in which sliced or halved onions are cooked in generous hot fat over medium-to-high heat, with deliberate browning as the primary goal, contrasted with the slow-sauté method. At 170–190 °C pan temperature, the natural sugars (roughly 5 % of the onion's weight) undergo both caramelisation and Maillard reactions with the amino acids, producing deeply browned, almost crispy-edged onions with intense sweetness and bitterness in balance. This technique appears in South Asian birista (fried garnish), Alsatian Zwiebelkuchen bases, French *soubise* shortcuts, and Japanese-style glazed onion sides. A MEATER probe inserted horizontally into a thick half-onion near the centre reads the internal transition from crisp-dense (~60 °C) to fully soft (85–92 °C). Carryover is small but the browned rings crisp up further as they cool on a wire rack.

## Styckesprofil

Karameliserade lökar via pannstekning avser stilen där skivad eller halvad lök tillagas i rikligt hett fett vid medel till hög värme, med medveten bryning som primärt mål. Löken mjuknar och sötar sig under 15–25 minuter. Sötman från Maillard-reaktion och sockerkaramellisering skapar djupa, komplexa smaker. Innertemperatur: inte relevant — stek tills djupt guldenbrun och mjuk.

## Source recipes

### 1. Madhur Jaffrey — Birista / Fried Onions (Indian / South Asian tradition)
**Source**: Madhur Jaffrey, *Madhur Jaffrey's Indian Cookery* (BBC Books, 1982)
**Serves**: 4–6 (as a garnish) · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: drain on paper 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 medium onions (≈ 500 g), peeled and thinly sliced into rings
- Oil for shallow- or semi-deep-frying (sunflower or neutral) — enough to cover rings halfway in a wide pan, about 200–250 ml
- Fine sea salt to taste
- Optional: 1 g (¼ tsp) ground turmeric stirred into the oil before frying (for a golden colour)

**Method**
1. Separate the onion rings; pat very dry with paper towels. Any surface moisture will cause violent spattering and prevents browning. If the rings have high moisture, spread on a tray and allow to air-dry 30 minutes.
2. Heat the oil in a wide, heavy frying pan over medium-high heat until shimmering (≈ 175–180 °C).
3. Add the onion rings in a single, if piled, layer. They will initially steam and spit. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into one of the thicker half-rings near the centre of the pan so the tip sits in the densest part of the ring stack.
4. Fry, stirring and turning every 2 minutes, for 12–15 minutes, allowing the rings to go through pale gold → deep amber → rich brown. Jaffrey specifies "deep, mahogany-brown" as the target — pale birista is a failure; darkened-to-bitter is also wrong.
5. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** At this internal temperature, the rings are deeply browned and on the edge of becoming crispy as they cool. They will continue to darken slightly off the heat — pull when 2 shades lighter than desired final colour.
6. Scoop out with a slotted spoon; drain on a paper-lined wire rack. Spread them apart so they crisp individually. Season lightly with salt. Final serving temperature: **room temperature** (crispy-sweet, deeply flavourful — use as a garnish for biryani, raita or curry).

### 2. Shizuo Tsuji — Negi no Itame-yaki (Japanese pan-fried spring onions with soy and mirin)
**Source**: Shizuo Tsuji, *Japanese Cooking: A Simple Art* (Kodansha International, 1980)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g Japanese leek-onions (negi) or large spring onions with thick white stems, cut into 5 cm lengths
- 2 tbsp neutral oil (or sesame oil for a richer result)
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce (usukuchi-shoyu)
- 1 tbsp mirin
- 1 tsp caster sugar
- 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds, to finish

**Method**
1. Cut the negi (leek-onions) into uniform 5 cm pieces. Tsuji notes the negi must be bone-dry before going into the pan.
2. Heat a heavy sauté pan or skillet over medium-high heat. Add oil; heat until shimmering.
3. Place the negi pieces cut-side-down in a single layer. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally through one of the thicker pieces near the centre, with the tip at the geometric centre of the piece.
4. Pan-fry undisturbed 3–4 minutes until the cut face is deeply golden-brown and slightly charred at the edges.
5. Turn the pieces; pan-fry the other cut face for 2–3 minutes. Add soy sauce, mirin and sugar; toss to glaze. Cook a further 1–2 minutes until the glaze caramelises.
6. **Pull at 82–86 °C (180–187 °F).** Tsuji targets negi that are caramelised at the faces but still slightly firm in the centre — the contrast of the charred exterior and sweet, semi-firm interior is characteristic of this style.
7. Scatter sesame seeds. Final serving temperature: **82–86 °C / 180–187 °F** (sweet-savoury, sesame-glossed).

### 3. Francis Mallmann — Cebollas a la Plancha (Argentine / South American tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann with Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan Books, 2009)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 4 large white or yellow onions (≈ 800 g total), peeled and sliced into 1.5 cm rounds (not separated into rings — Mallmann keeps the round intact)
- 3 tbsp neutral oil
- Coarse sea salt
- 2 tbsp red-wine vinegar, to finish
- 15 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Heat a large cast-iron plancha or heavy griddle pan over medium-high heat until very hot (5–6 minutes). Brush with oil.
2. Lay the whole onion rounds on the plancha. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally through the side of one of the larger rounds into its centre.
3. Pan-fry undisturbed 6–7 minutes until the bottom face is deeply charred in spots and rich amber between the grill marks. Do not press down.
4. Using a wide spatula, carefully flip each round. Brush the caramelised face with a little more oil; sprinkle with coarse sea salt. Continue on the second face 6–7 minutes.
5. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** Mallmann wants rounds that are "charred black at the surface, sweet and yielding all the way to the centre."
6. Transfer to a platter; drizzle with red-wine vinegar; scatter parsley. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (charred-sweet, tangy, Argentine character).

### 4. Marcella Hazan — Cipolle all'Aceto (Italian pan-fried onions with vinegar)
**Source**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1992)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 800 g medium onions, peeled, halved, and each half cut into 3 mm wedges
- 40 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 1 tsp caster sugar
- 3 tbsp good-quality red-wine vinegar
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Place the butter and oil in a wide, heavy frying pan over medium heat. When the butter melts and foams subside, add the onion wedges. Toss to coat.
2. Insert the MEATER probe into one of the thicker wedge clusters near the centre of the pan.
3. Cook over medium heat, stirring occasionally, for 15 minutes until the onions are golden and beginning to brown.
4. Add the sugar; stir. Increase heat to medium-high; cook 3–4 minutes, stirring frequently, until the onions are deeply golden-brown and caramelised.
5. Add the vinegar; stir vigorously for 1 minute as it evaporates and glazes the onions. Season with salt and pepper.
6. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** Hazan describes this as *cipollata* style — onions that are deeply caramelised, sweet-sour from the vinegar, and lightly glazed. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (sweet-sour, butter-glossed).
