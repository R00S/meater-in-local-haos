# Caramelized Onions × Sauté — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
True caramelised onions — not the pale, slightly golden version described in hurried
recipes, but the deep mahogany, jammy, intensely sweet result of 40–60 minutes of patient
heat — represent one of the most profound transformations in cooking. The onion goes from
harsh and pungent to sweet and complex through a combination of Maillard browning and
sugar caramelisation; the water content drops from ~90% to something much less; and the
weight of a full pan of sliced onions reduces to a fraction of the original. The internal
temperature of fully caramelised onions, measured at the centre of the thickest individual
strand, is around 90–93°C. The colour and flavour, not the temperature, are the primary
doneness indicators.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Caramelized Onions (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/caramelized-onions
- **Method**: Thinly sliced onions; wide, heavy-bottomed pan; medium heat with butter and
  a pinch of salt. Cook 40–60 min, stirring every 3–5 min. Add 1 tbsp balsamic in
  last 5 min for depth. Internal ~92°C; mahogany colour.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**92°C** (fully broken down)
- **Doneness cues**: Deep mahogany throughout; no visible liquid remaining; jammy and sticky;
  massively reduced in volume; very sweet with no raw onion taste.
- **Why this method works**: Patience at medium heat allows both Maillard browning
  (protein-sugar reactions) and sugar caramelisation to develop without burning;
  the balsamic addition in the final minutes adds both colour depth and acidity.

### 2. Thomas Keller — Oignons Caramélisés (France/USA / Bouchon)
- **Source**: thomaskeller.com (French onion soup preparation — onion technique)
- **Method**: Julienned onions; clarified butter; medium-low heat; stir every 5–7 min
  for 60–75 min. Never add liquid (let the natural juices drive the process).
  Internal ~93°C; deep amber-brown.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**93°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Uniform mahogany; jammy; very sweet; massively reduced.
- **Why this method works**: Keller's prohibition on adding liquid forces the onions
  to cook in their own moisture; adding liquid (wine, water) would cause them to stew
  rather than caramelise and would produce a different, less concentrated flavour.

### 3. Delia Smith — Caramelized Onions (UK)
- **Source**: deliaonline.com/recipes/caramelized-onions
- **Method**: Thick-sliced onions; butter over very low heat; 45–60 min. Allow them
  to soften, collapse, and then begin to colour. Internal ~90°C; deep golden amber.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Soft, collapsed, and deep golden; all moisture evaporated.
- **Why this method works**: Delia's very low heat approach takes longer but is the
  most forgiving for home cooks — the slower pace gives more time between stirs
  without risk of burning.

### 4. ICA — Karamelliserade lökar (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/karamelliserade-lokar
- **Method**: Skivade lökar; smör på låg-medelvärme; 40–60 min med regelbunden omrörning.
  ~90–93°C inuti; mahognyfärgade och söta.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90–93°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Djupt bruna; inga råa fläckar; söta och jamiga.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides present caramelised onions as a
  long-project recipe; the 40–60 min time range is consistent across Nordic recipes
  and reflects the real time needed for true caramelisation.

### 5. Nigella Lawson — Slowly Caramelized Onions (UK / Nigella Express)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/caramelized-onions
- **Method**: Sliced; butter and small amount of olive oil over medium-low; 30–40 min
  for a good (if not perfect) caramelisation. Add a pinch of sugar at 20 min to
  accelerate. Internal ~90°C; golden-amber.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden-amber throughout; sweet and yielding; significantly reduced.
- **Why this method works**: Nigella's sugar addition is a shortcut that accelerates
  the colour development in the second half of cooking; it produces a good result
  in 30–40 min that would otherwise require 50–60 min without the added sugar.
