## Cut profile
Sautéed onions represent the threshold between raw pungency and sweet softness —
and the technique of sautéing spans a wide range from "softened and translucent" (5 min)
to "deeply golden and beginning to caramelise" (20–25 min). The MEATER probe in a pan
of sautéed onions measures the average temperature of the onion mass, which at the
"golden and beginning to caramelise" stage reads approximately 90°C at the centre
of the largest onion piece. Below 79°C the centres are still raw-tasting and the
pungency is not fully broken down.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — How to Caramelise Onions (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/caramelized-onions
- **Method**: Slice 6 large onions; medium-high heat; 2 tbsp butter + 1 tbsp oil.
  Cook stirring every 2–3 min; first 5 min browning, then reduce heat to medium
  and continue 20–30 min until deep amber throughout. Internal ~90°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C** (fully soft and golden)
- **Doneness cues**: Deep amber throughout (not just the edges); all moisture evaporated;
  jammy and concentrated; the pan has fond that is not burning.
- **Why this method works**: Kenji's high-start then lower-medium approach accelerates
  the early moisture evaporation before the longer slow caramelisation phase; starting
  high prevents a gummy, steamed texture from slow-starting.

### 2. Thomas Keller — Sautéed Onions for Onion Soup (USA / Bouchon)
- **Source**: thomaskeller.com (French onion soup recipe — onion caramelisation technique)
- **Method**: Sliced onions in butter over medium heat; very slow caramelisation 45–60 min;
  stirring every 5 min only. Deep amber result. Internal ~93°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**93°C** (fully jammy)
- **Doneness cues**: Mahogany-coloured; no resistance; all structural integrity dissolved
  into a sweet, jammy consistency.
- **Why this method works**: Keller's patience-dependent technique creates a depth of
  caramelisation impossible at higher heat; the slow Maillard reaction at medium heat
  builds complex flavour layers that 20-minute methods cannot replicate.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Softened Sautéed Onions (UK)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/sauteed-onions
- **Method**: Medium heat; olive oil; sliced or diced onions 8–10 min until soft,
  translucent, and just beginning to colour at edges. Internal ~82–85°C.
- **Target internal temp**: **82–85°C** (softened, not caramelised)
- **Doneness cues**: Translucent with golden edges; fully yielding; sweet-smelling.
- **Why this method works**: This is Nigella's base preparation for many dishes; the
  shorter cook targets softness without full caramelisation, which suits dishes where
  the onion is a background element rather than a feature.

### 4. ICA — Stekta lökar i panna (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/stekta-lokar
- **Method**: Skivade lökar; smör och olja på medelvärme 10–20 min beroende på
  önskat resultat. 82–90°C inuti; från genomskinliga till gyllenbruna.
- **Target internal temp**: **82–90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Genomskinliga till gyllenbruna; mjuka; söta.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipes acknowledge the spectrum from softened
  to caramelised; 82°C for softened, 90°C for golden — the cook's intention determines
  the endpoint.

### 5. Delia Smith — Slowly Sautéed Onions (UK)
- **Source**: deliaonline.com/recipes/sauteed-onions
- **Method**: Thick-sliced or wedged; butter over low-medium heat; 25–30 min slow sauté.
  Internal ~88°C; fully golden and tender throughout.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**88°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Uniform golden colour; edges slightly darker; completely yielding.
- **Why this method works**: Delia's low-medium temperature achieves a slow, even
  caramelisation without burning; the long time produces sweetness without the
  bitterness that high-heat browning can create.
