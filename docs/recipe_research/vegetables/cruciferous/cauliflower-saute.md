# Cauliflower × Saute — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Cauliflower (*Brassica oleracea* var. *botrytis*) is a tightly clustered head of immature flower buds (curd) attached to a thick fibrous stem. The curd is roughly 92 % water, with delicate cell walls and a very small amount of free sugar — when sautéed in a hot, fat-coated pan, the surface dehydrates quickly and the residual sugars caramelise into a brown, nutty crust while the interior softens but retains some structural integrity. Sauteing differs from roasting in that contact-area browning is concentrated on whichever face touches the pan: success depends on cutting florets with a flat side and not crowding the pan. Carryover is small but real because the dense curd traps heat in its centre after removal — pulling 1–2 °C below the target preserves a touch of bite.

## Source recipes

### 1. Yotam Ottolenghi — Sautéed Cauliflower with Tahini (Middle Eastern / Israeli tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi & Sami Tamimi, *Jerusalem* (Ebury Press, 2012); ottolenghi.co.uk/recipes  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 10–12 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 medium cauliflower (≈ 700 g), broken into 3 cm florets with one flat cut face
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) extra-virgin olive oil
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 60 g (¼ cup) tahini paste
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) fresh lemon juice
- 1 small garlic clove, finely grated
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) cold water (to loosen tahini)
- 15 g (2 tbsp) toasted pine nuts, to finish
- 5 g chopped flat-leaf parsley, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat the florets completely dry with a clean tea towel. Surface moisture is the single biggest cause of steaming instead of browning.
2. Whisk tahini, lemon juice, grated garlic and a pinch of salt with the cold water until smooth and pourable. Set aside.
3. Heat the olive oil in a wide heavy sauté pan over medium-high heat until shimmering.
4. Lay florets flat-side-down in a single uncrowded layer. Do not stir for the first 3–4 minutes — the contact face must brown.
5. Insert the MEATER probe horizontally into the central core of the largest floret, pushing through the stem so the tip sits at the geometric centre of that piece. Avoid the loose flower buds at the surface.
6. Flip florets, season with salt and pepper, and continue to sauté 5–7 minutes more, tossing every minute, until evenly browned on multiple faces.
7. **Pull at 82–85 °C (180–185 °F).** At this temperature the curd is cooked through but holds its bite — Ottolenghi's signature texture for this dish.
8. Transfer to a serving plate, drape with the tahini sauce, scatter pine nuts and parsley. Final serving temperature: **82–85 °C / 180–185 °F** (tender-crisp, deeply browned faces).

### 2. Marcella Hazan — Cavolfiore Saltato all'Aglio (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Knopf, 1992), pp. 545–546  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+ 5 min blanching) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 head cauliflower (≈ 700 g), cut into small florets
- 3 g (½ tsp) salt for the blanching water (large pot)
- 60 ml (¼ cup) extra-virgin olive oil
- 4 garlic cloves, peeled and lightly crushed
- 2 g (½ tsp) crushed dried red chilli (peperoncino)
- 5 g (1 tsp) salt to finish
- 15 g chopped flat-leaf parsley
- Freshly ground black pepper to finish

**Method**
1. Bring a large pot of salted water to a rolling boil. Drop in the florets and cook 4–5 minutes until just barely tender at the stem (a paring knife meets light resistance). Drain in a colander; spread on a tray to dry.
2. Heat the olive oil in a wide pan over medium heat. Add crushed garlic and chilli; cook 90 seconds until garlic is pale gold and aromatic. Do not let it darken — Hazan stresses pale gold for this preparation.
3. Increase heat to medium-high. Add the drained florets, cut faces down. Cook 2 minutes undisturbed.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the stem of one of the larger florets, central in the pan, with the tip at the geometric centre.
5. Toss and continue sauteing 3–5 minutes, until florets are evenly browned on at least two faces.
6. **Pull at 78–82 °C (172–180 °F).** Hazan targets a softer Italian *al dente* — the curd should be supple but the stem still has a hint of resistance.
7. Off the heat, season with salt, pepper and parsley. Toss once. Final serving temperature: **78–82 °C / 172–180 °F** (soft-tender, garlic-and-chilli infused).

### 3. Madhur Jaffrey — Phulgobi ki Bhaji (North Indian sautéed cauliflower)
**Source**: Madhur Jaffrey, *Madhur Jaffrey's Indian Cookery* (BBC Books, 1982), pp. 92–93  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 12–15 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 medium cauliflower (≈ 700 g), broken into small florets (3 cm)
- 45 ml (3 tbsp) vegetable oil or mustard oil
- 1 g (¼ tsp) whole cumin seeds
- 1 g (¼ tsp) whole brown mustard seeds
- 1 small dried red chilli, broken
- 2 g (½ tsp) ground turmeric
- 1 g (¼ tsp) ground cayenne or Kashmiri chilli
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine sea salt
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) water
- 1 g (¼ tsp) garam masala, to finish
- 5 g chopped fresh coriander, to finish

**Method**
1. Pat the florets dry. Heat the oil in a wide karahi or heavy sauté pan over medium-high heat until shimmering.
2. Add the mustard seeds; when they pop (10–15 s), add cumin and broken chilli. Sizzle 5 seconds.
3. Add turmeric and cayenne — they will darken instantly in the hot oil. Immediately add the cauliflower florets and salt. Toss to coat.
4. Reduce heat to medium. Insert the MEATER probe into the stem of a central floret, tip at its geometric centre.
5. Sauté 8–10 minutes, tossing every 90 seconds, until florets are flecked with brown and evenly tender. Sprinkle the water in if any sticking begins.
6. **Pull at 80–85 °C (176–185 °F).** Jaffrey targets cauliflower that is fully cooked but with intact floret shape — *bhaji*-style, not mushy.
7. Off the heat, dust with garam masala and coriander. Final serving temperature: **80–85 °C / 176–185 °F** (warm-spiced, soft-tender).

### 4. Julia Child — Chou-Fleur Étuvé au Beurre (French tradition)
**Source**: Julia Child, Louisette Bertholle & Simone Beck, *Mastering the Art of French Cooking, Vol. I* (Knopf, 1961), pp. 459–460  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+ 5 min blanching) · **Cook**: 7–9 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 head cauliflower (≈ 700 g), broken into florets and steamed or blanched 4 min until just tender
- 40 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) neutral oil (to raise the smoke point of the butter)
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 2 g (½ tsp) freshly ground white pepper
- 5 g chopped flat-leaf parsley
- A squeeze of lemon juice, to finish

**Method**
1. Drain the blanched florets thoroughly and pat dry with a tea towel.
2. Melt the butter with the oil in a wide heavy frying pan over medium heat until the butter foams and the foam subsides to a pale-gold *beurre noisette* hint.
3. Lay the florets in the pan in a single layer, cut face down. Sauté undisturbed 3–4 minutes until pale gold.
4. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of a representative floret. Toss, then sauté another 3–4 minutes until florets are caramelised on multiple faces.
5. **Pull at 80–82 °C (176–180 °F).** Child's French technique — the *étuvée* (slow-stewed) approach yields tender florets bathed in browned butter; she explicitly warns against pushing temperatures higher and turning the curd to mush.
6. Off the heat, add salt, white pepper, parsley and a final squeeze of lemon. Final serving temperature: **80–82 °C / 176–180 °F** (silky, butter-coated).
