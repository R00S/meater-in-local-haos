# Asparagus × Sauté — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Sautéing asparagus in a hot pan is the quick weeknight method — 4–6 minutes from pan
to plate — and when done correctly it produces a result close to oven roasting: golden
contact patches, tender spears, and concentrated flavour. The key difference is control:
a pan sauté is done in smaller batches with constant attention, while oven roasting
can handle 500 g of asparagus unattended. Internal temperature at doneness for sautéed
asparagus is around 77–79°C — slightly lower than oven-roasted because the shorter
cook time leaves the interior with a little more moisture and a fresher character.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Sautéed Asparagus (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/sauteed-asparagus
- **Method**: Large skillet over high heat; thin film of oil. Add asparagus in single
  layer; cook 2 min without moving; toss; cook 2–3 min more. Season and serve.
  Internal ~77°C at thickest base.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**77°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden contact patches on some spears; bright vivid green overall;
  bends without snapping; base has very slight resistance.
- **Why this method works**: High heat creates contact-patch caramelisation on the
  spears resting against the pan while the raised spears steam in the ambient pan heat;
  the combination gives both Maillard flavour and a fresh-green character.

### 2. Jacques Pépin — Sautéed Asparagus au Beurre (France)
- **Source**: jacquespepin.net (asparagus sauté technique)
- **Method**: Blanch 2 min; shock; dry. Sauté in clarified butter over medium-high
  heat 3–4 min, rolling the spears to caramelise all sides. Finish with lemon juice.
  Internal ~77–79°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**77–79°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Uniform golden colour all around each spear; tender throughout.
- **Why this method works**: Pépin's rolling technique develops colour on all sides of
  the round spear rather than just one face; the blanch step ensures the interior is
  already cooked when the sauté begins.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Pan-Fried Asparagus (UK)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/pan-fried-asparagus
- **Method**: Large pan; oil and butter; medium-high heat. Asparagus in single layer
  2 min per side. Season; finish with lemon zest. Internal ~77°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**77°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden patches; bright green; bends without snapping.
- **Why this method works**: Nigella's butter-oil combination adds flavour and extends
  the smoke point; the two-side method (not constant rolling) is simpler for home
  cooks and gives good caramelisation on two faces.

### 4. ICA — Stekt sparris i panna (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/stekt-sparris
- **Method**: Stor panna på hög värme; olja. Sparrisen i ett lager 3–4 min totalt,
  vänd en gång. ~77°C i tjockaste basen.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**77°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Guldbrun kontaktyta; levande grön; böjer sig mjukt.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipes present pan sautéing as the everyday
  asparagus method; the 3–4 min total time suits the Nordic thinner asparagus varieties
  that are common in Swedish supermarkets during the season.

### 5. BBC Good Food — Pan-Fried Asparagus with Lemon and Parmesan (UK)
- **Source**: bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/pan-fried-asparagus-lemon-parmesan
- **Method**: Frying pan over high heat; olive oil; asparagus in single layer 2–3 min
  per side. Remove from heat; toss with lemon juice and parmesan. Internal ~77°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**77°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden patches on both sides; vivid green; slightly tender throughout.
- **Why this method works**: Adding parmesan off the heat prevents it from clumping
  or burning; the residual heat of the pan melts it onto the asparagus surfaces while
  the lemon juice creates an emulsion with the olive oil and parmesan.
