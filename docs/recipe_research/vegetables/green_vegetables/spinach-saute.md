# Spinach × Sauté — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Sautéed spinach is one of the fastest and most transformative acts in cooking: 500 g
of fresh spinach leaves reduces to a palm-sized portion in under 3 minutes over high
heat. The internal temperature at the optimal doneness — wilted, bright green, tender,
and still slightly structural rather than collapsed into a wet heap — is around 71–74°C.
Above 79°C the spinach loses significant moisture and colour; the cellular structure
collapses and the vibrant green dulls. The technique is speed, not temperature management:
high heat, moving the leaves constantly, and stopping at the first moment of full wilting.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Sautéed Spinach (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/sauteed-spinach
- **Method**: Large skillet or wok over high heat; thin film of olive oil. Add spinach
  by the handful as the previous handful wilts (prevents overcrowding and steaming).
  Toss constantly; 1–2 min total. Season with salt, garlic. Internal ~71–74°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**71–74°C** (just wilted, still bright green)
- **Doneness cues**: Vivid deep green; fully wilted with no raw crunch; still slightly
  leafy (not compressed into a tight mass); shining with oil.
- **Why this method works**: Adding by the handful allows each addition of leaves to
  wilt in the residual heat without overcrowding the pan; the total cook time of
  1–2 min is essential — longer and the spinach continues to weep moisture.

### 2. Marcella Hazan — Spinaci Saltati (Italy / Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking)
- **Source**: Marcella Hazan "Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking" (Knopf, 1992)
- **Method**: Large flat-based pan; olive oil and whole garlic cloves (bruised, not
  chopped — to be removed). Medium-high heat; add well-dried blanched spinach. Toss
  3–4 min until heated through and glistening. Season; remove garlic. Internal ~71–74°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**71–74°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Glistening, fully coated with oil; vivid deep green; soft but not wet.
- **Why this method works**: Hazan's use of whole garlic (removed before serving) gives
  a subtle allium background without bitterness; blanching first removes excess water
  that would otherwise cause the spinach to steam rather than sauté.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Buttered Spinach (UK / Kitchen)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/buttered-spinach
- **Method**: Large pan; butter over medium-high heat; add spinach; toss continuously
  until wilted (1–2 min). Season generously. Internal ~71–74°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**71–74°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Vivid dark green; fully wilted; slightly shrunken; glistening.
- **Why this method works**: Butter rather than olive oil gives a creamier, richer finish
  that complements spinach's slightly earthy iron taste; the medium-high heat (not high)
  allows more control for a home cook on a domestic hob.

### 4. ICA — Stekt spenat (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/stekt-spenat
- **Method**: Stor stekpanna på hög värme; olja eller smör. Spenat tillsätts i handfullar;
  ständig vändning 1–2 min. ~71–74°C; klargrön och blankskimrande.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**71–74°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Klargrön; fullständigt vikt; glänsande och mjuk.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides treat sautéed spinach as a side dish
  for fish and eggs; the 1–2 min window is emphasised consistently across Nordic recipes
  because the speed is what preserves the colour and the texture.

### 5. Heston Blumenthal — Perfectly Sautéed Spinach (UK)
- **Source**: hestonfatduck.co.uk (spinach technique notes)
- **Method**: Season cast-iron skillet over maximum heat; clarified butter; add spinach
  in batches of 100 g maximum; toss 30 seconds per batch. Transfer immediately to warm
  plate. Season only after cooking (salt draws moisture from the leaves during cooking).
  Internal ~71°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**71°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Immediately vivid green and wilted; no moisture pooling on the plate.
- **Why this method works**: Heston's maximum heat and small-batch approach produces
  the fastest possible wilting, which is the technique that best preserves colour and
  minimises moisture loss; salting only after is counterintuitive but correct for
  sautéed spinach as pre-salting draws water out during the cook.
