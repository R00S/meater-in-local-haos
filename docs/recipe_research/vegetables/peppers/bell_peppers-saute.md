## Cut profile
Sautéed bell peppers are the foundation of dozens of essential preparations: the
base of peperonata, the starting point of a ratatouille, the filling for a frittata,
the complement to a fajita. The internal temperature at perfect sautéed doneness
ranges with intent: at 71–74°C the pepper strips are tender-crisp and vivid; at
79–82°C they are fully soft and beginning to caramelise; at 88°C they are collapsed,
concentrated, and sweet. Each temperature produces a different vegetable in the same pan.

## Source recipes

### 1. Marcella Hazan — Peperonata (Italy / Essentials)
- **Source**: Marcella Hazan "Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking" (Knopf, 1992)
- **Method**: Strips in olive oil over medium heat; cook stirring every 2–3 min until
  completely soft and beginning to caramelise (20–25 min). Final internal ~88°C;
  the peperonata collapses into a sweet, jammy stew.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**88°C** (fully collapsed, sweet, jammy)
- **Doneness cues**: Strips completely collapsed; deep caramelised edges; sweet-smoky
  aroma; the pan has concentrated juices at the bottom.
- **Why this method works**: Hazan's slow medium-heat approach breaks down the pepper's
  cell walls completely over 20–25 min; the result is peperonata — fundamentally
  different in flavour and texture from a quick-sauté.

### 2. Ottolenghi — Sautéed Peppers with Garlic (UK)
- **Source**: theguardian.com/lifeandstyle (Ottolenghi column)
- **Method**: Julienned strips; olive oil over medium-high heat; 8–10 min with
  occasional stirring. Add garlic in last 2 min. Internal ~82°C; tender and beginning
  to colour.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**82°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Tender strips with golden-brown edges; garlic golden; concentrated
  sweet pepper aroma.
- **Why this method works**: The 8–10 min medium-high sauté produces a tender-but-
  structured pepper rather than the collapsed jammy result of a long slow cook;
  suitable for salads, toppings, and dishes where textural integrity matters.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Pan-Fried Bell Peppers (UK)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/pan-fried-peppers
- **Method**: Strips; olive oil; medium-high 6–8 min. Season; serve warm or at room
  temperature. Internal ~79°C; tender and vivid.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**79°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Vivid colour; tender; slight caramelised edges; holds shape.
- **Why this method works**: Nigella's quick approach targets the tender-crisp end of
  the bell pepper sauté spectrum — suitable for side dishes where the pepper's fresh
  character is preferred over full caramelisation.

### 4. ICA — Stekta paprikaremsor (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/stekta-paprikaremsor
- **Method**: Remsor; olivolja på medelhög värme; 6–8 min med regelbunden rörelse.
  ~79–82°C inuti; mjuka och börjat karamellisera.
- **Target internal temp**: **79–82°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Mjuka remsor med gyllenbrun kanter; söt paprikadoft.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipes use sautéed bell peppers as a universal
  accompaniment; the 6–8 min medium-high time targets the tender-but-structured result
  used most commonly in Nordic cuisine.

### 5. Jamie Oliver — Flash-Fried Peppers (UK)
- **Source**: jamieoliver.com/recipes/vegetables-recipes/flash-fried-peppers
- **Method**: Strips; very hot pan with olive oil; 3–4 min tossing frequently.
  Add a splash of sherry vinegar at end. Internal ~74°C; blistered surfaces.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**74°C** (crisp-tender)
- **Doneness cues**: Blistered surfaces; vivid colour maintained; slightly resistant
  to bite.
- **Why this method works**: Jamie's flash-fry at maximum heat targets the blistered-
  crisp end of the spectrum; the sherry vinegar finish adds acid that lifts the
  pepper's natural sweetness without additional cooking time.
