# Roasted Sweet Potato × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
The sweet potato has one of the highest natural sugar contents of any vegetable, and
oven roasting is the method that most fully realises this: at 200°C the sugars caramelise
against the pan and against the cut surfaces, the flesh softens from starchy to almost
custardy, and the skin — when present — crisps into a sweet, papery shell. The internal
temperature target of 96–99°C corresponds to the point at which the starch is fully
gelatinised and the characteristic "sticky sweetness" of a fully cooked sweet potato
emerges. A probe at the thickest point of the sweet potato (which is rarely uniform in
shape) gives the most accurate reading.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Roasted Sweet Potatoes (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/roasted-sweet-potatoes
- **Method**: Halve or cube; toss with oil and salt. Pre-heated baking sheet at 230°C /
  450°F. Roast 20–25 min for cubes, 30–35 min for halves, flipping once.
  Internal at thickest point: 99°C.
- **Target internal temp**: **210°F / 99°C** (fully cooked starch + caramelisation)
- **Doneness cues**: Deep caramelised amber on cut surfaces; tip of a paring knife
  passes through with zero resistance; slight puffing at the centre.
- **Why this method works**: The pre-heated baking sheet causes immediate caramelisation
  on the cut surface on contact; the sweet potato's starch and sugar content means the
  same technique used for potatoes produces more dramatic caramelisation.

### 2. Samin Nosrat — Roasted Sweet Potatoes with Brown Butter (USA)
- **Source**: saltfatacidheat.com / NYT Cooking (Samin Nosrat column)
- **Method**: Halve lengthwise; brush with oil; oven cut-face-down at 200°C for 25–35 min.
  Probe the thickest point; pull at 99°C. Top with brown butter and salt.
- **Target internal temp**: **99°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Cut face is caramelised and slightly set; the interior is visibly
  soft and slightly puffed; the skin has wrinkled and is slightly crispy.
- **Why this method works**: Cut-face-down gives maximum Maillard contact area;
  Samin's brown butter finish adds a nutty counterpoint to the sweet caramelisation.

### 3. Ottolenghi — Oven-Roasted Sweet Potato with Za'atar (UK / Simple)
- **Source**: theguardian.com/lifeandstyle (Ottolenghi column) + Simple (cookbook)
- **Method**: Wedges or halves; toss with olive oil; oven 220°C for 25–30 min.
  Internal ~99°C at thickest; deeply caramelised cut surfaces.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**99°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Edges caramelised to dark amber; interior yields completely to
  a knife; slightly crispy skin where exposed.
- **Why this method works**: High heat (220°C) accelerates surface caramelisation
  before the centre is fully cooked, creating a contrast between caramelised exterior
  and custardy interior that is one of the defining pleasures of roasted sweet potato.

### 4. ICA — Ugnsstekt sötpotatis (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/ugnsstekt-sotpotatis
- **Method**: Klyftor eller halvor; olja och salt. Ugn 200°C i 25–30 min.
  Kärntemperatur 99°C; gyllenbrun karamelliserad yta.
- **Target internal temp**: **99°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Karamelliserad skuren yta; mjuk hela vägen igenom; skalet krispy.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides have adopted the sweet potato as a
  mainstream vegetable; 200°C oven at 25–30 min is calibrated for the common
  Nordic sweet potato size available in supermarkets.

### 5. Jamie Oliver — Sticky Roasted Sweet Potatoes (UK)
- **Source**: jamieoliver.com/recipes/vegetables-recipes/roasted-sweet-potatoes
- **Method**: Wedges; toss in oil, honey, chilli flakes. Oven 200°C for 25–30 min.
  Internal ~99°C; extremely caramelised at edges.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**99°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Honey glaze has caramelised to a sticky amber; flesh is soft
  and slightly sticky; tips are almost charred.
- **Why this method works**: The honey addition to sweet potato creates a glaze that
  caramelises at a lower temperature than plain sweet potato sugars alone; the
  result is an extremely deeply flavoured caramelisation at 200°C that would
  require 230°C without the honey addition.
