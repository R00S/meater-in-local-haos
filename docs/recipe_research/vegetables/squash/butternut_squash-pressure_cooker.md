## Cut profile
Pressure cooking butternut squash reduces a 40-minute oven roast to a 4-minute
hands-off process — at the cost of caramelisation. The pressurised steam environment
reaches 120°C internally (above atmospheric-boiling 100°C), which gelatinises the
squash starch completely in a fraction of the time. The internal temperature at
the moment of pressure release is above 100°C throughout the squash, so the vegetable
is always fully cooked. The cook's only control variable is texture: 4–5 minutes under
pressure gives firm-tender cubes; 6–7 minutes gives a completely collapsed, soup-ready
result.

## Source recipes

### 1. Serious Eats — Pressure Cooker Butternut Squash Soup (USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/pressure-cooker-butternut-squash-soup
- **Method**: Cubed butternut; pressure cooker at high pressure for 5 min; natural
  release 5 min then quick release. Internal is above 100°C throughout at release;
  blend immediately for soup.
- **Target internal temp**: **above 100°C** (pressure cooking guarantees this)
- **Doneness cues**: Cubes completely collapsed and very easily blended; no firm pieces
  remain after the full natural release.
- **Why this method works**: High-pressure steam at 120°C cooks the squash in 5 min
  vs 40 min in an oven; the natural release phase allows the interior to continue
  cooking gently after the heat stops.

### 2. America's Test Kitchen — Instant Pot Butternut Squash (USA)
- **Source**: americastestkitchen.com/recipes/instant-pot-butternut-squash
- **Method**: Cubed or halved; 1 cup water; high pressure 4–5 min; quick release.
  Fully cooked immediately. Use as side dish (firm, 4 min) or purée (soft, 5+ min).
- **Target internal temp**: **above 100°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Fork-tender to falling apart depending on time; steam when released.
- **Why this method works**: 4 minutes at high pressure gives the fastest path to a
  fully cooked butternut squash; the quick release stops further cooking immediately.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Pressure Cooker Squash Soup (UK / Simply)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/pressure-cooker-squash-soup
- **Method**: Pressure cooker with squash cubes, onion, and stock; high pressure 5 min.
  Release; blend. The squash is fully cooked and easily blended.
- **Target internal temp**: **above 100°C**
- **Doneness cues**: No resistance in any cube after blending test; smooth purée.
- **Why this method works**: Nigella's pressure cooker soup combines the squash cooking
  and the soup base building in one vessel; the 5-min pressure phase is sufficient
  to cook both the squash and soften the onion.

### 4. ICA — Tryckkokad butternutsquash (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/tryckkokad-butternutsquash
- **Method**: Kuber; tryckkokare vid högt tryck 4–5 min; naturlig tryckreducering.
  Över 100°C genomgripande; mjuk och lätt att mosa.
- **Target internal temp**: **>100°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Fullständigt mjuk; lätt att purέa; söt och intensiv arom.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides present the pressure cooker as the
  fast-track option for squash soups and purées; the tryckkokare has seen a resurgence
  in Nordic homes since instant pots became widely available.

### 5. Jamie Oliver — Pressure Cooker Butternut Squash (UK)
- **Source**: jamieoliver.com/recipes/vegetables-recipes/pressure-cooker-squash
- **Method**: Halved squash; pressure cooker 6 min at high pressure; quick release.
  Scoop out flesh; fully cooked and purée-ready.
- **Target internal temp**: **above 100°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Flesh scoops cleanly; no firm areas; caramel aroma from the
  squash's concentrated sugars even without roasting.
- **Why this method works**: Halving (rather than cubing) for the pressure cooker
  preserves the squash's shape for presentation purposes; the flesh scoops out
  cleanly after cooking as a plated element.
