# Zucchini × Sauté — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Sautéed zucchini (courgette) at high heat develops a golden-brown crust on its cut
surfaces that raw zucchini's watery interior would otherwise prevent. The trick is
dry heat, a very hot pan, and not overcrowding — overcrowding causes the zucchini to
steam in its own water, producing the soft, grey, flavourless result that gives this
vegetable an undeserved reputation. Internal temperature at perfect doneness: around
79–82°C — the flesh is tender but still holds its shape, with golden-brown contact
surfaces. Above 88°C the zucchini collapses into a watery mass.

## Source recipes

### 1. Ottolenghi — Sautéed Zucchini with Herbs (UK / Plenty)
- **Source**: theguardian.com/lifeandstyle (Ottolenghi column)
- **Method**: Thin rounds or batons; large pan over high heat; olive oil; single layer.
  Cook 2 min undisturbed; flip; cook 2 min more. Season and add fresh mint or basil.
  Internal ~79°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**79°C** (golden but holding shape)
- **Doneness cues**: Golden-brown on contact faces; slightly translucent; mint/basil
  wilts on the residual heat; holds its shape.
- **Why this method works**: The undisturbed approach on a single-layer high-heat pan
  creates the Maillard browning that transforms zucchini; the fresh herbs added off
  heat preserve their colour and volatile oils.

### 2. Marcella Hazan — Zucchine Trifolate (Italy / Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking)
- **Source**: Marcella Hazan "Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking" (Knopf, 1992)
- **Method**: Thin coins; olive oil and garlic over medium-high heat; cook stirring
  occasionally 8–10 min until golden and tender. Salt only at end. Internal ~82°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**82°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden patches on all surfaces; tender; the garlic is softened
  and golden; no water pooling in the pan.
- **Why this method works**: Hazan's longer sauté time (8–10 min) at medium-high heat
  slowly caramelises the coins rather than flashing them; the result is more yielding
  and concentrated in flavour than the quick Ottolenghi method.

### 3. Nigella Lawson — Sautéed Courgette (UK)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/sauteed-courgette
- **Method**: Thick rounds; pan over medium-high heat; 1 tbsp olive oil + pinch of chilli.
  Cook 3–4 min per side. Internal ~79–82°C; golden cut faces.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**79–82°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Golden; tender; chilli fragrant; no raw centre.
- **Why this method works**: Thick rounds at medium-high (not high) give the cook more
  control for a less familiar vegetable; the chilli addition blooms in the oil before
  the zucchini is added.

### 4. ICA — Stekt zucchini (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/stekt-zucchini
- **Method**: Skivor 1 cm tjocka; olja på hög värme; 2 min per sida utan omrörning.
  ~79°C inuti; gyllenbrun kontaktyta.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**79°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Gyllenbrun på en sida; mjuk men fast; inget vattenflöde.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides emphasise the importance of high heat
  and single-layer cooking for zucchini; the vegetable's high water content means
  lower heat causes steaming rather than browning.

### 5. Jamie Oliver — Flash-Fried Courgette (UK)
- **Source**: jamieoliver.com/recipes/vegetables-recipes/flash-fried-courgette
- **Method**: Thick rounds or wedges; very hot pan with olive oil. 2–3 min per side.
  Squeeze lemon over; season with fresh basil. Internal ~79°C.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**79°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Deep golden on contact surfaces; tender interior; holds shape.
- **Why this method works**: Lemon squeezed over the zucchini immediately after cooking
  draws out the remaining moisture that the high heat didn't evaporate, sharpening
  the final flavour and preventing the dish from becoming watery on the plate.
