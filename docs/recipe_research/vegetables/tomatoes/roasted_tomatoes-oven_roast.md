# Roasted Tomatoes × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Roasted tomatoes — halved and placed cut-face-up in a low-to-medium oven — are one of
cooking's most reliable flavour amplifiers. The slow heat concentrates the tomato's water
content, caramelises its natural sugars, and drives off the watery, acidic raw character
to reveal a deep, sweet, almost jammy intensity. Internal temperature at the ideal "slow
roasted" doneness: 88–93°C throughout, at which point the tomatoes have shrunk to about
40% of their original size, the cut surface is caramelised, and the flesh has concentrated
into a sticky, flavourful paste.

## Source recipes

### 1. Ottolenghi — Slow-Roasted Tomatoes (UK / Plenty)
- **Source**: theguardian.com/lifeandstyle (Ottolenghi column) + Plenty (Ebury, 2010)
- **Method**: Halved tomatoes cut-face-up; drizzle with olive oil; season. Oven at
  150°C for 1.5–2.5 h until concentrated and semi-dried. Internal ~90°C throughout.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C** (concentrated throughout)
- **Doneness cues**: Tomatoes have reduced to 40–50% of original size; cut surface is
  caramelised and slightly charred at the edge; the flesh is jammy and concentrated;
  the skin is slightly wrinkled.
- **Why this method works**: The very low temperature (150°C) allows the water to evaporate
  slowly without burning the sugars; the long time produces deep caramelisation without
  the bitterness of high-heat charring.

### 2. Nigella Lawson — Slow-Roasted Tomatoes (UK / How to Eat)
- **Source**: nigella.com/recipes/slow-roasted-tomatoes
- **Method**: Halved; cut-face-up; generously oiled and seasoned; oven 130°C for 3–4 h.
  Internal ~90°C; very concentrated.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Extremely concentrated; wrinkled; deeply flavourful; the oil in
  the pan has absorbed the tomato juices.
- **Why this method works**: Nigella's 130°C over 3–4 h is even more patient than
  Ottolenghi's approach; the result is closer to a semi-dried tomato and can be
  stored in olive oil for a week.

### 3. J. Kenji López-Alt — Quick Roasted Tomatoes (Serious Eats / USA)
- **Source**: seriouseats.com/roasted-tomatoes
- **Method**: Halved cherry or plum tomatoes; cut-face-up; olive oil; oven 220°C for
  20–30 min. Internal ~90°C; golden-caramelised cut surfaces, concentrated but not
  dried.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Caramelised cut surface; tomatoes have shrunk but still hold
  their shape; juices have concentrated into a thick pool around each tomato.
- **Why this method works**: The high-temperature quick roast is Kenji's weeknight
  version — less concentrated than a slow roast but available in 30 minutes; the
  220°C temperature caramelises the cut surfaces before the tomatoes collapse.

### 4. ICA — Ugnsrostade tomater (Sweden)
- **Source**: ica.se/recept/ugnsrostade-tomater
- **Method**: Halverade; snittytan uppåt; olivolja, salt, timjan. Ugn 150°C i 1.5–2 h
  (lång, koncentrerad) eller 200°C i 25 min (snabb variant). ~90°C inuti.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Koncentrerade; karamelliserade snittytor; skrynkliga skal.
- **Why this method works**: Swedish recipe guides present both the slow and fast methods;
  the slow 150°C version is for sauce bases and sandwich toppings where maximum
  concentration is desired.

### 5. BBC Good Food — Oven-Roasted Tomatoes (UK)
- **Source**: bbcgoodfood.com/recipes/oven-roasted-tomatoes
- **Method**: Halved; cut-face-up; oil, garlic halves, fresh thyme. Oven 200°C for
  25–30 min. Internal ~90°C; caramelised and concentrated.
- **Target internal temp**: ~**90°C**
- **Doneness cues**: Caramelised cut surfaces; garlic golden; thyme crisped;
  tomatoes concentrated and slightly collapsed.
- **Why this method works**: Garlic halves placed alongside the tomatoes caramelise at
  the same rate; the thyme's volatile oils infuse the tomato juices in the hot pan;
  the result is a complete aromatic unit.
