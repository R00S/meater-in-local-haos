# Roasted Tomatoes × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile
Tomatoes (*Solanum lycopersicum*) oven-roasted undergo a profound transformation: the high water content (≈ 94 %) evaporates in the oven heat, concentrating the glutamate-rich flesh into an intensely sweet, umami-packed result. Low-and-slow roasting (120–150 °C for 1–3 hours) produces almost-dried, jammy results with deep flavour concentration; high-heat roasting (200–230 °C for 20–35 min) achieves blistered, caramelised edges while the interior stays juicy. Cherry tomatoes, plum tomatoes cut lengthwise, and whole large tomatoes all lend themselves to oven roasting. A MEATER probe inserted through the wall of a plum tomato or a larger cherry tomato monitors the internal temperature: at 82–88 °C (180–190 °F) the skin is blistered and the interior is collapsing but still holds shape; at 88–95 °C the tomato has fully collapsed and is releasing concentrated juices. Carryover is negligible — the high water content prevents heat accumulation.

## Source recipes

### 1. Diana Kennedy — Jitomates Asados (Mexican tradition)
**Source**: Diana Kennedy, *The Art of Mexican Cooking* (Bantam Books, 1989)
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 30–35 min · **Rest**: 5 min (cooling for peeling)

**Ingredients**
- 6 large ripe plum tomatoes (about 600 g), cored (stems removed)
- 2 tbsp neutral oil (corn or sunflower), to brush lightly
- Fine sea salt, to taste
- 3 garlic cloves, left in their skins (to roast alongside)
- 1–2 serrano or jalapeño chillies (to roast alongside — these become the base of a salsa once all are charred)

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 230 °C (450 °F). Line a heavy metal tray with aluminium foil. (Kennedy often specifies a *comal* or a cast-iron skillet but a heavy oven tray is the home adaptation.)
2. Arrange the tomatoes and unpeeled garlic cloves on the foil-lined tray. Add whole chillies alongside. Brush the tomatoes lightly with oil.
3. Insert the MEATER Plus probe through the side of the largest tomato into its centre.
4. Roast in the upper third of the oven for 30–35 minutes, turning once at 15 minutes, until the tomato skins are blistered and charred in spots, and the flesh has softened considerably.
5. **Pull at 90–95 °C (194–203 °F).** Kennedy notes: "The tomatoes are done when they are very soft, somewhat collapsed, and the skin has charred in places — these are exactly the qualities you want for salsa asada." The charred spots contribute bitterness that balances the salsa's sweetness.
6. Peel (the skins slide off easily), squeeze the garlic from its paper, and remove stems from the chillies. Blend or chop to a salsa with salt. Alternatively, serve the roasted tomatoes whole as a side, drizzled with their own juices. Final serving temperature: **90–95 °C / 194–203 °F** (charred, concentrated, smoky-sweet).

### 2. Marcella Hazan — Pomodori al Forno (Italian tradition)
**Source**: Marcella Hazan, *Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking* (Alfred A. Knopf, 1992)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 25–35 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 6 ripe plum tomatoes (about 600 g), halved lengthways, seeds and core gently squeezed out
- 3 garlic cloves, very finely sliced
- 15 g (3 tbsp) flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- 2 tsp dried breadcrumbs (pangrattato)
- 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil (divided)
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200 °C (400 °F). Oil a shallow baking dish large enough to hold the tomato halves in a single layer.
2. Arrange tomato halves cut-side-up in the dish. Season each half generously with salt and pepper.
3. Combine garlic, parsley and breadcrumbs; spoon this mixture evenly into each tomato half. Drizzle with the olive oil.
4. Insert the MEATER probe through the side wall of one of the larger tomato halves so the tip sits in the thickest part of the flesh.
5. Roast 25–35 minutes until the tomatoes have softened and shrunk, the tops are golden-brown and the juices are bubbling around the edges. Hazan notes they should be "entirely tender with no rawness remaining — test with a fork."
6. **Pull at 88–92 °C (190–198 °F).** The flesh should be melting-soft, golden-crusted on top, and the juices concentrated in the dish.
7. Allow to rest 5 minutes; serve warm or at room temperature with all the pan juices. Final serving temperature: **88–92 °C / 190–198 °F** (golden, herb-bright, olive oil–glossed).

### 3. Claudia Roden — Tomates Confits (Moroccan / North African tradition)
**Source**: Claudia Roden, *Arabesque: A Taste of Morocco, Turkey and Lebanon* (Alfred A. Knopf, 2005)
**Serves**: 4–6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 1 hour 30 min – 2 hours (slow-roast) · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 500 g cherry tomatoes or small plum tomatoes, halved
- 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 2 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 1 tbsp caster sugar
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp sweet paprika
- ½ tsp ground ginger
- ½ tsp ground cinnamon
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- Small handful fresh coriander or flat-leaf parsley, to finish

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 130 °C (265 °F). Line a large baking sheet with parchment.
2. Toss the tomatoes gently with olive oil, garlic, sugar, cumin, paprika, ginger, cinnamon, salt and pepper. Spread cut-side-up on the baking sheet in a single layer.
3. Insert the MEATER probe through the side of one of the larger tomato halves, with the tip at the geometric centre of the flesh.
4. Slow-roast for 1 hour 30 minutes – 2 hours until the tomatoes have lost most of their moisture, collapsed into concentrated, jammy rounds, and turned deep red-orange. Roden describes them as "sweet, intensely flavoured, and almost candied."
5. **Pull at 90–96 °C (194–205 °F).** The internal temperature of slow-roasted tomatoes is consistently near this range when they reach the desired concentrated, jammy texture.
6. Cool 10 minutes on the tray. Scatter fresh coriander. Serve as a relish, spread on flatbread, or as a side to grilled fish or meat. Final serving temperature: **warm or room temperature** (spiced, sweet-savoury, deeply concentrated).

### 4. Yotam Ottolenghi — Burst Cherry Tomatoes with Za'atar (Middle Eastern / Israeli tradition)
**Source**: Yotam Ottolenghi, *Plenty* (Ebury Press, 2010)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 600 g cherry tomatoes (mixed colours if possible), left whole
- 3 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- 2 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 1½ tsp za'atar
- ½ tsp sumac
- ½ tsp fine sea salt
- ¼ tsp crushed dried chilli flakes
- 15 g fresh basil or flat-leaf parsley, to scatter

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Toss the cherry tomatoes with olive oil, garlic, za'atar, sumac, salt and chilli flakes in a large bowl.
2. Spread the tomatoes in a single layer in a heavy roasting dish or half-sheet pan.
3. Insert the MEATER probe through the skin of one of the larger cherry tomatoes into its centre.
4. Roast 20–25 minutes until the tomato skins begin to burst and char lightly at the shoulders, and the tomatoes have released their juices into the pan.
5. **Pull at 86–90 °C (187–194 °F).** Ottolenghi wants tomatoes that are "bursting with juice, slightly caramelised at the edges, and sitting in a fragrant, oily puddle of their own concentrated juices."
6. Scatter fresh herbs and allow to rest 5 minutes. Serve warm over yoghurt or bread. Final serving temperature: **86–90 °C / 187–194 °F** (bursting, spiced, herb-fragrant).
