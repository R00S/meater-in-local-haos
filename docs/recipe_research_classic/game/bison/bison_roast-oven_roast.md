## Cut profile

Bison roast cuts are drawn from the same primals as beef roasting joints — most commonly the
shoulder (chuck/Schulter), topside (Oberschale), silverside (Unterschale), or loin (Rücken).
The fillet roast (whole tenderloin) is also used in high-end German preparations. Like bison
steak, bison roast is substantially leaner than equivalent beef cuts: the marbling is sparse
and the outer fat layer thin, which has two important consequences. First, a bison roast dries
out faster in a hot oven than a comparably sized beef joint, making temperature monitoring with
a probe essential. Second, without intramuscular fat to buffer heat and provide moisture, the
off-flavour threshold at which overcooked meat tastes "liver-like" is reached earlier — roughly
65–68 °C for a roast compared to around 63 °C for a steak.

The two dominant German cooking traditions for bison roast diverge sharply in oven temperature
but agree on a pull temperature of approximately 60–63 °C (medium). The high-heat tradition
(200 °C, 45–60 minutes for a 1 kg joint) produces a crisper exterior but requires careful
attention because the meat approaches the pull temperature quickly. The low-heat tradition
(80–90 °C, 90–120 minutes) relies on the oven temperature being close to the desired internal
temperature, preventing overshoot and producing an evenly pink interior across the full
cross-section. Both traditions use an initial sharp sear to develop a Maillard crust before
the oven phase, since at low oven temperatures browning will not occur.

Unlike bison steak, a large roast does carry some collagen from the connective tissue that
surrounds muscle groups — particularly in shoulder or topside cuts. However, bison roast is
not a braise candidate: the collagen content is insufficient to benefit from the 85–95 °C
sustained-hold window required for full collagen conversion, and the lean muscle would be
ruined at those temperatures. The correct window is medium (60–65 °C), where the muscle is
still moist and pink throughout. Resting is critical for roasts: a minimum of 10 minutes
allows the thermal gradient within the joint to equalise and the muscle fibres to reabsorb
juices. A mozzarella-mustard herb crust, applied before the oven phase, is a distinctive
German preparation that acts as an insulating layer and baste, partially compensating for
the low fat content.

## Source recipes

### 1. Chefkoch.de — Bisonbraten (German tradition, high-heat oven)
**Source**: acigrand, Chefkoch.de community recipe;
chefkoch.de/rezepte/234021095857919/Bisonbraten.html (fetched 2026-06-08;
4.5/5 stars from 2 ratings)
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 20 min · **Cook**: 45–60 min · **Oven**: 200 °C

**Ingredients**
- 1 kg bison roast
- Salt, pepper, dried thyme, dried rosemary
- 1 onion, diced
- 2 garlic cloves, diced
- 2 cardamom pods
- 5 juniper berries
- 3 cloves
- 500 ml stock (Brühe)
- 200 ml red wine
- Olive oil for searing
- Sauce binder (Soßenbinder), optional

**Method**
1. Wash the bison roast and pat thoroughly dry. Season all over with thyme, rosemary, salt and
   pepper.
2. Heat olive oil in a Dutch oven (Bräter) over high heat on the hob. Sear the seasoned roast
   on all sides until a deep brown crust has formed.
3. Dice the onion and garlic. Add to the Dutch oven alongside the roast and sauté briefly until
   beginning to colour.
4. Pour over the red wine and stock. Lightly crush the cardamom pods and add them with the
   juniper berries and cloves to the braising liquid. Stir to combine.
5. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the roast, ensuring the tip is in the
   geometric centre and does not touch the sides of the Dutch oven or any bone.
6. Put a lid on the Dutch oven and roast in the oven at 200 °C for 45–60 minutes, basting
   occasionally with some of the cooking stock. Cooking time varies by desired doneness: 45
   minutes produces a pinker result; 60 minutes produces a more through-cooked result.
7. **Pull at 60–63 °C (140–145 °F)** for medium (the centre will be tender pink). At 200 °C
   the roast will carryover approximately 2–3 °C after pulling; accounting for rest the final
   internal temperature will be approximately 63–65 °C.
8. Remove the roast from the Dutch oven and keep warm. Strain the cooking juices through a
   sieve into a saucepan. Bring to the boil and season with salt and pepper. Thicken with sauce
   binder if desired. Serve sliced with the sauce.
9. Final serving temperature: **63–65 °C / 145–149 °F** (medium, after 10 min rest; tender
   pink throughout).

### 2. Chefkoch.de — Bisonbraten mit Mozzarella-Senfkruste (German tradition, low-heat oven with herb crust)
**Source**: backfee_65, Chefkoch.de community recipe;
chefkoch.de/rezepte/2763771428258460/Bisonbraten-mit-Mozzarella-Senfkruste.html
(fetched 2026-06-08; 5/5 stars from 2 ratings)
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 30 min · **Cook**: 90–120 min · **Oven**: 90 °C

**Ingredients**

*For the roast*
- 1.3 kg bison fillet
- 3 tbsp neutral oil, or 50 g clarified butter (Butterschmalz)
- 1 medium onion, cut into rough rings
- 1 buffalo mozzarella ball
- Salt, pepper, sweet paprika

*For the herb-mustard crust*
- 1 tbsp Dijon mustard
- 2 tbsp sweet mustard (süßer Senf)
- 2 tbsp coarse-grain mustard (körniger Senf)
- 4 garlic cloves
- ½ tsp salt
- 1 tsp dried tarragon
- 1 tsp roughly crushed peppercorns
- 1 tsp dried oregano
- 1 tsp dried rosemary
- 50 g blue cheese (Blauschimmelkäse), crumbled
- Breadcrumbs and oil as needed to adjust consistency

*For the sauce*
- ⅓ bunch soup vegetables (Suppengrün), finely chopped
- 1 medium onion, finely chopped
- 2 garlic cloves, crushed
- 5 tbsp balsamic cream (Balsamico-Creme)
- 5 tbsp dry sherry
- 200 ml stock
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 tbsp juniper berries, lightly crushed
- 1 tsp whole black peppercorns
- 50 g blue cheese, crumbled
- 1 tbsp red berries (Rote Beeren / lingonberries or redcurrants)
- 1 tbsp green pepper (Grüner Pfeffer)

**Method**
1. Remove the bison fillet from the refrigerator several hours before cooking and allow to
   come fully to room temperature. Rinse under cold water and pat dry.
2. Heat the oil or clarified butter in a heavy pan over very high heat. Sear the fillet slowly
   on all sides, 3–4 minutes per side, taking care not to let the surface burn dark. Meanwhile,
   preheat the oven to 90 °C (top/bottom heat) and place a flat ovenproof form (e.g. the lid
   of the Dutch oven) inside to warm. Cut the onion into rough rings.
3. For the mustard-herb crust: mince the 4 garlic cloves finely, then mash with ½ tsp salt
   using a fork until a paste forms. Stir in the Dijon mustard, sweet mustard, coarse-grain
   mustard, tarragon, crushed peppercorns, oregano and rosemary. Crumble the blue cheese with
   a fork and stir into the mixture. The paste should be spreadable but not runny. If too thin,
   add breadcrumbs to firm; if too thick, add a little oil to loosen.
4. Slice the mozzarella into thin rounds and set aside.
5. Remove the seared fillet from the pan and pat off excess oil. Season with salt, pepper and a
   little sweet paprika. Place the onion rings in the warmed ovenproof form and set the fillet
   on top of them (the onions act as a rack).
6. Coat the fillet all around with the mustard-herb paste — it should adhere. Lay the mozzarella
   slices over the top of the coated fillet.
7. Insert the MEATER probe into the thickest part of the roast, ensuring the tip is in the
   geometric centre and does not touch bone or the baking dish.
8. Place the form in the preheated 90 °C oven and roast for approximately 90–120 minutes.
9. **Pull at 60 °C (140 °F).** Switch off the oven and allow the roast to rest inside the
   switched-off oven for a further 10 minutes — the internal temperature will continue to rise
   2–3 °C to approximately 62–63 °C. At this temperature the meat is tender and pink
   throughout (medium). Do not exceed 65 °C or the bison will begin to dry and the off-flavour
   threshold will be approached.
10. About 20 minutes before pulling (when the probe reads approximately 57 °C), begin the sauce
    and prepare side dishes. The author serves Bratkartoffeln (pan-fried potatoes) and mangetout.
11. For the sauce: reheat the pan drippings from the sear. Sauté the finely chopped onions,
    garlic and soup vegetables until beginning to brown. Add the juniper berries, bay leaves and
    peppercorns. Deglaze with balsamic cream and allow to evaporate almost completely. Add the
    sherry and reduce. Deglaze with the stock and bring to the boil; reduce until the pan is
    nearly dry. Refill with 200 ml water and simmer on low heat for ~20 minutes (do not reduce
    further — this repeated reduce-and-refill process builds deep flavour). Stir in the crumbled
    blue cheese until dissolved. After 20 minutes, remove bay leaves and blend the sauce with a
    hand blender; press through a fine sieve. Add the red berries and green pepper and simmer on
    very low heat for a final 20 minutes.
12. Slice the rested roast and serve with the sauce, Bratkartoffeln and mangetout.
13. Final serving temperature: **62–63 °C / 144–145 °F** (medium — tender pink interior;
    result of pull at 60 °C plus 10 min rest in switched-off 90 °C oven).

## Temperature consensus

| Source | Pull temperature | Method |
|--------|----------------|--------|
| Chefkoch.de — Bisonbraten / acigrand (Germany) | 60–63 °C | Lidded Dutch oven, 45–60 min at 200 °C; medium |
| Chefkoch.de — Mozzarella-Senfkruste / backfee_65 (Germany) | 60 °C (rest to 62–63 °C) | Pan sear + 90–120 min at 90 °C; medium |

**Consensus pull**: **60–63 °C (140–145 °F)**. Both German sources converge on medium as the
correct doneness for bison roast. The high-heat recipe implies a pull around 60–63 °C (the
stated 45–60 min at 200 °C for a 1 kg joint). The low-heat recipe explicitly states "pull at
60 °C, rest 10 min, will reach 62–63 °C." Both describe the result as tender and pink
throughout (medium). Consistent with bison steak guidance, the off-flavour threshold is
approached above approximately 65–68 °C; therefore the absolute maximum safe pull is 63 °C
with a maximum serving temperature of 65 °C after rest.

**Representative coding value**: **63 °C (145 °F)** as the nominal pull, with a practical
range of 60–65 °C. This is higher than the bison steak pull (57–60 °C) because a roast
does not carryover as quickly per gram of mass as a thin steak, and the pull-at-60-then-rest
approach in Source 2 lands at 62–63 °C serving temperature.

## Notes for cooking_data.py

- `doneness`: `medium`
- `pull_temp_c`: 63
- `pull_temp_range_c`: [60, 65]
- `safety_level`: `medium` (whole-muscle; surface pathogens destroyed by searing; internal
  at 60–63 °C is above the minimum safe temperature for beef/bison)
- Bison roast must not exceed 65 °C internal temperature. Above this threshold the lean
  muscle dries out and develops the same iron-oxidation off-flavour ("liver-like") noted for
  bison steak.
- Both sources sear first before oven cooking, regardless of oven temperature. The sear
  creates the Maillard crust that cannot form at low oven temperatures.
- Rest is mandatory: minimum 10 minutes after pulling. The low-heat recipe (90 °C oven)
  exploits residual oven heat by resting the roast in the switched-off oven.
- Slow braising (85–95 °C sustained hold for collagen conversion) is contraindicated for
  bison roast — the lean muscle is ruined at those temperatures.
