# Ny Strip × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

The New York strip steak (also called strip loin, sirloin strip, Kansas City strip, or *contre-filet* in French) is cut from the short loin of the beef carcass — the longissimus dorsi muscle on the posterior side of the backbone, behind the ribeye primal and ahead of the sirloin. It is a single-muscle steak with moderate marbling, firmer texture than a ribeye (the muscle does slightly more work), and a pronounced beefy flavour. The fat cap along one edge is a defining characteristic and flavour source. Pan searing is a primary preparation — the strip's firm, uniform cross-section sears evenly, and the fat cap can be rendered against the pan edge by standing the steak on its side. Carryover is 3–5 °C for a thick strip steak; the lean-to-moderate marbling means less self-basting than a ribeye.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Seared New York Strip (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/perfect-pan-seared-steaks-recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 bone-in or boneless New York strip steaks, approximately 350–450 g each, 3–4 cm thick
- Kosher salt (for dry brine, applied 1–24 h ahead)
- Freshly ground black pepper (applied immediately before searing)
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or canola oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly crushed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Salt the steaks generously on all surfaces 24 hours in advance if possible; refrigerate uncovered on a wire rack. The dry brine improves crust formation and deeply seasons the meat.
2. Remove from fridge 30–45 minutes before cooking. Apply freshly ground pepper.
3. Heat a cast-iron or carbon-steel pan over the highest heat for 5 minutes until smoking. Add oil.
4. Place steaks in the pan. Flip every 15–30 seconds — Kenji's rapid-flip method reduces the grey band and distributes heat more evenly than a static sear.
5. After 2 minutes, insert the MEATER probe from the side of the steak into the centre of the meat, clear of the fat cap edge.
6. After 2 minutes total, add butter, garlic and thyme. Baste continuously, continuing to flip every 30 seconds.
7. Stand the steak on its fat-cap edge using tongs; sear the fat cap until rendered and crisp, about 1–2 minutes.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Kenji's target for NY strip is medium-rare; the firm texture of the strip muscle benefits from the full medium-rare warmth.
9. Rest 5 minutes on a wire rack. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 130–135 °F** (medium-rare, warm red-pink centre).

### 2. Gordon Ramsay — Pan-Seared Strip Steak with Red Wine Jus (British restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 New York strip steaks, 300–400 g each, 3 cm thick
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly smashed
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme
- **Red wine jus**: 1 shallot, minced; 150 ml (⅔ cup) red wine; 150 ml (⅔ cup) beef stock; 15 g cold unsalted butter

**Method**
1. Remove steaks from fridge 30 minutes before cooking. Pat dry; season both sides generously with salt and pepper.
2. Heat a heavy stainless or cast-iron pan over very high heat. Add oil; heat until smoking.
3. Place steaks in the pan. Leave undisturbed 3 minutes.
4. Flip. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of one steak into its centre.
5. Add butter, garlic and thyme; baste.
6. Cook second side 3 minutes while basting.
7. Stand on fat cap; render 1–2 minutes.
8. Make red wine jus in the resting pan: sauté shallot; add wine; reduce to a glaze; add stock; reduce by half; swirl in cold butter. Season.
9. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Ramsay's restaurant-style target is medium-rare.
10. Rest 5 minutes. Serve with red wine jus. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare).

### 3. Jacques Pépin — Contre-Filet Sauté au Beurre (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Jacques Pépin, *New Complete Techniques* (Black Dog & Leventhal Publishers, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min (+20 min tempering) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 *contre-filet* (boneless strip) steaks, 250–300 g each, 2.5 cm thick
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground white or black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) neutral oil
- 40 g (3 tbsp) unsalted butter, divided
- 1 shallot, finely minced
- 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine or cognac
- 20 g flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**Method**
1. Temper steaks 20 minutes. Season immediately before searing.
2. Heat a heavy sauté pan over high heat. Add oil; heat until just smoking.
3. Sear steak 2–3 minutes first side without moving.
4. Flip; insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre of the meat.
5. Add 20 g butter; baste 2–3 minutes.
6. Stand on fat cap; render 1 minute.
7. Rest steak on a warm plate 5 minutes. Drain fat from pan.
8. Sauté shallot; deglaze with wine; reduce by half; swirl in remaining 20 g butter; add parsley.
9. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Pépin's French preference is *saignant* — a warm, bright-red centre, the classic French standard for strip steak.
10. Rest 5 minutes. Spoon pan sauce over. Final serving temperature: **52–55 °C / 126–131 °F** (*saignant*, warm red interior).

### 4. Heston Blumenthal — Perfect Steak (Strip Steak Method) (British Modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2011)  
**Serves**: 1–2 · **Prep**: 24 h (+1 h dry age) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 thick-cut New York strip steak, 400–500 g, 3.5–5 cm thick (thick cut is Blumenthal's preference)
- Kosher or sea salt (applied 24 h ahead)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) high-smoke-point oil (e.g., rice bran)
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 2 sprigs thyme

**Method**
1. Salt the steak 24 hours in advance; refrigerate uncovered. The advance salting ensures deep seasoning and forms an exceptionally dry surface that sears faster.
2. Remove from fridge 1 hour before cooking. Apply pepper.
3. Heat a cast-iron pan over the highest heat. Add oil; heat until smoking.
4. Sear the steak, flipping every minute — Blumenthal's equal-time flip method. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre after the first flip.
5. Total cook time 8–10 minutes with consistent flipping.
6. Add butter and thyme; baste each time after flipping.
7. Stand on fat cap to render.
8. **Pull at 49–51 °C (120–124 °F).** Blumenthal targets a cool-red medium-rare, trusting 5 minutes of rest to bring the centre to the ideal medium-rare serving temperature.
9. Rest 5 minutes on a rack. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (medium-rare, Blumenthal standard).

### 5. Neil Perry — Pan-Seared Strip Steak with Bone Marrow Butter (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Neil Perry, *The Food I Love* (Murdoch Books, 2005)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min tempering) · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 Angus strip steaks (Australian grain-fed or grass-fed), 350–400 g each, 3 cm thick
- Sea salt flakes and freshly cracked black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Bone marrow butter**: 60 g raw bone marrow, extracted and softened; 40 g unsalted butter, softened; 10 g fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped; 5 g fresh thyme; sea salt and black pepper

**Method**
1. Mix bone marrow with softened butter, herbs and seasoning. Shape into a log; refrigerate.
2. Season steaks; heat a heavy cast-iron pan over very high heat. Add oil until smoking.
3. Sear first side 3–4 minutes; do not move.
4. Flip; insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre.
5. Sear second side 3–4 minutes; baste with pan drippings.
6. Render fat cap 1–2 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Perry's Australian standard for strip steak is medium-rare — slightly warmer than the French preference, with a firm warm-pink centre.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Top with a round of bone marrow butter to melt over the resting steak. Final serving temperature: **55–58 °C / 131–136 °F** (medium-rare, Australian standard).
