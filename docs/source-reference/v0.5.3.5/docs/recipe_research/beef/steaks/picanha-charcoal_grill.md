# Picanha × Charcoal Grill — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Picanha over live charcoal is the definitive Brazilian *churrasco* preparation — in Brazil, the whole *rump cap* is threaded onto long metal skewers in a horseshoe shape (fat cap outside) and rotated continuously above the charcoal fire of the *churrascaria*, the fat dripping and basting the meat as it cooks. At home on a kettle or kamado grill, the rotisserie method can be replicated or a simplified two-zone direct-then-indirect approach is used. The charcoal smoke compounds penetrate the fat cap and impart a distinctive aroma that is a signature of Brazilian *churrasco*. Carryover from a hot charcoal fire is 5–7 °C.

## Source recipes

### 1. Francis Mallmann — Churrasco Picanha over Charcoal (Brazilian / Argentine tradition)
**Source**: Francis Mallmann and Peter Kaminsky, *Seven Fires: Grilling the Argentine Way* (Artisan Books, 2009)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 25–35 min over embers · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole picanha (rump cap), 1.2–1.5 kg, fat cap intact and scored in a 2 cm crosshatch
- Coarse sea salt (*sal gruesa*) — the only seasoning; Mallmann's Brazilian-influenced tradition is aggressively minimal
- Hardwood charcoal (quebracho preferred)
- Long metal skewers (optional, for the horseshoe preparation)

**Method**
1. Score fat cap in a 2 cm crosshatch, 5 mm deep. Season generously with coarse salt only.
2. Optional horseshoe preparation: fold the picanha fat-cap-out into a crescent/horseshoe shape; thread on 2 long parallel metal skewers through the fold to hold the shape.
3. Light hardwood charcoal; allow to reach white-ash embers stage.
4. Place picanha (skewered or flat) fat-cap-side toward the coals.
5. Cook fat-cap-side 8–10 minutes; flip (or rotate if using skewers); cook meat-side 6–8 minutes.
6. If cooking flat: move to indirect side and insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre. Close lid; cook 8–12 minutes more.
7. **Pull at 49–51 °C (120–124 °F).** Mallmann's Brazilian-influenced standard: *malpassado* (Brazilian term for rare-medium-rare). The heavy charcoal carryover means very early pulling.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Slice 1 cm thick against the grain. Serve with only coarse salt alongside. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (*malpassado*, Brazilian churrasco standard).

### 2. Steven Raichlen — Picanha over Live Charcoal (American adaptation of Brazilian tradition)
**Source**: Steven Raichlen, *Planet Barbecue!* (Workman Publishing, 2010)  
**Serves**: 6 · **Prep**: 10 min (+1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole picanha, 1.2 kg, fat cap scored
- 15 g (1 tbsp) coarse salt
- 5 g (1 tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- Hardwood charcoal; 2 wood chunks (oak or pecan), soaked
- Farofa (*farofa* — Brazilian toasted cassava flour side dish) and vinegrete (*molho vinagrete* — Brazilian chopped onion and tomato vinaigrette), to serve

**Method**
1. Dry-brine picanha 1 hour. Light charcoal with wood chunks added.
2. Grill fat-cap-side down over direct coals 6–8 minutes.
3. Flip; grill meat-side down 4–5 minutes.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre; move to indirect. Close lid; cook 8–10 minutes.
5. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Raichlen's Brazilian-adapted standard: medium-rare with carryover allowance.
6. Rest 8 minutes. Slice against the grain. Serve with farofa and vinagrete. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium-rare, Brazilian-American adaptation standard).

### 3. Lennox Hastie — Rump Cap over Australian Charcoal (Australian wood-fire tradition)
**Source**: Lennox Hastie, *Finding Fire* (Hardie Grant, 2017)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+1 h dry brine) · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 picanha (rump cap), 1–1.2 kg, fat cap scored
- Coarse sea salt
- Freshly ground black pepper
- Ironbark or applewood charcoal

**Method**
1. Dry-brine 1 hour; season with pepper just before grilling.
2. Build a two-zone charcoal fire.
3. Grill fat-cap-side down over direct heat 5–7 minutes.
4. Flip; grill meat-side down 3–4 minutes.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end; move to indirect; close lid; cook 8–12 minutes.
6. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Hastie's live-fire target for picanha: medium-rare.
7. Rest 8 minutes. Slice. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium-rare, Australian live-fire standard).

### 4. Bobby Flay — Charcoal-Grilled Picanha with Chimichurri (American Southwest tradition)
**Source**: Bobby Flay, *Bobby Flay's Grill It!* (Clarkson Potter, 2008)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 18–22 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 picanha, 1–1.2 kg, fat cap scored
- Sea salt and freshly cracked black pepper
- Hardwood charcoal
- Chimichurri: 40 g parsley; 4 garlic cloves; 1 jalapeño; 30 ml red wine vinegar; 80 ml olive oil; salt

**Method**
1. Season picanha; start charcoal chimney.
2. Two-zone charcoal fire. Grill fat-cap-side down 5–6 minutes; flip; grill meat-side down 3–4 minutes.
3. Insert the MEATER probe; move to indirect; close lid; cook 8–10 minutes.
4. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Flay's standard: medium-rare.
5. Rest 8 minutes. Slice; serve with chimichurri. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium-rare).

### 5. Nobu Matsuhisa — Charcoal-Grilled Picanha with Ponzu (Japanese-Brazilian tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa, *Nobu Miami: The Party Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2009)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min dry brine) · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 8 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 picanha, 1 kg, fat cap scored
- Coarse sea salt and white pepper
- **Ponzu**: 60 ml yuzu or lemon juice; 45 ml soy sauce; 15 ml mirin; 10 ml rice wine vinegar
- Hardwood charcoal

**Method**
1. Dry-brine 30 minutes; mix ponzu; set aside.
2. Light charcoal; two-zone fire.
3. Grill fat-cap-side down 5–7 minutes; flip; grill meat-side down 3–4 minutes.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end; move to indirect; close lid; cook 8–10 minutes.
5. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Nobu's target: medium-rare.
6. Rest 8 minutes. Slice thin; serve with ponzu for dipping. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium-rare, Japanese-Brazilian fusion standard).
