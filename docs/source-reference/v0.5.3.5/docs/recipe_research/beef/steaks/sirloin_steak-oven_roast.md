# Sirloin Steak × Oven Roast — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Oven-roasted sirloin steak most commonly means one of two approaches: a reverse-sear for very thick-cut steaks (4+ cm), or a sear-then-oven-finish for steaks 3–4 cm thick. For a large whole top sirloin roast (rather than individual steaks), the oven roast is the primary method — the sirloin primal roasts well at 180–200 °C, developing a flavoured crust while the interior reaches a precise pull temperature. The lean composition of top sirloin means carryover is significant — 5–7 °C from a hot oven — requiring a pull temperature well below the final serving target. A loose foil tent or resting on a rack allows the temperature to equilibrate without continuing to cook.

## Source recipes

### 1. America's Test Kitchen — Reverse-Sear Top Sirloin (American tradition)
**Source**: America's Test Kitchen, *The Complete Cook's Country TV Show Cookbook* (America's Test Kitchen, 2015)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 45–60 min low oven + 3 min sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 extra-thick top sirloin steaks, 400–600 g each, 4–5 cm thick
- Kosher salt (applied 24 h ahead)
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil (for post-oven sear)
- 30 g unsalted butter (for post-oven sear)

**Method**
1. Dry-brine steaks with kosher salt overnight; refrigerate uncovered.
2. Preheat oven to 120 °C (250 °F). Place steaks on a wire rack over a rimmed baking sheet.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of one steak into its centre.
4. Roast in the low oven until internal temperature reaches 5 °C below final target.
5. **Pull from oven at 48–50 °C (118–122 °F)** (to hit medium-rare after the sear).
6. Pat the surface dry (the low oven has dehydrated it — ideal for the sear).
7. Heat a cast-iron pan over maximum heat; add oil; sear 45–60 seconds per side.
8. Final serving temperature: **53–57 °C / 127–135 °F** (medium-rare, reverse-sear standard).

### 2. Heston Blumenthal — Ultra-Low Oven Sirloin (British Modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 45–60 min at 50 °C oven + 2 min sear · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 sirloin steaks, 350–450 g each, 4–5 cm thick
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) high-smoke-point oil (for final sear)

**Method**
1. Season steaks; place on a wire rack.
2. Set oven to exactly **50 °C (122 °F)** — the target internal temperature. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of one steak into its centre.
3. Cook in the 50 °C oven for 45–60 minutes. The oven cannot overshoot since it matches the target temperature. The surface becomes extremely dry — ideal for searing.
4. Heat a cast-iron pan to maximum; add oil; sear each side 60–90 seconds.
5. **Pull from oven at 50 °C (122 °F)** — the core will not exceed the oven temperature. Final serving temperature: **50–52 °C / 122–126 °F** at the core (rare to medium-rare, Blumenthal ultra-low standard).

### 3. Gordon Ramsay — Sear-and-Oven Sirloin (British restaurant tradition)
**Source**: Gordon Ramsay, *Gordon Ramsay's Ultimate Cookery Course* (Hodder & Stoughton, 2012)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 4 min sear + 8–10 min oven · **Rest**: 5 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 sirloin steaks, 300–400 g each
- Sea salt and freshly cracked black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 3 garlic cloves, skin-on
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 200 °C (400 °F). Season steaks.
2. Heat an oven-proof pan over very high heat on the stovetop. Add oil.
3. Sear steaks 2 minutes per side on the stovetop.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side into the centre after searing.
5. Add butter, garlic and thyme; baste. Transfer entire pan to the oven.
6. Roast 6–8 minutes, basting once.
7. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** Carryover from a 200 °C oven will bring steak to medium-rare on resting.
8. Rest 5 minutes. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 130–135 °F** (medium-rare, restaurant standard).

### 4. Alice Waters — Oven-Roasted Sirloin (American / California tradition)
**Source**: Alice Waters, *The Chez Panisse Menu Cookbook* (Random House, 1982)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min (+overnight dry-brine) · **Cook**: 15–20 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 whole top sirloin steak, 1–1.5 kg, approximately 4–5 cm thick
- Coarse sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) extra virgin olive oil

**Method**
1. Dry-brine whole sirloin overnight with coarse salt; refrigerate uncovered.
2. Preheat oven to 230 °C (450 °F). Remove steak from fridge 30 minutes before cooking.
3. Heat a large oven-proof frying pan over high heat on the stovetop; add olive oil.
4. Sear the steak 2 minutes per side.
5. Insert the MEATER probe into the centre of the meat from the thickest side.
6. Transfer pan to the oven. Roast 12–15 minutes.
7. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** Waters' farm-to-table standard: medium-rare, bright pink centre.
8. Rest 10 minutes. Slice against the grain. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, California standard).

### 5. Fergus Henderson — Roasted Sirloin (British nose-to-tail tradition)
**Source**: Fergus Henderson, *The Complete Nose to Tail* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2012)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 20–25 min · **Rest**: 10 min

**Ingredients**
- 1 thick sirloin steak or small sirloin roast, 1–1.2 kg
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) beef dripping

**Method**
1. Preheat oven to 220 °C (425 °F). Season the sirloin generously.
2. Heat an oven-proof pan with dripping over high heat; sear the sirloin 2–3 minutes per side on the stovetop.
3. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end into the centre of the meat.
4. Transfer to the oven. Roast 15–20 minutes.
5. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Henderson's British standard: "beef must be pink."
6. Rest 10 minutes. Slice and serve with roasting juices. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium-rare, British standard).
