# Salmon Fillet × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Salmon fillet comes from the side of the fish, running the full length of the body from behind the head to the tail, with the pin bones removed. The flesh is divided into distinct fat-layered muscle segments (myotomes) separated by connective tissue (myocommata); that connective tissue softens and gives the characteristic flaking texture when the protein reaches its set point. Atlantic and Pacific salmon differ in fat distribution — farmed Atlantic salmon is uniformly rich, while wild sockeye and king salmon have denser, more mineral flesh. Pan searing suits salmon because the high conductive heat of a stainless or cast-iron pan rapidly crisps the skin (if skin-on) and caramelises the exposed flesh, producing textural contrast between crust and interior. Carryover in a seared salmon fillet is modest — 2–3 °C — because the thin geometry dissipates heat quickly, so accurate pull timing is essential to avoid overcooking.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Crispy-Skin Pan-Seared Salmon (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/the-best-way-to-cook-salmon  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 7–9 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets, 170–200 g each, approximately 3 cm at the thickest point
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 2 g (¼ tsp) freshly ground black pepper
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or canola oil
- 15 g (1 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 1 lemon, halved, for serving

**Method**
1. Pat salmon fillets completely dry with paper towels — surface moisture is the primary barrier to crispy skin. Season flesh side with salt and pepper; leave skin side unseasoned until just before cooking.
2. Heat a stainless-steel or cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat for 3 minutes. Add the oil and heat until shimmering and just beginning to smoke.
3. Season the skin side lightly with salt. Place salmon skin-side down in the pan. Press each fillet firmly with a fish spatula for the first 10 seconds to prevent curling.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the side of the thicker end of the fillet, angling toward the centre of the flesh — avoid the skin layer and the thin tapered tail end.
5. Cook undisturbed, skin-side down, for 5–6 minutes. The flesh will turn opaque from the bottom upward; when the opaque line reaches three-quarters up the fillet, flip.
6. Flip and cook flesh-side down for 30–60 seconds only.
7. Add butter; baste briefly with foaming butter.
8. **Pull at 50–52 °C (122–126 °F).** A translucent pink centre remains at this temperature; Kenji argues this is the optimal point for Atlantic farmed salmon — fully safe, maximally moist.
9. Rest 2 minutes. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (medium — warm, moist, slightly translucent centre).

### 2. Michel Roux Jr. — Saumon Poêlé au Beurre Blanc (French classical tradition)
**Source**: Michel Roux Jr., *The French Kitchen* (Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 2009)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets (ideally wild Atlantic or Pacific king), 180–200 g each
- Fine sea salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) clarified butter or vegetable oil
- **Beurre blanc**: 1 shallot, finely minced; 60 ml (¼ cup) dry white wine; 30 ml (2 tbsp) white wine vinegar; 120 g cold unsalted butter, cubed; fine salt and white pepper

**Method**
1. Score the skin of each salmon fillet 3–4 times at an angle with a sharp knife to prevent curling. Season flesh side with fine salt and white pepper.
2. Prepare the beurre blanc: sweat the shallot in a small saucepan with wine and vinegar until reduced by two-thirds. Over low heat, whisk in the cold butter cube by cube — the sauce should never boil. Season and strain through a fine sieve. Keep warm.
3. Heat a heavy sauté pan over high heat until very hot. Add the clarified butter; it should smoke gently.
4. Place salmon skin-side down. Press briefly with a spatula.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet, angled into the centre of the flesh, parallel to the skin.
6. Cook skin-side down for 5–6 minutes — Roux's standard is to cook almost entirely on the skin side and turn only briefly at the end.
7. Flip for 30 seconds only. **Pull at 52 °C (126 °F).** The French classical kitchen prefers salmon à peine cuit (just-cooked) with a glassy, slightly translucent centre.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Spoon beurre blanc around, not over, the salmon. Final serving temperature: **54–55 °C / 130–131 °F** (just-cooked, *à peine cuit*).

### 3. Nobu Matsuhisa — Pan-Seared Salmon with Miso Marinade (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa and Mark Edwards, *Nobu: The Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2001)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+overnight marinade) · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 salmon fillets, 180–200 g each (skin-on or skinless)
- **Den miso marinade**: 60 ml (¼ cup) sake; 60 ml (¼ cup) mirin; 60 g (¼ cup) white *shiro miso* (*shiro miso* — pale, sweet Japanese fermented soybean paste); 60 g (¼ cup) caster sugar
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or rice bran oil
- Pickled ginger (*gari*) and shiso leaves, to serve

**Method**
1. Prepare den miso: bring sake and mirin to a boil in a small saucepan; flambe to burn off alcohol. Whisk in miso and sugar; stir over low heat until smooth and glossy, about 3 minutes. Cool completely.
2. Coat salmon fillets generously with den miso. Marinate in the fridge for a minimum of 6 hours, ideally 24–48 hours.
3. Scrape most of the marinade from the surface before cooking — the residual sugars will caramelise; excess marinade burns.
4. Heat a non-stick or well-seasoned cast-iron pan over medium heat (not high — the miso sugars scorch easily). Add oil.
5. Insert the MEATER probe from the thicker side of the fillet into the centre of the flesh.
6. Cook 3–4 minutes per side, watching the crust — the golden-brown miso lacquer should develop slowly without burning.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** The sugar coating extends apparent cook time; the interior may appear less cooked than the caramelised exterior suggests.
8. Rest 2 minutes. Serve with pickled ginger. Final serving temperature: **54–57 °C / 130–135 °F** (medium, Nobu standard).

### 4. Heston Blumenthal — Seared Salmon with Rendered Skin (Modernist British tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 8–10 min · **Rest**: 3 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets, 200 g each, even thickness preferred
- Fine salt
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) vegetable oil
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 1 sprig thyme
- 1 garlic clove, crushed

**Method**
1. Score the skin lightly, 3–4 cuts. Dry the skin thoroughly — Blumenthal uses kitchen paper pressed firmly against the skin for 2 minutes to wick away subcutaneous moisture.
2. Season flesh side lightly with fine salt. Do not season skin — unseasoned skin renders more evenly.
3. Heat a non-stick pan over medium-low heat. Add oil. Place salmon skin-side down.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end, parallel to the skin, into the centre of the flesh.
5. Press gently with a spatula every 60 seconds for the first 3 minutes.
6. Cook skin-side down for 7–8 minutes total on medium-low heat — the slow render blisters the skin from beneath without browning the flesh through.
7. Add butter, thyme and garlic. Tilt pan; baste flesh briefly.
8. **Pull at 48–50 °C (118–122 °F).** Blumenthal targets the lower range, arguing that salmon's protein is at its most agreeable just below conventional medium-rare.
9. Rest 3 minutes skin-side up. Final serving temperature: **50–52 °C / 122–126 °F** (medium-rare, translucent pink centre).

### 5. Neil Perry — Pan-Fried Salmon with Herbs (Australian tradition)
**Source**: Neil Perry, *The Food I Love* (Murdoch Books, 2005)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 5 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 2 min

**Ingredients**
- 2 skin-on salmon fillets, 200 g each (Australian Atlantic salmon or ocean trout)
- Sea salt flakes
- Freshly ground black pepper
- 20 ml (1½ tbsp) extra virgin olive oil
- 20 g unsalted butter
- Juice of 1 lemon
- Fresh dill or chervil, to serve

**Method**
1. Pat fillets dry. Season flesh side with sea salt flakes and black pepper.
2. Heat a heavy stainless-steel or cast-iron pan over high heat until very hot. Add olive oil.
3. Place salmon skin-side down. Press firmly for 5–10 seconds to prevent curling.
4. Insert the MEATER probe from the thick end of the fillet into the centre of the flesh, clear of the skin layer.
5. Cook skin-side down, undisturbed, 4–5 minutes — until the flesh is opaque two-thirds of the way up the fillet.
6. Flip. Add butter; cook flesh-side down for 1–2 minutes.
7. Squeeze lemon juice into the pan; it will sizzle and lift caramelised bits.
8. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Perry's style targets medium — a warm, opaque but still moist centre, slightly more cooked than the French preference.
9. Rest 2 minutes. Scatter herbs. Final serving temperature: **55–57 °C / 131–135 °F** (medium, Australian standard).
