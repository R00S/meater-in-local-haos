# Scallops × Pan Sear — Recipe Temperature Research

## Cut profile

Sea scallops (*Pecten maximus* in Europe; *Placopecten magellanicus*, the Atlantic sea scallop, in North America) are the large adductor muscle that opens and closes the bivalve shell. The flesh is cream-white, sweet, and extremely tender with almost no connective tissue; it is composed mainly of striated muscle fibres held in a dense but delicate matrix. Pan searing is the defining method for large sea scallops because extreme heat produces rapid caramelisation of the natural sugars on the flat cut surfaces — the golden-brown crust contrasts dramatically with the cool, almost raw centre that is the mark of a well-executed scallop. Overcooking tightens the fibres sharply and produces a rubbery, squeaky texture. The critical requirements are: completely dry exterior, a blazing pan, and a very short cook time. Carryover is significant relative to the small mass — 3–5 °C — so pull early.

## Source recipes

### 1. J. Kenji López-Alt — Pan-Seared Scallops with Brown Butter (American tradition)
**Source**: J. Kenji López-Alt, *The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science* (W. W. Norton & Company, 2015); seriouseats.com/the-best-pan-seared-scallops-recipe  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min dry rest) · **Cook**: 3–4 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 450 g (1 lb) large dry-packed sea scallops (10–12 count per pound), side muscle removed
- 5 g (1 tsp) kosher salt
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable or canola oil
- 30 g (2 tbsp) unsalted butter
- 2 garlic cloves, skin-on, lightly smashed
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme
- Lemon wedges and flaky sea salt, to serve

**Method**
1. Peel the tough crescent-shaped side muscle from each scallop. Pat completely dry with paper towels. Place on a wire rack and refrigerate uncovered for 30 minutes — surface dryness is the single most important factor in achieving a crust.
2. Season scallops with kosher salt on both flat faces just before cooking.
3. Heat a stainless-steel or cast-iron skillet over the highest heat for 4–5 minutes. Add the oil; it should smoke within 2–3 seconds.
4. Place scallops flat-side down in the pan, working clockwise from 12 o'clock. Do NOT touch or move them. The clock order allows you to flip them in the same order they went in, giving each scallop exactly the same sear time.
5. Note: MEATER probe placement for scallops is challenging given their small size. For large scallops (>40 g), insert the probe from the flat top surface straight down through the centre of the scallop. For smaller scallops, use the first-flip visual cue (golden-brown base) as the primary timing indicator.
6. After 90 seconds, check the first scallop — the bottom should be deep golden-brown. If not, wait another 20–30 seconds.
7. Flip scallops in order. Add butter, garlic and thyme. Baste as the butter foams.
8. Cook 60–90 seconds on the second side — the second side cooks faster; the scallop should have a slight give when pressed, not completely firm.
9. **Pull at 46–50 °C (115–122 °F).** Kenji's published target for scallops is medium — warm, barely opaque at the deepest centre, with a glassy translucency just inside the crust.
10. Rest 1 minute. Sprinkle with flaky salt; serve with lemon. Final serving temperature: **50–54 °C / 122–130 °F** (medium, warm translucent centre).

### 2. Thomas Keller — Seared Diver Scallops with Cauliflower Purée (French haute cuisine tradition)
**Source**: Thomas Keller, *Bouchon* (Artisan Books, 2004)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 4–5 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 16 large diver-harvested sea scallops (U10 — under 10 per pound, approximately 50–60 g each), side muscles removed
- Fine salt and freshly ground white pepper
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) grapeseed or canola oil
- 60 g (4 tbsp) unsalted butter, divided
- **Cauliflower purée**: 1 small cauliflower; 30 g butter; salt and white pepper (make separately — cook cauliflower until very tender; blend with butter until silky)

**Method**
1. Pat scallops completely dry; season both flat faces with fine salt and white pepper immediately before cooking.
2. Heat a heavy stainless-steel pan over high heat 4 minutes. Add oil; heat until wisps of smoke appear.
3. Place scallops flat-side down, evenly spaced. Resist any urge to move them.
4. For large U10 scallops, insert the MEATER probe straight down from the flat top surface into the centre of the scallop after placing in the pan, before the crust sets.
5. Cook undisturbed 90–120 seconds — the crust should be deep golden-brown when lifted.
6. Add half the butter; tilt pan and baste. Flip scallops.
7. Cook second side 60 seconds while continuing to baste. Add remaining butter.
8. **Pull at 48–50 °C (118–122 °F).** Keller's Bouchon standard for scallops is a warm, barely opaque centre — he describes it as "translucent at the deepest point, the texture barely resistant."
9. Rest 1 minute on a warm plate. Serve over cauliflower purée. Final serving temperature: **52–54 °C / 126–130 °F** (medium-rare to medium, warm barely-set centre).

### 3. Heston Blumenthal — Seared Scallops with Caramelised Cauliflower (British Modernist tradition)
**Source**: Heston Blumenthal, *Heston Blumenthal at Home* (Bloomsbury Publishing, 2011)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min (+30 min air-dry) · **Cook**: 3–4 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 8 large hand-dived sea scallops, roe on or off (Blumenthal usually removes roe for this preparation to ensure even cooking of the adductor muscle)
- Fine salt
- 30 ml (2 tbsp) sunflower oil
- 40 g unsalted butter
- 1 vanilla pod, split (Blumenthal's signature pairing — vanilla's aromatic compounds complement scallop sweetness)
- Fleur de sel, to finish

**Method**
1. Remove roe and side muscles. Pat scallops absolutely dry; spread on a wire rack and leave uncovered in the fridge for 30 minutes.
2. Season flat faces with fine salt immediately before cooking.
3. Heat a stainless-steel pan over high heat 3–4 minutes. Add oil; heat until faintly smoking.
4. Place scallops flat-side down. Do not move.
5. For large scallops, insert the MEATER probe straight down through the top flat surface into the centre.
6. After 90 seconds, the underside should be uniformly golden. Flip.
7. Add butter and vanilla pod. Baste continuously for 60 seconds.
8. **Pull at 46–48 °C (115–118 °F).** Blumenthal's target is rare to medium-rare — the deepest centre should remain cool and semi-translucent, barely warmed.
9. Rest 1 minute. Finish with fleur de sel. Final serving temperature: **48–52 °C / 118–126 °F** (medium-rare, cool translucent interior, golden crusted exterior).

### 4. Nobu Matsuhisa — Seared Scallops with Wasabi Salsa (Japanese tradition)
**Source**: Nobu Matsuhisa and Mark Edwards, *Nobu: The Cookbook* (Kodansha International, 2001)  
**Serves**: 4 · **Prep**: 15 min · **Cook**: 3 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 16 large sea scallops (*hotate*, the large Japanese scallop prized in Japanese cuisine), side muscle removed
- 5 g (1 tsp) fine salt
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) vegetable oil
- **Wasabi salsa**: 10 g (2 tsp) wasabi paste; 30 ml (2 tbsp) soy sauce; 15 ml (1 tbsp) fresh lemon juice; 15 ml (1 tbsp) olive oil; 5 ml (1 tsp) mirin; 30 g finely diced red onion; 30 g finely diced tomato
- Micro shiso (*shiso* — perilla) or thin shiso strips, to garnish

**Method**
1. Combine wasabi salsa ingredients; set aside.
2. Pat scallops completely dry. Season lightly with fine salt on both flat faces.
3. Heat a carbon-steel pan over the highest heat. Add oil; it should smoke immediately.
4. Place scallops flat-side down. For large *hotate*, insert the MEATER probe straight down through the flat top into the centre.
5. Sear undisturbed 60–90 seconds. Flip.
6. Sear second side 45–60 seconds.
7. **Pull at 44–46 °C (111–115 °F).** Nobu's preference is for scallops that are rare at the core — the Japanese tradition (*hotate no teppan yaki*) prizes the raw-silk texture of barely-warmed scallop centre.
8. Rest 1 minute. Plate with wasabi salsa spooned around base. Garnish with shiso. Final serving temperature: **46–50 °C / 115–122 °F** (rare to medium-rare, raw-silk centre, Nobu standard).

### 5. Rick Stein — Hand-Dived Scallops with Bacon and Black Pudding (British seafood tradition)
**Source**: Rick Stein, *Rick Stein's Seafood* (BBC Books, 2001)  
**Serves**: 2 · **Prep**: 10 min · **Cook**: 6–8 min · **Rest**: 1 min

**Ingredients**
- 8 large hand-dived scallops, roe on (British tradition retains the roe as a delicacy), side muscle removed
- 4 rashers smoked streaky bacon (*rashers* — thin British-cut bacon strips)
- 100 g black pudding (*black pudding* — British blood sausage), cut into 5 mm slices
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 15 ml (1 tbsp) sunflower or vegetable oil
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- Lemon juice, to finish

**Method**
1. Fry bacon rashers in a dry pan until crisp; drain. Fry black pudding slices in the same pan until crisp on both sides; drain.
2. Pat scallops dry; season both flat faces with salt and pepper.
3. Heat a clean heavy pan over high heat. Add oil and half the butter.
4. Place scallops (and roe, separately if large) flat-side down. For large scallops, insert the MEATER probe straight down through the top flat surface into the adductor muscle centre.
5. Sear 90 seconds without moving — deep golden crust is essential.
6. Flip scallops; add remaining butter. Sear 60 seconds.
7. **Pull at 52–54 °C (126–130 °F).** Stein's British seafood tradition targets a fully cooked but still just-tender scallop — he notes that the roe (which is slightly less dense) cooks faster and should be monitored separately.
8. Rest 1 minute. Plate with bacon and black pudding alongside; finish with a squeeze of lemon. Final serving temperature: **54–56 °C / 130–133 °F** (medium to medium-well, fully opaque, British standard).
